# Kidney Stone Disease (Nephrolithiasis) - Comprehensive Clinical Guide

## Metadata
**Core Domain:** Clinical Nephrology I
**Difficulty Level:** Intermediate
**Frequency of Use:** Daily-Weekly
**Clinical Impact:** High (affects ~10% of population)
**Regulatory Requirements:** None specific
**Prerequisites:** Renal anatomy, urinary tract physiology, metabolic disorders
**Competency Development Time:** 6-12 months
**Cross-references:** Tubular Disorders (Domain I.4), Electrolytes (Domain I.6), Nutrition (Domain I.19), Genetic Diseases (Domain I.14)

---

## 1. Epidemiology & Risk Factors

### Prevalence & Incidence
- **Lifetime prevalence**: 10-12% in general population
- **Gender**: Men > Women (2-3:1 ratio)
- **Age**: Peak incidence 30-50 years
- **Geographic variation**: Higher in "stone belt" (Southeast US)
- **Recurrence**: 50% within 10 years without preventive therapy

### Risk Factors

**Non-Modifiable:**
- Male gender
- White race > Black race
- Family history (2-3× increased risk if first-degree relative)
- Personal history of prior stones
- Genetic disorders (cystinuria, primary hyperoxaluria)

**Modifiable:**
- Low fluid intake (most important)
- High sodium diet
- High animal protein diet
- Low calcium diet (paradoxically increases risk)
- Obesity (BMI >30)
- Diabetes mellitus
- Metabolic syndrome
- Chronic diarrheal states
- Medications (see below)

**Medications Associated with Stones:**
- Topiramate (calcium phosphate stones)
- Acetazolamide (calcium phosphate)
- Triamterene (triamterene stones)
- Indinavir, atazanavir (protease inhibitor stones)
- Excessive vitamin C (oxalate stones)
- Excessive vitamin D (calcium stones)

## 2. Stone Composition & Pathophysiology

### Stone Types & Frequencies

**Calcium Stones (70-80% of all stones):**

**1. Calcium Oxalate (60-70%)**
- Most common stone type
- **Pathophysiology**: Supersaturation of urine with calcium + oxalate
- **Risk factors**: Hypercalciuria, hyperoxaluria, hypocitraturia, low urine volume
- **Appearance**: Dark brown/black, hard
- **X-ray**: Radiopaque

**2. Calcium Phosphate (5-10%)**
- Often mixed with calcium oxalate
- **Pathophysiology**: High urinary pH, hypercalciuria
- **Associations**: Renal tubular acidosis (distal RTA), primary hyperparathyroidism
- **Appearance**: White/light tan
- **X-ray**: Radiopaque

**Uric Acid Stones (5-10%):**
- **Pathophysiology**: Low urinary pH (<5.5), hyperuricosuria
- **Risk factors**: Gout, high purine diet, chronic diarrhea, metabolic syndrome
- **Appearance**: Orange/red, smooth
- **X-ray**: **Radiolucent** (NOT visible on plain X-ray)
- **Unique feature**: Can be dissolved with urinary alkalinization

**Struvite Stones (Infection/Magnesium Ammonium Phosphate, 10-15%):**
- **Pathophysiology**: Urinary tract infection with urease-producing bacteria
  - Proteus, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas, Staphylococcus saprophyticus
  - Urease splits urea → ammonia → alkaline urine (pH >7.2)
- **Classic presentation**: Staghorn calculi (fill renal pelvis)
- **Appearance**: Dirty white, friable
- **X-ray**: Radiopaque
- **Treatment challenge**: Require surgical removal + antibiotics (cannot pass spontaneously)

**Cystine Stones (1-2%):**
- **Pathophysiology**: Genetic defect (autosomal recessive cystinuria)
  - Defective renal tubular reabsorption of cystine, ornithine, lysine, arginine (COLA)
  - Only cystine forms stones (poorly soluble)
- **Appearance**: Yellow/brown, hexagonal crystals on urinalysis
- **X-ray**: Faintly radiopaque
- **Diagnosis**: Cyanide-nitroprusside test positive, 24-hour urine cystine >250 mg/day

**Drug-Induced Stones (Rare, <1%):**
- Triamterene, indinavir, atazanavir, acyclovir (high dose IV)
- **Appearance**: Drug-specific
- **X-ray**: Usually radiolucent

## 3. Clinical Presentation

### Acute Renal Colic

**Classic Triad:**
1. Sudden-onset severe flank pain (10/10)
2. Colicky/intermittent pain radiating to groin
3. Hematuria (gross or microscopic)

**Pain Characteristics:**
- **Location**: Flank (costovertebral angle) → radiates to lower abdomen → groin/testicle/labia
- **Quality**: Severe, colicky (waxes and wanes)
- **Aggravating factors**: Movement does NOT worsen (vs. peritonitis)
- **Patient behavior**: Restless, cannot find comfortable position (vs. peritonitis - still)

**Associated Symptoms:**
- Nausea, vomiting (70-80%)
- Urinary urgency, frequency
- Dysuria (if stone at ureterovesical junction)
- Fever/chills (if concurrent infection - urologic emergency)

**Physical Examination:**
- Severe distress, restlessness
- Costovertebral angle tenderness
- Abdominal exam: Benign (no peritoneal signs)
- Fever: Suggests obstruction + infection (emergency)

### Asymptomatic Nephrolithiasis
- Found incidentally on imaging
- 10-20% of stone formers
- Risk of future symptomatic episodes

## 4. Diagnostic Evaluation

### Acute Setting (Emergency Department)

**Urinalysis:**
- **Hematuria**: Present in 85-90% (absence does NOT rule out stones)
- **Crystalluria**: May see crystals (calcium oxalate, uric acid, cystine)
- **Pyuria/bacteriuria**: Suggests infection (treat urgently)
- **pH**:
  - <5.5: Uric acid stones
  - >7.2: Struvite (infection) stones
  - 6.5-7.0: Calcium phosphate, RTA

**Basic Labs:**
- CBC: Leukocytosis if infection
- Creatinine: Assess renal function
- Calcium, phosphorus, uric acid: Screen for metabolic causes
- If infection suspected: Blood cultures, urine culture

**Imaging:**

**Non-Contrast CT Abdomen/Pelvis (GOLD STANDARD):**
- **Sensitivity**: 95-98%
- **Specificity**: 96-100%
- **Advantages**:
  - Detects ALL stone types (including radiolucent uric acid)
  - Measures stone size, location
  - Rules out alternative diagnoses (appendicitis, AAA)
  - Assesses degree of hydronephrosis
- **Stone characteristics**:
  - Size: Measured in millimeters
  - Location: Kidney, ureter (proximal/mid/distal), bladder
  - Hounsfield units (HU): Predicts fragility (high HU = harder)
- **Limitations**: Radiation exposure (~10 mSv), cost

**Renal Ultrasound:**
- **Use**: First-line in pregnancy, children, young adults
- **Sensitivity**: 60-70% for ureteral stones (better for renal stones)
- **Advantages**: No radiation, detects hydronephrosis
- **Limitations**: Misses small stones, operator-dependent

**Plain KUB (Kidney-Ureter-Bladder X-ray):**
- **Detects**: Radiopaque stones only (NOT uric acid)
- **Use**: Monitor stone passage if CT already done
- **Sensitivity**: 45-60%
- **Largely replaced by CT**

### Metabolic Evaluation (Outpatient)

**Indications for Full Metabolic Workup:**
- First stone in high-risk patients: Children, young adults (<25 years), family history
- Recurrent stone formers (≥2 stones)
- Single kidney
- Nephrocalcinosis
- Cystine or uric acid stones
- Infection stones (struvite)
- GI disease (inflammatory bowel disease, gastric bypass)

**Timing**: 6-8 weeks after acute event (not during acute episode)

**24-Hour Urine Collection (×2 collections for accuracy):**

**Standard Panel:**
- **Volume**: Goal >2 L/day (most important preventive factor)
- **Calcium**: Normal <250 mg/day (women), <300 mg/day (men)
  - Hypercalciuria: >250-300 mg/day
- **Oxalate**: Normal <40-45 mg/day
  - Hyperoxaluria: >45 mg/day
- **Citrate**: Normal >320 mg/day (women), >450 mg/day (men)
  - Hypocitraturia: Low levels (citrate inhibits crystallization)
- **Uric acid**: Normal <800 mg/day
  - Hyperuricosuria: >800 mg/day
- **Sodium**: Normal <100 mEq/day
  - High sodium increases calcium excretion
- **Creatinine**: Confirms adequate collection (20-25 mg/kg/day)
- **pH**: Measured on fresh sample
- **Cystine**: If cystine stones suspected (normal <30 mg/day)

**Serum Tests:**
- Calcium, phosphorus, PTH (rule out hyperparathyroidism)
- Creatinine, eGFR
- Uric acid
- Electrolytes, bicarbonate (screen for RTA)
- 25-OH vitamin D
- If hypercalciuria + hypercalcemia: Check PTH, vitamin D intoxication, sarcoidosis, malignancy

## 5. Acute Management

### Pain Control

**First-Line: NSAIDs (Preferred over opioids):**
- **Ketorolac**: 30-60 mg IV/IM initial, then 15-30 mg q6h
- **Ibuprofen**: 600-800 mg PO q6-8h
- **Mechanism**: Reduces renal blood flow, decreases ureteral spasm
- **Evidence**: NSAIDs superior to opioids for renal colic pain
- **Contraindications**: CKD stage 4-5, GI bleeding, allergy

**Second-Line: Opioids (if NSAIDs contraindicated):**
- Morphine 2-10 mg IV q2-4h
- Hydromorphone 0.5-2 mg IV q2-4h
- **Caution**: Nausea/vomiting, does not address underlying pathophysiology

**Adjunct: Antiemetics:**
- Ondansetron 4-8 mg IV (if vomiting)
- Metoclopramide 10 mg IV (dual antiemetic + prokinetic)

### Medical Expulsive Therapy (MET)

**Indications:**
- Stone <10 mm (especially 5-10 mm)
- Distal ureteral location
- No infection, normal renal function
- Tolerable pain

**Medication:**
- **Tamsulosin (Flomax) 0.4 mg PO daily**
  - Alpha-blocker, relaxes ureteral smooth muscle
  - **Evidence**: Increases stone passage rate 29% → 47% (NNT = 5)
  - **Duration**: Until stone passes or 4-6 weeks
- **Alternative**: Nifedipine 30 mg PO daily (calcium channel blocker)

**Stone Passage Prediction:**
- **Size**:
  - <5 mm: 90% pass spontaneously within 4 weeks
  - 5-7 mm: 50-60% pass within 6 weeks
  - >7 mm: 20-30% pass (usually require intervention)
- **Location**:
  - Proximal ureter: Lower passage rate
  - Distal ureter: Higher passage rate

### Urgent/Emergent Indications for Urology Consultation

**Immediate Intervention Required:**
1. **Obstructed infected kidney** (pyonephrosis) - TRUE EMERGENCY
   - Fever + obstructing stone = sepsis risk
   - Requires emergent decompression (PCN or ureteral stent) + antibiotics
   - Mortality risk if delayed
2. **Bilateral obstruction** or obstruction in solitary kidney
   - Risk of acute kidney injury/renal failure
3. **Intractable pain** despite maximal medical therapy
4. **Intractable nausea/vomiting** → dehydration

**Urgent (24-48 hours):**
- Stone >10 mm (unlikely to pass)
- Persistent symptoms >4-6 weeks despite MET
- High-grade obstruction with declining renal function

## 6. Surgical/Interventional Management

### Indications for Intervention
- Stone >10 mm
- Failed medical expulsive therapy (>4-6 weeks)
- Persistent pain
- Infection + obstruction
- Progressive hydronephrosis
- Bilateral obstruction
- Solitary kidney obstruction
- Declining renal function

### Treatment Options

**1. Extracorporeal Shock Wave Lithotripsy (ESWL):**
- **Mechanism**: Shock waves fragment stone externally
- **Indications**:
  - Renal stones <2 cm
  - Proximal ureteral stones <1 cm
  - Radiopaque stones (better fragmentation)
- **Advantages**: Non-invasive, outpatient, no anesthesia
- **Disadvantages**:
  - Lower stone-free rate vs ureteroscopy (60-80%)
  - Multiple sessions may be needed
  - Fragments must pass (pain, obstruction risk)
  - Less effective for hard stones (cystine, calcium oxalate monohydrate)
- **Contraindications**: Pregnancy, bleeding disorders, anatomic obstruction
- **Complications**: Hematuria, pain from fragments, steinstrasse (stone street - obstructing fragments)

**2. Ureteroscopy (URS) with Laser Lithotripsy:**
- **Mechanism**: Endoscopic access via urethra → bladder → ureter → kidney
  - Laser (holmium:YAG) fragments stone, fragments extracted or left to pass
- **Indications**:
  - Ureteral stones (any size)
  - Renal stones <2 cm (especially lower pole)
  - ESWL failure
  - Hard stones (cystine, calcium oxalate monohydrate)
- **Advantages**:
  - Higher stone-free rate (90-95%)
  - Immediate visualization
  - Can treat ureteral strictures concurrently
- **Disadvantages**: Invasive, anesthesia required, ureteral stent usually needed
- **Complications**: Ureteral perforation/injury, bleeding, stricture, stent discomfort

**3. Percutaneous Nephrolithotomy (PCNL):**
- **Mechanism**: Percutaneous access to kidney → nephroscopy → stone fragmentation and extraction
- **Indications**:
  - Large stones (>2 cm)
  - Staghorn calculi (struvite)
  - ESWL/URS failure
  - Cystine stones (hard, ESWL-resistant)
- **Advantages**:
  - Highest stone-free rate for large stones (80-95%)
  - Can remove large burden in single session
- **Disadvantages**: Most invasive, general anesthesia, hospitalization
- **Complications**: Bleeding (may require transfusion), infection, adjacent organ injury (pleura, colon), nephrostomy tube required

**4. Open/Laparoscopic Surgery:**
- **Rare** (<1% of cases)
- **Indications**: Complex anatomy, failed other methods, concurrent need for reconstructive surgery

**Stone-Free Rates by Modality:**
| Stone Size | ESWL | URS | PCNL |
|-----------|------|-----|------|
| <10 mm | 80-90% | 95% | N/A |
| 10-20 mm | 60-70% | 85-90% | 90-95% |
| >20 mm | 40-50% | 60-70% | 80-90% |

## 7. Prevention & Long-Term Management

### Universal Recommendations (ALL Stone Formers)

**1. Increase Fluid Intake (MOST IMPORTANT):**
- **Goal**: Urine output >2 L/day (ideally 2.5-3 L)
- **Practical**: Drink enough to produce clear/pale yellow urine
- **Distribution**: Spread throughout day, including before bed
- **Evidence**: 50% reduction in recurrence with high fluid intake

**2. Dietary Sodium Restriction:**
- **Goal**: <2,300 mg/day (ideally <1,500 mg/day)
- **Mechanism**: High sodium → increased urinary calcium excretion
- **Effect**: 10 mEq Na → 0.6 mmol (24 mg) increased urinary calcium

**3. Normal Calcium Intake (NOT Low):**
- **Goal**: 1,000-1,200 mg/day (dietary sources)
- **Mechanism**: Dietary calcium binds intestinal oxalate → reduces oxalate absorption
- **Paradox**: Low calcium diet INCREASES stone risk
- **Avoid**: Calcium supplements between meals (increases risk)

**4. Limit Animal Protein:**
- **Goal**: 0.8-1 g/kg/day
- **Mechanism**: High protein → increased calcium, uric acid, decreased citrate excretion
- **Sources to limit**: Red meat, poultry, fish, eggs

**5. Limit Oxalate-Rich Foods:**
- **High-oxalate foods** (limit, don't eliminate):
  - Spinach, rhubarb, beets
  - Nuts (almonds, cashews, peanuts)
  - Chocolate, tea (black tea)
  - Wheat bran, soy products
- **Note**: Moderate limitation only, not elimination

---

### Stone Type-Specific Management

**Calcium Oxalate Stones:**

**1. Hypercalciuria (urine Ca >250-300 mg/day):**
- **First-line: Thiazide diuretics**
  - Hydrochlorothiazide 25-50 mg daily OR chlorthalidone 25 mg daily
  - Mechanism: Increases renal tubular calcium reabsorption
  - Reduces urinary calcium by 30-50%
  - Side effects: Hypokalemia (also decreases urine citrate), hyperglycemia, hyperuricemia
  - **Monitor**: K+, consider potassium citrate supplementation
- **Second-line: Amiloride** 5-10 mg daily (if thiazide not tolerated)
- Dietary sodium restriction (essential for thiazide efficacy)

**2. Hyperoxaluria (urine oxalate >45 mg/day):**
- **Dietary oxalate restriction** (see above)
- **Increase dietary calcium** (binds intestinal oxalate)
- **Enteric hyperoxaluria** (IBD, gastric bypass, chronic diarrhea):
  - Calcium citrate 400-600 mg with meals (binds oxalate)
  - Cholestyramine (if bile acid malabsorption)
  - Treat underlying GI disease
- **Primary hyperoxaluria** (genetic):
  - Pyridoxine (vitamin B6) 25-400 mg/day (Type 1 only)
  - High fluid intake
  - Consider lumasiran (RNAi therapy, FDA approved 2020 for PH1)

**3. Hypocitraturia (urine citrate <320-450 mg/day):**
- **Potassium citrate** 10-20 mEq PO BID-TID
  - Goal: Urine citrate >320 mg/day
  - Mechanism: Citrate is stone inhibitor (chelates calcium)
  - Also treats hypokalemia from thiazides
  - Side effects: GI upset (take with meals)
  - **Monitor**: Urine pH (avoid >7.5, risk of calcium phosphate stones)
- **Lemonade therapy**: 4 oz lemon juice in 2 L water daily (alternative, less effective)

**Calcium Phosphate Stones:**
- Often associated with **distal renal tubular acidosis (dRTA)**
- **Workup**: Check serum bicarbonate, urine pH
- **If dRTA**: Potassium citrate to correct acidosis (goal urine pH 6.5-7.0)
- **Avoid**: Over-alkalinization (worsens calcium phosphate precipitation)
- **Thiazides** if hypercalciuria

**Uric Acid Stones:**

**1. Urinary Alkalinization (MOST IMPORTANT):**
- **Goal**: Urine pH 6.0-6.5 (sweet spot for uric acid solubility)
- **Potassium citrate**: 10-20 mEq PO BID-TID
  - Monitor urine pH with home pH strips
  - Avoid pH >7.0 (risk of calcium phosphate stones)
- **Sodium bicarbonate**: 650 mg (1 tablet) PO BID-TID (alternative)
- **Effect**: Can dissolve existing stones if pH consistently >6.5

**2. Reduce Uric Acid Production (if hyperuricosuria >800 mg/day):**
- **Allopurinol** 100-300 mg daily OR **febuxostat** 40-80 mg daily
- Mechanism: Xanthine oxidase inhibition → decreased uric acid production
- **Indications**: Hyperuricosuria, gout, failure of alkalinization alone
- **Monitor**: Serum uric acid, 24-hour urine uric acid

**3. Dietary Modification:**
- Limit high-purine foods: Red meat, organ meats, seafood, alcohol (especially beer)
- Avoid high-fructose corn syrup

**Struvite (Infection) Stones:**
- **Surgical removal REQUIRED** (cannot dissolve, prevent with antibiotics alone)
- **Complete stone removal** essential (fragments → reinfection)
- **Antibiotics**: Culture-directed, 2-4 weeks post-surgery
- **Prophylaxis**: Suppressive antibiotics if recurrent UTIs
- **Urease inhibitor**: Acetohydroxamic acid (Lithostat) 250 mg TID-QID
  - Rarely used (side effects: DVT risk, hemolytic anemia)
  - Only if surgery contraindicated

**Cystine Stones:**
- **Goal**: Urine cystine <250 mg/L
- **Hydration**: Very high (3-4 L/day) - most important
- **Urinary alkalinization**: Potassium citrate (goal urine pH >7.5)
  - Higher pH needed than uric acid stones
- **Thiol drugs** (if fluids + alkalinization insufficient):
  - **Tiopronin (Thiola)** 800-1200 mg/day (divided TID) - first-line
  - **D-penicillamine** 1-2 g/day (divided QID) - second-line (more side effects)
  - Mechanism: Forms soluble complex with cystine
  - Side effects: Nephrotic syndrome, bone marrow suppression, rash
  - **Monitor**: CBC, urinalysis for proteinuria
- **Captopril**: 75-150 mg/day (forms soluble cysteine-captopril complex, less effective)
- **Surgical**: Often required (cystine stones very hard, ESWL-resistant)

## 8. Special Populations

### Pregnancy
- **Epidemiology**: Stones occur in 1 in 200-1,500 pregnancies
- **Timing**: Most commonly second or third trimester
- **Imaging**: Ultrasound first-line (avoid CT radiation)
  - MR urography if ultrasound inconclusive
- **Management**:
  - Conservative: Hydration, analgesia (acetaminophen preferred, avoid NSAIDs in third trimester)
  - MET: Tamsulosin considered safe (Category B)
  - Surgical: Ureteroscopy if necessary (safest in second trimester)
  - Avoid ESWL (contraindicated)
- **Prevention**: Maintain hydration, continue prenatal vitamins with calcium

### Pediatric Patients
- **Increasing incidence** (obesity, dietary factors)
- **Genetic causes** more common: Cystinuria, primary hyperoxaluria, dRTA
- **Evaluation**: Full metabolic workup for first stone
- **Management**: Similar to adults, weight-based dosing
- **ESWL**: Effective, lower energy settings

### Bariatric Surgery Patients (Gastric Bypass)
- **Enteric hyperoxaluria**:
  - Fat malabsorption → bile acids bind calcium → free oxalate absorbed
  - Colonic hyperpermeability to oxalate
- **Prevention**:
  - High fluid intake
  - Calcium citrate 1,200-1,500 mg/day (with meals to bind oxalate)
  - Low oxalate diet
  - Probiotics (Oxalobacter formigenes - experimental)

### Chronic Kidney Disease
- **Stone prevalence**: 5-10% of CKD patients
- **Challenge**: Many medications require dose adjustment
- **Thiazides**: Ineffective if eGFR <30 mL/min
- **Citrate**: Avoid if eGFR <30 mL/min (risk of metabolic alkalosis, hypercalcemia)
- **Surgical**: Higher complication risk

## 9. Follow-Up & Monitoring

### Imaging Surveillance

**After First Stone:**
- Repeat imaging NOT routinely needed if asymptomatic after passage
- Imaging if symptoms recur

**After Surgical Intervention:**
- CT at 3-6 months to confirm stone-free status
- Annual ultrasound if residual fragments

**Asymptomatic Stones:**
- Annual ultrasound or low-dose CT
- Monitor for growth, new stones

### Metabolic Monitoring

**Repeat 24-Hour Urine:**
- 6 months after starting prevention therapy
- Annually if stable
- More frequently if recurrent stones

**Goals:**
- Urine volume >2 L/day
- Calcium <250 mg/day
- Oxalate <45 mg/day
- Citrate >320 mg/day (women), >450 mg/day (men)
- Uric acid <800 mg/day
- Sodium <100 mEq/day

**Serum Monitoring:**
- Electrolytes (if on thiazides, citrate)
- Creatinine (baseline renal function)
- Calcium, PTH (if hypercalciuria, rule out hyperparathyroidism)

## 10. Complications

### Acute Complications
- **Acute kidney injury**: Bilateral obstruction or solitary kidney
- **Urosepsis**: Obstructed infected kidney - EMERGENCY
- **Steinstrasse**: "Stone street" - obstructing fragments after ESWL
- **Ureteral stricture**: From chronic stone impaction

### Chronic Complications
- **Recurrent stones**: 50% at 10 years without prevention
- **Chronic kidney disease**: Recurrent stones + obstruction + infections
- **Nephrocalcinosis**: Diffuse renal calcification (dRTA, primary hyperoxaluria)
- **Loss of kidney**: From chronic obstruction, recurrent infections

## 11. Key Practice Points

### Clinical Pearls
1. **NSAIDs > opioids** for acute renal colic pain
2. **Fluid intake** is the single most important preventive measure
3. **Low calcium diet INCREASES** stone risk (counterintuitive)
4. **Uric acid stones** are radiolucent (won't see on KUB)
5. **Obstructed infected kidney** (fever + stone) is a TRUE emergency
6. **Calcium supplements** should be taken with meals (binds dietary oxalate)
7. **Medical expulsive therapy** (tamsulosin) increases passage rate for 5-10 mm stones
8. **24-hour urine** should be collected 6-8 weeks after acute event, not during

### Red Flags
- **Fever + flank pain + stone** = Pyonephrosis (emergency decompression needed)
- **Bilateral obstruction** or obstruction in solitary kidney (risk of AKI)
- **Anuria** (suggests complete obstruction)
- **Pediatric stone formers** (genetic cause until proven otherwise)
- **Staghorn calculus** (struvite - infection stone requiring surgery)

### Board Exam High-Yield
1. **Hypocitraturia** is protective - citrate inhibits crystallization
2. **Distal RTA** → calcium phosphate stones (alkaline urine, hypocitraturia)
3. **Thiazides** for hypercalciuria (increase renal calcium reabsorption)
4. **Struvite stones** = urease-producing bacteria (Proteus, Klebsiella)
5. **Cystine stones** = hexagonal crystals, autosomal recessive cystinuria (COLA amino acids)
6. **Primary hyperoxaluria** = genetic, treat with pyridoxine (Type 1)
7. **Enteric hyperoxaluria** = IBD, gastric bypass (fat malabsorption)

## Summary & Integration

Kidney stone disease affects 10% of the population and is highly recurrent without preventive therapy. The cornerstone of management is:

1. **Acute management**: NSAIDs for pain, medical expulsive therapy (tamsulosin) for 5-10 mm stones
2. **Emergency recognition**: Fever + obstruction = pyonephrosis (immediate decompression)
3. **Prevention (ALL patients)**: Increase fluid intake to >2 L urine output/day
4. **Metabolic evaluation**: 24-hour urine collection for recurrent stone formers
5. **Targeted therapy**: Stone type-specific interventions
   - Calcium stones: Thiazides (hypercalciuria), citrate (hypocitraturia)
   - Uric acid: Alkalinization (pH 6-6.5), allopurinol if needed
   - Cystine: High fluids, alkalinization (pH >7.5), thiol drugs
   - Struvite: Surgical removal + antibiotics
