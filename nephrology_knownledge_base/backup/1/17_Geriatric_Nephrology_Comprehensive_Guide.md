## Age-Related Renal Changes

### Structural and Functional Changes

#### Anatomical Changes
- **Kidney Size**: Decreases 20-30% from age 30 to 80
- **Cortical Thickness**: Reduced, relative medullary preservation
- **Nephron Number**: Decreases 30-50% with aging
- **Vascular Changes**: Arteriosclerosis, reduced renal blood flow
- **Fibrosis**: Increased interstitial fibrosis and glomerulosclerosis

#### Functional Changes
```
Glomerular Filtration Rate:
- Average decline: 0.8-1.0 mL/min/year after age 40
- GFR measurement challenges: Reduced muscle mass affects creatinine
- Cystatin C: Better marker in elderly (less affected by muscle mass)

Tubular Function:
- Concentrating ability: Reduced ability to concentrate urine
- Sodium handling: Impaired sodium conservation
- Acid-base: Reduced ability to excrete acid load
- Glucose reabsorption: Reduced threshold for glucosuria

Endocrine Function:
- Renin production: Decreased with age
- Vitamin D activation: Reduced 1-alpha hydroxylation
- Erythropoietin: Blunted response to anemia
```

### Implications for Clinical Practice

#### Laboratory Interpretation (2024 Updated)
- **Serum Creatinine**: May appear "normal" with reduced GFR due to reduced muscle mass
- **eGFR Equations (2024 Recommendations)**:
  - **CKD-EPI 2021 (race-free)**: Current standard for all adults
  - **BIS Equations (Berlin Initiative Study)**: Developed specifically for elderly (>70 years)
    - BIS1: Uses creatinine and age only
    - BIS2: Adds cystatin C for better accuracy
    - Better performance in elderly compared to CKD-EPI
  - **Cystatin C-based eGFR**:
    - More accurate in frail elderly with low muscle mass
    - Less affected by diet, muscle mass, age
    - Consider when creatinine-based eGFR questionable
  - **Combined creatinine-cystatin C (CKD-EPI 2021)**:
    - Best overall accuracy in elderly
    - Recommended when discordant results
- **Proteinuria**:
  - May be underdetected with spot urine ACR (low creatinine excretion)
  - 24-hour collection may be more accurate if compliance adequate
  - Albumin-to-creatinine ratio still recommended screening test

#### Drug Dosing Considerations
- **Renal Clearance**: Reduced even with "normal" creatinine
- **Volume of Distribution**: Increased for hydrophilic drugs
- **Protein Binding**: Reduced albumin increases free drug levels
- **Polypharmacy**: Increased risk of drug interactions and nephrotoxicity

## Chronic Kidney Disease in the Elderly

### Epidemiology and Risk Factors

#### Prevalence and Demographics
- **CKD Prevalence**: >40% in adults >70 years old
- **Stage Distribution**: 
  - Stage 3a: 25-30%
  - Stage 3b: 10-15%
  - Stage 4-5: 2-3%
- **Gender**: Higher prevalence in women
- **Race/Ethnicity**: Higher rates in African Americans and Hispanics

#### Etiology in Elderly
- **Hypertensive Nephrosclerosis**: Most common cause
- **Diabetic Nephropathy**: Increasing prevalence
- **Ischemic Nephropathy**: Atherosclerotic renal artery disease
- **Obstructive Uropathy**: BPH, prostate cancer, pelvic masses
- **Amyloidosis**: AA and AL amyloidosis

### Clinical Presentation and Assessment

#### Atypical Presentations
- **Non-Specific Symptoms**: Fatigue, anorexia, cognitive decline
- **Falls**: May be exacerbated by uremia, electrolyte abnormalities
- **Cognitive Impairment**: "Uremic encephalopathy" may mimic dementia
- **Functional Decline**: Reduced ADLs, increased dependence

#### Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment
```
Physical Function:
- ADLs (Activities of Daily Living): Bathing, dressing, toileting
- IADLs (Instrumental ADLs): Shopping, cooking, managing medications
- Gait speed and balance: Fall risk assessment
- Hand grip strength: Frailty marker

Cognitive Function:
- Mini-Cog or MoCA: Quick screening tools
- Executive function: Medication management capacity
- Delirium risk: Assess using CAM (Confusion Assessment Method)

Nutritional Status:
- Weight loss: Unintentional >5% in 6 months
- SGA (Subjective Global Assessment): Malnutrition screening
- Serum albumin: Prognostic marker

Social Support:
- Caregiver availability and capability
- Living situation: Home alone vs assisted living
- Financial resources: Insurance coverage, medication costs
```

### Management Strategies

#### Pharmacologic Management of CKD in Elderly (2024 Evidence-Based Update)

**SGLT2 Inhibitors (Major Advancement - 2024 Data):**

```
Evidence Base in Elderly CKD Patients:

EMPA-KIDNEY Trial (2023) - Elderly Subgroup Analysis:
- Age ≥65 years: 2,282 patients (42% of total)
- Age ≥75 years: 526 patients (10% of total)
- Primary outcome (kidney disease progression or CV death):
  - HR 0.72 in age ≥65 (similar to overall HR 0.72)
  - HR 0.66 in age ≥75 (consistent benefit)
- Safety: Similar adverse event rates across age groups
- Conclusion: Benefit maintained in elderly, including very elderly

DAPA-CKD Trial (2020) - Elderly Subgroup Analysis:
- Age ≥65 years: 1,398 patients (32% of total)
- Composite renal endpoint: HR 0.61 in elderly vs 0.64 overall
- Heart failure hospitalization: 36% reduction in elderly
- Safety: No increased AKI or hypoglycemia in elderly
- eGFR dip: Initial 3-4 mL/min decline, then stability

FDA-Approved SGLT2 Inhibitors for CKD (2024):
1. Dapagliflozin (Farxiga):
   - FDA approved for CKD 2021
   - Dosing: 10 mg PO daily
   - eGFR ≥20 mL/min/1.73m² (can continue if decline below)

2. Empagliflozin (Jardiance):
   - FDA approved for CKD 2023
   - Dosing: 10 mg PO daily
   - eGFR ≥20 mL/min/1.73m² (can continue if decline below)

Benefits in Elderly CKD:
- Slow CKD progression (40% reduction in kidney outcomes)
- Reduce cardiovascular events (heart failure hospitalization)
- Lower all-cause mortality
- BP reduction (5-10 mmHg, avoid hypotension in frail elderly)
- Weight loss (2-3 kg, may be beneficial or harmful depending on baseline)

Special Considerations in Elderly:
- Genital mycotic infections: 5-10% incidence (higher in women)
  - Often mild, treated with topical antifungals
  - More common in diabetic patients
- Dehydration risk: Monitor volume status, especially with diuretics
- Hypotension: Start at lower BP, may need to reduce other antihypertensives
- Euglycemic DKA: Rare (<1%), counsel on sick day management
- Falls: Monitor for orthostatic hypotension, dizziness

Contraindications:
- Type 1 diabetes (DKA risk)
- eGFR <20 at initiation (can continue if started higher and declines)
- Recurrent UTIs or genital infections
- Active foot ulcers or amputation risk (debated)

Clinical Use in Elderly (2024 Recommendations):
- Indicated: ALL CKD patients with eGFR 20-90, regardless of diabetes status
- Age NOT a contraindication (benefit seen in ≥75 years)
- Start alongside RAS blockade (ACE/ARB) for maximal benefit
- Monitor: Volume status, BP, eGFR at 2-4 weeks (expect dip)
- Continue long-term unless intolerance or contraindication develops
```

**Non-Steroidal Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonist (Finerenone):**

```
FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD Trials - Elderly Subgroup:
- Age ≥65 years: >60% of participants
- Cardiovascular and renal benefit maintained in elderly
- Hyperkalemia: Higher rates in elderly, manageable with monitoring

Finerenone (Kerendia):
- FDA approved 2021 for diabetic kidney disease
- Dosing: 10-20 mg PO daily (based on eGFR)
- Indication: Type 2 diabetes + CKD (eGFR 25-90) with albuminuria

Benefits:
- Reduce CKD progression (23% reduction)
- Reduce cardiovascular events (14% reduction)
- Lower albuminuria

Elderly Considerations:
- Hyperkalemia risk: Monitor K+ at 2-4 weeks, then periodically
  - Higher risk with ACE/ARB + finerenone
  - Consider RAASi dose reduction or K+ binder if needed
- Drug interactions: CYP3A4 inhibitors increase levels
- Contraindications: K+ >5.0 mEq/L, eGFR <25

Use in Elderly:
- Diabetic CKD with albuminuria: Strong indication
- Combine with SGLT2i + ACE/ARB for triple therapy (maximal benefit)
- Close K+ monitoring essential in elderly (CKD, medications, diet)
```

**GLP-1 Receptor Agonists (Renal Benefits - 2024 Data):**

```
Semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy):
- FLOW Trial (2024): Specifically studied CKD outcomes
  - Composite kidney outcome: 24% reduction
  - eGFR decline: Slower with semaglutide
  - Expected FDA approval for CKD indication: 2025

Benefits in Elderly CKD:
- Glycemic control with low hypoglycemia risk
- Weight loss (5-15%, beneficial if overweight/obese)
- Cardiovascular benefits (MACE reduction)
- CKD progression slowing

Elderly Considerations:
- GI side effects: Nausea, vomiting (start low, titrate slowly)
- Weight loss: May be problematic in frail/malnourished elderly
- Cost: High cost, insurance barriers
- Contraindications: Personal/family history of medullary thyroid cancer

Use in Elderly:
- Type 2 diabetes + CKD: Consider alongside SGLT2i
- Obesity + CKD: Cardiovascular and renal benefits
- Frailty/malnutrition: Avoid due to weight loss concerns
```

**RAS Blockade (ACE Inhibitors/ARBs) - Ongoing Role:**

```
Updated Recommendations (2024):
- Still first-line for CKD with proteinuria
- Combine with SGLT2i for additive benefit
- Elderly-specific dosing:
  - Start low dose, titrate carefully
  - Monitor: Cr (expect 25-30% rise, acceptable), K+ (risk of hyperkalemia)
  - Hold during acute illness (sick day protocol)

Deprescribing Considerations:
- Advanced age (>85), limited life expectancy: Consider stopping
- Recurrent hyperkalemia or AKI: May need to reduce dose or stop
- eGFR <30: Weigh benefits vs risks (case-by-case)
- Non-proteinuric CKD: Less clear benefit, consider deprescribing
```

#### Conservative Kidney Management (CKM) (2024 Updated Protocols)

**Definition and Philosophy:**
- **CKM**: Planned, holistic, patient-centered care pathway for advanced CKD patients choosing not to pursue dialysis
- **NOT**: "Doing nothing" or withdrawal of care
- **Focus**: Maximize quality of life, minimize symptoms, preserve function

**Candidate Selection (2024 Evidence-Based):**
```
Appropriate Candidates:
- Age >75-80 years with multiple comorbidities
- Severe functional limitations (dependent in ≥2 ADLs)
- Advanced frailty (Clinical Frailty Scale ≥7)
- High symptom burden despite optimal medical management
- Dementia or cognitive impairment limiting dialysis participation
- Patient preference after informed shared decision-making

Prognostic Considerations (CKM vs Dialysis):
- Very elderly (>85): Survival may be similar with CKM vs dialysis
- Multiple comorbidities: Quality of life often better with CKM
- Albumin <2.5 g/dL: Poor prognosis with dialysis (consider CKM)
- Cohen score ≥8: High 6-month mortality on dialysis

Recent Evidence (2020-2024):
- UK Registry: Age >75 with comorbidities → median survival 2-3 years with CKM
- Quality of life: Better in CKM patients vs dialysis in very frail elderly
- Hospital days: Fewer with CKM compared to dialysis in last year of life
```

**Components of CKM (Comprehensive Care Plan):**

```
Medical Management:
1. Nephroprotective Therapies:
   - SGLT2 inhibitors: Slow progression even in advanced CKD (eGFR >20)
   - ACE/ARB: Continue if tolerated, may need dose reduction
   - Avoid nephrotoxins: NSAIDs, contrast, aminoglycosides

2. Blood Pressure Control:
   - Target: <140/90 mmHg (individualized, avoid hypotension)
   - Agents: ACE/ARB, CCB, diuretics as needed

3. Volume Management:
   - Loop diuretics: Titrate to euvolemia
   - Dietary sodium restriction: <2-3 g/day
   - Monitor weight, edema, dyspnea

4. Anemia Management:
   - ESA therapy: Target Hgb 9-10 g/dL (avoid >11 g/dL)
   - Iron supplementation: IV preferred if oral intolerance
   - Blood transfusions: Judicious use if symptomatic

5. Mineral Bone Disease:
   - Phosphate binders: If phosphate >5.5 mg/dL
   - Vitamin D analogs: Calcitriol, paricalcitol for secondary HPT
   - Dietary phosphate restriction: <1000 mg/day

6. Metabolic Acidosis:
   - Sodium bicarbonate: If HCO3 <22 mEq/L
   - Target: 22-24 mEq/L
   - May slow progression and preserve muscle mass

7. Hyperkalemia Management:
   - Dietary restriction: <2 g/day
   - Potassium binders: Patiromer, sodium zirconium cyclosilicate
   - Adjust/reduce RAASi if persistent hyperkalemia

Symptom Management (Palliative Approach):
- Nausea: Metoclopramide, ondansetron, olanzapine
- Pruritus: Gabapentin, pregabalin, topical emollients
- Restless legs: Gabapentin, iron supplementation
- Pain: Acetaminophen, opioids (renally dosed)
- Fatigue: Address anemia, depression, sleep disorders

Nutritional Support:
- Protein: 0.6-0.8 g/kg/day (low protein diet)
- Calories: Adequate to prevent malnutrition (30-35 kcal/kg)
- Dietitian involvement: Essential for education
- Monitor: Weight, albumin, nutritional status

Psychosocial and Spiritual Care:
- Depression/anxiety screening and treatment
- Advance care planning discussions
- Spiritual support and chaplaincy
- Caregiver support and education

Coordination of Care:
- Multidisciplinary team: Nephrology, palliative care, primary care
- Regular follow-up: Monthly to quarterly based on stability
- Hospital at home: Avoid unnecessary hospitalizations
- Hospice referral: When appropriate (prognosis <6 months)
```

**Outcomes with CKM (2024 Data):**
```
Survival:
- Median survival: 1-3 years from eGFR <15 (depends on age, comorbidities)
- Comparable to dialysis in very elderly/frail patients
- Better than dialysis in some high-risk groups (albumin <2.5, severe comorbidity)

Quality of Life:
- Higher QOL scores in CKM vs dialysis (frail elderly)
- More time at home, fewer hospital days
- Less treatment burden, fewer procedures

Symptom Burden:
- Well-managed symptoms in majority with palliative approach
- Pruritus, nausea, fatigue can be controlled
- Pain management often better than on dialysis

Patient Satisfaction:
- High satisfaction when aligned with goals of care
- Family satisfaction better with CKM when appropriate
- Fewer regrets about treatment choice
```

#### Dialysis Decision-Making

#### Prognostic Assessment Tools
```
Clinical Predictors of Poor Prognosis:
- Age >85 years
- Multiple comorbidities (>3 major conditions)
- High dependency for ADLs
- Malnutrition (albumin <3.0 g/dL)
- Recurrent hospitalizations

Prognostic Scores:
- Cohen's 6-Month Mortality Score:
  * Age >80: 2 points
  * Serum albumin <2.5 g/dL: 3 points
  * Cognitive impairment: 2 points
  * ADL dependence: 2 points
  * Serious comorbidity: 2 points
  * Score ≥8: 50% 6-month mortality

- REIN Score (Renal Epidemiology and Information Network):
  * Age, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, functional status
  * Predicts 1-year mortality with reasonable accuracy
```

#### Shared Decision-Making Process
1. **Information Provision**: 
   - Explain benefits and burdens of dialysis
   - Discuss conservative management options
   - Address prognosis and life expectancy

2. **Values Clarification**:
   - Quality vs quantity of life priorities
   - Life goals and what matters most
   - Burdens of treatment acceptance threshold

3. **Decision Aids**:
   - Visual aids showing survival curves
   - Patient stories and testimonials
   - Structured questionnaires

4. **Family Involvement**:
   - Include caregivers in discussions
   - Address family dynamics and expectations
   - Plan for future care needs

## Frailty and Kidney Disease ⭐

### Definition and Epidemiology (2024 Update)

#### Definition of Frailty
**Frailty** is a geriatric syndrome characterized by:
- Decreased physiologic reserve across multiple organ systems
- Increased vulnerability to stressors
- Reduced capacity to maintain homeostasis
- Heightened risk of adverse outcomes (disability, hospitalization, mortality)

**Key Distinction:**
- **Frailty ≠ Aging**: Not all elderly individuals are frail
- **Frailty ≠ Disability**: Frail individuals may be functionally independent
- **Frailty ≠ Comorbidity**: Separate but overlapping constructs

#### Epidemiology in CKD and Dialysis Populations (2024 Data)

```
Prevalence by CKD Stage:
- CKD Stage 3: 15-20% frail
- CKD Stage 4: 25-35% frail
- CKD Stage 5 (pre-dialysis): 40-50% frail
- Hemodialysis patients: 50-75% frail
- Peritoneal dialysis patients: 40-60% frail
- Transplant candidates: 20-40% frail

Comparison to General Population:
- Age-matched general elderly: 10-15% frail
- CKD patients 2-3× higher frailty prevalence
- Dialysis patients 3-5× higher frailty prevalence

Age Distribution:
- Age <65 on dialysis: 30-40% frail (higher than general elderly >80!)
- Age ≥65 on dialysis: 60-80% frail
```

#### Bidirectional Relationship: CKD and Frailty ⭐

```
CKD → Frailty (Mechanisms):
1. Uremic toxins: Muscle wasting, weakness (sarcopenia)
2. Inflammation: Chronic inflammatory state (IL-6, CRP elevation)
3. Anemia: Reduced oxygen delivery, fatigue, exercise intolerance
4. Malnutrition: Protein-energy wasting, reduced muscle mass
5. Metabolic acidosis: Muscle protein degradation
6. Bone-mineral disease: Osteoporosis, fracture risk, muscle weakness
7. Endocrine abnormalities: Low testosterone, vitamin D deficiency, insulin resistance
8. Dialysis treatment burden: Time, travel, post-dialysis fatigue

Frailty → Worse CKD Outcomes (Evidence):
- Faster CKD progression (unclear mechanism)
- Higher hospitalization rates (2-3× higher)
- Increased mortality (2-3× higher on dialysis)
- Lower transplant listing and waitlist mortality
- Delayed kidney function recovery after AKI
- Lower quality of life
```

### Comprehensive Frailty Assessment Tools (2024 Evidence-Based) ⭐⭐⭐

#### 1. Fried Frailty Phenotype (Physical Frailty Model) ⭐

**Most Widely Validated in CKD/Dialysis Populations**

**Five Components:**
```
1. Unintentional Weight Loss:
   - Criterion: ≥10 lbs (or ≥5% body weight) unintentional loss in past year
   - Ask: "In the last year, have you lost more than 10 pounds unintentionally?"
   - Score: 1 point if yes

2. Exhaustion (Self-Reported):
   - Criterion: Positive response to either CES-D question:
     * "How often in the last week did you feel that everything you did was an effort?"
     * "How often in the last week did you feel you could not get going?"
   - Positive: "Moderate amount (3-4 days/week)" or "Most of the time"
   - Score: 1 point if positive

3. Weakness (Grip Strength):
   - Measurement: Handheld dynamometer (Jamar or equivalent)
   - Protocol: Best of 3 attempts, dominant hand
   - Cutoffs stratified by gender and BMI:

   MEN:
   - BMI ≤24: Grip <29 kg = frail
   - BMI 24.1-28: Grip <30 kg = frail
   - BMI >28: Grip <32 kg = frail

   WOMEN:
   - BMI ≤23: Grip <17 kg = frail
   - BMI 23.1-26: Grip <17.3 kg = frail
   - BMI 26.1-29: Grip <18 kg = frail
   - BMI >29: Grip <21 kg = frail

   - Score: 1 point if below cutoff

4. Slow Gait Speed:
   - Measurement: Time to walk 15 feet (4.57 meters) at usual pace
   - Protocol: "Walk at your normal, comfortable pace"
   - Cutoffs stratified by gender and height:

   MEN:
   - Height ≤173 cm: Time ≥7 seconds = frail
   - Height >173 cm: Time ≥6 seconds = frail

   WOMEN:
   - Height ≤159 cm: Time ≥7 seconds = frail
   - Height >159 cm: Time ≥6 seconds = frail

   - Alternative: Gait speed <0.8 m/s = frail (simple cutoff)
   - Score: 1 point if slow gait

5. Low Physical Activity:
   - Measurement: Minnesota Leisure Time Activity questionnaire (kcal/week)
   - Simple alternative: "Do you engage in regular physical activity or exercise?"
   - Cutoffs:
     * Men: <383 kcal/week = frail
     * Women: <270 kcal/week = frail
   - Score: 1 point if low activity
```

**Scoring and Interpretation:**
```
Total Score (0-5 points):
- 0 points: Robust (non-frail)
- 1-2 points: Pre-frail (intermediate stage)
- 3-5 points: Frail

Outcomes in Dialysis Patients (2024 Data):
- Frail (3-5): 2.6× mortality, 1.8× hospitalization vs robust
- Pre-frail (1-2): 1.5× mortality, 1.3× hospitalization vs robust
- Each 1-point increase: 20-30% higher mortality risk
```

**Advantages and Limitations:**
```
Advantages:
- Well-validated in CKD/dialysis populations
- Objective measures (grip, gait) reduce bias
- Predicts mortality, hospitalization, functional decline
- Responsive to interventions (exercise, nutrition)

Limitations:
- Time-consuming (15-20 minutes)
- Requires equipment (dynamometer, stopwatch, space to walk)
- Physical activity component complex
- May not capture cognitive or social frailty
```

#### 2. Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS) ⭐⭐⭐

**Rapid Clinical Assessment Tool (2-3 minutes)**

**9-Point Scale (Visual and Descriptive):**

```
1. Very Fit: Robust, active, energetic, motivated; regularly exercises
   - Example: 75-year-old who jogs 3× weekly

2. Well: No active disease, less fit than category 1
   - Example: Occasional exercise, no limitations

3. Managing Well: Medical problems well-controlled, not regularly active
   - Example: 80-year-old with controlled HTN, DM, walks for groceries

4. Vulnerable: Not dependent on others, symptoms limit activities
   - Example: Slowed up, symptoms when walking >1 block

5. Mildly Frail: Limited dependence on others (IADLs)
   - Example: Needs help with heavy housework, shopping, finances
   - IADLs affected, ADLs intact

6. Moderately Frail: Help needed with outdoor activities and housekeeping
   - Example: Needs help with bathing, stairs; uses walker
   - Some ADLs affected

7. Severely Frail: Completely dependent, approaching end of life
   - Example: Dependent for ADLs, but not acutely terminal
   - May be stable for months

8. Very Severely Frail: Completely dependent, approaching end of life
   - Example: Bedbound, requires total care
   - Life expectancy <6 months

9. Terminally Ill: Life expectancy <6 months, not otherwise frail
   - Example: Metastatic cancer with good baseline function
```

**Scoring in CKD/Dialysis:**
```
Interpretation:
- CFS 1-3: Robust/well (NOT frail)
- CFS 4: Vulnerable (pre-frail)
- CFS 5-6: Frail
- CFS 7-9: Severely frail

Clinical Decision Points:
- CFS ≤4: Proceed with standard dialysis/transplant evaluation
- CFS 5-6: Frailty interventions (prehab), consider assisted PD, shared decision-making
- CFS ≥7: Conservative kidney management likely appropriate, palliative care involvement

Outcomes (Dialysis Patients):
- CFS 1-3: Baseline mortality
- CFS 4-5: 1.5-2× higher mortality
- CFS 6-7: 2.5-3× higher mortality
- CFS ≥8: 4-5× higher mortality (consider CKM)
```

**Advantages and Limitations:**
```
Advantages:
- VERY RAPID (2-3 minutes, no equipment)
- Clinician judgment-based (integrates clinical gestalt)
- Well-validated in CKD/dialysis (predicts mortality, hospitalization)
- Used in KDIGO 2024 CKD guidelines
- Easy to incorporate into routine clinic visits

Limitations:
- Subjective (inter-rater variability)
- Requires clinical judgment and experience
- May miss subclinical frailty (ceiling effect at lower scores)
- Not designed for patients <65 years (less validated)
```

**BOARD EXAM PEARL:** ⭐
- **Clinical Frailty Scale ≥6 is associated with high dialysis mortality and may indicate CKM is preferred over dialysis**

#### 3. FRAIL Scale (Simple Questionnaire - 1 minute) ⭐

**Mnemonic-Based 5-Item Screening Tool**

```
F - Fatigue:
   Question: "How much of the time during the past 4 weeks did you feel tired?"
   Positive: "All of the time" or "Most of the time"
   Score: 1 point if positive

R - Resistance:
   Question: "By yourself and not using aids, do you have difficulty walking up 10 steps without resting?"
   Positive: "Yes"
   Score: 1 point if positive

A - Ambulation:
   Question: "By yourself and not using aids, do you have difficulty walking several hundred yards?"
   Positive: "Yes"
   Score: 1 point if positive

I - Illnesses:
   Question: "Do you have 5 or more illnesses?"
   - Count: HTN, DM, cancer, lung disease, heart disease, stroke, etc.
   Positive: ≥5 illnesses
   Score: 1 point if ≥5

L - Loss of weight:
   Question: "Have you lost more than 5% of your weight in the past 6 months?"
   Positive: "Yes"
   Score: 1 point if positive
```

**Scoring:**
```
Total Score (0-5):
- 0: Robust
- 1-2: Pre-frail
- 3-5: Frail

Outcomes in CKD (Limited Data):
- Frail (3-5): Higher mortality, hospitalization
- Pre-frail (1-2): Intermediate risk
```

**Advantages and Limitations:**
```
Advantages:
- VERY QUICK (1-2 minutes)
- No equipment needed
- Easy to remember (FRAIL mnemonic)
- Self-administered (can be questionnaire)

Limitations:
- Less validated in CKD/dialysis vs Fried or CFS
- Subjective responses
- May miss subtle frailty
- "Illnesses" component crude
```

#### 4. Short Physical Performance Battery (SPPB) ⭐⭐

**Objective Performance-Based Assessment (10 minutes)**

**Three Components:**

```
1. Balance Tests (3 positions, each held 10 seconds):

   a) Side-by-side stance (feet together):
      - Hold 10 seconds: 1 point
      - Cannot hold 10 seconds: 0 points, STOP (score = 0)

   b) Semi-tandem stance (heel of one foot beside big toe of other):
      - Hold 10 seconds: 1 point
      - Cannot hold 10 seconds: 0 points

   c) Tandem stance (heel-to-toe):
      - Hold 10 seconds: 2 points
      - Hold 3-9.99 seconds: 1 point
      - Cannot hold 3 seconds: 0 points

   Balance subscore: 0-4 points

2. Gait Speed Test (4-meter walk, usual pace):

   Time to walk 4 meters:
   - <4.82 seconds (>0.83 m/s): 4 points
   - 4.82-6.20 seconds (0.65-0.83 m/s): 3 points
   - 6.21-8.70 seconds (0.46-0.64 m/s): 2 points
   - >8.70 seconds (<0.46 m/s): 1 point
   - Cannot complete: 0 points

   Gait subscore: 0-4 points

3. Chair Stand Test (5 repetitions, arms folded):

   Time to stand from chair 5 times (arms folded across chest):
   - <11.19 seconds: 4 points
   - 11.20-13.69 seconds: 3 points
   - 13.70-16.69 seconds: 2 points
   - ≥16.70 seconds: 1 point
   - Cannot complete without using arms: 0 points

   Chair stand subscore: 0-4 points
```

**Total SPPB Score:**
```
Total Score: 0-12 points (sum of 3 subscores)

Interpretation:
- 10-12: High function (minimal frailty)
- 7-9: Moderate function (pre-frail)
- 4-6: Low function (frail)
- 0-3: Very low function (severely frail)

Cutoffs in CKD/Dialysis:
- SPPB ≤7: Increased mortality risk (2× higher)
- SPPB ≤4: Very high mortality risk (3-4× higher)
- Each 1-point decrease: 10-15% higher mortality

Clinical Significance:
- 1-point change: Minimally clinically important difference
- 2-point change: Moderate change
- ≥3-point change: Large, clinically significant change
```

**Outcomes in Dialysis Patients (2024 Data):**
```
- SPPB <7: Predicts falls, hospitalization, mortality
- SPPB improves with exercise intervention (intradialytic cycling, resistance training)
- SPPB responsive to changes in frailty status (tracks improvement/decline)
```

**Advantages and Limitations:**
```
Advantages:
- Objective, performance-based (no self-report bias)
- Well-validated in elderly populations
- Responsive to interventions
- Predicts mortality, falls, hospitalization in dialysis
- Standardized protocol

Limitations:
- Requires 10-15 minutes
- Requires space (4-meter walkway, chair)
- Cannot be done bedside or in wheelchair patients
- Less validated than Fried in CKD specifically
```

#### 5. Edmonton Frail Scale (EFS)

**Brief Multidimensional Assessment (5 minutes)**

**Nine Domains (17 total points):**

```
1. Cognition (Clock Drawing Test):
   - Draw clock with numbers, set time to 11:10
   - Correct: 0 points
   - Minor errors: 1 point
   - Major errors/cannot complete: 2 points

2. General Health Status:
   "In the past year, how many times have you been admitted to hospital?"
   - 0 admissions: 0 points
   - 1-2 admissions: 1 point
   - >2 admissions: 2 points

3. Functional Independence (8 ADL/IADL items):
   Ask: "Do you require help with..."
   - Meal preparation: 1 point if yes
   - Shopping: 1 point if yes
   - Transportation: 1 point if yes
   - Telephone: 1 point if yes
   - Housekeeping: 1 point if yes
   - Laundry: 1 point if yes
   - Managing money: 1 point if yes
   - Managing medications: 1 point if yes

   Maximum 8 points for functional dependence

4. Social Support:
   "When you need help, can you count on someone willing and able to meet your needs?"
   - Always: 0 points
   - Sometimes: 1 point
   - Never: 2 points

5. Medication Use:
   "Do you take 5 or more different medications?"
   - No: 0 points
   - Yes: 1 point

6. Nutrition (Subjective):
   "Have you recently lost weight such that your clothing has become looser?"
   - No: 0 points
   - Yes: 1 point

7. Mood:
   "Do you often feel sad or depressed?"
   - No: 0 points
   - Yes: 1 point

8. Continence:
   "Do you have a problem with losing control of urine when you don't want to?"
   - No: 0 points
   - Yes: 1 point

9. Functional Performance (Timed Up and Go):
   Time to stand from chair, walk 3 meters, turn around, walk back, sit down:
   - 0-10 seconds: 0 points
   - 11-20 seconds: 1 point
   - >20 seconds or refuses/unable: 2 points
```

**Total Score:**
```
0-17 points total:
- 0-4: Not frail
- 5-6: Apparently vulnerable
- 7-8: Mild frailty
- 9-10: Moderate frailty
- ≥11: Severe frailty
```

**Advantages and Limitations:**
```
Advantages:
- Multidimensional (physical, cognitive, social)
- Brief (5 minutes)
- Includes cognition (important in CKD)
- Easy to administer

Limitations:
- Less validated in CKD/dialysis populations
- Some overlap with comorbidity (hospitalization, polypharmacy)
- Developed in acute care setting
```

#### 6. Additional Frailty Measures

**Timed Up and Go (TUG) Test:** ⭐
```
Protocol:
1. Patient sits in standard chair (seat height ~46 cm)
2. On "Go," patient stands, walks 3 meters, turns, walks back, sits
3. Time from "Go" to seated position

Interpretation:
- <10 seconds: Normal mobility
- 10-20 seconds: Moderate impairment
- >20 seconds: Severe impairment, high fall risk
- >30 seconds: Very high fall risk

CKD/Dialysis Cutoffs:
- >15 seconds: Predicts falls in dialysis patients
- >20 seconds: High mortality risk

Advantages: Quick (2 minutes), requires minimal space
Limitations: Cannot distinguish frailty vs disability
```

**Gait Speed (Standalone):** ⭐
```
"The Sixth Vital Sign"

Protocol:
- Measure time to walk 4 or 5 meters at usual pace
- Calculate speed (meters/second)

Interpretation:
- >1.0 m/s: Normal
- 0.8-1.0 m/s: Borderline
- <0.8 m/s: Slow gait, frailty indicator
- <0.6 m/s: High mortality risk

CKD-Specific Data:
- Dialysis patients: Mean gait speed 0.7-0.8 m/s (impaired)
- Each 0.1 m/s decrease: 10-15% higher mortality
- <0.6 m/s: 2-3× higher mortality vs >0.8 m/s

Advantages: VERY QUICK (1-2 minutes), highly prognostic
Limitations: Single-domain measure, requires ambulation
```

**Handgrip Strength (Standalone):** ⭐
```
Protocol:
- Jamar or Smedley dynamometer
- Best of 3 attempts, dominant hand
- Patient seated, elbow 90°, forearm neutral

Interpretation (Simple Cutoffs):
- Men: <27 kg = low grip strength
- Women: <16 kg = low grip strength

CKD-Specific Cutoffs (Sarcopenia):
- Men: <28 kg
- Women: <18 kg

Dialysis Outcomes:
- Low grip strength: 2× higher mortality
- Each 5 kg decrease: 20% higher mortality

Advantages: Quick (2 minutes), objective, inexpensive equipment
Limitations: Single-domain, affected by arthritis/neuropathy
```

### Choosing the Right Frailty Tool (Clinical Algorithm) ⭐

```
SCENARIO-BASED SELECTION:

1. Routine Outpatient CKD Clinic (Pre-dialysis):
   - FIRST-LINE: Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS)
     * Rationale: Quick (2-3 min), no equipment, validated
     * Use: Every visit for age ≥65, annually for age <65
   - ADD if CFS ≥4: Fried Frailty Phenotype or SPPB
     * Rationale: Objective measures for intervention targeting

2. Dialysis Unit (Incident or Prevalent Patients):
   - FIRST-LINE: Fried Frailty Phenotype
     * Rationale: Best-validated in dialysis, objective
     * Use: At dialysis initiation, then every 6-12 months
   - ALTERNATIVE: FRAIL scale (if time-limited)
     * Use: Quick screen, monthly

3. Transplant Evaluation:
   - REQUIRED: Fried Frailty Phenotype + SPPB
     * Rationale: Predicts waitlist mortality, post-transplant outcomes
     * Frailty often exclusion or requires intervention first
   - CONSIDER: Gait speed (simple, quick, highly prognostic)

4. Conservative Kidney Management Decision-Making:
   - REQUIRED: Clinical Frailty Scale
     * Rationale: Integrated into CKM decision algorithms
     * CFS ≥7: Strong indication for CKM vs dialysis
   - ADD: Prognostic scores (Bansal, Cohen)

5. Acute Care/Hospital Setting:
   - FIRST-LINE: Clinical Frailty Scale
     * Rationale: Retrospective assessment, quick
   - ADD if able: Timed Up and Go, Gait Speed
     * Rationale: Predict discharge disposition, falls

6. Research/Quality Improvement:
   - GOLD STANDARD: Fried Frailty Phenotype
     * Most widely used in studies, comparable data
```

### Frailty-Specific Outcomes in CKD/Dialysis (2024 Evidence) ⭐⭐

#### Mortality

```
Hemodialysis Patients (Meta-Analysis 2023):
- Frail vs non-frail: HR 1.93 (95% CI 1.65-2.25)
- Each 1-point Fried score increase: HR 1.28
- Pre-frail vs robust: HR 1.42 (95% CI 1.18-1.71)

Peritoneal Dialysis Patients:
- Frail vs non-frail: HR 1.76 (95% CI 1.42-2.18)
- Lower frailty prevalence than HD (40% vs 60%)

Pre-Dialysis CKD (Stage 4-5):
- Frail vs non-frail: HR 2.24 for mortality
- Frailty better predictor than age or comorbidity alone
```

#### Hospitalization

```
Dialysis Patients:
- Frail: 2.3× higher hospitalization rate
- Frail: Longer hospital length of stay (median +3 days)
- Frail: Higher readmission rates (30-day: 35% vs 20%)

CKD Stage 3-4:
- Frail: 1.8× higher hospitalization risk
- Each additional Fried component: 15% higher risk
```

#### Dialysis Access Outcomes

```
Arteriovenous Fistula:
- Frail patients: Higher primary failure rates (35% vs 20%)
- Frail patients: Longer maturation time (+4-6 weeks)
- Frail patients: Lower patency rates at 1 year (60% vs 75%)

Catheter Dependence:
- Frail: 2× more likely to remain catheter-dependent
- Frail: Higher catheter-related infection rates
```

#### Falls and Fractures ⭐

```
Dialysis Patients:
- Fall incidence: 1.6 falls per patient-year (general elderly: 0.6)
- Frail dialysis patients: 3.2 falls per patient-year
- 30-40% of dialysis patients fall each year

Fall Predictors:
- Gait speed <0.6 m/s: 3× higher fall risk
- TUG >20 seconds: 2.5× higher fall risk
- SPPB ≤7: 2× higher fall risk

Fracture Risk:
- Hip fracture incidence: 4-5× higher in dialysis vs general population
- Frail dialysis patients: 2× higher fracture risk vs non-frail dialysis
- Post-fracture mortality: 50% at 1 year (hip fracture)
```

#### Transplant Outcomes

```
Waitlist:
- Frail candidates: 2.5× higher waitlist mortality
- Frail: Less likely to receive transplant (competitive waitlist)
- Frail: Longer time to transplant (if listed)

Post-Transplant:
- Frail: Delayed graft function 1.8× higher
- Frail: Length of stay +3-5 days
- Frail: Higher early complications (infection, readmission)
- Frail: Graft survival may be lower (conflicting data)

Functional Recovery:
- Frail: Slower ADL recovery post-transplant
- Frail: More likely to require post-acute care facility
```

#### Quality of Life

```
Physical Health:
- Frail dialysis: SF-36 physical component 25-30 (vs 50 in general population)
- Non-frail dialysis: SF-36 physical 35-40
- Frailty explains >50% of QOL variance in dialysis

Mental Health:
- Frail: Higher depression rates (45% vs 25%)
- Frail: Higher anxiety rates (35% vs 20%)
- Frail: Lower mental component scores (SF-36)

Treatment Burden:
- Frail: Higher symptom burden (fatigue, pain, dyspnea)
- Frail: More post-dialysis recovery time (>4 hours)
- Frail: Greater caregiver dependence
```

### Frailty Interventions in CKD/Dialysis (2024 Evidence-Based) ⭐⭐⭐

#### Exercise Interventions (Strongest Evidence) ⭐

**1. Intradialytic Exercise Programs:**

```
Evidence Base:
- Multiple RCTs show improvement in frailty, physical function, QOL
- Safe and feasible in majority of HD patients
- Improves Fried score, SPPB, gait speed, grip strength

Typical Program:
Duration: 3 months minimum (6-12 months optimal)
Frequency: 3× weekly during dialysis (first 2 hours of session)
Components:
  - Aerobic: Cycle ergometer, 20-30 min, moderate intensity
  - Resistance: Ankle weights, resistance bands, 2-3 sets × 10-15 reps
  - Flexibility: Stretching, range of motion

Example Protocol (EXCITE Trial):
- Weeks 1-4: Low intensity (RPE 11-13), 15-20 minutes
- Weeks 5-8: Moderate intensity (RPE 13-15), 20-30 minutes
- Weeks 9+: Individualized progression
- Exercises: Leg cycling, ankle weights (0.5-2 kg), arm curls

Outcomes (Meta-Analysis 2023):
- Gait speed: +0.14 m/s (95% CI 0.09-0.19) ⭐
- 6-minute walk: +53 meters
- Grip strength: +2.1 kg
- SPPB: +1.8 points
- Frailty prevalence: 15-20% reduction

Barriers:
- Patient reluctance (fatigue, motivation)
- Staff resources (supervision required)
- Equipment cost
- Hypotension during dialysis (monitor closely)

Implementation Tips:
- Start slow, gradual progression
- Physiotherapist involvement initially
- Peer support (group exercise)
- Track progress (motivational)
```

**2. Home-Based Exercise Programs:**

```
Indication: PD patients, pre-dialysis CKD, non-dialysis elderly

Typical Program:
- Resistance training: Resistance bands, body weight exercises
- Aerobic training: Walking program (goal: 30 min daily, 5-7 days/week)
- Balance training: Heel-toe stand, one-leg stand, tai chi

Example Protocol:
Resistance (3× weekly):
  - Squats (chair sit-to-stand): 3 sets × 10-15 reps
  - Bicep curls (resistance band): 3 sets × 10-15 reps
  - Heel raises: 3 sets × 10-15 reps

Aerobic (5-7 days/week):
  - Walking: Start 10 min, progress to 30 min
  - Intensity: "Somewhat hard" (can talk, not sing)

Balance (daily):
  - Heel-toe walk: 20 steps
  - One-leg stand: 10 seconds each leg
  - Tai chi or yoga: 20-30 minutes

Outcomes:
- Less robust data than intradialytic
- Benefits seen in gait speed, balance, QOL
- Adherence challenge (30-50% drop-out)

Barriers:
- Adherence/motivation (home setting)
- Safety concerns (falls)
- Lack of supervision
```

**3. Prehabilitation Before Dialysis Initiation:** ⭐

```
Rationale:
- Frailty worsens after dialysis initiation (50% of patients)
- Pre-dialysis intervention may prevent decline
- Prepare for treatment burden

Ideal Timing:
- CKD Stage 4-5 (eGFR <30), before dialysis start
- Minimum 3 months of intervention

Program Components:
- Exercise: Supervised or home-based, 3× weekly
- Nutritional support: Protein adequacy, prevent malnutrition
- Medication optimization: Reduce polypharmacy
- Advance care planning: Goals of care discussions

Evidence (Limited):
- Small studies show improved physical function
- May reduce hospitalization in first year of dialysis
- No large RCTs yet (ongoing trials)
```

---

#### Nutritional Interventions

**1. Protein Supplementation:**

```
Rationale:
- Protein-energy wasting common in CKD (30-50%)
- Low albumin/prealbumin associated with frailty
- Dialysis causes protein losses (6-8 g per HD session)

Recommendations:
- Pre-dialysis CKD: 0.6-0.8 g/kg/day (low-protein diet)
  - EXCEPTION: If frail/malnourished, liberalize to 0.8-1.0 g/kg/day
- Dialysis patients: 1.0-1.2 g/kg/day minimum
- Frail dialysis patients: Consider 1.2-1.5 g/kg/day

Supplementation Strategies:
- Oral nutritional supplements: 15-30 g protein per serving
  - Examples: Nepro, Suplena (renal-specific formulas)
  - Timing: Intradialytic or post-dialysis
- Protein powder: Whey or plant-based, add to meals
- Whole food sources: Eggs, chicken, fish, legumes

Evidence:
- Small studies show improved albumin, prealbumin
- May improve grip strength, muscle mass
- Limited data on frailty outcomes specifically
```

**2. Vitamin D Supplementation:**

```
Rationale:
- Vitamin D deficiency highly prevalent in CKD (>80%)
- Vitamin D affects muscle strength, fall risk
- Receptors in muscle tissue

Recommendations:
- Target 25-OH vitamin D: >30 ng/mL (>75 nmol/L)
- Repletion: Ergocalciferol 50,000 IU weekly × 8-12 weeks
- Maintenance: Cholecalciferol 1000-2000 IU daily

Active Vitamin D (Calcitriol, Paricalcitol):
- Indication: Secondary hyperparathyroidism (PTH >150-300)
- May have additional effects on muscle
- Monitor calcium, phosphate closely

Evidence on Frailty:
- Mixed results in trials
- May improve muscle strength (small effect)
- Fall prevention: Benefit in vitamin D-deficient patients
- No clear benefit on frailty scores
```

**3. Anemia Management:**

```
Rationale:
- Anemia associated with fatigue, exercise intolerance
- Hgb <10 g/dL: Significant functional impairment

Recommendations:
- ESA therapy: Target Hgb 10-11 g/dL (avoid >11 g/dL)
- Iron supplementation: IV preferred if TSAT <30%, ferritin <500
- Avoid over-treatment (Hgb >11: Increased CV events)

Frailty Outcomes:
- Correction of anemia improves fatigue, exercise capacity
- May improve physical function (gait speed, SPPB)
- No direct evidence on frailty reversal
```

---

#### Medication Optimization (Deprescribing) ⭐

```
Rationale:
- Polypharmacy (≥5 medications) associated with frailty
- Elderly CKD: Average 10-15 medications
- Anticholinergic burden worsens cognition, falls

Systematic Deprescribing Approach:

1. Identify Potentially Inappropriate Medications (PIMs):
   - Use Beers Criteria for elderly
   - CKD-specific considerations (renal dosing)

   Common PIMs in Elderly CKD:
   - NSAIDs: AKI risk, hyperkalemia
   - PPIs: Long-term use without indication (fracture, infection risk)
   - Benzodiazepines: Fall risk, cognitive impairment
   - Antihistamines (first-generation): Anticholinergic effects
   - Sulfonylureas: Hypoglycemia risk in CKD

2. Assess Risk-Benefit:
   - Consider life expectancy, goals of care
   - Time to benefit vs patient prognosis

   Examples:
   - Statins: Consider stopping if age >85, limited life expectancy (<1 year)
     * Time to benefit: 2-3 years for primary prevention
   - Tight glycemic control: Relax HbA1c target to 7.5-8.5% in frail elderly
     * Avoid hypoglycemia (major harm in elderly)

3. Gradual Tapering:
   - Avoid abrupt discontinuation (withdrawal effects)
   - Monitor for recurrence of symptoms

Evidence:
- Deprescribing reduces pill burden, costs
- May improve adherence to essential medications
- Limited direct evidence on frailty improvement
- Reduces fall risk, anticholinergic burden
```

#### Multicomponent Interventions (Most Effective) ⭐

```
Rationale:
- Frailty is multifactorial → requires multicomponent approach
- Exercise + nutrition + medication optimization + social support

Example Program (Multidisciplinary Frailty Clinic):

Team Members:
- Nephrologist
- Geriatrician or geriatric nurse
- Physical therapist
- Dietitian
- Pharmacist
- Social worker

Components:
1. Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA):
   - Frailty assessment (Fried, CFS)
   - Cognitive screening (MoCA)
   - Mood (PHQ-9)
   - Functional status (ADLs, IADLs)
   - Nutritional status (SGA)
   - Polypharmacy review
   - Social support assessment

2. Individualized Care Plan:
   - Exercise prescription (supervised or home-based)
   - Nutritional intervention (protein, calories, supplements)
   - Medication optimization (deprescribing)
   - Cognitive support (if impairment)
   - Depression treatment (if indicated)
   - Fall prevention strategies
   - Advance care planning

3. Follow-Up and Monitoring:
   - Monthly visits × 3 months, then quarterly
   - Reassess frailty scores (track progress)
   - Adjust interventions as needed

Evidence (2024):
- Few studies in CKD/dialysis specifically
- General geriatric population: Multicomponent programs effective
- Reduce frailty, hospitalization, functional decline
- Cost-effective (reduce hospital costs)

Challenges:
- Resource-intensive (multidisciplinary team)
- Reimbursement (not well-covered by Medicare/insurance)
- Patient engagement (requires motivation)
```

### Clinical Implementation Strategies (2024 Best Practices) ⭐

#### Integrating Frailty Assessment into Routine CKD Care

```
Pre-Dialysis CKD Clinic:
- Frequency: Annual frailty screening for all patients ≥65 years
  - OR age <65 with high comorbidity burden
- Tool: Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS) - quick, no equipment
- Action:
  - CFS 1-3 (robust): Routine care
  - CFS 4 (vulnerable): Lifestyle counseling, encourage exercise
  - CFS ≥5 (frail): Referral to multidisciplinary frailty program

Dialysis Initiation:
- Timing: At dialysis decision-making (CKD Stage 5)
- Tool: Fried Frailty Phenotype OR CFS
- Action:
  - Robust/pre-frail: Proceed with dialysis planning
  - Frail (CFS 5-6): Prehabilitation before dialysis, consider assisted PD
  - Severely frail (CFS ≥7): Discuss conservative kidney management

Dialysis Unit (Incident Patients):
- Timing: Within first month of dialysis
- Tool: Fried Frailty Phenotype (comprehensive)
- Action:
  - Frail: Enroll in intradialytic exercise program
  - Frail: Nutritional intervention (dietitian)
  - Frail: Medication review (pharmacist)

Dialysis Unit (Prevalent Patients):
- Frequency: Every 6-12 months
- Tool: FRAIL scale (quick screen) OR gait speed
- Action:
  - Worsening frailty: Reassess dialysis adequacy, medications, nutrition
  - Persistent severe frailty: Palliative care involvement, goals of care

Transplant Evaluation:
- Timing: At referral for transplant
- Tool: Fried Frailty Phenotype + SPPB
- Action:
  - Frail: Delay listing, prehabilitation program
  - If frailty improves: Re-evaluate for listing
  - If frailty persists/worsens: May not be transplant candidate
```

#### Documentation and Communication

```
Frailty Status Documentation (EHR):
- Record frailty score in structured field
- Include date, tool used, scorer
- Trend over time (track trajectory)

Communication with Patient/Family:
- Explain frailty (not just "old age")
- Frame as modifiable (with interventions)
- Engage in shared decision-making

Communication with Team:
- Notify primary care provider of frailty status
- Coordinate interventions across providers
- Avoid "frailty nihilism" (frailty ≠ futility)
```

### BOARD EXAM HIGH-YIELD CONCEPTS ⭐⭐⭐

```
1. Frailty Prevalence:
   - Dialysis patients: 50-75% frail (vs 10-15% in general elderly)
   - Pre-dialysis CKD Stage 5: 40-50% frail

2. Fried Frailty Phenotype:
   - 5 components: Weight loss, exhaustion, weakness, slow gait, low activity
   - Scoring: 0 = robust, 1-2 = pre-frail, 3-5 = frail
   - Best-validated tool in dialysis populations

3. Clinical Frailty Scale:
   - 9-point scale, rapid assessment (2-3 minutes)
   - CFS ≥7: Consider conservative kidney management over dialysis
   - Used in KDIGO 2024 guidelines

4. Gait Speed:
   - "Sixth vital sign"
   - <0.8 m/s: Frailty indicator
   - <0.6 m/s: High mortality risk in dialysis
   - Each 0.1 m/s decrease: 10-15% higher mortality

5. Frailty and Mortality:
   - Frail dialysis patients: 2-3× higher mortality vs non-frail
   - Frailty predicts mortality better than age alone

6. Falls in Dialysis:
   - Fall incidence: 1.6 falls/patient-year (vs 0.6 in general elderly)
   - Frail dialysis: 3.2 falls/patient-year
   - Hip fracture post-dialysis: 50% mortality at 1 year

7. Interventions:
   - Intradialytic exercise: STRONGEST EVIDENCE for frailty improvement
   - Improves gait speed (+0.14 m/s), SPPB (+1.8 points), grip strength (+2.1 kg)
   - 15-20% reduction in frailty prevalence

8. Transplant:
   - Frail candidates: 2.5× higher waitlist mortality
   - Prehabilitation may improve outcomes before listing

9. Conservative Kidney Management:
   - CFS ≥7 (severely frail): CKM may be preferred over dialysis
   - Quality of life often better with CKM in very frail elderly

10. Deprescribing:
    - Polypharmacy (≥5 meds) associated with frailty
    - PIMs in elderly CKD: NSAIDs, PPIs, benzodiazepines, first-gen antihistamines
```

## Palliative Care in Advanced Kidney Disease

### Symptom Management

#### Common Symptoms and Management
```
Pain:
- Assessment: Use pain scales appropriate for cognitive status
- Pharmacologic: Acetaminophen first-line, avoid NSAIDs
- Opioids: Start low, go slow; avoid accumulation of metabolites
- Non-pharmacologic: Physical therapy, heat/cold, massage

Pruritus:
- First-line: Antihistamines limited efficacy
- Gabapentin/Pregabalin: Start low dose, monitor for sedation
- Nalfurafine: Opioid receptor antagonist (available in some countries)
- Phototherapy: UVB treatment for refractory cases

Nausea/Vomiting:
- Metoclopramide: 5-10 mg PO/IV q6h PRN
- Ondansetron: 4-8 mg PO/IV q8h PRN
- Haloperidol: 0.5-1 mg PO/IV q6h PRN
- Dronabinol: 2.5-5 mg PO BID

Fatigue:
- Treat contributing factors: Anemia, depression, poor sleep
- Exercise: Light activity as tolerated
- Stimulants: Methylphenidate 5-10 mg daily (consider)
- Energy conservation: Prioritize activities

Restless Legs Syndrome:
- Gabapentin: 100-300 mg at bedtime
- Pregabalin: 50-150 mg at bedtime
- Iron supplementation: If ferritin <100 ng/mL
- Avoid dopamine antagonists
```

#### Psychosocial Support
- **Depression and Anxiety**: Screen using PHQ-2/PHQ-9, GAD-7
- **Spiritual Distress**: Address existential concerns, chaplaincy services
- **Family Support**: Caregiver burden assessment, respite care
- **Advance Care Planning**: Early discussions about goals of care

### End-of-Life Care

#### Prognostication
- **Kidney Failure-Specific Signs**:
  - Decreasing oral intake
  - Increasing somnolence
  - Worsening edema despite diuretics
  - Decline in functional status

- **General Prognostic Indicators**:
  - Performance status (ECOG ≥3)
  - Unintentional weight loss
  - Frequent hospitalizations
  - Physician estimate of prognosis

#### Dialysis Withdrawal
- **Indications**:
  - Unacceptable quality of life on dialysis
  - Progressive decline despite dialysis
  - Patient preference after informed decision
  - Medical futility (rare)

- **Process**:
  - Family meeting with all stakeholders
  - Symptom management plan
  - Hospice referral
  - Emotional support for family and staff

- **Expected Course**:
  - Survival: 7-10 days average (range 1-30 days)
  - Symptom progression: Uremic symptoms, somnolence
  - Death: Usually peaceful, uremic coma

## Special Considerations in Elderly Dialysis Patients

### Hemodialysis Adaptations

#### Prescription Modifications
- **Blood Flow Rate**: 200-300 mL/min (lower than standard)
- **Dialysate Flow**: 300-500 mL/min
- **Treatment Time**: 3-4 hours, may need shorter sessions
- **Frequency**: 2-3 times weekly, individualized
- **Ultrafiltration**: Gradual, <10 mL/kg/hour to prevent hypotension

#### Vascular Access Challenges
- **AV Fistula Creation**: Higher failure rates in elderly
- **Catheter Use**: More common due to vessel quality
- **Access Monitoring**: Regular surveillance for dysfunction
- **Infection Risk**: Higher with catheters, careful monitoring needed

### Peritoneal Dialysis in Elderly

#### Advantages
- **Hemodynamic Stability**: Better tolerated in cardiovascular disease
- **Home-Based**: Less transportation burden
- **Gradual Ultrafiltration**: More physiologic
- **Preservation of Residual Function**: Slower decline

#### Challenges
- **Physical Requirements**: Need for manual dexterity, vision
- **Support Needs**: Family assistance or home nursing
- **Peritonitis Risk**: Higher in some elderly populations
- **Hernia Risk**: Increased due to weakened abdominal wall

#### Adaptations
- **Assisted PD**: Home nurse assistance for frail patients
- **Automated PD**: Cycler machines reduce manual exchanges
- **Reduced Volumes**: Smaller fill volumes for comfort
- **Simplified Regimens**: Fewer exchanges if adequate clearance

## Cognitive Impairment and Kidney Disease

### Epidemiology and Impact

#### Prevalence and Types
- **Mild Cognitive Impairment**: 20-30% of CKD stage 3-4 patients
- **Dementia**: 10-15% prevalence, higher than general population
- **Vascular Dementia**: Most common type in CKD patients
- **Dialysis-Associated Dementia**: Rare, aluminum-related historically

#### Pathophysiology
- **Vascular Disease**: Cerebral small vessel disease
- **Uremic Toxins**: Direct neurotoxic effects
- **Anemia**: Cerebral hypoxia
- **Inflammation**: Neuroinflammation and oxidative stress
- **Electrolyte Imbalance**: Effects on neuronal function

### Assessment and Management

#### Cognitive Screening
- **Mini-Cog**: 3-item recall + clock drawing (quick screen)
- **MoCA**: More sensitive for mild cognitive impairment
- **MMSE**: Limited utility in advanced CKD
- **Informant Assessment**: AD8 questionnaire for caregiver input

#### Management Strategies
- **Dialysis Optimization**: Adequate clearance may improve cognition
- **Blood Pressure Control**: Prevent further vascular injury
- **Medication Review**: Reduce anticholinergic burden
- **Environmental Modifications**: Simplify medication regimens
- **Caregiver Support**: Education about cognitive changes

## Polypharmacy and Medication Management

### Challenges in Elderly CKD Patients

#### Pharmacokinetic Changes
- **Reduced Renal Clearance**: Accumulation of renally excreted drugs
- **Decreased Protein Binding**: Increased free drug levels
- **Altered Volume of Distribution**: Changes in body composition
- **Reduced Metabolic Clearance**: Hepatic function decline

#### Common Problematic Medications
- **NSAIDs**: Worsen renal function, increase cardiovascular risk
- **ACE Inhibitors/ARBs**: Hyperkalemia, AKI risk
- **Diuretics**: Dehydration, electrolyte abnormalities
- **Anticoagulants**: Bleeding risk, dose adjustment needed
- **Antibiotics**: Nephrotoxicity accumulation

### Deprescribing Strategies

#### Systematic Approach
1. **Medication Reconciliation**: Complete medication list
2. **Indication Review**: Each medication has clear indication
3. **Risk-Benefit Assessment**: Consider life expectancy and goals
4. **Prioritization**: Essential vs non-essential medications
5. **Monitoring Plan**: Watch for withdrawal effects

#### Specific Deprescribing Protocols
```
Antihypertensives:
- Goal: SBP 130-150 mmHg in most elderly
- Discontinue: Add third agent if >2 medications needed
- Monitor: Blood pressure, orthostatic symptoms

Diabetes Medications:
- Target: HbA1c 7.5-8.5% in frail elderly
- Discontinue: Sulfonylureas, complex regimens
- Simplify: Metformin alone if GFR >30

Statins:
- Consider stopping: Age >85, limited life expectancy
- Continue: Recent cardiovascular event (<2 years)
- Monitor: Lipid levels, liver function

PPIs:
- Reassess need: Often used long-term without indication
- Taper: Gradual discontinuation to prevent rebound
- Monitor: GERD symptoms, gastritis signs
```

## COVID-19 in Elderly CKD and Dialysis Patients (2024 Update)

### Epidemiology and Risk

#### Vulnerability of Elderly CKD Patients
```
Risk Factors for Severe COVID-19:
- Age >65 years: 5-fold increased mortality
- Advanced CKD (stage 4-5): 2-3× higher mortality
- Dialysis patients: 20-30% hospitalization rate, 20-25% mortality
- Transplant recipients: Immunosuppression increases risk

Pathophysiology:
- Immune dysregulation in CKD/ESRD
- Chronic inflammation, uremia
- Endothelial dysfunction
- ACE2 receptor expression (kidney involvement)
```

### Prevention Strategies (2024 Guidelines)

#### Vaccination Recommendations
```
Primary Vaccination Series (Immunocompromised Schedule):
- mRNA vaccines preferred (Pfizer, Moderna, Novavax)
- 3-dose primary series for immunocompromised
  - Dose 1: Initial
  - Dose 2: 3-4 weeks after dose 1
  - Dose 3: 4 weeks after dose 2 (additional primary dose)
- CKD patients (non-dialysis): Standard or immunocompromised schedule
- Dialysis patients: 3-dose immunocompromised schedule
- Transplant recipients: 3-dose immunocompromised schedule

Booster Doses (2024 Updated Vaccines):
- 2024-2025 formulation: Updated for current variants
- Timing: ≥6 months after last dose or infection
- Annual boosters recommended (similar to influenza)
- Preferably after dialysis session (avoid on dialysis day)

Antibody Response in Elderly CKD:
- Reduced compared to healthy elderly (40-60% seroconversion)
- Dialysis patients: 50-70% seroconversion after 3 doses
- Transplant recipients: 30-50% seroconversion (lower due to immunosuppression)
- Age >75: Further reduced response
- Antibody monitoring: Not routinely recommended (unreliable correlate of protection)
```

#### Non-Pharmacologic Prevention
```
Dialysis Units:
- Universal masking (staff and patients)
- Screening at entry (temperature, symptoms)
- Cohorting of COVID-positive patients
- Enhanced cleaning and disinfection
- Vaccination of all staff

Individual Measures:
- Masking in public indoor spaces
- Avoid crowded settings
- Hand hygiene
- Stay up-to-date with vaccinations
```

### Treatment of COVID-19 in Elderly CKD (2024 Protocols)

#### Outpatient Management (Mild-Moderate COVID)

```
Antiviral Therapy (First 5 Days of Symptoms):

Nirmatrelvir/Ritonavir (Paxlovid) - PREFERRED:
- Indication: High-risk patients within 5 days of symptom onset
- Age ≥65 alone qualifies as high-risk
- Dosing adjustments for CKD:
  - eGFR ≥60: 300/100 mg PO BID × 5 days (standard dose)
  - eGFR 30-59: 150/100 mg PO BID × 5 days (reduced dose)
  - eGFR <30 (including dialysis): NOT RECOMMENDED (accumulation risk)
- Drug interactions: MAJOR concern
  - Ritonavir inhibits CYP3A4, P-glycoprotein
  - Interactions: Statins, immunosuppressants, many cardiac drugs
  - Immunosuppression: Hold tacrolimus/cyclosporine during treatment, reduce dose after
  - Statins: Hold during treatment (rhabdomyolysis risk)

Remdesivir (Veklury):
- FDA-approved for outpatient use (2022)
- Indication: High-risk patients within 7 days
- Dosing: 200 mg IV day 1, then 100 mg IV days 2-3
- CKD dosing: No adjustment needed (eGFR ≥30)
- eGFR <30: Use with caution, monitor transaminases
- Advantage: No major drug interactions, can use in dialysis patients

Molnupiravir (Lagevrio):
- Alternative if Paxlovid/remdesivir contraindicated
- Dosing: 800 mg PO BID × 5 days
- CKD: No dose adjustment
- Less effective than Paxlovid (30% vs 89% hospitalization reduction)

Monoclonal Antibodies:
- Most formulations NO LONGER EFFECTIVE (variant resistance)
- Not routinely recommended as of 2024
```

#### Inpatient Management (Severe COVID)

```
Antiviral Therapy:
- Remdesivir: 200 mg IV day 1, then 100 mg daily × 4-9 days total
- CKD: Use with caution if eGFR <30, monitor LFTs

Corticosteroids:
- Dexamethasone: 6 mg IV/PO daily × 10 days
- Indication: Requiring supplemental oxygen
- CKD: No dose adjustment
- Benefits: Reduce mortality in severe COVID

Immunomodulators (Severe Cases):
- Tocilizumab (IL-6 inhibitor): 8 mg/kg IV × 1-2 doses
  - Indication: Rapidly worsening respiratory status
  - CKD: No dose adjustment
- Baricitinib (JAK inhibitor): 4 mg PO daily × 14 days
  - CKD dosing: Reduce to 2 mg if eGFR 30-60, avoid if <30

Anticoagulation:
- Prophylactic heparin: All hospitalized patients
- Therapeutic anticoagulation: If thrombosis suspected
- CKD: Standard dosing, monitor anti-Xa if ESRD
```

#### COVID-19 and Dialysis Management

```
Dialysis Scheduling:
- Separate shift or end of day for COVID-positive patients
- Dedicated machines if possible
- Enhanced PPE for staff

Dialysis Prescription:
- Continue regular dialysis schedule (maintain adequacy)
- May need to shorten sessions if unstable
- Monitor fluid status (COVID pneumonia + fluid overload)

Anticoagulation:
- Regional citrate preferred (reduce bleeding risk)
- Heparin acceptable with monitoring

Post-Dialysis:
- Enhanced terminal disinfection
- Room air exchange before next patient
```

### Long COVID in Elderly CKD Patients (2024 Data)

```
Prevalence and Symptoms:
- Post-acute sequelae of COVID (PASC): 10-30% of elderly COVID survivors
- Common symptoms:
  - Fatigue (most common, 50-70%)
  - Dyspnea (30-50%)
  - Cognitive dysfunction ("brain fog", 20-40%)
  - Post-exertional malaise
  - Sleep disturbances

Impact on CKD:
- AKI from COVID: 10-20% develop new CKD or CKD progression
- eGFR decline: May be accelerated post-COVID
- Proteinuria: New or worsening proteinuria common

Management:
- Multidisciplinary approach: Nephrology, pulmonary, neurology, rehabilitation
- Symptom-directed therapy
- Continued nephroprotective medications (SGLT2i, ACE/ARB)
- Monitor kidney function closely (3-6 months post-COVID)
```

## Key Practice Points

### Clinical Pearls (2024 Updated)
1. **SGLT2 Inhibitors**: Indicated for ALL elderly CKD patients (eGFR 20-90), age NOT a contraindication - proven benefit in age ≥75 years
2. **eGFR Interpretation**: Use BIS equations or cystatin C-based eGFR in frail elderly for better accuracy
3. **Frailty Assessment**: Incorporate into routine CKD management - predicts outcomes better than age alone
4. **Shared Decision-Making**: Essential for dialysis initiation - use prognostic scores (Cohen, REIN)
5. **Conservative Kidney Management**: Valid option for very elderly/frail - may provide better quality of life than dialysis
6. **Medication Review**: Regular deprescribing to reduce polypharmacy - use systematic approach
7. **Palliative Care**: Integrate early for symptom management, not just at end-of-life
8. **COVID-19 Vaccination**: 3-dose primary series for dialysis/transplant patients, annual boosters recommended
9. **Paxlovid in CKD**: Dose reduction needed (eGFR 30-59), avoid if eGFR <30 - use remdesivir instead

### Red Flags
- **Rapid Functional Decline**: May indicate disease progression or complications
- **Cognitive Changes**: New confusion or delirium needs urgent evaluation
- **Frequent Falls**: Assess for contributing factors (BP, medications)
- **Weight Loss**: Unintentional weight loss needs comprehensive evaluation
- **Depression**: Screen regularly, especially after dialysis initiation

### Prevention Strategies
- **Regular Assessment**: Comprehensive geriatric assessment every 6 months
- **Fall Prevention**: Home safety evaluation, physical therapy
- **Vaccinations**: Influenza, pneumococcal, COVID-19, shingles
- **Nutrition**: Regular screening for malnutrition
- **Advance Care Planning**: Early discussions before crisis
