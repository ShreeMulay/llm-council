## Pharmacokinetic Principles in Kidney Disease

### Drug Absorption
- **Gastrointestinal Changes**: Uremic gastritis, delayed gastric emptying
- **Bioavailability**: May be increased due to reduced first-pass metabolism
- **Oral Bioavailability**: Variable for some drugs (e.g., furosemide 40-60% in CKD vs 50-70% normal)

### Drug Distribution
- **Volume of Distribution**: 
  - Increased for hydrophilic drugs (fluid overload)
  - Decreased for highly protein-bound drugs (hypoalbuminemia)
- **Protein Binding**: 
  - Reduced albumin increases free fraction of acidic drugs
  - Uremic toxins may displace drugs from binding sites
- **Tissue Binding**: Accumulation in tissues with impaired elimination

### Drug Metabolism
- **Hepatic Metabolism**: Generally preserved but may be altered
- **Phase I Reactions**: Oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis (CYP450)
- **Phase II Reactions**: Conjugation (glucuronidation, sulfation)
- **Non-renal Clearance**: May increase as renal clearance decreases

### Drug Elimination
- **Glomerular Filtration**: Primary route for many drugs and metabolites
- **Tubular Secretion**: Active transport (OAT, OCT systems)
- **Tubular Reabsorption**: Passive diffusion, pH-dependent
- **Renal Clearance**: CLr = (Cu × Vu) / Cp

## Dosing Adjustment Strategies

### Estimating Renal Function

#### Creatinine-Based Equations
```
Cockcroft-Gault Equation:
CrCl (mL/min) = [(140 - Age) × Weight (kg)] / [72 × SCr (mg/dL)]
× 0.85 (if female)

MDRD Equation (2006):
eGFR = 175 × (SCr)^-1.154 × (Age)^-0.203 × (0.742 if female) × (1.212 if African American)

CKD-EPI Equation (2009):
More accurate at higher GFR levels, preferred for staging
```

#### Cystatin C-Based Equations
- **Advantages**: Less affected by muscle mass, diet, inflammation
- **Indications**: Elderly, malnourished, amputees, muscle-wasting diseases
- **CKD-EPI Cystatin C**: Alternative when creatinine unreliable

### Dosing Adjustment Methods

#### Dose Reduction Method
- **Formula**: Adjusted Dose = Normal Dose × (Patient CrCl / Normal CrCl)
- **Use**: Drugs with concentration-dependent toxicity
- **Example**: Gentamicin, vancomycin, lithium

#### Dose Interval Extension
- **Formula**: Adjusted Interval = Normal Interval × (Normal CrCl / Patient CrCl)
- **Use**: Drugs with time-dependent efficacy
- **Example**: Beta-lactams, penicillins

#### Combined Method
- **Both dose reduction and interval extension**
- **Use**: Drugs with narrow therapeutic windows
- **Example**: Digoxin, aminoglycosides

### Loading Dose Considerations
- **Generally Unchanged**: Based on volume of distribution
- **Exceptions**: Significant volume of distribution changes
- **Formula**: Loading Dose = Target Cp × Vd

## Major Drug Classes & Renal Dosing

### Antibiotics

#### Beta-Lactams
```
Penicillins:
- Amoxicillin: CrCl <30: q12h dosing
- Amoxicillin-Clavulanate: CrCl <30: q12h dosing
- Piperacillin-tazobactam: CrCl <20: 2.25g q8h

Cephalosporins:
- Cefazolin: CrCl <30: q12h dosing
- Ceftriaxone: No adjustment needed (biliary excretion)
- Cefepime: CrCl <30: q24h dosing

Carbapenems:
- Imipenem-cilastatin: CrCl <30: q12h dosing
- Meropenem: CrCl <30: q24h dosing
- Ertapenem: CrCl <30: q24h dosing
```

#### Aminoglycosides
- **Gentamicin, Tobramycin**: 
  - Loading: 2 mg/kg ideal body weight
  - Maintenance: CrCl <30: q24-48h dosing
  - Monitoring: Peak 5-10 μg/mL, Trough <2 μg/mL
- **Amikacin**: Similar dosing, higher target levels

#### Vancomycin
- **Loading**: 25-30 mg/kg actual body weight
- **Maintenance**: 
  - CrCl 30-50: q24h dosing
  - CrCl 10-29: q48-72h dosing
  - CrCl <10: q72-96h dosing
- **Monitoring**: Trough 15-20 μg/mL (AUC/MC preferred)

#### Fluoroquinolones
- **Ciprofloxacin**: CrCl <30: q24h dosing
- **Levofloxacin**: CrCl <20: q48h dosing
- **Moxifloxacin**: No adjustment needed

#### Antifungals
- **Fluconazole**: CrCl <50: 50% dose reduction
- **Voriconazole**: No adjustment needed (hepatic metabolism)
- **Echinocandins**: No adjustment needed

### Cardiovascular Drugs

#### ACE Inhibitors & ARBs
```
ACE Inhibitors:
- Lisinopril: CrCl <30: Start 5mg q48h
- Enalapril: CrCl <30: 5mg q24h
- Ramipril: CrCl <30: 2.5mg q24h

ARBs:
- Losartan: CrCl <30: 25mg q24h
- Valsartan: CrCl <30: 80mg q24h
- Irbesartan: CrCl <30: 75mg q24h
```

#### Beta-Blockers
- **Metoprolol**: No adjustment needed (hepatic metabolism)
- **Atenolol**: CrCl <35: 50mg q24h
- **Carvedilol**: No adjustment needed

#### Calcium Channel Blockers
- **Amlodipine**: No adjustment needed
- **Diltiazem**: No adjustment needed
- **Verapamil**: No adjustment needed

#### Diuretics
```
Loop Diuretics:
- Furosemide: CrCl <20: Higher doses needed (40-80mg q6-8h)
- Torsemide: CrCl <30: 20-100mg daily
- Bumetanide: CrCl <20: 1-2mg q6-8h

Thiazide Diuretics:
- Hydrochlorothiazide: Ineffective when CrCl <30
- Metolazone: Can be used down to CrCl 10-20
- Chlorthalidone: CrCl <30: Avoid

Potassium-Sparing:
- Spironolactone: CrCl <30: Avoid or use 12.5mg q24h
- Eplerenone: CrCl <30: Avoid
- Amiloride: CrCl <30: Avoid
```

### Antidiabetic Drugs

#### Insulin
- **Clearance**: Reduced renal clearance
- **Dosing**: 20-50% dose reduction when eGFR <30
- **Monitoring**: Frequent glucose monitoring, avoid hypoglycemia

#### Oral Agents
```
Metformin:
- Contraindicated when eGFR <30
- Caution when eGFR 30-45
- Hold during acute illness, contrast procedures

Sulfonylureas:
- Glyburide: Avoid when eGFR <60
- Glipizide: No adjustment needed
- Glimepiride: CrCl <30: Start 1mg daily

DPP-4 Inhibitors:
- Sitagliptin: CrCl <30: 25mg q24h
- Saxagliptin: CrCl <30: 2.5mg q24h
- Linagliptin: No adjustment needed

SGLT2 Inhibitors:
- Generally ineffective when eGFR <30-45
- Canagliflozin: eGFR <30: Avoid
- Empagliflozin: eGFR <20: Avoid
- Dapagliflozin: eGFR <30: Avoid (except for HF/CKD indication)

GLP-1 Agonists:
- Exenatide: CrCl <30: Avoid
- Liraglutide: No adjustment needed
- Semaglutide: No adjustment needed
```

## Nephrotoxic Drugs

### Aminoglycosides
- **Mechanism**: Proximal tubular cell injury, mitochondrial dysfunction
- **Risk Factors**: High peak levels, prolonged therapy, concurrent nephrotoxins
- **Prevention**: 
  - Once-daily dosing
  - Therapeutic drug monitoring
  - Adequate hydration
  - Avoid concurrent NSAIDs, contrast

### Amphotericin B
- **Mechanism**: Tubular cell membrane damage, vasoconstriction
- **Risk Factors**: High cumulative dose, rapid infusion
- **Prevention**:
  - Lipid formulations (less nephrotoxic)
  - Sodium loading (0.9% saline 1L before and after)
  - Slow infusion over 2-6 hours
  - Monitor potassium and magnesium

### Contrast Media
- **Mechanism**: Renal vasoconstriction, direct tubular toxicity
- **Risk Factors**: Pre-existing CKD, diabetes, dehydration, high contrast volume
- **Prevention**:
  - Hydration: 0.9% saline 1mL/kg/hour 12 hours before and after
  - Low-osmolar or iso-osmolar contrast
  - Minimal effective dose
  - N-acetylcysteine: controversial benefit
  - Hold nephrotoxic medications

### NSAIDs
- **Mechanism**: Prostaglandin synthesis inhibition → afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction
- **Risk Factors**: Volume depletion, CKD, heart failure, cirrhosis
- **Prevention**:
  - Avoid in high-risk patients
  - Use lowest effective dose
  - Short duration only
  - Consider acetaminophen or COX-2 selective agents

### Calcineurin Inhibitors
- **Cyclosporine, Tacrolimus**: Afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction
- **Prevention**:
  - Therapeutic drug monitoring
  - Avoid high trough levels
  - Maintain adequate hydration
  - Consider CNI-sparing protocols

## Drug Interactions in Kidney Disease

### Pharmacokinetic Interactions

#### Altered Protein Binding
- **Displacement**: Highly protein-bound drugs displaced by uremic toxins
- **Examples**: Warfarin, phenytoin, valproic acid
- **Management**: Monitor free drug levels, adjust doses

#### Reduced Renal Clearance
- **Accumulation**: Drugs primarily renally excreted accumulate
- **Examples**: Digoxin, lithium, metformin
- **Management**: Dose adjustment, therapeutic monitoring

#### Competition for Tubular Secretion
- **Mechanism**: Competition for OAT/OCT transport systems
- **Examples**: Probenecid, cimetidine, trimethoprim
- **Management**: Monitor for increased levels of affected drugs

### Pharmacodynamic Interactions

#### Additive Nephrotoxicity
- **Combinations**: Aminoglycosides + NSAIDs, contrast + ACE inhibitors
- **Management**: Avoid combinations, monitor renal function closely

#### Enhanced Electrolyte Effects
- **Hyperkalemia**: ACE inhibitors + potassium-sparing diuretics + NSAIDs
- **Hyponatremia**: Thiazides + SSRIs
- **Management**: Monitor electrolytes, adjust doses

## Special Population Considerations

### Elderly Patients
- **Physiologic Changes**: Decreased GFR, reduced muscle mass
- **Dosing Principles**:
  - Start low, go slow
  - Use renal function estimates (not serum creatinine alone)
  - Monitor for adverse effects
  - Consider non-renal clearance changes

### Pediatric Patients
- **Dosing**: Weight-based dosing (mg/kg)
- **Renal Function**: Schwartz equation for eGFR estimation
- **Special Considerations**: 
  - Immature renal function in neonates
  - Volume of distribution differences
  - Long-term effects of nephrotoxic drugs

### Pregnancy
- **Physiologic Changes**: Increased GFR (50%), increased plasma volume
- **Dosing**: May need increased doses for renally excreted drugs
- **Safety**: Avoid known teratogens (ACE inhibitors, ARBs, warfarin)

### Liver Disease
- **Hepatorenal Syndrome**: Reduced renal perfusion
- **Drug Metabolism**: Impaired phase I/II reactions
- **Protein Binding**: Reduced albumin → increased free drug
- **Management**: Conservative dosing, monitor closely

## Dialysis and Drug Removal

### Hemodialysis

#### Drug Characteristics Affecting Removal
- **Molecular Weight**: <500 Da removed efficiently
- **Water Solubility**: Hydrophilic drugs removed better
- **Protein Binding**: <90% binding required for significant removal
- **Volume of Distribution**: <1 L/kg required for efficient removal

#### Dosing Adjustments
- **Supplemental Dosing**: Post-dialysis dosing for significantly removed drugs
- **Examples**: 
  - Gentamicin: 1-2 mg/kg after dialysis
  - Vancomycin: 500-1000 mg after dialysis
  - Phenytoin: Minimal removal, no adjustment needed

### Peritoneal Dialysis
- **Lower Clearance**: Generally less efficient than hemodialysis
- **Continuous Nature**: Steady-state concentrations
- **Drug-Specific**: Individual assessment required

### CRRT
- **High Clearance**: Similar to native kidney function
- **Dosing**: Often require increased doses or continuous infusion
- **Monitoring**: Frequent drug level monitoring essential

## Therapeutic Drug Monitoring

### Drugs Requiring Monitoring
- **Aminoglycosides**: Peak and trough levels
- **Vancomycin**: Trough or AUC/MIC
- **Digoxin**: Trough levels 0.5-0.9 ng/mL
- **Lithium**: Trough levels 0.6-1.2 mEq/L
- **Phenytoin**: Total and free levels
- **Calcineurin Inhibitors**: Trough levels

### Monitoring Principles
- **Timing**: 
  - Trough: Immediately before next dose
  - Peak: 30-60 minutes after IV infusion
- **Frequency**: 
  - Initial: 2-3 times weekly
  - Stable: Weekly to monthly
- **Sample Handling**: Proper collection and storage

## Novel FDA-Approved Nephrology Medications (2020-2024)

### SGLT2 Inhibitors (Cardio-Renal Protection)
**Empagliflozin (Jardiance):**
- **FDA Approval:** T2DM (2014), Heart Failure (2021), **CKD (2023)**
- **Indication:** CKD with eGFR ≥20 mL/min
- **Dosing:** 10 mg once daily
- **Trials:** EMPA-KIDNEY (2023) - 28% reduction in kidney disease progression
- **Renal Dosing:** Can initiate down to eGFR 20 mL/min

**Dapagliflozin (Farxiga):**
- **FDA Approval:** T2DM (2014), Heart Failure (2020), **CKD (2021)**
- **Indication:** CKD with eGFR ≥25 mL/min
- **Dosing:** 10 mg once daily
- **Trials:** DAPA-CKD (2020) - 39% reduction in kidney failure/eGFR decline
- **Renal Dosing:** Can initiate down to eGFR 25 mL/min

**Canagliflozin (Invokana):**
- **FDA Approval:** T2DM (2013), **CKD reduction (2019)**
- **Dosing:** 100-300 mg once daily
- **Trials:** CREDENCE (2019) - 30% reduction in kidney outcomes
- **Renal Dosing:** 100 mg if eGFR 30-59; not recommended <30

### Non-Steroidal Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists
**Finerenone (Kerendia):**
- **FDA Approval:** July 2021 for diabetic kidney disease
- **Indication:** CKD associated with T2DM (reduce kidney disease progression and CV events)
- **Dosing:**
  - eGFR ≥60: 20 mg once daily
  - eGFR 25-59: 10 mg once daily, may increase to 20 mg
  - eGFR <25: Not recommended
- **Trials:**
  - FIDELIO-DKD (2020): 18% reduction in kidney outcomes
  - FIGARO-DKD (2021): 13% reduction in CV death/HF events
  - FINEARTS-HF (2024): Heart failure with preserved EF
- **Renal Dosing:** Titrate based on eGFR and potassium
- **Monitoring:** Potassium at 4 weeks, then quarterly; may need patiromer/SZC

### GLP-1 Receptor Agonists (FDA-Approved for CKD)

**Semaglutide (Ozempic®, Wegovy®):**
- **FDA Approvals:**
  - T2DM (2017)
  - Weight loss (Wegovy 2021)
  - **CKD indication (January 28, 2025)** - First and ONLY GLP-1 approved for CKD
- **CKD Indication:** Reduce risk of kidney disease progression, kidney failure, and CV death in adults with T2DM + CKD
- **FLOW Trial (2024) - Landmark CKD Study:**
  - Stopped early for overwhelming efficacy
  - 24% reduction in kidney disease progression (primary endpoint)
  - 18% reduction in MACE
  - First GLP-1 trial with hard kidney endpoint
  - Study population: T2DM + CKD (eGFR 25-75 mL/min with albuminuria)
- **Dosing:**
  - T2DM/CKD: Start 0.25 mg weekly × 4 weeks → 0.5 mg weekly (target 1.0 mg weekly)
  - Weight loss (Wegovy): Up to 2.4 mg weekly
- **Mechanism of Kidney Protection:**
  - Weight loss (5-15% body weight)
  - BP reduction (systolic BP ↓ 3-6 mmHg)
  - Albuminuria reduction
  - Anti-inflammatory effects
  - Direct GLP-1 receptor effects in kidney
  - Glycemic control improvement
- **5-Pillar CKD Therapy:** ACEi/ARB + SGLT2i + Finerenone + Statin + **Semaglutide**
- **Renal Dosing:** No adjustment needed (safe down to eGFR 25 mL/min per FLOW trial)
- **Side Effects:** Nausea (20%), vomiting, diarrhea, pancreatitis (rare), gallbladder disease
- **Contraindications:** Personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma, MEN 2
- **Cost:** ~$900-1000/month (patient assistance programs available)

**Dulaglutide (Trulicity®):**
- **FDA Approval:** T2DM (2014), CV risk reduction (NO specific CKD indication)
- **Kidney Benefits:** REWIND trial (2019) - 15% reduction in composite kidney outcome
- **Dosing:** 0.75-4.5 mg subcutaneous weekly
- **2022 KDIGO Guidelines:** Preferred GLP-1 for CV/kidney benefits (alongside semaglutide, liraglutide)
- **Renal Dosing:** No adjustment needed

**Liraglutide (Victoza®):**
- **FDA Approval:** T2DM (2010), CV risk reduction (NO specific CKD indication)
- **Kidney Benefits:** LEADER trial showed nephroprotective effects
- **Dosing:** 1.2-1.8 mg subcutaneous daily
- **2022 KDIGO Guidelines:** Preferred GLP-1 for CV/kidney benefits
- **Renal Dosing:** No adjustment needed

### Targeted Glomerular Disease Therapies
**Sparsentan (Filspari):**
- **FDA Approval:** August 2023 for IgA nephropathy
- **Mechanism:** Dual endothelin receptor antagonist/angiotensin receptor blocker
- **Indication:** Reduce proteinuria in IgA nephropathy
- **Dosing:** 200 mg once daily (may increase to 400 mg)
- **Trials:** PROTECT (2023) - 49.8% proteinuria reduction vs. 15.1% with irbesartan
- **Renal Dosing:** No adjustment for mild-moderate CKD
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy (teratogenic), use with ACEi/ARB
- **Monitoring:** BP, hemoglobin, liver enzymes

**Atrasentan (Vanrafia®):**
- **FDA Approval:** April 2, 2025 for IgA nephropathy (accelerated approval) ⭐ **NEWEST**
- **Mechanism:** Selective endothelin A receptor antagonist (ET-A selective, spares ET-B)
- **Indication:** Reduce proteinuria in adults with primary IgA nephropathy at risk of rapid disease progression
- **Dosing:** 0.75 mg PO once daily
- **ALIGN Trial (2024-2025):**
  - 36.1% proteinuria reduction vs. placebo at 36 weeks
  - 37.4% reduction in patients also on SGLT2 inhibitors (synergistic effect)
  - Published NEJM October 2024
- **Advantages over non-selective endothelin antagonists:**
  - More selective (preserves ET-B receptor function)
  - Lower dose
  - Can combine with SGLT2 inhibitors for additive benefit
- **Renal Dosing:** No adjustment needed
- **Contraindications:**
  - Pregnancy (teratogenic - BOXED WARNING)
  - Concomitant use with other endothelin antagonists
- **Drug Interactions:** Strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (may increase levels)
- **Monitoring:**
  - Fluid retention/edema (most common AE - weight, BP monitoring)
  - Hemoglobin (may decrease)
  - Pregnancy testing (baseline for women of childbearing potential)
- **Confirmatory Data:** eGFR slope data expected 2026 for traditional approval
- **Cost:** ~$10,000-15,000/month

**Nefecon/Tarpeyo (Targeted-Release Budesonide):**
- **FDA Approval:** December 2021 for IgA nephropathy
- **Mechanism:** Corticosteroid targeting Peyer's patches in ileum
- **Indication:** IgA nephropathy with proteinuria >1 g/day, eGFR 25-90
- **Dosing:** 16 mg once daily × 9 months
- **Trials:** NefIgArd (2023) - 31% reduction in eGFR decline
- **Renal Dosing:** Not recommended if eGFR <25
- **Monitoring:** BP, glucose, bone density

**Voclosporin (Lupkynis):**
- **FDA Approval:** January 2021 for lupus nephritis
- **Mechanism:** Calcineurin inhibitor with renal selectivity
- **Indication:** Active lupus nephritis (in combination with MMF)
- **Dosing:** 23.7 mg BID
- **Trials:** AURORA (2021) - Higher complete renal response vs. MMF alone
- **Renal Dosing:** Adjust based on eGFR and TDM
- **Monitoring:** Trough levels, eGFR, BP

### Complement Inhibitors
**Eculizumab (Soliris):**
- **FDA Approval:** Atypical HUS (2011), PNH (2007)
- **Mechanism:** C5 complement inhibitor
- **Dosing:** 900 mg weekly × 4, then 1200 mg Q2 weeks
- **Requirement:** Meningococcal vaccination mandatory
- **Renal Dosing:** No adjustment needed

**Ravulizumab (Ultomiris):**
- **FDA Approval:** Atypical HUS (2019), PNH (2018)
- **Mechanism:** Long-acting C5 inhibitor
- **Dosing:** Weight-based loading, then Q8 weeks
- **Advantage:** Less frequent dosing vs. eculizumab

**Iptacopan (Fabhalta):**
- **FDA Approval:**
  - December 2023 for PNH
  - **August 2024 for IgA nephropathy** (accelerated approval)
- **Mechanism:** Oral Factor B inhibitor (alternative complement pathway)
- **Dosing:** 200 mg BID
- **IgA Nephropathy Indication:**
  - Reduction of proteinuria in adults with primary IgA nephropathy at risk of rapid disease progression
  - **APPLAUSE-IgAN Trial (2024):** 44% proteinuria reduction vs 9% placebo (38% difference, p<0.0001)
  - October 2025 update: Positive final results showing slowing of eGFR decline over 2 years
  - **First-in-class oral complement inhibitor for IgAN**
- **FDA-approved for C3 glomerulopathy (2025):**
  - APPEAR-C3G trial: 35% proteinuria reduction at 6 months
  - Dual indication: IgA nephropathy AND C3G
- **Renal Dosing:** No adjustment needed

**Pegcetacoplan (Empaveli®):**
- **FDA Approval:**
  - PNH (2021)
  - **C3 glomerulopathy and primary IC-MPGN (July 28, 2025)** ⭐ **FIRST FDA-APPROVED C3G TREATMENT**
- **Mechanism:** C3 complement inhibitor (binds C3 and C3b, blocks alternative and classical pathways)
- **Dosing:** 1080 mg subcutaneous twice weekly
- **C3G Indication:** Reduce proteinuria in C3 glomerulopathy or primary IC-MPGN (ages ≥12 years)
- **VALIANT Trial (2025):**
  - 68.3% relative proteinuria reduction vs. placebo
  - 71% complete C3c staining clearance on biopsy
  - Stabilized kidney function (eGFR)
- **Monitoring:**
  - **Critical:** Meningococcal vaccination mandatory before starting
  - Encapsulated bacteria infection risk (pneumococcus, H. influenzae)
  - Proteinuria, eGFR, C3 levels
  - Consider repeat biopsy at 6-12 months (assess C3 deposit clearance)
- **Renal Dosing:** No adjustment needed
- **Place in Therapy:** First-line for C3 glomerulopathy and IC-MPGN
- **Cost:** ~$400,000-500,000/year (prior authorization required)

**Avacopan (Tavneos):**
- **FDA Approval:** October 2021 for ANCA-associated vasculitis
- **Mechanism:** Selective C5a receptor antagonist (blocks neutrophil activation)
- **Dosing:** 30 mg PO BID × 52 weeks (with food)
- **Indication:** Adjunct to rituximab or cyclophosphamide as alternative to glucocorticoid taper
- **Pivotal Trial:** ADVOCATE (2021)
  - Non-inferior to prednisone for remission at 26 weeks (72.3% vs 70.1%)
  - Superior sustained remission at 52 weeks (65.7% vs 54.9%, p=0.007)
  - Greater eGFR improvement, fewer relapses
  - 60% reduction in cumulative steroid dose
- **Clinical Use:**
  - Use with rituximab (preferred) or cyclophosphamide
  - Avoids long-term steroid toxicity (diabetes, osteoporosis, weight gain)
  - Better renal outcomes than traditional prednisone taper
- **Monitoring:**
  - **Critical:** LFTs monthly (hepatotoxicity - most important adverse effect)
  - Baseline: HBV/HCV serology, TB screening
  - Discontinue if ALT/AST >5× ULN
- **Side Effects:**
  - Hepatotoxicity (5-10% - BOXED WARNING)
  - Upper respiratory infections (15-20%)
  - Nausea, diarrhea, hypertension
- **Contraindications:**
  - Active hepatitis or cirrhosis
  - Severe liver impairment (Child-Pugh C)
  - Strong CYP3A4 inducers (rifampin, phenytoin, carbamazepine)
- **Drug Interactions:**
  - Substrate of CYP3A4 - avoid strong inducers/inhibitors
  - No dosage adjustment needed for mild-moderate hepatic impairment
- **Renal Dosing:** No adjustment needed (not renally eliminated)
- **Place in Therapy:**
  - FIRST-LINE steroid-sparing option for ANCA vasculitis patients with:
    - Diabetes mellitus or steroid intolerance
    - Osteoporosis or fragility fractures
    - Obesity or metabolic syndrome
  - Cost: ~$200,000-250,000/year (prior authorization typically required)

## Key Practice Points

### Clinical Pearls
1. **eGFR vs CrCl**: Use eGFR for drug dosing when available
2. **Loading Doses**: Usually unchanged in renal failure
3. **Therapeutic Monitoring**: Essential for narrow therapeutic index drugs
4. **Hydration**: Key prevention strategy for nephrotoxic drugs
5. **Drug Interactions**: Consider additive nephrotoxicity and electrolyte effects

### Red Flags
- **Rising Creatinine**: Evaluate all medications for nephrotoxicity
- **Electrolyte Abnormalities**: Consider drug-induced causes
- **Drug Levels**: Toxic levels require immediate dose adjustment
- **Allergic Reactions**: May be more severe in renal failure
- **Bleeding**: Consider accumulation of anticoagulants, antiplatelets

### Prevention Strategies
- **Baseline Assessment**: Renal function before initiating nephrotoxic drugs
- **Regular Monitoring**: Creatinine, electrolytes, drug levels
- **Dose Adjustments**: Proactive adjustments based on eGFR
- **Hydration**: Adequate volume status before procedures
- **Drug Selection**: Choose less nephrotoxic alternatives when possible
