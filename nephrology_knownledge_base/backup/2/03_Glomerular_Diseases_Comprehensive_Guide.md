# Glomerular Diseases - Comprehensive Clinical Guide

## Metadata
**Core Domain:** Clinical Nephrology I  
**Difficulty Level:** Intermediate-Advanced  
**Frequency of Use:** Weekly-Daily  
**Clinical Impact:** High  
**Regulatory Requirements:** None specific  
**Prerequisites:** Immunology, pathology basics, renal physiology  
**Competency Development Time:** 12-24 months  
**Cross-references:** Kidney biopsy (Domain IV.11), Immunosuppression (Domain V.12), Transplantation (Domain III)

## 1. Glomerular Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Normal Glomerular Structure

#### Cellular Components
**Podocytes:**
- Visceral epithelial cells
- Primary and secondary foot processes
- Slit diaphragm proteins (nephrin, podocin)
- Synthesize GBM components
- Limited regenerative capacity

**Glomerular Endothelial Cells:**
- Fenestrated endothelium
- Glycocalyx layer
- Produce nitric oxide, prostacyclin
- Anti-thrombotic surface
- Source of VEGF for podocytes

**Mesangial Cells:**
- Contractile properties
- Phagocytic function
- Matrix production
- Support glomerular capillaries
- Regulate glomerular filtration surface area

**Parietal Epithelial Cells:**
- Line Bowman's capsule
- Can become activated in disease
- Contribute to crescent formation
- Potential progenitor cell function

#### Extracellular Matrix
**Glomerular Basement Membrane (GBM):**
- Type IV collagen network
- Heparan sulfate proteoglycans
- Laminin, entactin, nidogen
- Charge and size barrier
- 300-350 nm thickness

**Mesangial Matrix:**
- Type IV collagen
- Fibronectin, laminin
- Proteoglycans
- Provides structural support

### Pathophysiological Mechanisms

#### Immunologic Injury
**Humoral Immunity:**
- Antibody deposition (in situ, circulating)
- Complement activation
- Immune complex formation
- Anti-GBM antibodies

**Cellular Immunity:**
- T-cell mediated injury
- Cytokine release
- Macrophage infiltration
- Direct cellular cytotoxicity

#### Non-Immunologic Injury
**Hemodynamic Factors:**
- Hyperfiltration injury
- Glomerular hypertension
- Shear stress
- RAAS activation

**Metabolic Factors:**
- Advanced glycation end-products
- Lipid toxicity
- Hyperglycemia effects
- Insulin resistance

**Genetic Factors:**
- Structural protein mutations
- Complement regulatory defects
- Podocyte protein abnormalities
- APOL1 risk variants

## 2. Clinical Syndromes Classification

### Nephrotic Syndrome

#### Definition & Diagnostic Criteria
**Core Features:**
- Proteinuria >3.5 g/24h (>3.5 g/day or >40 mg/m²/h in children)
- Serum albumin <3.0 g/dL
- Edema (peripheral, periorbital, anasarca)
- Hyperlipidemia (LDL, VLDL elevation)
- Lipiduria (oval fat bodies, fatty casts)

**Pathophysiology:**
- Massive protein loss → hypoalbuminemia
- Decreased plasma oncotic pressure → edema
- Hepatic lipoprotein synthesis ↑ → hyperlipidemia
- Loss of antithrombin III → hypercoagulable state
- Vitamin D binding protein loss → secondary hyperparathyroidism

#### Etiologies by Age
**Children (1-12 years):**
- Minimal change disease (80%)
- FSGS (10-15%)
- Membranous nephropathy (5%)
- Mesangial proliferative GN (<5%)

**Adolescents (13-18 years):**
- FSGS (30-40%)
- Minimal change disease (30-35%)
- Membranous nephropathy (15-20%)
- Lupus nephritis (10-15%)

**Adults (19-40 years):**
- FSGS (35-40%)
- Minimal change disease (20-25%)
- Membranous nephropathy (20-25%)
- Lupus nephritis (10-15%)

**Adults (40-60 years):**
- Membranous nephropathy (35-40%)
- FSGS (25-30%)
- Minimal change disease (15-20%)
- Amyloidosis (10-15%)

**Elderly (>60 years):**
- Membranous nephropathy (30-35%)
- Amyloidosis (25-30%)
- FSGS (20-25%)
- Minimal change disease (10-15%)

### Nephritic Syndrome

#### Definition & Diagnostic Criteria
**Core Features:**
- Hematuria (dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts)
- Proteinuria (sub-nephrotic, <3.5 g/day)
- Hypertension
- Reduced GFR (elevated creatinine)
- Oliguria (may be present)
- Edema (mild to moderate)

**Pathophysiology:**
- Glomerular inflammation → proliferative changes
- Capillary wall damage → RBC leakage
- Reduced filtration surface → GFR decline
- Sodium/water retention → hypertension
- Inflammatory mediators → systemic symptoms

#### Clinical Presentations
**Acute Nephritic Syndrome:**
- Sudden onset (days)
- Gross hematuria (cola-colored)
- Hypertension, edema
- Often post-infectious
- Usually self-limited

**Rapidly Progressive GN:**
- Very rapid decline (days-weeks)
- Severe renal failure
- Often requires dialysis
- Medical emergency
- Multiple etiologies

**Chronic Nephritic Syndrome:**
- Insidious onset (months-years)
- Progressive GFR decline
- Persistent hematuria
- Often leads to CKD

### Asymptomatic Urinary Abnormalities

#### Isolated Hematuria
**Definition:**
- >3 RBCs/hpf on 2+ occasions
- No proteinuria
- Normal renal function
- No hypertension

**Differential Diagnosis:**
- IgA nephropathy
- Thin basement membrane disease
- Alport syndrome (early)
- Hereditary nephritis
- Vascular malformations

#### Isolated Proteinuria
**Definition:**
- Proteinuria <3.5 g/day
- No hematuria
- Normal renal function
- No edema

**Classifications:**
- Orthostatic proteinuria
- Intermittent proteinuria
- Persistent proteinuria
- Exercise-induced

## 3. Primary Glomerular Diseases

### Minimal Change Disease (MCD)

#### Epidemiology & Pathology
**Epidemiology:**
- Most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in children
- 10-15% of adult nephrotic syndrome
- Peak incidence: 2-6 years (children), 16-24 years (adults)
- Male predominance in children (2:1)

**Pathology:**
**Light Microscopy:**
- Normal glomeruli
- No proliferation, no sclerosis
- May show mild tubular vacuolization

**Immunofluorescence:**
- Negative for immune deposits
- No IgG, IgA, IgM, C3 deposition

**Electron Microscopy:**
- Diffuse foot process effacement
- No electron-dense deposits
- No basement membrane abnormalities

#### Clinical Features
**Presentation:**
- Sudden onset nephrotic syndrome
- Selective proteinuria (albumin > IgG)
- Normal renal function
- No hypertension
- May be triggered by infections, allergies

**Laboratory Findings:**
- Proteinuria 3-10 g/day
- Serum albumin <2.5 g/dL
- Normal complement levels
- Negative serologies

#### Treatment & Prognosis
**Children:**
- Prednisone 60 mg/m²/day (max 60 mg) for 4-6 weeks
- Then alternate day for 4-6 weeks
- 80-90% complete remission
- Relapse common (60-70%)

**Adults:**
- Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day for 8-12 weeks
- Slower response than children
- 70-80% remission rate
- Higher relapse rate

**Steroid-Dependent/Resistant:**
- Calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus)
- Mycophenolate mofetil
- Rituximab (emerging)
- Cyclophosphamide (rarely)

**Prognosis:**
- Excellent long-term survival
- <5% progress to CKD/ESRD
- Most complications from treatment, not disease

### Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)

#### Classification & Pathology
**Primary (Idiopathic) FSGS:**
- No identifiable cause
- Often presents with nephrotic syndrome
- May recur after transplant

**Secondary FSGS:**
- Adaptive (hyperfiltration)
- Virus-associated (HIV, parvovirus)
- Drug-induced (pamidronate, interferon)
- Genetic forms

**Histologic Variants:**
**Collapsing Variant:**
- Collapsed glomerular tuft
- Hyperplastic podocytes
- Poor prognosis
- Associated with HIV, APOL1

**Tip Variant:**
- Segmental sclerosis at tubular pole
- Best prognosis
- High remission rate
- Nephrotic presentation

**Cellular Variant:**
- Endocapillary hypercellularity
- Foam cells, macrophages
- Intermediate prognosis
- May progress to collapsing

**Perihilar Variant:**
- Sclerosis at vascular pole
- Associated with hyperfiltration
- Common in obesity, reflux
- Moderate prognosis

**Not Otherwise Specified (NOS):**
- Mixed features
- Most common variant
- Variable prognosis

#### Clinical Features
**Presentation:**
- Nephrotic syndrome (70-80%)
- Non-nephrotic proteinuria (20-30%)
- Hypertension common
- Reduced GFR at presentation (30-40%)
- Hematuria may be present

**Risk Factors:**
- African American ethnicity
- APOL1 risk variants
- Obesity
- Low birth weight
- Premature birth

#### Treatment Strategies (2024 Update)

**Supportive Care (ALL Patients - Foundational):**
**RAAS Blockade:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs at max tolerated dose
- Target blood pressure <130/80 mmHg
- Continue even if proteinuria improves

**SGLT2 Inhibitors:**
- **Empagliflozin 10 mg daily** or **Dapagliflozin 10 mg daily**
- Reduce proteinuria and slow progression
- Can use down to eGFR 20 mL/min
- Consider in all patients with FSGS

**Additional Supportive Measures:**
- Diuretics for edema (loop diuretics often needed)
- Statins for hyperlipidemia
- Anticoagulation if albumin <2.0 g/dL (consider prophylactic)
- Dietary sodium restriction

**First-Line Immunosuppression (Primary FSGS):**
**Corticosteroids:**
- **Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (max 80 mg)** for 12-16 weeks
- If no response, may extend to 24 weeks before declaring resistance
- **Taper:** Once remission achieved, taper slowly over 6-12 months
- **Response Rates:**
  - Complete remission: 30-40% (tip variant 50-60%)
  - Partial remission: 20-30%
- **Monitor:** Glucose, blood pressure, bone density, infections

**Novel Targeted Therapies:**
**Sparsentan (Filspari):**
- Dual endothelin/angiotensin receptor antagonist
- **Under investigation** for FSGS (Phase 3 DUPLEX trial)
- **DUET Trial (2023):**
  - Greater proteinuria reduction vs. irbesartan in FSGS
  - Similar efficacy profile to IgA nephropathy
- Expected FDA decision 2024-2025
- Dosing: 200-400 mg daily
- May become first-line alternative to steroids

**Steroid-Resistant FSGS:**
**Calcineurin Inhibitors (CNI) - Current Second Line:**
**Cyclosporine:**
- 3-5 mg/kg/day divided BID (target trough 100-175 ng/mL)
- **FSGS-CT Trial:** Remission in 40-50%
- Combine with low-dose prednisone (0.15 mg/kg/day)
- Duration: 6-12 months minimum, may need longer

**Tacrolimus:**
- 0.05-0.1 mg/kg/day divided BID (target trough 5-10 ng/mL)
- Similar efficacy to cyclosporine
- May have better tolerability
- Lower cosmetic side effects

**CNI Considerations:**
- Monitor trough levels regularly
- Watch for nephrotoxicity (may worsen GFR)
- High relapse rate after discontinuation (40-60%)
- Consider long-term maintenance at lower dose

**Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF):**
- 1500-2000 mg/day divided BID
- Good steroid-sparing agent
- Less nephrotoxic than CNIs
- **May be combined with CNIs** for synergistic effect
- Duration: 12 months minimum

**Rituximab:**
- 375 mg/m² IV weekly × 4 doses
- OR 1 g IV × 2 doses (2 weeks apart)
- **Mixed efficacy data** in FSGS (better in children)
- **Consider for:**
  - CNI-intolerant patients
  - Genetic mutations affecting B-cell regulation
  - Post-transplant recurrence
- Monitor B-cell depletion (CD19/CD20)

**Novel/Investigational Therapies:**
**Abatacept:**
- CTLA4-Ig fusion protein
- **Under investigation** for B7-1 positive FSGS
- Phase 2 trials ongoing
- May be beneficial in specific genetic subtypes

**Fresolimumab:**
- Anti-TGF-β antibody
- Reduces podocyte injury
- Early phase trials

**Combination Therapy (Refractory FSGS):**
- **CNI + MMF + Low-dose prednisone**
- **Rituximab + CNI**
- Consider in difficult cases
- Higher side effect profile

**Genetic FSGS:**
- **Supportive care ONLY** (immunosuppression ineffective)
- RAAS blockade, SGLT2 inhibitors
- Early transplant evaluation
- Genetic counseling for family members

**Post-Transplant Recurrent FSGS:**
- Occurs in 30-50% (higher in primary FSGS)
- **Plasmapheresis:**
  - Daily to every other day initially
  - Continue based on proteinuria response
  - May need indefinite maintenance
- **Rituximab:** 375 mg/m² × 1-4 doses
- **High-dose cyclosporine or tacrolimus**
- **Eculizumab:** Case reports of efficacy (off-label)
- Consider prophylactic plasmapheresis peri-transplant

#### Prognosis
**Response to Treatment:**
- Complete remission: 5-10% (primary), 20-30% (secondary)
- Partial remission: 20-30%
- No response: 60-70%

**Long-term Outcomes:**
- 50% progress to ESRD in 10 years
- Better prognosis with remission
- Worse prognosis with collapsing variant
- High recurrence after transplant (30-40%)

### Membranous Nephropathy (MN)

#### Pathogenesis & Classification
**Primary MN:**
- Anti-PLA2R antibodies (70-80%)
- Anti-THSD7A antibodies (5-10%)
- Other antigens being identified
- Autoimmune disease mechanism

**Secondary MN:**
**Infections:**
- Hepatitis B (most common)
- Hepatitis C
- Syphilis
- Malaria

**Autoimmune:**
- Lupus nephritis (Class V)
- Rheumatoid arthritis
- Sjögren's syndrome
- Sarcoidosis

**Malignancy:**
- Lung cancer (most common)
- Colon cancer
- Prostate cancer
- Hematologic malignancies

**Drugs:**
- NSAIDs
- Penicillamine
- Gold salts
- Anti-TNF agents

#### Pathology
**Light Microscopy:**
- Thickened glomerular basement membrane
- Subepithelial deposits
- "Spike and hole" appearance on silver stain
- No cellular proliferation

**Immunofluorescence:**
- Granular IgG deposition along capillary loops
- C3 often present
- IgG4 predominance in primary MN

**Electron Microscopy:**
- Subepithelial electron-dense deposits
- Podocyte foot process effacement
- Basement membrane reaction (spike formation)

#### Clinical Features
**Presentation:**
- Nephrotic syndrome (80%)
- Edema, proteinuria >3.5 g/day
- Hypertension (30-40%)
- Normal or mildly reduced GFR
- May have microscopic hematuria

**Laboratory Findings:**
- Anti-PLA2R antibodies (if primary)
- Low complement (rare, usually secondary)
- Hyperlipidemia
- Hypercoagulable state

#### Risk Stratification
**Prognostic Factors:**
- Persistent proteinuria >8 g/day
- Age >50 years
- Male gender
- Reduced GFR at presentation
- Anti-PLA2R antibody titer

**Risk Categories (KDIGO):**
- Low risk: Proteinuria <4 g/day, normal GFR
- Medium risk: Proteinuria 4-8 g/day, normal GFR
- High risk: Proteinuria >8 g/day or reduced GFR

#### Treatment Approach (2024 Update)
**Conservative Management (Low Risk):**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs (max tolerated dose)
- **SGLT2 inhibitors** (empagliflozin 10 mg daily or dapagliflozin 10 mg daily)
  - Reduce proteinuria by 30-40%
  - Slow GFR decline
  - Cardiovascular benefits
- Diuretics for edema, statins for hyperlipidemia
- Anticoagulation if albumin <2.5 g/dL (consider prophylactic)
- Observe for 6 months before immunosuppression

**Immunosuppression (Medium/High Risk):**
**Rituximab-Based (First-Line per KDIGO 2021):**
**Standard Dosing:**
- Rituximab 1 g IV on days 1 and 15 (most common)
- OR 375 mg/m² IV weekly × 4 doses
- Repeat dosing based on:
  - Anti-PLA2R antibody titers (goal: undetectable)
  - B-cell depletion (CD19/CD20 count)
  - Clinical response

**RI-CYCLO Trial Data (2021):**
- Rituximab non-inferior to cyclophosphamide
- Better safety profile (less toxicity)
- Complete remission: 60% at 24 months
- Partial remission: additional 20%

**MENTOR Trial (2019):**
- Rituximab vs. cyclosporine
- Superior sustained remission with rituximab
- Lower relapse rate after discontinuation

**Novel Therapies (2024):**
**Sparsentan (Filspari):**
- **FDA-approved August 2023** for IgA nephropathy
- **Under investigation** for membranous nephropathy (Phase 3 trials)
- Dual endothelin/angiotensin receptor antagonist
- Superior proteinuria reduction vs. irbesartan
- Dosing: 200-400 mg daily
- Monitor: Blood pressure, potassium, edema

**Obinutuzumab:**
- Next-generation anti-CD20 antibody
- More potent B-cell depletion than rituximab
- Phase 2 trials ongoing for MN
- May be option for rituximab-refractory cases

**Complement Inhibitors (Investigational):**
- C5 inhibitors (eculizumab) under study
- Rationale: Complement activation in MN pathogenesis
- May be considered in refractory cases

**Alternative Regimens:**
**Calcineurin Inhibitors (CNI):**
- **Cyclosporine:** 3-5 mg/kg/day (target trough 100-175 ng/mL)
- **Tacrolimus:** 0.05-0.1 mg/kg/day (target trough 5-8 ng/mL)
- Remission rate: 70-80%
- **Major Limitation:** High relapse rate (40-60%) after discontinuation
- Consider for rituximab contraindication or bridge therapy

**Ponticelli Regimen (Cyclophosphamide + Steroids):**
- Month 1: Methylprednisolone 1 g IV daily × 3 days, then prednisone 0.5 mg/kg/day × 27 days + cyclophosphamide 2 mg/kg/day
- Month 2: Prednisone 0.5 mg/kg/day × 30 days
- Month 3: Repeat Month 1
- Continue alternating for 6 months total
- **Efficacy:** Complete remission 60%, partial remission 30%
- **Drawbacks:** Toxicity (malignancy risk, infertility), now second-line

**Mycophenolate Mofetil:**
- 1500-2000 mg/day divided BID
- Less effective than rituximab or cyclophosphamide
- May consider in specific populations (younger patients desiring fertility preservation)

**Refractory Disease Approach:**
- Repeat rituximab with different schedule
- Combination therapy: Rituximab + CNI
- Obinutuzumab (if available)
- Consider complement inhibitors (clinical trials)
- Re-biopsy to assess activity vs. chronicity

#### Prognosis
**Spontaneous Remission:**
- 20-30% within 2 years
- More common in low-risk patients
- Monitor anti-PLA2R titers

**Treatment Response:**
- Complete remission: 60-70% with rituximab
- Partial remission: 20-30%
- Relapse rate: 20-30%

**Long-term Outcomes:**
- 10-year renal survival: 70-80%
- Better with early remission
- Worse with persistent high proteinuria

### IgA Nephropathy (Berger's Disease)

#### Epidemiology & Pathogenesis
**Epidemiology:**
- Most common primary glomerulonephritis worldwide
- Peak incidence: 20-30 years
- Male predominance (2-3:1)
- Higher prevalence in Asian populations

**Pathogenesis:**
- Aberrant IgA1 glycosylation
- Formation of anti-glycan antibodies
- Immune complex deposition
- Complement activation (alternative pathway)
- Mucosal immunity dysfunction

#### Pathology
**Light Microscopy:**
- Mesangial hypercellularity
- Matrix expansion
- May have segmental sclerosis
- Crescent formation in severe disease

**Immunofluorescence:**
- Dominant mesangial IgA deposition
- IgG and C3 often present
- Capillary loop deposition in severe disease

**Electron Microscopy:**
- Mesangial electron-dense deposits
- Paramesangial deposits
- Foot process effacement (variable)

**Oxford Classification (MEST-C):**
**M (Mesangial hypercellularity):**
- M0: ≤50% glomeruli with >3 mesangial cells
- M1: >50% glomeruli with >3 mesangial cells

**E (Endocapillary hypercellularity):**
- E0: Absent
- E1: Present

**S (Segmental sclerosis):**
- S0: Absent
- S1: Present

**T (Tubular atrophy/interstitial fibrosis):**
- T0: ≤25% of cortical area
- T1: 26-50% of cortical area
- T2: >50% of cortical area

**C (Crescents):**
- C0: No crescents
- C1: Crescents in <25% of glomeruli
- C2: Crescents in ≥25% of glomeruli

#### Clinical Presentation
**Spectrum of Disease:**
- Asymptomatic hematuria (40-50%)
- Nephritic syndrome (30-40%)
- Nephrotic syndrome (10-15%)
- Rapidly progressive GN (5-10%)

**Typical Features:**
- Gross hematuria after URI (synpharyngitic)
- Flank pain with gross hematuria
- Normal complement levels
- May have elevated IgA levels

#### Prognostic Assessment
**Risk Factors for Progression:**
- Proteinuria >1 g/day
- Hypertension
- Reduced GFR at presentation
- Oxford MEST-C score
- Persistent hematuria

**Risk Categories:**
- Low risk: Proteinuria <0.5 g/day, normal GFR
- Medium risk: Proteinuria 0.5-1 g/day
- High risk: Proteinuria >1 g/day or reduced GFR

#### Treatment Strategies (2024 KDIGO Update)

**Supportive Care (ALL Patients - Foundational Therapy):**
**RAAS Blockade:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs at max tolerated dose
- Target blood pressure <120/80 mmHg (2024 KDIGO)
- Continue even if proteinuria <0.5 g/day
- Monitor potassium, creatinine

**SGLT2 Inhibitors (New Standard of Care):**
- **Empagliflozin 10 mg daily** or **Dapagliflozin 10 mg daily**
- **DAPA-CKD & EMPA-KIDNEY trials:** 30-40% reduction in progression
- Can use down to eGFR 20 mL/min
- Benefits independent of diabetes status
- Monitor for genital infections, euglycemic DKA

**Additional Supportive Measures:**
- Statins if LDL >100 mg/dL or cardiovascular disease
- Dietary protein restriction (0.8 g/kg/day)
- Sodium restriction (<2 g/day)
- Smoking cessation
- Weight management

**Immunosuppression (High-Risk Patients):**
**High-Risk Criteria (2024 KDIGO):**
- Persistent proteinuria >1 g/day despite 3-6 months optimized supportive care
- Progressive GFR decline
- Crescentic IgAN (>50% crescents on biopsy)

**Targeted-Release Budesonide (Nefecon/Tarpeyo) - FDA APPROVED 2021:**
- **First FDA-approved therapy** specifically for IgAN
- **Dosing:** 16 mg once daily for 9 months
- **Mechanism:** Targets Peyer's patches in ileum (site of aberrant IgA production)
- **NEFIGAN Trial (2017):** 24% reduction in proteinuria
- **NefIgArd Trial (2023):**
  - 31% reduction in eGFR decline vs. placebo
  - Sustained proteinuria reduction
  - Lower risk of progression to ESRD
- **Indications:** Proteinuria >1 g/day, eGFR 25-90 mL/min
- **Advantages:** Minimal systemic steroid exposure, reduced side effects
- **Monitoring:** Blood pressure, glucose, bone density

**Sparsentan (Filspari) - FDA APPROVED August 2023:**
- **First-in-class** dual endothelin receptor antagonist/angiotensin receptor blocker
- **PROTECT Trial (2023):**
  - 49.8% proteinuria reduction vs. 15.1% with irbesartan at 36 weeks
  - Sustained benefit at 110 weeks
  - Improved eGFR slope
- **Dosing:** Start 200 mg daily, may increase to 400 mg daily
- **Indications:** IgAN with proteinuria >1 g/day despite ACEi/ARB
- **Monitoring:**
  - Blood pressure (may cause hypotension)
  - Hemoglobin (may decrease)
  - Edema, hepatotoxicity
  - Contraindicated in pregnancy (teratogenic)

**Atrasentan (Vanrafia®) - FDA APPROVED April 2, 2025** ⭐ **NEWEST IgAN MEDICATION**
- **First-and-only selective endothelin A receptor antagonist** for IgA nephropathy
- **FDA Status:** Accelerated approval April 2025 for proteinuria reduction in primary IgAN
- **Mechanism:** Selective endothelin A receptor antagonist (blocks ET-A, spares ET-B)
- **ALIGN Trial (2024-2025):**
  - 36.1% proteinuria reduction vs. placebo at 36 weeks
  - 37.4% reduction in patients also on SGLT2 inhibitors
  - Published in NEJM October 2024
  - Study duration: 132 weeks (ongoing for confirmatory eGFR data expected 2026)
- **Dosing:** 0.75 mg PO once daily
- **Indications:** Adults with primary IgA nephropathy at risk of rapid disease progression
- **Advantages:**
  - More selective than sparsentan (ET-A selective, preserves ET-B function)
  - Once-daily oral administration
  - Can combine with SGLT2 inhibitors
  - Lower dose, potentially better tolerability
- **Monitoring:**
  - Fluid retention/edema (most common AE - monitor weight, BP)
  - Hemoglobin (may decrease)
  - Contraindicated in pregnancy (teratogenic - BOXED WARNING)
  - Baseline pregnancy test required for women of childbearing potential
- **Drug Interactions:**
  - Strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (may increase atrasentan levels)
  - Do NOT use with other endothelin antagonists
- **Place in Therapy:**
  - Alternative to sparsentan for patients with proteinuria >1 g/day
  - Particularly consider if patient is on SGLT2 inhibitor (additive benefit)
  - May have better fluid retention profile than non-selective endothelin antagonists
- **Confirmatory Data:** eGFR data expected in 2026 for traditional FDA approval
- **Cost:** ~$10,000-15,000/month (prior authorization required)

**Complement Inhibitors:**

**Iptacopan (Fabhalta) - FDA APPROVED August 2024:**
- **First-in-class oral complement inhibitor** for IgA nephropathy
- **FDA Status:** Accelerated approval August 2024 for reduction of proteinuria in primary IgAN
- **Mechanism:** Oral Factor B inhibitor (blocks alternative complement pathway)
- **Dosing:** 200 mg BID (twice daily)
- **APPLAUSE-IgAN Trial (2024):**
  - 44% proteinuria reduction from baseline vs. 9% with placebo
  - 38% relative reduction vs. placebo (p<0.0001) at 9 months
  - **October 2025 Final Results:** Statistically significant slowing of eGFR decline over 2 years
- **Indications:** Adults with primary IgA nephropathy at risk of rapid disease progression
- **Advantages:**
  - Oral administration (convenient)
  - Targets underlying complement dysregulation
  - Dual benefit: Proteinuria reduction AND eGFR preservation
- **Monitoring:**
  - Baseline meningococcal vaccination mandatory
  - Monitor for infections (complement pathway inhibition)
  - Hemoglobin monitoring (may cause mild anemia)
- **Place in Therapy:**
  - Consider for patients with persistent proteinuria despite ACEi/ARB
  - Can be used in combination with other IgAN therapies
  - Alternative to or in combination with Sparsentan/Tarpeyo
- **Cost:** ~$15,000-20,000/month (prior authorization typically required)

**Cemdisiran (Investigational):**
- siRNA targeting complement C5
- Phase 2 trials completed
- 38% proteinuria reduction
- Quarterly subcutaneous injections

**Corticosteroids (Traditional - Now Second Line):**
**Systemic Corticosteroids:**
- Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day for 2 months, then taper over 4-6 months
- **TESTING Trial (2022):** Increased serious adverse events without clear benefit
- **Current Recommendation:** Use with caution, consider only if:
  - Nefecon/sparsentan not available
  - Rapidly progressive disease
  - Minimal contraindications
- **STOP-IgAN Trial (2015):** No benefit of immunosuppression over supportive care

**Mycophenolate Mofetil:**
- 1000-1500 mg twice daily for 6-12 months
- **Limited efficacy** in most trials
- May consider in specific populations (Asian patients, steroid intolerance)
- Not routinely recommended per 2024 KDIGO

**Azathioprine:**
- 2 mg/kg/day
- Limited evidence
- Alternative to MMF in specific cases
- Check TPMT levels before initiation

**Novel Therapies (Investigational):**
**Anti-APRIL Antibodies:**
- **Atacicept, Telitacicept:** Block APRIL (proliferation-inducing ligand)
- Reduces IgA production
- Phase 2/3 trials ongoing
- Promising mechanism targeting pathogenesis

**Anti-BAFF/APRIL Agents:**
- Belimumab (approved for lupus, being studied in IgAN)
- Targets B-cell activation

**RPGN/Crescentic IgAN Treatment:**
- Pulse methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg IV daily × 3 days
- Followed by oral prednisone taper
- **Cyclophosphamide** 2 mg/kg/day for 3-6 months
- OR **Rituximab** 1 g × 2 doses (2 weeks apart)
- Plasmapheresis if anti-GBM antibody coexistence (rare)
- Urgent nephrology consultation

#### KDIGO 2025 IgA Nephropathy Guideline - Treatment Algorithm ⭐

**PARADIGM SHIFT: Focus on Immunologic Root Cause Targeting**

The KDIGO 2025 IgA Nephropathy Guideline represents a paradigm shift from supportive-care-only approaches to early immunologic intervention targeting the pathophysiology of IgAN. The guideline emphasizes:
1. **Multi-targeted therapy** combining supportive care with disease-modifying agents
2. **Early intervention** before irreversible kidney damage
3. **Risk stratification** to guide treatment intensity
4. **Patient-centered decision-making** considering treatment burden, cost, access

---

**STEP 1: FOUNDATIONAL THERAPY (ALL PATIENTS)**

**Optimize Supportive Care (3-6 Month Trial Before Escalation):**

1. **RAAS Blockade (First-Line):**
   - ACE inhibitor or ARB at maximum tolerated dose
   - Target BP <120/80 mmHg (systolic <120)
   - Continue even if proteinuria <0.5 g/day
   - Examples: Lisinopril 40 mg daily, Losartan 100 mg daily

2. **SGLT2 Inhibitor (New Standard of Care - 2025 Guideline Elevation):**
   - **Empagliflozin 10 mg daily** OR **Dapagliflozin 10 mg daily**
   - Start in ALL patients with eGFR ≥20 mL/min
   - 30-40% reduction in CKD progression (DAPA-CKD, EMPA-KIDNEY)
   - Continue down to eGFR 20 mL/min
   - Benefits independent of diabetes status

3. **Lifestyle & Diet:**
   - Dietary protein 0.8 g/kg/day (avoid high-protein diets)
   - Sodium restriction <2 g/day (1500 mg optimal)
   - Smoking cessation (mandatory counseling)
   - Weight management (BMI <25 kg/m²)
   - Omega-3 fatty acids (1-3 g/day EPA+DHA) - optional, modest benefit

4. **Cardiovascular Risk Reduction:**
   - Statin if LDL >100 mg/dL or ASCVD risk >7.5%
   - Aspirin if cardiovascular disease (not routine)

**Reassess after 3-6 months of optimized supportive care:**
- If proteinuria <0.5 g/day → Continue supportive care only, monitor
- If proteinuria ≥0.5 g/day → Proceed to STEP 2

---

**STEP 2: RISK STRATIFICATION & TREATMENT ESCALATION**

**Risk Assessment Criteria (2025 KDIGO):**

**LOW RISK (Continue Supportive Care Only):**
- Proteinuria <0.5 g/day on optimized supportive care
- eGFR stable or slow decline (<2 mL/min/year)
- Minimal biopsy chronicity (T0-T1, no crescents)
- **Management:** Continue ACEi/ARB + SGLT2i, monitor every 3-6 months

**MODERATE RISK (Consider Disease-Modifying Therapy):**
- Proteinuria 0.5-1.0 g/day despite 3-6 months optimized supportive care
- eGFR decline 2-5 mL/min/year
- Oxford M1, E1, S1, T1, C0-C1
- **Management:** Shared decision-making for disease-modifying therapy (see STEP 3)

**HIGH RISK (Disease-Modifying Therapy Recommended):**
- Proteinuria >1 g/day despite 3-6 months optimized supportive care
- Progressive eGFR decline >5 mL/min/year
- Crescentic disease (C2: ≥25% crescents)
- Severe biopsy features (T2, extensive crescents)
- **Management:** Initiate disease-modifying therapy (see STEP 3)

---

**STEP 3: DISEASE-MODIFYING THERAPY (Moderate-High Risk)**

**KDIGO 2025 Treatment Options - Preference Order:**

**Option A: Complement Inhibition (PREFERRED FOR MANY PATIENTS):**

**Iptacopan (Fabhalta®) 200 mg PO BID** ⭐ **2025 GUIDELINE PREFERRED**
- **Rationale:** Targets complement-mediated pathophysiology directly
- **Evidence:** 44% proteinuria reduction, slowed eGFR decline (APPLAUSE-IgAN 2024)
- **Advantages:**
  - Oral, convenient (BID dosing)
  - Dual benefit: proteinuria + eGFR preservation
  - Well-tolerated
- **Requirements:**
  - **MANDATORY meningococcal vaccination** (MenACWY + MenB) ≥2 weeks before starting
  - Booster every 3-5 years
  - Antibiotic prophylaxis during high-risk periods (optional)
- **Monitoring:** CBC, infection surveillance
- **Cost:** $15,000-20,000/month (prior authorization required)
- **Who benefits most:** High-risk patients, those wanting oral therapy, combination with SGLT2i

**Option B: Endothelin Receptor Antagonism:**

**B1. Atrasentan (Vanrafia®) 0.75 mg PO once daily** ⭐ **NEWEST FDA APPROVAL (April 2025)**
- **Rationale:** Selective ET-A receptor antagonism reduces proteinuria, preserves eGFR
- **Evidence:** 36% proteinuria reduction (ALIGN Trial 2024-2025)
- **Advantages:**
  - Once-daily dosing (better adherence)
  - Selective ET-A blockade (spares ET-B, less fluid retention)
  - Additive benefit with SGLT2 inhibitors
- **Monitoring:**
  - Fluid retention (weight, edema, BP)
  - Hemoglobin (may decrease)
  - **Contraindicated in pregnancy** (teratogenic - BOXED WARNING)
- **Cost:** $10,000-15,000/month
- **Who benefits most:** Patients on SGLT2i, those preferring once-daily dosing

**B2. Sparsentan (Filspari®) 200-400 mg PO once daily**
- **Rationale:** Dual ERA/ARB mechanism reduces proteinuria more than ARB alone
- **Evidence:** 50% proteinuria reduction vs. irbesartan (PROTECT Trial 2023)
- **Advantages:**
  - Strong proteinuria reduction
  - Replaces ARB (one medication instead of two)
- **Disadvantages:**
  - More fluid retention than atrasentan (non-selective ERA)
  - Hypotension risk
  - **Contraindicated in pregnancy** (teratogenic)
- **Monitoring:** BP, edema, hemoglobin, LFTs
- **Cost:** Similar to atrasentan
- **Who benefits most:** Patients not yet on SGLT2i, high proteinuria burden

**Option C: Targeted-Release Budesonide (Mucosal Immunity Approach):**

**Nefecon/Tarpeyo® 16 mg PO once daily × 9 months**
- **Rationale:** Targets Peyer's patches (site of aberrant IgA production)
- **Evidence:** 31% reduction in eGFR decline (NefIgArd Trial 2023)
- **Advantages:**
  - Minimal systemic steroid exposure
  - Targets immunologic root cause
  - Finite duration (9 months)
- **Disadvantages:**
  - Steroid-related side effects (though reduced vs. systemic steroids)
  - BP elevation, glucose intolerance
  - Not suitable if eGFR <25 mL/min
- **Monitoring:** BP, glucose, bone density (if prolonged use)
- **Cost:** $10,000-12,000/month
- **Who benefits most:** eGFR 25-90, contraindication to pregnancy, prefer finite therapy

**Option D: Combination Therapy (Emerging Approach - KDIGO 2025 Consideration):**

**Multi-targeted Approach:**
- **ACEi/ARB + SGLT2i + Iptacopan** (complement + hemodynamic)
- **ACEi/ARB + SGLT2i + Atrasentan** (endothelin + hemodynamic)
- **Nefecon + SGLT2i** (mucosal immunity + hemodynamic)

**Rationale:**
- Address multiple pathophysiologic mechanisms
- Potentially additive/synergistic effects
- Individualize based on risk, tolerance, cost

**KDIGO 2025 Suggestion:**
- Consider combination therapy for highest-risk patients
- Insufficient long-term data, use with caution
- Close monitoring for adverse effects

---

**STEP 4: CRESCENTIC IgAN / RPGN (Urgent Treatment)**

**Criteria:** ≥50% crescents on biopsy, rapidly declining GFR, severe AKI

**Treatment Protocol:**
1. **Pulse IV Methylprednisolone:** 500-1000 mg IV daily × 3 days
2. **Oral Prednisone:** 1 mg/kg/day (max 60 mg), taper over 6 months
3. **Cyclophosphamide:** 2 mg/kg/day PO × 3-6 months
   - OR **Rituximab:** 1 g IV × 2 doses (2 weeks apart)
4. **Plasmapheresis:** If anti-GBM antibody coexistence (rare)
5. **Supportive Care:** Continue ACEi/ARB, SGLT2i as tolerated

**Goal:** Prevent irreversible glomerular scarring, preserve kidney function

---

**STEP 5: MONITORING & FOLLOW-UP**

**Initial Monitoring (First 3-6 Months):**
- Every 4-6 weeks: BP, weight, eGFR, proteinuria (UPCR or 24-hour urine)
- Every 3 months: CBC, CMP, lipid panel
- Assess treatment response:
  - **Complete remission:** Proteinuria <0.3 g/day, stable eGFR
  - **Partial remission:** ≥50% reduction in proteinuria, stable eGFR
  - **No response:** <25% reduction in proteinuria or declining eGFR

**Long-term Monitoring (After Achieving Response):**
- Every 3-6 months: BP, eGFR, proteinuria
- Annually: Full metabolic panel, lipids, bone health assessment
- Reassess therapy if:
  - Proteinuria increases ≥50% from nadir
  - eGFR declines >5 mL/min/year
  - New complications arise

---

**STEP 6: TREATMENT ADJUSTMENT STRATEGIES**

**If Inadequate Response at 6-12 Months:**
1. **Verify adherence** to medications, lifestyle modifications
2. **Optimize supportive care** (maximize ACEi/ARB, ensure SGLT2i on board)
3. **Switch or add disease-modifying therapy:**
   - If on nefecon → switch to iptacopan or atrasentan
   - If on atrasentan → add iptacopan (combination therapy)
   - If on sparsentan → switch to atrasentan + SGLT2i
4. **Reassess biopsy indications** (if >2 years since initial biopsy, consider repeat)

**If Adverse Effects:**
- Iptacopan infection → antibiotic prophylaxis, consider switching
- Atrasentan/sparsentan fluid retention → diuretics, reduce dose, switch
- Nefecon steroid side effects → switch to iptacopan or atrasentan

---

**KDIGO 2025 KEY PRACTICE POINTS:**

1. **Start SGLT2 inhibitors in ALL IgAN patients** (eGFR ≥20) - new standard of care
2. **Target BP <120/80 mmHg** (systolic <120) with ACEi/ARB
3. **Reassess after 3-6 months** of optimized supportive care before adding immunosuppression
4. **Disease-modifying therapy recommended** if proteinuria >1 g/day despite supportive care
5. **Shared decision-making** for moderate-risk patients (proteinuria 0.5-1.0 g/day)
6. **Meningococcal vaccination mandatory** before iptacopan
7. **Pregnancy screening/contraception mandatory** for ERA therapy (atrasentan, sparsentan)
8. **Combination therapy** may be considered for highest-risk patients
9. **Systemic corticosteroids are SECOND-LINE** (use only if newer agents unavailable/contraindicated)
10. **Monitor for treatment response** at 6-12 months, adjust if inadequate

---

#### Prognosis (2024 Update)
**Natural History (Untreated or Supportive Care Only):**
- 20-30% progress to ESRD in 20 years
- 10-20% have complete spontaneous remission
- 30-40% have stable disease with slow progression
- Variable course, highly dependent on proteinuria level

**Impact of Modern Treatment (2024):**
**With Optimized Supportive Care (RAAS blockade + SGLT2i):**
- 30-40% reduction in progression to ESRD
- Significantly slower eGFR decline
- Reduced cardiovascular mortality

**With Targeted-Release Budesonide (Nefecon/Tarpeyo):**
- **NefIgArd Trial:** 31% reduction in eGFR decline
- Sustained proteinuria reduction (27% at 2 years)
- Number needed to treat (NNT) = 4-5 to prevent one ESRD event

**With Sparsentan (Filspari):**
- **PROTECT Trial:**
  - 49.8% proteinuria reduction (vs. 15.1% with irbesartan)
  - Improved eGFR slope (slower decline)
  - Sustained benefit through 110 weeks
- May reduce long-term ESRD risk by 40-50%

**Predictors of Better Prognosis:**
- Proteinuria <0.5 g/day (excellent prognosis)
- Early initiation of SGLT2 inhibitors
- Achievement of remission (complete or partial)
- Good blood pressure control (<120/80 mmHg)
- Minimal chronicity on biopsy (T0-T1)

**Predictors of Poor Prognosis:**
- Persistent proteinuria >1 g/day despite treatment
- eGFR <30 mL/min at presentation
- Crescentic disease (>50% crescents)
- Severe chronicity on biopsy (T2)
- Hypertension, smoking

**Post-Transplant:**
- Recurrence rate: 30-50% (histologic), 10-20% (clinical)
- Usually recurs within 5 years
- Rarely causes graft loss in first 10 years
- Continue SGLT2 inhibitors, RAAS blockade post-transplant

### C3 Glomerulopathy (C3G) & Complement-Mediated Diseases

#### Definition & Classification
**C3 Glomerulopathy:**
- Glomerular disease characterized by dominant C3 deposition on immunofluorescence
- Minimal or absent immunoglobulin deposition
- Results from alternative complement pathway dysregulation

**Subtypes:**
**Dense Deposit Disease (DDD):**
- Intramembranous electron-dense deposits
- Highly electron-dense osmiophilic deposits on EM
- Formerly called MPGN Type II
- More severe course

**C3 Glomerulonephritis (C3GN):**
- Mesangial, subendothelial, subepithelial deposits
- Less electron-dense than DDD
- Similar to MPGN Type I but without immunoglobulin
- More common than DDD

#### Pathogenesis
**Complement Dysregulation:**
- Alternative pathway overactivation
- C3 convertase (C3bBb) stabilization
- Impaired regulation of C3 convertase

**Genetic Abnormalities:**
- Complement factor H (CFH) mutations or autoantibodies
- Complement factor I (CFI) deficiency
- Complement factor B (CFB) gain-of-function mutations
- C3 mutations
- CFHR1-5 abnormalities

**Acquired Factors:**
- C3 nephritic factors (C3NeF) - autoantibodies that stabilize C3 convertase
- Factor H autoantibodies (anti-FH)
- Monoclonal gammopathy-associated C3G

#### Clinical Presentation
**Demographics:**
- Any age (mean 20-30 years)
- No clear gender predominance
- DDD more common in children/young adults

**Presentation:**
- Hematuria (microscopic or gross) - 80-90%
- Proteinuria (often nephrotic range) - 70-80%
- Hypertension - 50-60%
- Reduced GFR at presentation - 30-40%
- Low C3 with normal C4 - 50-70%

#### Diagnostic Workup
**Laboratory Tests:**
- **Complement Levels:** Low C3, normal C4 (alternative pathway activation)
- **C3 Nephritic Factor (C3NeF):** Positive in 40-80%
- **Anti-Factor H Antibodies:** Check if low C3
- **Serum Protein Electrophoresis:** Rule out monoclonal gammopathy
- **Genetic Testing:** CFH, CFI, CFB, C3 mutations

**Kidney Biopsy:**
**Light Microscopy:**
- Mesangial proliferation
- Endocapillary proliferation
- Membranoproliferative pattern
- May have crescents

**Immunofluorescence:**
- **Dominant C3 staining (2+ intensity more than any immunoglobulin)**
- Minimal to absent IgG, IgM, IgA
- C3-dominant IF is diagnostic hallmark

**Electron Microscopy:**
- **DDD:** Dense intramembranous deposits (sausage-shaped, ribbon-like)
- **C3GN:** Subendothelial, mesangial, subepithelial deposits

#### Treatment Strategies (2024 Update)
**Supportive Care:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs for proteinuria/hypertension
- SGLT2 inhibitors (reduce proteinuria, slow progression)
- Blood pressure target <130/80 mmHg
- Anticoagulation if nephrotic syndrome

**Immunosuppression (Limited Evidence):**
**Mycophenolate Mofetil:**
- 1000-1500 mg twice daily
- May reduce proteinuria in some cases
- 6-12 month trial

**Corticosteroids:**
- Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day
- Limited efficacy, often used in crescentic disease
- Taper over 6 months

**Complement-Targeted Therapies (Emerging):**
**Eculizumab (Soliris):**
- C5 inhibitor
- Case reports and small series show benefit
- Variable response (30-50% respond)
- May stabilize GFR, reduce proteinuria
- Off-label use, high cost

**Pegcetacoplan (Empaveli®) - FDA APPROVED July 28, 2025** ⭐ **FIRST-EVER C3G TREATMENT**
- **FDA Status:** First and ONLY FDA-approved treatment for C3 glomerulopathy and primary IC-MPGN
- **Approval Date:** July 28, 2025 for patients ≥12 years of age
- **Mechanism:** C3 complement inhibitor (binds C3 and C3b, blocks both classical and alternative pathways)
- **VALIANT Trial (Phase 3):**
  - 68.3% relative reduction in proteinuria vs. placebo (mean UPCR reduction -67.3% vs +3.2%)
  - Stabilization of kidney function (eGFR preserved)
  - 71% complete clearance of C3c staining on biopsy vs. placebo
  - Study duration: 26 weeks primary endpoint
- **Dosing:** 1080 mg subcutaneous twice weekly
- **Indications:** C3 glomerulopathy (C3GN or DDD) or primary IC-MPGN to reduce proteinuria (ages ≥12)
- **Advantages:**
  - First FDA-approved targeted therapy for C3G
  - Dual benefit: Proteinuria reduction AND C3 deposit clearance
  - Biopsy-proven histologic improvement
- **Monitoring:**
  - **Critical:** Meningococcal vaccination mandatory (increased infection risk)
  - Encapsulated bacteria infections (pneumococcus, H. influenzae)
  - Proteinuria, eGFR monitoring
  - Consider repeat biopsy at 6-12 months to assess C3 clearance
- **Place in Therapy:**
  - FIRST-LINE for C3G and primary IC-MPGN
  - Consider early (before significant fibrosis develops)
  - May prevent progression to ESRD
- **Cost:** ~$400,000-500,000/year (requires prior authorization)

**Iptacopan (Fabhalta®) - FDA APPROVED 2025 for C3G**
- **FDA Status:** Approved 2025 for C3 glomerulopathy
- **Mechanism:** Oral Factor B inhibitor (alternative complement pathway)
- **APPEAR-C3G Trial:**
  - 35% proteinuria reduction at 6 months vs. placebo
  - Oral administration (major advantage over subcutaneous pegcetacoplan)
- **Dosing:** 200 mg BID (twice daily)
- **Advantages:**
  - Oral administration (convenient)
  - Alternative to subcutaneous pegcetacoplan
  - Dual indication: IgA nephropathy AND C3G
- **Monitoring:** Meningococcal vaccination mandatory, infection surveillance

**Treatment of Underlying Causes:**
- **Monoclonal Gammopathy:** Treat clone (chemotherapy, anti-CD20)
- **Anti-FH Antibodies:** Consider rituximab, immunosuppression
- **C3NeF Positive:** May respond to complement inhibitors

#### Prognosis
**Natural History:**
- 50% progress to ESRD in 10 years (untreated)
- DDD has worse prognosis than C3GN
- Worse outcomes with:
  - Nephrotic-range proteinuria
  - Reduced GFR at presentation
  - Crescentic disease
  - >50% chronicity on biopsy

**Post-Transplant:**
- Recurrence rate: 60-80% (histologic), 30-50% (clinical)
- DDD recurrence nearly universal
- Eculizumab may reduce recurrence risk
- Consider complement inhibitors peri-transplant

### Atypical Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (aHUS)

#### Definition & Pathogenesis
**Definition:**
- Thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA) characterized by:
  - Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (MAHA)
  - Thrombocytopenia
  - Acute kidney injury
- **Not** associated with Shiga toxin (STEC-HUS) or severe ADAMTS13 deficiency (TTP)

**Pathogenesis:**
- Alternative complement pathway dysregulation on endothelial surfaces
- Uncontrolled C3 convertase and C5b-9 (MAC) formation
- Endothelial injury → platelet activation → microvascular thrombosis

**Genetic Mutations (50-60% of cases):**
- **Complement Factor H (CFH):** 25-30% (loss of function)
- **Complement Factor I (CFI):** 10-15% (loss of function)
- **Membrane Cofactor Protein (MCP/CD46):** 10-15% (loss of function)
- **Complement Factor B (CFB):** Rare (gain of function)
- **C3:** Rare (gain of function)
- **Thrombomodulin (THBD):** Rare

**Acquired Factors:**
- **Anti-Factor H Antibodies:** 5-10% (more common in children)
- **ADAMTS13 inhibitor:** Rarely <10% activity

#### Clinical Presentation
**Demographics:**
- Bimodal distribution: Children <5 years, adults 30-40 years
- No clear gender predominance
- Family history in 10-20%

**Triggering Events (40-50%):**
- Pregnancy/postpartum (10-20%)
- Infection (URI, GI illness) - 30%
- Medications (calcineurin inhibitors, chemotherapy)
- Surgery, trauma
- Autoimmune disease

**Clinical Features:**
**Hematologic:**
- Hemolytic anemia (schistocytes on smear)
- Thrombocytopenia (usually <150,000, often <50,000)
- Elevated LDH, indirect bilirubin
- Low haptoglobin
- Negative direct Coombs test

**Renal:**
- Acute kidney injury (80-90%)
- Oliguria or anuria
- Hematuria, proteinuria
- Hypertension (malignant in 20%)

**Extrarenal (30-50%):**
- Neurologic: Seizures, altered mental status, stroke
- Cardiac: Heart failure, myocardial infarction
- GI: Abdominal pain, colitis, pancreatitis

#### Diagnostic Workup
**Laboratory Evaluation:**
**Essential Tests:**
- CBC with smear (schistocytes, thrombocytopenia)
- LDH (elevated), haptoglobin (low)
- Direct Coombs test (negative)
- **ADAMTS13 activity:** >5-10% (rules out TTP)
- Complement levels: May have low C3, normal/low C4

**Additional Tests:**
- **Shiga toxin/Stool culture:** Rule out STEC-HUS
- **Anti-Factor H Antibodies:** If suspected
- **Genetic Testing:** CFH, CFI, MCP, CFB, C3, THBD
  - Should be sent in all cases
  - Results guide long-term management

**Kidney Biopsy:**
- Not required for diagnosis if classic presentation
- Shows TMA: endothelial swelling, mesangiolysis, capillary thrombosis
- Consider if diagnosis uncertain

#### Treatment (2024 Standard of Care)
**Complement Inhibition - FIRST-LINE THERAPY:**

**Eculizumab (Soliris):**
- **FDA-approved** for aHUS (first-line, standard of care)
- **Dosing Regimen:**
  - Week 1-4: 900 mg IV weekly × 4 doses
  - Week 5: 1200 mg IV (fifth dose)
  - Maintenance: 1200 mg IV every 2 weeks
- **Pre-Treatment:**
  - Meningococcal vaccination (MenACWY and MenB) 2-4 weeks prior
  - If urgent: Give with prophylactic antibiotics (penicillin or azithromycin)
- **Outcomes:**
  - TMA resolution in 80-90%
  - Dialysis independence in 80-90% (if started early)
  - Platelet recovery within 7-14 days
  - Hematologic normalization within 2-4 weeks

**Ravulizumab (Ultomiris):**
- **FDA-approved** for aHUS (2019)
- Longer-acting C5 inhibitor
- **Dosing:** Weight-based loading, then maintenance every 8 weeks
- Equivalent efficacy to eculizumab
- **Advantages:** Less frequent dosing, improved quality of life

**Duration of Therapy:**
- **Genetic mutation:** Likely lifelong therapy
- **Acquired (anti-FH antibodies):** May discontinue after antibody clearance
- **Pregnancy-triggered:** Individualized (often continue through pregnancy)
- **No identified mutation:** Trial discontinuation after 6-12 months with close monitoring

**Alternative/Adjunctive Therapies:**
**Plasma Exchange (PLEX):**
- **NOT first-line** (eculizumab superior)
- May use as bridge if eculizumab unavailable
- Daily PLEX until diagnosis confirmed
- Discontinue once complement inhibitor started

**For Anti-Factor H Antibodies:**
- Immunosuppression: Prednisone + mycophenolate or rituximab
- Plasma exchange (removes antibodies)
- Eculizumab (blocks downstream complement)
- Monitor antibody titers

**Supportive Care:**
- Dialysis if needed (goal: avoid if possible with early eculizumab)
- Blood pressure control (often requires multiple agents)
- Transfusions as needed (platelets only if bleeding)
- Avoid platelet transfusions unless life-threatening bleeding

#### Monitoring & Follow-Up
**Acute Phase:**
- Daily CBC, LDH, creatinine
- Monitor for TMA resolution
- Watch for infections (meningococcal risk with eculizumab)

**Chronic Therapy:**
- CBC, LDH, creatinine every 2-4 weeks initially, then monthly
- Monitor for breakthrough TMA (5-10%)
- Annual meningococcal vaccination boosters
- Genetic counseling for family members

#### Prognosis
**With Eculizumab/Ravulizumab:**
- Excellent TMA control (80-90% complete response)
- Dialysis independence: 80-90% if started early
- 5-year survival: >90%
- Quality of life improves significantly

**Without Complement Inhibition:**
- High mortality (25-30% in acute phase)
- ESRD in 50-60%
- Frequent relapses (60-80%)

**Post-Transplant:**
- Recurrence risk: 50-90% without prophylaxis
- **With eculizumab prophylaxis:** Recurrence <10%
- Continue eculizumab peri- and post-transplant
- Genetic testing guides transplant planning

### Other Thrombotic Microangiopathies (TMAs)

#### Overview of TMA Disorders

**TMA Triad:**
1. Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (MAHA) - schistocytes on smear
2. Thrombocytopenia
3. Organ dysfunction (kidney, brain, heart most common)

**Differential Diagnosis of TMA:**
- **Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP)**: ADAMTS13 deficiency (<10% activity)
- **Atypical HUS (aHUS)**: Complement dysregulation (see Section 3.6)
- **Shiga toxin-mediated HUS (STEC-HUS)**: E. coli O157:H7, Shigella
- **Drug-induced TMA**: Multiple medications
- **Transplant-associated TMA**: Post-kidney or stem cell transplant
- **Pregnancy-associated TMA**: HELLP, severe preeclampsia
- **Malignant hypertension-associated TMA**
- **Systemic sclerosis (scleroderma renal crisis)**
- **Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)**: Overlapping features

---

#### Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (TTP)

**Definition & Pathogenesis:**
- **Primary defect**: Severe ADAMTS13 deficiency (<10% activity)
- **ADAMTS13 function**: Cleaves ultra-large von Willebrand factor (UL-vWF) multimers
- **Result of deficiency**: Uncleaved UL-vWF → platelet aggregation → microthrombi

**Types:**
- **Acquired TTP (90%)**: Anti-ADAMTS13 autoantibodies (IgG)
- **Congenital TTP (Upshaw-Schulman syndrome, 10%)**: Hereditary ADAMTS13 mutations

**Clinical Presentation:**
- **Classic Pentad** (rare - only 5-10% have all 5):
  1. Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia
  2. Thrombocytopenia (typically <30,000/μL)
  3. **Neurologic symptoms** (50-80%): headache, confusion, seizures, stroke, coma
  4. Renal dysfunction (less severe than aHUS typically)
  5. Fever
- **More common presentation**: MAHA + thrombocytopenia + neurologic or renal symptoms

**Laboratory Diagnosis:**
- **ADAMTS13 activity <10%** (DIAGNOSTIC - send BEFORE plasma exchange!)
- **Anti-ADAMTS13 antibodies**: Present in acquired TTP
- **MAHA**:
  - Schistocytes on peripheral smear (>1%)
  - Elevated LDH (often >1000 U/L, can be >3000)
  - Low haptoglobin (<10 mg/dL)
  - Elevated indirect bilirubin
  - Negative direct Coombs test
- **Thrombocytopenia**: Often severe (<30,000/μL)
- **Coagulation studies**: Normal PT/PTT/fibrinogen (distinguishes from DIC)
- **Creatinine**: May be elevated but typically less severe than aHUS

**Treatment (Emergency - DO NOT DELAY):**

**1. Therapeutic Plasma Exchange (TPE) - Immediate, Life-Saving:**
- **Start immediately** - even before ADAMTS13 results return
- Delay increases mortality (50% if untreated → 10-20% with TPE)
- **Procedure**:
  - 1.5 plasma volumes daily (usually ~60 mL/kg)
  - Replace with fresh frozen plasma (FFP) or cryopoor plasma
  - Continue daily until:
    - Platelet count >150,000/μL for 2 consecutive days AND
    - LDH normalizing AND
    - No hemolysis
  - Then taper over 2-4 weeks
- **Duration**: Typically 5-14 days (median 7-10 days)

**2. Caplacizumab (Cablivi®) - FDA Approved 2019** ⭐
- **Mechanism**: Anti-vWF nanobody (blocks vWF-platelet interaction)
- **Indication**: Acquired TTP (with TPE and immunosuppression)
- **Dosing**:
  - Loading: 10 mg IV bolus BEFORE first TPE
  - Maintenance: 10 mg SC daily (start after first TPE)
  - Duration: Continue daily TPE + 30 days after stopping TPE
  - If anti-ADAMTS13 antibodies persist after 30 days: continue until resolved
- **Benefits**:
  - 74% faster platelet normalization vs placebo
  - 67% reduction in TTP recurrence
  - 33% reduction in TTP-related death or recurrence
- **Adverse effects**: Bleeding risk (epistaxis, gingival bleeding)
- **Monitoring**: CBC with platelets (may continue to decrease in first 1-2 days)

**3. Immunosuppression (To Suppress Anti-ADAMTS13 Antibodies):**
- **Corticosteroids (First-line):**
  - Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day OR
  - Methylprednisolone 1 g IV daily × 3 days, then prednisone 1 mg/kg
- **Rituximab (CD20 antibody - Standard of Care 2024):**
  - Dosing: 375 mg/m² IV weekly × 4 weeks
  - **Evidence**: Reduces exacerbations from 47% to 12%
  - **Start early** (within first week of diagnosis)
  - Well-tolerated, improves long-term outcomes
  - Should be given to ALL acquired TTP patients (not just refractory)
- **Refractory TTP**: Cyclosporine, cyclophosphamide, vincristine, bortezomib

**4. Supportive Care:**
- **Transfusion thresholds**:
  - Platelets: AVOID unless life-threatening bleeding (platelet transfusion may worsen TTP)
  - RBC: Transfuse for Hgb <7 g/dL or symptomatic anemia
- Folic acid 1 mg daily (hemolysis)
- **Avoid**: Antiplatelet agents (aspirin, clopidogrel)

**5. Congenital TTP (Upshaw-Schulman Syndrome):**
- Regular plasma infusions (10-15 mL/kg every 2-4 weeks) to maintain ADAMTS13 >10%
- Does NOT require immunosuppression (no antibodies)
- Lifelong therapy required

**Monitoring:**
- **Acute phase**: Daily CBC, LDH, ADAMTS13 activity (if rising)
- **Response indicators**:
  - Platelet count rising
  - LDH decreasing
  - Schistocytes decreasing
  - Neurologic symptoms improving
- **Long-term**: ADAMTS13 activity every 3-6 months (relapse risk if <10%)

**Prognosis:**
- **With TPE + caplacizumab + rituximab**: 90-95% survival
- **Without treatment**: 90% mortality
- **Relapse rate**: 30-50% at 10 years (acquired TTP)
- **Predictors of poor outcome**: Cardiac involvement, delayed treatment, severe neurologic symptoms

#### Drug-Induced Thrombotic Microangiopathy

**High-Risk Medications:**

**1. Immunosuppressants (Most Common):**
- **Calcineurin inhibitors (CNI)**:
  - Tacrolimus, cyclosporine
  - Mechanism: Endothelial toxicity
  - **Transplant-associated TMA**: 0.5-14% incidence post-transplant
  - Risk factors: High trough levels, concurrent sirolimus
  - Management: Reduce dose OR switch to belatacept/mycophenolate
- **mTOR inhibitors**:
  - Sirolimus, everolimus
  - Especially when combined with CNI

**2. Chemotherapy Agents:**
- **Gemcitabine**: Most common chemo-associated TMA (~0.4% of patients)
  - Median onset: After 6-8 months of therapy
  - Often irreversible, poor renal prognosis
  - Treatment: Stop gemcitabine, supportive care, consider eculizumab
- **Mitomycin C**: Classic association (5-10% incidence)
- **Platinum agents**: Cisplatin, oxaliplatin

**3. Anti-VEGF Agents:**
- Bevacizumab (Avastin), sunitinib, sorafenib
- Mechanism: Anti-angiogenic → endothelial dysfunction
- Usually improves with drug discontinuation

**4. Antiplatelet Agents:**
- **Ticlopidine**: 0.02-0.04% incidence (removed from market in many countries)
- **Clopidogrel**: Very rare (<1 in 10,000)
- Quinine: Antibody-mediated

**5. Interferon:**
- Interferon-alpha, interferon-beta
- Risk increases with duration of therapy

**Diagnosis:**
- TMA triad present
- Temporal relationship with drug exposure
- ADAMTS13 activity >10% (rules out TTP)
- Complement studies: Usually normal (rules out aHUS, but some cases have complement activation)

**Treatment:**
- **Stop offending drug** (most important)
- Supportive care
- **Plasma exchange**: Consider if severe, but less effective than in TTP
- **Eculizumab**: Case reports of benefit in severe gemcitabine-associated TMA
- **Do NOT rechallenge** with causative drug

**Prognosis:**
- Variable depending on drug
- Gemcitabine-TMA: Often irreversible, 50% progress to ESRD
- CNI-TMA: Usually improves with dose reduction or discontinuation

---

#### Transplant-Associated Thrombotic Microangiopathy (TA-TMA)

**Types:**
1. **Kidney transplant-associated TMA** (0.5-14%)
2. **Hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT)-associated TMA** (10-30%)

**Kidney Transplant-Associated TMA:**
- **Causes**:
  - Calcineurin inhibitor toxicity (tacrolimus > cyclosporine)
  - Antibody-mediated rejection (ABMR) with TMA features
  - Recurrent aHUS (if underlying complement disorder)
  - Infections (CMV, BK virus, parvovirus B19)
- **Clinical presentation**: Thrombocytopenia, hemolysis, graft dysfunction
- **Diagnosis**:
  - Biopsy: Acute TMA lesions (endothelial swelling, thrombosis)
  - Rule out ABMR (C4d staining, DSA testing)
  - Complement studies if recurrent aHUS suspected
- **Treatment**:
  - Reduce/stop CNI (switch to belatacept or mycophenolate-based regimen)
  - Treat underlying rejection if present (IVIG, plasmapheresis, rituximab)
  - Eculizumab if aHUS recurrence

**HSCT-Associated TMA:**
- **Risk factors**: Total body irradiation, graft-versus-host disease, CNI use, viral infections
- **Diagnosis**: Elevated LDH, schistocytes, proteinuria, hypertension
- **Treatment**: Stop CNI, defibrotide (approved for veno-occlusive disease), supportive care

---

#### Pregnancy-Associated TMA

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. **HELLP syndrome** (Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets)
   - Part of severe preeclampsia spectrum
   - Onset: Usually third trimester or postpartum
   - Treatment: Delivery, supportive care
   - Usually resolves after delivery
2. **Severe preeclampsia/eclampsia**
   - Hypertension, proteinuria, TMA features
   - Treatment: Delivery
3. **Acute fatty liver of pregnancy**
   - Microvesicular steatosis, liver failure
   - Can have TMA features
4. **aHUS triggered by pregnancy**
   - ADAMTS13 >10%, complement mutations
   - Does NOT improve after delivery
   - Requires eculizumab
5. **TTP in pregnancy**
   - ADAMTS13 <10%
   - Requires TPE + caplacizumab

**Distinguishing Features:**
- **HELLP/Preeclampsia**: Improves within 48-72 hours post-delivery
- **aHUS/TTP**: Persists or worsens post-delivery
- **Key test**: ADAMTS13 activity (send before delivery if TMA suspected)

**Management:**
- Deliver baby if HELLP/preeclampsia
- If TMA persists >72 hours post-delivery:
  - Check ADAMTS13 (if <10% → TTP → TPE + caplacizumab)
  - Check complement (consider aHUS → eculizumab)

---

#### Approach to Undifferentiated TMA

**Step 1: Confirm TMA Diagnosis**
- MAHA (schistocytes, elevated LDH, low haptoglobin, negative Coombs)
- Thrombocytopenia
- Organ dysfunction

**Step 2: Send Urgent Labs (BEFORE starting treatment)**
- **ADAMTS13 activity and inhibitor** (if <10% → TTP)
- Complement studies (C3, C4, CH50, Factor H, Factor I, MCP, C3 nephritic factor)
- Shiga toxin stool assay (if diarrhea)
- Pregnancy test (if applicable)

**Step 3: Initiate Empiric Treatment**
- **If neurologic symptoms OR ADAMTS13 pending**: Start TPE immediately (assume TTP until proven otherwise)
- **If renal-predominant**: Consider eculizumab if aHUS suspected

**Step 4: Refine Diagnosis Based on Results**
- ADAMTS13 <10% → **TTP** → TPE + caplacizumab + rituximab
- ADAMTS13 >10% + complement disorder → **aHUS** → Eculizumab
- Shiga toxin positive → **STEC-HUS** → Supportive care only (NO TPE, NO eculizumab)
- Drug exposure → **Drug-induced TMA** → Stop drug
- Post-transplant → **TA-TMA** → Stop CNI, treat rejection

**Step 5: Biopsy if Diagnosis Unclear**
- Kidney biopsy can confirm TMA but does NOT distinguish etiology
- Shows: Endothelial swelling, mesangiolysis, capillary thrombosis, arteriolar mucoid intimal change

## 4. Secondary Glomerular Diseases

### Lupus Nephritis

#### Classification System (ISN/RPS 2003)
**Class I: Minimal Mesangial LN**
- Normal LM
- Mesangial IF deposits
- Excellent prognosis

**Class II: Mesangial Proliferative LN**
- Mesangial hypercellularity
- Mesangial immune deposits
- Good prognosis

**Class III: Focal LN**
- <50% glomeruli involved
- Active or chronic lesions
- Subdivided by activity/chronicity

**Class IV: Diffuse LN**
- ≥50% glomeruli involved
- Most severe form
- Subdivided into segmental (S) and global (G)

**Class V: Membranous LN**
- Subepithelial immune deposits
- May coexist with Class III/IV
- Variable prognosis

**Class VI: Advanced Sclerosing LN**
- >90% glomeruli sclerosed
- Irreversible damage
- Poor prognosis

#### Clinical Features
**Systemic Features:**
- Malar rash, photosensitivity
- Arthritis, serositis
- Neurologic involvement
- Hematologic abnormalities

**Renal Features:**
- Proteinuria (nephrotic in Class V)
- Hematuria, RBC casts
- Reduced GFR
- Hypertension

**Laboratory Findings:**
- Positive ANA (always)
- Anti-dsDNA antibodies
- Low complement (C3, C4)
- Elevated ESR/CRP

#### Treatment Protocols
**Induction Therapy (3-6 months):**
**Class III/IV:**
- MMF 2-3 g/day divided BID
- OR low-dose IV cyclophosphamide (Euro-Lupus)
- PLUS high-dose steroids
- Consider belimumab addition

**Class V:**
- MMF or calcineurin inhibitors
- Plus steroids
- Longer induction period

**Maintenance Therapy (2-3 years):**
- MMF 1-2 g/day
- OR azathioprine 2 mg/kg/day
- Low-dose steroids (5-10 mg prednisone)
- Consider belimumab

**Refractory Disease:**
- Rituximab 1 g × 2 doses (2 weeks apart)
- Consider cyclophosphamide retreatment
- Voclosporin (new CNI option)

#### KDIGO 2024 Lupus Nephritis Guideline - Treatment Algorithm ⭐

**PARADIGM SHIFT: Belimumab Triple Therapy as First-Line Standard of Care**

The KDIGO 2024 Lupus Nephritis Guideline Update (published March 2024) represents a major paradigm shift in the management of Class III/IV lupus nephritis by elevating **belimumab triple therapy** from "optional add-on" to **first-line standard-of-care recommendation**. This represents the first major change in lupus nephritis treatment algorithms in over a decade.

---

**KDIGO 2024 KEY RECOMMENDATIONS:**

1. **Belimumab triple therapy** (MMF + steroids + belimumab) is now **first-line preferred therapy** for Class III/IV LN
2. Early initiation of belimumab improves renal outcomes and reduces flares
3. Voclosporin is an alternative calcineurin inhibitor option for Class III/IV LN
4. Hydroxychloroquine should be continued in ALL lupus nephritis patients unless contraindicated
5. Lower cumulative steroid doses (avoid prolonged high-dose steroids)
6. Treat-to-target approach with proteinuria and complement normalization goals

---

**CLASS III/IV LUPUS NEPHRITIS - PREFERRED INDUCTION THERAPY (KDIGO 2024)**

**TRIPLE THERAPY (MMF + Steroids + Belimumab)** ⭐ **FIRST-LINE PREFERRED**

**Rationale for Triple Therapy:**
- **BLISS-LN Trial (2020):** Belimumab + standard therapy (MMF + steroids) vs. placebo + standard therapy
  - Primary efficacy renal response (PERR): 43% vs. 32% (p=0.03)
  - Complete renal response: 30% vs. 20%
  - Time to renal-related event or death: 51% risk reduction (HR 0.49, p=0.001)
  - Reduced flares: 34% vs. 49%
  - Sustained proteinuria reduction
- **FDA Approval:** December 2020 for active lupus nephritis
- **KDIGO 2024 Recommendation:** First-line preferred based on superior outcomes

**Triple Therapy Protocol:**

**1. Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF):**
- **Dosing:** 2-3 g/day PO divided BID
  - Start: 1 g BID, may increase to 1.5 g BID (3 g/day total) if tolerated
  - Asian patients: May start at 1.5-2 g/day (potentially more responsive to lower doses)
- **Target MMF level:** 1.5-3 mg/L (if monitoring available)
- **Duration:** 6 months induction, then continue as maintenance (reduce to 1-2 g/day)
- **Monitoring:** CBC weekly × 1 month, then monthly (leukopenia, anemia)
- **Adverse effects:** GI upset (most common), infection risk, teratogenic

**2. Glucocorticoids (Lower Doses Emphasized in 2024 Guideline):**
- **Pulse IV Methylprednisolone (Optional for Severe Disease):**
  - 500-1000 mg IV daily × 3 days
  - Use for severe presentation (Cr >2 mg/dL, nephrotic syndrome, rapidly progressive GN)
- **Oral Prednisone:**
  - **Initial:** 0.5-0.75 mg/kg/day (max 60 mg/day)
    - **2024 Guideline Change:** Lower initial doses preferred (0.5 mg/kg) vs. historical 1 mg/kg
  - **Taper Schedule (2024 Guideline-Aligned):**
    - Weeks 1-2: 0.5 mg/kg/day (e.g., 30-40 mg/day)
    - Weeks 3-4: Reduce to 20-30 mg/day
    - Months 2-3: Taper to 10-15 mg/day
    - Months 4-6: Taper to 5-7.5 mg/day
    - Maintenance: 5 mg/day or less (goal: discontinue by 12 months if possible)
  - **Rationale:** Minimize cumulative steroid exposure, reduce infection and metabolic complications

**3. Belimumab (Benlysta®)** ⭐ **NEW STANDARD-OF-CARE (KDIGO 2024)**
- **Mechanism:** Monoclonal antibody inhibiting B-lymphocyte stimulator (BLyS/BAFF)
- **FDA Approval:** December 2020 for active lupus nephritis (Class III, IV, or V)
- **Dosing:**
  - **IV Formulation (Preferred for LN):**
    - 10 mg/kg IV at weeks 0, 2, 4, then every 4 weeks
    - Infusion over 1 hour
  - **SC Formulation (Alternative):**
    - 200 mg SC weekly
    - Self-administered, more convenient
- **When to Start:** Initiate with MMF and steroids at diagnosis (don't wait for MMF response)
- **Duration:** Continue for at least 12 months, often 24+ months
- **Monitoring:**
  - Infusion reactions (rare with premedication)
  - Infections (monitor CBC, clinical symptoms)
  - Depression/suicidality (rare, <2%)
- **Advantages:**
  - 51% reduction in renal-related events or death (BLISS-LN)
  - Reduced flare rate (34% vs. 49%)
  - Steroid-sparing effect
  - Improved complete renal response
- **Cost:** ~$5,000-7,000/month (prior authorization typically required, often approved for LN)

**Monitoring During Induction (First 6 Months):**
- Every 2-4 weeks: BP, weight, urine protein (UPCR or 24-hour), creatinine
- Monthly: CBC, CMP, C3/C4, anti-dsDNA
- Every 3 months: Comprehensive metabolic panel, lipids (steroid effects)
- Assess response at 3 and 6 months

**Goals of Induction:**
- Complete renal response: UPCR <0.5 g/g, stable/improved eGFR, normalized C3/C4
- Partial renal response: ≥50% reduction in UPCR, stable eGFR, improved C3/C4
- No response: <50% reduction in UPCR or worsening eGFR → switch therapy

---

**ALTERNATIVE INDUCTION REGIMENS (KDIGO 2024)**

**Option B: Dual Therapy (MMF + Steroids Only) - Second-Line**
- Use if belimumab unavailable, contraindicated, or patient preference/cost concerns
- Same MMF and steroid dosing as above (without belimumab)
- Lower response rates than triple therapy (BLISS-LN: 32% PERR vs. 43% with belimumab)
- Consider belimumab addition if incomplete response at 3-6 months

**Option C: Low-Dose IV Cyclophosphamide (Euro-Lupus Protocol)**
- **Dosing:** 500 mg IV every 2 weeks × 6 doses (12 weeks total)
- **Indications:** Severe disease, inability to tolerate MMF, certain geographic regions (Europe)
- **Advantages:** Lower cumulative dose than historical NIH protocol, similar efficacy to MMF
- **Disadvantages:** IV administration, gonadal toxicity, bladder toxicity, infection risk
- **Concurrent:** Prednisone taper (same as above)
- **GnRH agonist:** Consider for ovarian protection in women of childbearing age
- **PLUS Belimumab:** Can add belimumab to cyclophosphamide + steroids (emerging data)

**Option D: Voclosporin (Lupkynis®) + MMF + Steroids - FDA Approved 2021** ⭐
- **Mechanism:** Novel calcineurin inhibitor (CNI), next-generation cyclosporine analog
- **FDA Approval:** January 2021 for active lupus nephritis in combination with background immunosuppression
- **AURORA 1 Trial (2021):**
  - Voclosporin + MMF + steroids vs. placebo + MMF + steroids
  - Complete renal response: 40.8% vs. 22.5% (p<0.001) at 52 weeks
  - Faster proteinuria reduction
  - Sustained response through 2 years (AURORA 2)
- **Dosing:**
  - 23.7 mg PO BID (fixed dose, no levels needed)
  - Take on empty stomach (1 hour before or 2 hours after meals)
- **Concurrent Therapy:**
  - MMF 1 g BID (2 g/day total - lower than triple therapy)
  - Low-dose steroids (taper to ≤2.5 mg/day by week 16)
- **Advantages:**
  - Rapid proteinuria reduction (within weeks)
  - Steroid-sparing (goal prednisone ≤2.5 mg/day)
  - Oral, BID dosing
- **Monitoring:**
  - **eGFR:** Check baseline and every 2-4 weeks (CNI nephrotoxicity risk)
    - If eGFR declines >20% from baseline → reduce dose or discontinue
  - Blood pressure (may increase)
  - No therapeutic drug level monitoring required (vs. cyclosporine/tacrolimus)
- **Contraindications:**
  - eGFR <45 mL/min (relative contraindication - monitor closely)
  - Strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (increase voclosporin levels)
- **Cost:** ~$6,000-8,000/month
- **Who benefits most:**
  - Patients with rapid progressive disease needing fast proteinuria reduction
  - Contraindication to belimumab
  - Preference for oral therapy over IV infusions

---

**MAINTENANCE THERAPY (After 6-Month Induction) - KDIGO 2024**

**Preferred Maintenance (After Triple Therapy Induction):**

**1. Mycophenolate Mofetil:** 1-2 g/day PO (reduce from induction dose)
**2. Prednisone:** 5 mg/day or less (goal: taper off by 12-24 months)
**3. Belimumab:** Continue 10 mg/kg IV every 4 weeks OR 200 mg SC weekly
- Duration: At least 24 months, often indefinite
- KDIGO 2024: Continue belimumab during maintenance for sustained remission

**4. Hydroxychloroquine:** 200-400 mg/day (ALL patients unless contraindicated)
- **KDIGO 2024 Strong Recommendation:** Continue HCQ indefinitely in all LN patients
- Reduces flares by 50%, improves long-term outcomes
- Monitor ophthalmology (retinal toxicity screening annually after 5 years)

**Alternative Maintenance Options:**
- **Azathioprine** 2 mg/kg/day (alternative to MMF if intolerance or pregnancy planning)
- **Voclosporin** 23.7 mg BID (if used for induction, continue for maintenance)
- **Calcineurin inhibitors** (tacrolimus 2-4 mg/day, cyclosporine) - second-line

**Maintenance Duration:**
- Minimum: 2-3 years after achieving complete renal response
- Many experts recommend indefinite maintenance (lifelong for Class IV)
- Taper immunosuppression only if sustained remission >3-5 years

---

**REFRACTORY LUPUS NEPHRITIS (Inadequate Response to First-Line)**

**Criteria for Refractory Disease:**
- <50% reduction in proteinuria after 6 months of induction
- Worsening eGFR during treatment
- Persistent active urine sediment
- Persistently low C3/C4 despite therapy

**KDIGO 2024 Refractory Disease Management:**

**Step 1: Optimize Current Regimen**
- Verify adherence (MMF, belimumab, prednisone)
- Check MMF levels (target 1.5-3 mg/L)
- Ensure adequate steroid dosing
- Add belimumab if not already on triple therapy

**Step 2: Switch Induction Agent**
- If on MMF triple therapy → switch to **voclosporin + MMF + steroids**
- If on cyclophosphamide → switch to **MMF + belimumab triple therapy**
- Consider **rituximab** (see below)

**Step 3: Rituximab (Anti-CD20 Therapy)**
- **Dosing:** 1 g IV × 2 doses (2 weeks apart) OR 375 mg/m² weekly × 4
- **Evidence:** LUNAR trial negative, but real-world data supports use in refractory disease
- **KDIGO 2024:** Consider rituximab for refractory disease or frequent flares
- **Advantages:** B-cell depletion, may achieve remission in MMF/cyclophosphamide failures
- **Monitoring:** IgG levels, infection surveillance, repeat dosing every 6-12 months
- **Combination:** Can use with MMF, belimumab (emerging data on safety)

**Step 4: Other Options**
- **Tacrolimus:** 2-4 mg/day (target trough 4-6 ng/mL) + MMF
- **Cyclosporine:** 2.5-5 mg/kg/day (target trough 100-200 ng/mL)
- **Calcineurin inhibitor + belimumab** (limited data)
- **Plasmapheresis:** Rarely used, severe RPGN only

**Step 5: Clinical Trial Enrollment**
- Anifrolumab (anti-interferon α receptor) - investigational for LN
- Obinutuzumab (anti-CD20, glycoengineered) - trials ongoing
- Iscalimab (anti-CD40) - Phase 2/3 trials
- Dapirolizumab pegol (anti-CD40L) - investigational

---

**CLASS V (MEMBRANOUS) LUPUS NEPHRITIS - KDIGO 2024**

**First-Line Options:**

**Option A: MMF + Steroids + Belimumab (Preferred)**
- Similar regimen to Class III/IV
- Belimumab FDA-approved for Class V LN
- MMF 2-3 g/day, prednisone taper, belimumab 10 mg/kg monthly

**Option B: Calcineurin Inhibitor-Based**
- **Tacrolimus** 2-4 mg/day + MMF + low-dose steroids
- **Voclosporin** 23.7 mg BID + MMF + low-dose steroids
- Faster proteinuria reduction vs. MMF alone
- Particularly effective for nephrotic-range proteinuria

**Duration:** Longer induction period (6-12 months) due to slower response in membranous disease

---

**KDIGO 2024 LUPUS NEPHRITIS KEY PRACTICE POINTS:**

1. **Belimumab triple therapy (MMF + steroids + belimumab) is first-line standard of care** for Class III/IV LN ⭐
2. **Initiate belimumab at diagnosis** (don't wait for MMF failure)
3. **Hydroxychloroquine should be continued in ALL patients** unless contraindicated
4. **Lower cumulative steroid doses** - taper to ≤5 mg/day by 6 months, goal discontinuation by 12 months
5. **Voclosporin is an alternative first-line option** for rapid proteinuria reduction
6. **Rituximab is recommended for refractory disease** despite LUNAR trial negativity
7. **Maintenance therapy should be at least 2-3 years** after complete renal response
8. **Treat-to-target approach:** Aim for complete renal response (UPCR <0.5, normal eGFR, normalized complements)
9. **Monitor response at 3 and 6 months** - switch therapy if inadequate response
10. **Repeat kidney biopsy** if unclear response or suspected flare (especially if >12-24 months from initial biopsy)

---

#### Monitoring & Prognosis
**Response Criteria:**
- Complete response: Proteinuria <0.5 g/day, normal GFR
- Partial response: ≥50% proteinuria reduction, stable GFR
- No response: <50% proteinuria reduction

**Long-term Outcomes:**
- 10-year renal survival: 80-90%
- Better with early treatment
- Worse with African American ethnicity
- Flare rate: 30-40% in 5 years

### Diabetic Nephropathy

#### Natural History
**Stage 1: Hyperfiltration**
- GFR >120 mL/min
- No structural changes
- Reversible with good control

**Stage 2: Silent Stage**
- Microalbuminuria (30-300 mg/day)
- Normal GFR
- Early structural changes

**Stage 3: Overt Nephropathy**
- Macroalbuminuria (>300 mg/day)
- GFR decline begins
- Hypertension common

**Stage 4: Advanced Nephropathy**
- GFR 15-29 mL/min
- Nephrotic-range proteinuria
- Hypertension severe

**Stage 5: ESRD**
- GFR <15 mL/min
- Need for renal replacement

#### Pathology
**Light Microscopy:**
- Mesangial expansion
- Basement membrane thickening
- Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules (advanced)
- Arteriolar hyalinosis

**Immunofluorescence:**
- Linear IgG along GBM (non-specific)
- IgM in mesangium (hyalinosis)
- No immune complex deposition

**Electron Microscopy:**
- GBM thickening (400-1000 nm)
- Mesangial matrix expansion
- Podocyte foot process effacement

#### Treatment Strategies
**Glycemic Control:**
- Target HbA1c 7-8%
- SGLT2 inhibitors (first-line)
- GLP-1 receptor agonists
- Consider early insulin

**Blood Pressure Control:**
- Target <130/80 mmHg
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs
- SGLT2 inhibitors
- Multiple agents often needed

**SGLT2 Inhibitors:**
- Dapagliflozin, empagliflozin, canagliflozin
- Cardiovascular and renal benefits
- Can be used to eGFR 20 mL/min
- Reduce progression by 30-40%

**GLP-1 Receptor Agonists:**
- Liraglutide, semaglutide, dulaglutide
- Cardiovascular benefits
- Weight loss
- Modest renal benefits

**Non-Steroidal MRAs:**
- Finerenone (FIDELIO-DKD, FIGARO-DKD)
- Cardio-renal benefits
- Hyperkalemia monitoring
- Use with ACEi/ARB

#### Prognosis
**Risk Factors for Progression:**
- Poor glycemic control
- Uncontrolled hypertension
- High proteinuria
- Genetic factors (APOL1)
- Smoking

**Outcomes:**
- Leading cause of ESRD worldwide
- 20-40% progress to ESRD in 20 years
- Better outcomes with modern therapy
- Cardiovascular mortality higher than ESRD

### Amyloidosis

#### Types & Classification
**AL Amyloidosis (Primary):**
- Light chain production by plasma cells
- Lambda light chains more common
- Associated with multiple myeloma
- Rapid progression

**AA Amyloidosis (Secondary):**
- Serum amyloid A protein
- Chronic inflammatory conditions
- Rheumatoid arthritis, IBD, infections
- Slower progression

**ATTR Amyloidosis:**
- Transthyretin protein
- Hereditary or wild-type
- Cardiac involvement common
- New therapies available

#### Clinical Features
**Renal Presentation:**
- Nephrotic syndrome (90%)
- Progressive renal failure
- Hypertension (less common)
- May have restrictive cardiomyopathy

**Systemic Features:**
- Macroglossia (AL)
- Periorbital purpura (AL)
- Hepatomegaly
- Neuropathy
- Cardiac involvement

#### Diagnosis
**Laboratory Findings:**
- Proteinuria (nephrotic range)
- Serum/urine protein electrophoresis
- Free light chain assay
- Serum amyloid A (AA type)

**Biopsy Findings:**
**Congo Red Stain:**
- Apple-green birefringence under polarized light
- Amyloid deposition in mesangium, vessels
- Congo red positivity required for diagnosis

**Immunofluorescence:**
- Light chain restriction (AL)
- Negative for AA in AL type
- Mass spectrometry for typing

#### Treatment
**AL Amyloidosis:**
- Autologous stem cell transplant (if eligible)
- Bortezomib-based regimens
- Daratumumab (anti-CD38)
- Supportive care

**AA Amyloidosis:**
- Treat underlying inflammatory condition
- Anti-IL-1 agents (canakinumab)
- Tocilizumab (anti-IL-6)
- Eprodisate (experimental)

**ATTR Amyloidosis:**
- Tafamidis (TTR stabilizer)
- Patisiran, inotersen (RNA silencing)
- Liver transplantation (hereditary)

**Renal-Specific:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs
- Diuretics for edema
- Anticoagulation if nephrotic
- Early transplant evaluation

#### Prognosis
**AL Amyloidosis:**
- Median survival 12-18 months (untreated)
- Improved to 5+ years with modern therapy
- Cardiac involvement determines prognosis
- Renal response predicts overall response

**AA Amyloidosis:**
- Better prognosis than AL
- Dependent on underlying disease control
- 5-year survival 50-70%
- Renal survival variable

## 5. Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN)

### Classification & Etiology

#### Type I: Anti-GBM Disease
**Pathogenesis:**
- Anti-glomerular basement membrane antibodies
- Linear IgG deposition along GBM
- Associated with pulmonary involvement (Goodpasture's)
- No immune complex deposition

**Clinical Features:**
- Rapid renal failure (days-weeks)
- Pulmonary hemorrhage (50-60%)
- Hemoptysis, dyspnea
- Severe anemia

**Laboratory Findings:**
- Positive anti-GBM antibodies
- Linear IF on biopsy
- No complement consumption
- High creatinine at presentation

#### Type II: Immune Complex GN
**Etiologies:**
- Post-infectious GN
- Lupus nephritis
- IgA nephropathy
- Cryoglobulinemic GN
- Essential mixed cryoglobulinemia

**Pathology:**
- Granular immune complex deposition
- "Lumpy-bumpy" IF pattern
- Complement activation
- Crescent formation

#### Type III: Pauci-Immune GN
**ANCA-Associated Vasculitis:**
**Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA):**
- c-ANCA (PR3-ANCA) positive
- Upper airway, lung, kidney involvement
- Granulomatous inflammation

**Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA):**
- p-ANCA (MPO-ANCA) positive
- Pulmonary-renal syndrome
- No granulomas

**Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (EGPA):**
- Asthma, eosinophilia
- p-ANCA positive (50%)
- Allergic granulomatosis

### Clinical Presentation

#### Renal Manifestations
- Rapid rise in creatinine (days)
- Oliguria or anuria
- Active urinary sediment
- Hematuria, RBC casts
- Proteinuria (usually sub-nephrotic)

#### Extrarenal Manifestations
**Pulmonary:**
- Hemoptysis (anti-GBM, ANCA)
- Pulmonary infiltrates
- Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage

**Systemic:**
- Fever, weight loss
- Arthralgias, myalgias
- Skin lesions (purpura, ulcers)
- Neurologic involvement

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Urgent Evaluation
**Laboratory Tests:**
- Anti-GBM antibodies
- ANCA panel (PR3, MPO)
- Complement levels (C3, C4)
- Hepatitis serologies
- Cryoglobulins

**Imaging:**
- Chest X-ray/CT (pulmonary involvement)
- Sinus CT (GPA)
- Consider PET scan for systemic disease

#### Kidney Biopsy
**Indications:**
- All suspected RPGN cases
- Guide treatment decisions
- Determine prognosis
- Rule out other causes

**Findings:**
- Cellular crescents (>50% glomeruli)
- Fibrinoid necrosis
- Breaks in GBM
- IF pattern determines type

### Treatment Protocols

#### General Principles
- Urgent treatment required
- Plasmapheresis for severe cases
- High-dose immunosuppression
- Supportive care (dialysis if needed)

#### Anti-GBM Disease
**Induction:**
- Plasmapheresis daily × 14 days, then every 3 days × 3 weeks
- Methylprednisolone 1 g IV daily × 3 days
- Cyclophosphamide 2 mg/kg/day
- Then oral prednisone taper

**Maintenance:**
- No maintenance therapy needed
- Monitor for relapse (rare)
- Consider transplant after 6-12 months

#### ANCA-Associated Vasculitis
**Induction:**
**CYCLOPS Regimen:**
- Pulse cyclophosphamide 15 mg/kg every 2-3 weeks × 6 doses
- Plus high-dose steroids
- Equivalent efficacy to daily oral

**Rituximab Regimen:**
- Rituximab 375 mg/m² weekly × 4 doses
- Or 1 g × 2 doses (2 weeks apart)
- Non-inferior to cyclophosphamide
- Preferred for relapsing disease

**Avacopan (Tavneos) - Steroid-Sparing Agent (FDA-Approved 2021):**
- **Mechanism:** Selective C5a receptor antagonist (blocks neutrophil activation)
- **Dosing:** 30 mg PO twice daily × 52 weeks (with food)
- **Indication:** Alternative to glucocorticoid taper in ANCA vasculitis
- **ADVOCATE Trial (2021):**
  - Non-inferior to prednisone for remission at 26 weeks (72.3% vs 70.1%)
  - Superior sustained remission at 52 weeks (65.7% vs 54.9%, p=0.007)
  - Greater eGFR improvement and fewer relapses
  - 60% reduction in cumulative steroid dose
- **Use in combination with:**
  - Rituximab (preferred) OR Cyclophosphamide
  - Allows avoidance of high-dose glucocorticoids
- **Advantages:**
  - Avoids long-term steroid toxicity (diabetes, osteoporosis, weight gain)
  - Better renal outcomes than prednisone taper
  - Oral administration (convenient)
- **Monitoring:**
  - LFTs monthly (hepatotoxicity risk - most important AE)
  - Baseline: HBV/HCV serology, TB screening
  - Discontinue if ALT/AST >5× ULN
- **Side Effects:**
  - Hepatotoxicity (monitor LFTs monthly)
  - Upper respiratory infections (15-20%)
  - Nausea, diarrhea, hypertension
- **Contraindications:**
  - Active hepatitis or cirrhosis
  - Severe liver impairment (Child-Pugh C)
  - Strong CYP3A4 inducers (rifampin, phenytoin)
- **Place in Therapy:**
  - Consider FIRST-LINE steroid-sparing option for:
    - Diabetes or steroid intolerance
    - Osteoporosis/fragility fractures
    - Obesity or metabolic syndrome
  - Cost: ~$200,000-250,000/year (prior authorization required)

**Plasmapheresis:**
- Consider for creatinine >5.6 mg/dL
- Pulmonary hemorrhage
- Anti-GBM overlap

**Maintenance:**
- Azathioprine 2 mg/kg/day for 18-24 months
- OR methotrexate 20-25 mg weekly
- OR rituximab every 6 months
- Low-dose steroids (5-10 mg)

#### Immune Complex RPGN
- Treat underlying disease
- High-dose steroids
- Cyclophosphamide or MMF
- Plasmapheresis if anti-GBM overlap

### Prognosis & Outcomes

#### Predictors of Poor Outcome
- Creatinine >5.6 mg/dL at presentation
- Oliguria/anuria
- >50% crescents on biopsy
- Advanced age
- Delay in treatment

#### Recovery Rates
**Anti-GBM Disease:**
- 40% recover renal function if treated early
- <10% if creatinine >5.6 mg/dL
- Pulmonary recovery better than renal

**ANCA Vasculitis:**
- 70-80% achieve remission
- 50% maintain renal function
- Relapse rate 30-50%

**Immune Complex:**
- Variable based on underlying disease
- Better prognosis than anti-GBM
- Dependent on early treatment

## 6. Special Situations & Complications

### Pregnancy and Glomerular Disease

#### Pre-conception Counseling
- Disease quiescence >6 months preferred
- Medication safety review
- Prognosis discussion
- Genetic counseling if indicated

#### Medication Adjustments
**Continue:**
- Prednisone (lowest effective dose)
- Azathioprine
- Calcium channel blockers
- Hydroxychloroquine (lupus)

**Discontinue:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs
- Mycophenolate mofetil
- Cyclophosphamide
- Methotrexate

**Alternatives:**
- Azathioprine instead of MMF
- Low-dose steroids
- Plasma exchange if needed

#### Monitoring
- Monthly renal function
- Blood pressure control
- Proteinuria monitoring
- Fetal growth assessment

### Infection-Related GN

#### Post-Infectious GN
**Typical Pathogens:**
- Streptococcus pyogenes (pharyngitis, skin)
- Staphylococcus aureus
- Hepatitis B/C
- HIV

**Clinical Features:**
- 1-3 weeks after infection
- Nephritic syndrome
- Low complement (C3)
- Usually self-limited

**Treatment:**
- Supportive care
- Treat underlying infection
- Consider steroids if severe
- Monitor for progression

#### Hepatitis-Related GN
**Hepatitis B:**
- Membranous nephropathy pattern
- Polyarteritis nodosa (HBV-PAN)
- Antiviral therapy essential
- Consider interferon, nucleos(t)ide analogs

**Hepatitis C:**
- Membranoproliferative GN pattern
- Cryoglobulinemic GN
- Direct-acting antivirals
- Consider rituximab for cryoglobulinemia

### Monoclonal Gammopathy-Related GN

#### Types of Disease
**AL Amyloidosis:**
- Light chain deposition
- Systemic involvement
- Rapid progression

**Light Chain Deposition Disease:**
- Non-amyloid deposits
- Nodular sclerosis
- Better prognosis than AL

**Immunotactoid Glomerulopathy:**
- Organized deposits
- Associated with lymphoproliferative disorders
- Variable prognosis

**Fibrillary GN:**
- Randomly oriented fibrils
- Congo red negative
- Often progresses to ESRD

#### Diagnostic Approach
- Serum/urine protein electrophoresis
- Free light chain assay
- Bone marrow biopsy
- Kidney biopsy with EM
- Flow cytometry if needed

#### Treatment Strategies
- Treat underlying plasma cell disorder
- Bortezomib-based regimens
- Daratumumab (anti-CD38)
- Consider autologous stem cell transplant
- Supportive renal care

## 7. Treatment Monitoring & Toxicity

### Immunosuppressive Monitoring

#### Corticosteroids
**Efficacy Monitoring:**
- Proteinuria reduction
- Renal function stabilization
- Serologic markers (anti-PLA2R, ANCA)
- Clinical symptoms

**Toxicity Monitoring:**
- Blood glucose, HbA1c
- Blood pressure
- Bone density (DEXA scan)
- Mood changes, cataracts
- Infection surveillance

#### Calcineurin Inhibitors
**Therapeutic Drug Monitoring:**
- Cyclosporine: trough 100-200 ng/mL
- Tacrolimus: trough 5-10 ng/mL
- Draw 12 hours post-dose
- Monitor levels weekly initially

**Toxicity Monitoring:**
- Serum creatinine (nephrotoxicity)
- Blood pressure
- Magnesium, potassium
- Lipid profile
- Neurologic symptoms

#### Mycophenolate Mofetil
**Monitoring:**
- CBC weekly for first month
- Liver function tests
- GI side effects
- Consider level monitoring in transplant

**Toxicity:**
- Bone marrow suppression
- GI intolerance
- Increased infection risk
- Potential teratogenicity

#### Cyclophosphamide
**Monitoring:**
- CBC weekly
- Urinalysis for hemorrhagic cystitis
- Liver function tests
- Pregnancy testing

**Long-term Toxicity:**
- Gonadal toxicity (sperm banking)
- Hemorrhagic cystitis (mesna protection)
- Secondary malignancies
- Pulmonary fibrosis

#### Rituximab
**Monitoring:**
- B-cell counts (CD19/20)
- IgG levels
- Hepatitis B reactivation
- Infusion reactions

**Toxicity:**
- Infusion reactions
- Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (rare)
- Hepatitis B reactivation
- Hypogammaglobulinemia

### Response Assessment

#### Proteinuria Response
**Complete Remission:**
- Proteinuria <0.3 g/day (nephrotic diseases)
- <0.5 g/day (non-nephrotic diseases)
- Stable or improved GFR

**Partial Remission:**
- ≥50% reduction in proteinuria
- Proteinuria <3.5 g/day (if initially nephrotic)
- Stable GFR (<25% decline)

**No Response:**
- <50% proteinuria reduction
- Progressive GFR decline
- Persistent active sediment

#### Time to Response
- Minimal change disease: 2-8 weeks
- Membranous nephropathy: 3-6 months
- FSGS: 3-6 months
- Lupus nephritis: 3-6 months
- ANCA vasculitis: 2-4 months

#### Relapse Monitoring
- Regular proteinuria testing
- Clinical symptom review
- Medication adherence assessment
- Consider biomarker trends

## 8. Emerging Therapies & Future Directions

### Targeted Biologic Agents

#### Complement Inhibitors (2024 Update)

**C5 Inhibitors (Terminal Pathway Blockade):**
**Eculizumab (Soliris):**
- **FDA-approved** for atypical HUS (2011)
- Monoclonal antibody targeting C5
- 900 mg IV weekly × 4, then 1200 mg every 2 weeks
- **Indications:**
  - Atypical HUS (first-line therapy)
  - C3 glomerulopathy (off-label, investigational)
  - Refractory antibody-mediated transplant rejection
- **Requirements:** Meningococcal vaccination (2-4 weeks prior or with prophylactic antibiotics)
- **Monitoring:** Terminal complement activity (CH50)

**Ravulizumab (Ultomiris):**
- **FDA-approved** for atypical HUS (2019)
- Long-acting C5 inhibitor (8-week dosing interval)
- Loading dose based on weight, then maintenance every 8 weeks
- **Advantages:** Less frequent dosing, improved patient compliance
- Equivalent efficacy to eculizumab

**C3 Inhibitors (Alternative Pathway Blockade):**
**Pegcetacoplan (Empaveli):**
- **FDA-approved:**
  - PNH (2021)
  - **C3 glomerulopathy and primary IC-MPGN (July 28, 2025)** - First FDA-approved C3G treatment
- Targeted C3 inhibitor (binds C3 and C3b, prevents C3 activation)
- 1080 mg subcutaneous infusion twice weekly
- **VALIANT Trial (Phase 3):**
  - 68.3% relative proteinuria reduction vs. placebo
  - 71% complete C3c staining clearance on biopsy
  - Stabilized eGFR
- **Indication:** C3G or primary IC-MPGN in patients ≥12 years to reduce proteinuria
- **Monitoring:** C3 levels, proteinuria, eGFR, meningococcal vaccination mandatory

**Factor D Inhibitors:**
**Danicopan:**
- Oral alternative pathway inhibitor
- Alternative pathway blockade at Factor D
- **Under investigation** for C3G, IgA nephropathy, PNH
- Phase 2/3 trials ongoing
- Potential for oral outpatient therapy

**Factor B Inhibitors:**
**Iptacopan (Fabhalta):**
- **FDA-approved:**
  - PNH (December 2023)
  - **IgA nephropathy (August 2024)** - Accelerated approval
- Oral Factor B inhibitor
- 200 mg twice daily
- **APPLAUSE-IgAN Trial:** 44% proteinuria reduction vs. 9% placebo (p<0.0001)
- **October 2025:** Positive final results showing slowing of eGFR decline
- **FDA-approved:**
  - IgA nephropathy (August 2024)
  - **C3 glomerulopathy (2025)** - 35% proteinuria reduction at 6 months
- **Under investigation** for:
  - Atypical HUS (as oral maintenance)
- **Advantages:** Oral administration, dual indication (IgAN + C3G), dual benefit (proteinuria + eGFR)

**Clinical Applications in Glomerular Diseases (2025 UPDATE):**
- **Atypical HUS:** Eculizumab or ravulizumab (standard of care)
- **C3 Glomerulopathy:** **Pegcetacoplan or iptacopan (FDA-APPROVED 2025)** - First-line therapy
- **IgA Nephropathy:** Iptacopan, sparsentan, atrasentan (all FDA-approved)
- **Antibody-Mediated Transplant Rejection:** Eculizumab (off-label)
- **Membranous Nephropathy:** Under investigation for complement-driven disease

#### B-Cell Targeted Therapies
**Anti-CD20 Agents:**
- Rituximab (established)
- Ocrelizumab, ofatumumab
- Obinutuzumab (more potent)

**Anti-CD19 Agents:**
- Inebilizumab
- Targets broader B-cell population
- Potential in refractory disease

#### Plasma Cell Targeted Agents
**Proteasome Inhibitors:**
- Bortezomib (AL amyloidosis)
- Carfilzomib, ixazomib
- Better toxicity profile

**Anti-CD38 Agents:**
- Daratumumab (AL amyloidosis)
- Isatuximab
- Rapid plasma cell depletion

## 9. Clinical Algorithms & Decision Trees

### Nephrotic Syndrome Evaluation

```
Nephrotic Syndrome (Proteinuria >3.5 g/day, Albumin <3.0 g/dL)
        ↓
Initial Evaluation:
- Age, ethnicity, comorbidities
- Medication review
- Infection screen
- Autoimmune serologies
        ↓
Secondary Cause Workup:
- Hepatitis B/C, HIV
- ANA, dsDNA (lupus)
- Serum/urine electrophoresis
- Blood glucose, HbA1c
        ↓
Primary Disease Suspicion → Kidney Biopsy
        ↓
Pathology-Directed Treatment:
- MCD → High-dose steroids
- FSGS → Steroids → CNI/MMF
- MN → Anti-PLA2R testing → Rituximab/Conservative
- Amyloidosis → Type → Disease-specific therapy
```

### RPGN Emergency Management

```
Rapidly Progressive GN (Creatinine rise >0.5 mg/day × 2+ days)
        ↓
Urgent Laboratory Evaluation:
- Anti-GBM antibodies
- ANCA panel (PR3, MPO)
- Complement levels
- Hepatitis serologies
        ↓
Empiric Therapy While Awaiting Results:
- Methylprednisolone 1 g IV daily × 3 days
- Consider plasmapheresis if severe
- Prepare for kidney biopsy
        ↓
Targeted Therapy Based on Results:
- Anti-GBM positive → Plasmapheresis + cyclophosphamide
- ANCA positive → Rituximab or cyclophosphamide
- Immune complex → Treat underlying disease
        ↓
Maintenance Therapy (18-24 months)
```

## 10. Key Take-Home Points

1. **Syndrome Recognition**: Differentiate nephrotic vs nephritic syndromes for initial approach
2. **Age-Specific Etiologies**: Consider age when evaluating nephrotic syndrome causes
3. **Biopsy Indications**: Most glomerular diseases require kidney biopsy for definitive diagnosis
4. **Treatment Individualization**: Tailor therapy to specific pathology, not just syndrome
5. **SGLT2 Inhibitors**: Use across most glomerular diseases for cardio-renal protection
6. **RPGN as Emergency**: Rapid recognition and treatment essential for renal recovery
7. **Toxicity Monitoring**: Regular monitoring essential for all immunosuppressive therapy
8. **Relapse Planning**: Anticipate and monitor for disease relapse after remission
9. **Multidisciplinary Care**: Collaborate with rheumatology, hepatology, oncology as needed
10. **Emerging Therapies**: Stay current with new biologic agents and targeted treatments
