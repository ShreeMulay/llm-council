## Normal Renal Development and Physiology

### Embryology of the Kidney

#### Three Stages of Kidney Development
- **Pronephros**: Transient, regresses by 4 weeks gestation
- **Mesonephros**: Functional until 10-12 weeks, contributes to male genital tract
- **Metanephros**: Permanent kidney, appears 5 weeks gestation

#### Metanephric Development
- **Ureteric Bud**: Arises from mesonephric duct, forms collecting system
- **Metanephric Mesenchyme**: Forms nephrons (glomeruli and tubules)
- **Nephronogenesis**: Completes by 36 weeks gestation
- **Nephron Number**: ~1 million per kidney (variable, determined prenatally)

### Normal Renal Physiology in Children

#### GFR Changes with Age
```
Neonatal GFR:
- Preterm: 0.6-0.8 mL/min/1.73m²
- Term: 2-4 mL/min/1.73m²
- Doubles by 2 weeks
- Adult levels by 2 years

Age-Adjusted eGFR Equations:
- Schwartz Formula (original): eGFR = k × Height(cm)/Serum Cr
- k values: 0.33 (preterm), 0.45 (term infants), 0.55 (children), 0.70 (adolescent males)
- Revised Schwartz: eGFR = 36.5 × Height/Scr (creatinine enzymatic assay)
```

#### Tubular Function Development
- **Concentrating Ability**: Limited in newborns, matures by 6 months
- **Acid-Base Regulation**: Reduced capacity in infants
- **Sodium Handling**: Higher fractional excretion in neonates
- **Glucose Reabsorption**: Limited in premature infants

## Congenital Anomalies of the Kidney and Urinary Tract (CAKUT)

### Epidemiology and Classification
- **Incidence**: 1 in 500 live births
- **Leading Cause**: Of CKD in children (40-50%)
- **Genetic Factors**: Mutations in 50+ genes identified (expanding with whole exome/genome sequencing)
- **Environmental Factors**: Maternal diabetes, medications (ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs), teratogens

### Genetic Basis of CAKUT (2024 Update)

#### Monogenic Causes
```
Major CAKUT Genes (2024 Comprehensive List):

Transcription Factors:
- PAX2: Renal-coloboma syndrome (renal hypoplasia, optic nerve coloboma)
- HNF1B (TCF2): MODY5 diabetes, renal cysts, renal hypoplasia
- EYA1, SIX1, SIX5: Branchio-oto-renal syndrome (BOR)
- SALL1: Townes-Brocks syndrome (anorectal, limb, ear anomalies)

Renal Development Genes:
- RET, GDNF: Ureteric bud development defects
- ROBO2, SLIT2: Vesicoureteral reflux (VUR)
- UPK3A: Renal adysplasia
- BMP4, GREM1: Renal hypoplasia, duplex kidneys

Ciliopathy Genes:
- PKD1, PKD2: Autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease
- PKHD1: Autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease
- NPHP1-20: Nephronophthisis (cystic kidney disease)

Novel Genes (Identified 2020-2024):
- GATA3: HDR syndrome (hypoparathyroidism, deafness, renal dysplasia)
- TRAP1: Mitochondrial dysfunction + CAKUT
- FRAS1, FREM2: Fraser syndrome (renal agenesis, cryptophthalmos)
- TBX18: Congenital ureteropelvic junction obstruction

Genetic Yield:
- Monogenic cause identified: 15-20% of sporadic CAKUT
- Family history present: 30-40% yield
- Syndromic features: 40-50% yield
```

#### Genetic Testing Strategies (2024)
```
Indications for Genetic Testing in CAKUT:
1. Bilateral renal anomalies
2. Family history of CAKUT or CKD
3. Extrarenal manifestations (syndromic features)
4. Isolated severe CAKUT (renal agenesis, severe hypoplasia)
5. Consanguinity

Testing Approaches:

Targeted Gene Panels:
- CAKUT-specific panels: 50-100 genes
- Turnaround: 2-4 weeks
- Cost-effective for typical presentations

Whole Exome Sequencing (WES):
- Comprehensive analysis (>20,000 genes)
- Recommended if panel negative and strong suspicion
- Diagnostic yield: 25-30% in unexplained CAKUT
- Identifies novel candidate genes

Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS):
- Detects copy number variants (CNVs), structural variants
- Useful for complex/syndromic cases
- Becoming more accessible (2024: decreasing costs)

Chromosomal Microarray (CMA):
- First-line if developmental delay or dysmorphic features
- Detects CNVs, aneuploidies
- Examples: 17q12 deletion (HNF1B), 22q11.2 deletion (DiGeorge)

AI-Powered Variant Interpretation:
- AlphaMissense (Google DeepMind, 2023): Predicts pathogenicity
- Integrated into clinical genetic testing platforms
- Improves VUS (variant of uncertain significance) classification
```

#### Prenatal Diagnosis and Intervention (2024)

```
Prenatal Screening:
- Routine prenatal ultrasound: Detects 80-90% of severe CAKUT
- Optimal timing: 18-22 weeks gestation (anatomic survey)
- Earlier detection: 12-14 weeks (some severe cases)

Ultrasound Findings Suggestive of CAKUT:
- Oligohydramnios (amniotic fluid index <5 cm)
- Bilateral renal agenesis → Potter sequence (lethal)
- Unilateral renal agenesis (favorable if contralateral normal)
- Hydronephrosis (AP diameter >4 mm second trimester, >7 mm third trimester)
- Renal cysts, echogenic kidneys
- Bladder distention, dilated posterior urethra (PUV)

Prenatal Counseling:
- Multidisciplinary team: MFM, pediatric nephrologist, urologist, geneticist
- Prognosis assessment based on:
  - Bilateral vs unilateral involvement
  - Amniotic fluid volume (oligohydramnios = poor prognosis)
  - Renal parenchymal appearance
  - Presence of extrarenal anomalies

Fetal MRI:
- Indicated if ultrasound findings unclear or complex
- Better soft tissue resolution than ultrasound
- Assess for associated CNS, GI anomalies

Prenatal Genetic Testing (If Indicated):
- Amniocentesis: 15-20 weeks (chromosomal microarray, WES)
- Cell-free fetal DNA: Non-invasive screening for aneuploidies
- Indications: Severe/bilateral CAKUT, extrarenal anomalies, family history

Fetal Intervention (Very Limited Indications):
- Vesicoamniotic shunt: Severe bladder outlet obstruction (PUV)
  - Goal: Relieve obstruction, preserve amniotic fluid, prevent pulmonary hypoplasia
  - Criteria: Isolated PUV, favorable urine biochemistry, gestational age <32 weeks
  - Outcomes: Variable, 30-50% survival, many still develop ESRD
- Controversy: Limited evidence, risk of preterm labor, not widely performed
```

### Specific Anomalies

#### Renal Agenesis and Hypoplasia
- **Bilateral Renal Agenesis**: Lethal (Potter sequence)
- **Unilateral Renal Agenesis**: 
  - Incidence: 1 in 1000 births
  - Compensatory hypertrophy of contralateral kidney
  - Risk of hypertension, proteinuria in adulthood
- **Renal Hypoplasia**: Small kidney with normal architecture
  - Diagnosis: Kidney length <2 SD below mean for age
  - Management: Monitor for hypertension and proteinuria

#### Renal Dysplasia and Multicystic Dysplastic Kidney (MCDK)
- **MCDK Characteristics**:
  - Non-functional cystic mass
  - No renal pelvis or collecting system
  - May involute over time (50% by age 5)
- **Management**:
  - Observation unless symptomatic
  - Monitor contralateral kidney for hypertrophy
  - Surgical removal only if complications (pain, hypertension, tumor)

#### Obstructive Uropathies
```
Ureteropelvic Junction (UPJ) Obstruction:
- Most common cause of neonatal hydronephrosis
- Prenatal ultrasound: Pelviectasis >10 mm
- Postnatal evaluation: Ultrasound + VCUG + diuretic renogram
- Indications for surgery: >40% differential function loss, symptoms

Posterior Urethral Valves (PUV):
- Male-only condition (1:8,000-12,500 male births)
- Prenatal: Bilateral hydronephrosis, oligohydramnios
- Postnatal: Poor urinary stream, palpable bladder
- Management: Primary valve ablation, vesicostomy if needed
- Long-term: 30% progress to ESRD
```

#### Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR)
- **Grading System** (International Reflux Study):
  - Grade I: Reflux to ureter only
  - Grade II: Reflux to renal pelvis without dilation
  - Grade III: Mild to moderate dilation
  - Grade IV: Moderate dilation with blunted fornices
  - Grade V: Severe dilation with loss of papillary impressions
- **Management**:
  - Grade I-III: Prophylactic antibiotics, observation
  - Grade IV-V: Surgical reimplantation or endoscopic injection
  - Goal: Prevent renal scarring and hypertension

## Neonatal Acute Kidney Injury

### Definition and Staging

#### Neonatal AKI Definition (KDIGO Neonatal - 2024 Updated)
```
Challenges in Neonatal AKI Diagnosis:
- Serum creatinine reflects maternal values for first 48-72 hours
- Baseline Cr varies by gestational age and postnatal age
- Urine output may be difficult to measure accurately

Modified KDIGO Criteria for Neonates (2024):

Stage 0 (At Risk):
- No AKI but risk factors present (asphyxia, sepsis, nephrotoxins)
- Normal creatinine and urine output

Stage 1:
- Serum creatinine rise ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours
OR
- Serum creatinine 1.5-1.9 times baseline (or presumed baseline)
OR
- Urine output <1.0 mL/kg/hour for 6-12 hours (term infants)
OR
- Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hour for 6-12 hours (preterm infants)

Stage 2:
- Serum creatinine 2-2.9 times baseline
OR
- Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hour for >12 hours

Stage 3:
- Serum creatinine ≥3 times baseline
OR
- Serum creatinine ≥2.5 mg/dL (term) or ≥1.8 mg/dL (preterm)
OR
- Urine output <0.3 mL/kg/hour for ≥24 hours
OR
- Anuria for ≥12 hours
OR
- Need for dialysis

Gestational Age-Specific Considerations:
- Extremely preterm (<28 weeks): Lower Cr thresholds
- Very preterm (28-32 weeks): Adjust baseline Cr expectations
- Late preterm/term (>32 weeks): Use standard criteria
```

#### Novel Biomarkers for Neonatal AKI (2024)

```
Traditional Limitations:
- Serum creatinine: Delayed rise (24-48 hours after injury)
- Affected by maternal Cr, muscle mass, fluid status

Emerging Biomarkers (2023-2024 Data):

Urinary Biomarkers (NICU Validation):

NGAL (Neutrophil Gelatinase-Associated Lipocalin):
- Rises within 2-6 hours of AKI
- Cutoff: >100 ng/mL (term infants)
- Sensitivity: 70-80% for AKI prediction
- Limitations: Elevated with sepsis, inflammation

Cystatin C (Serum):
- Less affected by muscle mass than creatinine
- Normal range: 1.0-1.5 mg/L (term infants)
- Useful in first week of life when Cr unreliable
- FDA-cleared for GFR estimation (2014)

KIM-1 (Kidney Injury Molecule-1):
- Marker of tubular injury
- Rises within 12-24 hours
- Correlates with severity of AKI
- Not yet FDA-approved for clinical use

L-FABP (Liver-type Fatty Acid Binding Protein):
- Early tubular injury marker
- Pediatric data: Limited but promising
- Available in some countries (Japan), not FDA-approved

Urinary [TIMP-2] × [IGFBP7] (NephroCheck):
- FDA-cleared for adult AKI risk stratification (2014)
- Neonatal Data (2023-2024): Small studies ongoing
- Identifies cell-cycle arrest (early injury)
- Challenges: Reference ranges not established in neonates

Clinical Use (2024):
- Most biomarkers remain investigational in neonates
- Cystatin C: Most widely available for clinical use
- NGAL: Available at some academic centers
- Combination of biomarkers may improve prediction
```

### Etiology and Risk Factors

#### Prerenal Causes
- **Perinatal Asphyxia**: 70% of neonatal AKI cases
- **Hypovolemia**: Dehydration, hemorrhage
- **Patent Ductus Arteriosus**: Reduced renal perfusion
- **Sepsis**: Systemic vasodilation, hypotension

#### Intrinsic Causes
- **Acute Tubular Necrosis**: Ischemia, nephrotoxins
- **Renal Vascular Thrombosis**: Umbilical artery catheters
- **Acute Interstitial Nephritis**: Drugs, infections

#### Postrenal Causes
- **Posterior Urethral Valves**: Obstructive uropathy
- **Ureteral Obstruction**: Stones, clots
- **Neurogenic Bladder**: Spinal cord abnormalities

### Management Strategies

#### Fluid Management
- **Initial Resuscitation**: 10-20 mL/kg isotonic fluid bolus
- **Maintenance Fluids**: 
  - Term infants: 60-80 mL/kg/day
  - Preterm infants: 100-150 mL/kg/day
- **Fluid Restriction**: In oliguric phase to prevent fluid overload

#### Electrolyte Management
- **Hyperkalemia**: 
  - Calcium gluconate 10% 0.5 mL/kg IV
  - Insulin 0.1 U/kg + glucose 0.5 g/kg
  - Sodium bicarbonate 1-2 mEq/kg IV
  - Consider dialysis if K+ >6.5 mEq/L or ECG changes
- **Hyponatremia**: 
  - Correct slowly (<8 mEq/L in 24 hours)
  - Hypertonic saline only if symptomatic

#### Renal Replacement Therapy
- **Peritoneal Dialysis**: Preferred in neonates
  - Catheter: Tenckhoff or rigid catheter
  - Volume: 10-20 mL/kg dwell
  - Exchange duration: 30-60 minutes
- **CRRT**: 
  - Requires expertise and specialized equipment
  - Circuit priming with blood or albumin
  - Anticoagulation: Regional citrate preferred

## Glomerular Diseases in Children

### Minimal Change Disease (MCD)

#### Epidemiology and Pathogenesis
- **Age Peak**: 2-6 years
- **Gender**: Male predominance (2:1)
- **Association**: Atopy, allergies, recent infections
- **Pathogenesis**: T-cell dysfunction, podocyte injury

#### Clinical Features
- **Edema**: Periorbital, peripheral, anasarca
- **Proteinuria**: Nephrotic range (>3.5 g/day or >40 mg/m²/hour)
- **Hypoalbuminemia**: <2.5 g/dL
- **Hyperlipidemia**: Elevated cholesterol and triglycerides

#### Management (2024 Updated Protocols)

**Initial Treatment:**
```
Prednisone (Standard Protocol - KDIGO 2021):
- Initial: 60 mg/m²/day or 2 mg/kg/day (max 60 mg) for 4-6 weeks
- Taper: 40 mg/m² every other day for 2-5 months
- Total duration: 12-16 weeks minimum
- Longer initial course (12 weeks vs 8 weeks) → reduced relapses

Edema Control:
- Salt restriction (<2 g/day)
- Furosemide: 1-2 mg/kg/dose BID-TID
- Albumin infusion: 1 g/kg if severe hypovolemia
- Avoid diuretics during acute infection (risk of AKI)

Supportive Care:
- Pneumococcal and varicella vaccination (if not immunized)
- Prophylactic antibiotics if IgG <400 mg/dL
- Vitamin D supplementation (common deficiency)
- VTE prophylaxis if albumin <2 g/dL + additional risk factors
```

**Frequently Relapsing Nephrotic Syndrome (FRNS):**
```
Definition:
- ≥2 relapses within 6 months of initial response
- ≥4 relapses in any 12-month period

Steroid-Sparing Agents (2024 Options):

Rituximab (PREFERRED FIRST-LINE):
- Dosing:
  - Option 1: 375 mg/m² weekly × 1-4 doses
  - Option 2: 750 mg/m² × 2 doses (2 weeks apart)
  - Option 3: 1000 mg/m² × 2 doses (2 weeks apart) - for adolescents
- Response rate: 60-70% maintain remission at 1 year
- Monitor B-cell counts (CD19+)
- Repeat dosing when B cells recover or relapse occurs
- Evidence: Largest pediatric database 2024 shows sustained benefit
- Side effects: Infusion reactions, infections (monitor IgG levels)

Cyclophosphamide:
- Dosing: 2-2.5 mg/kg/day PO for 8-12 weeks (cumulative 150-200 mg/kg)
- Alternative: 500-750 mg/m² IV monthly × 6 months
- Response rate: 60-70%
- Side effects: Gonadal toxicity, hemorrhagic cystitis, malignancy risk
- Less commonly used now (rituximab preferred)

Calcineurin Inhibitors:
- Cyclosporine: 4-5 mg/kg/day divided BID (target trough 80-150 ng/mL)
- Tacrolimus: 0.05-0.1 mg/kg/day divided BID (target trough 5-7 ng/mL)
- Response rate: 70-80% while on therapy
- Relapse common upon discontinuation (require prolonged therapy)
- Side effects: Nephrotoxicity, hypertension, hirsutism, gingival hyperplasia

Levamisole (Where Available):
- Dosing: 2.5 mg/kg on alternate days
- Response rate: 40-50% reduction in relapse rate
- Safe, well-tolerated
- Limited availability in some countries

Mycophenolate Mofetil:
- Dosing: 600 mg/m² BID (or 25-36 mg/kg/day divided BID)
- Less effective than CNI or rituximab
- Consider as adjunct therapy
```

**Steroid-Dependent Nephrotic Syndrome (SDNS):**
```
Definition:
- Relapses during steroid taper or within 14 days of stopping steroids

Treatment Strategy:
- First-line: Rituximab (same dosing as FRNS)
- Alternative: Calcineurin inhibitors (CNI)
- Combination: CNI + low-dose prednisone
- Newer option: Obinutuzumab (investigational, see below)
```

**Steroid-Resistant Nephrotic Syndrome (SRNS):**
```
Definition:
- Persistent proteinuria after 4-6 weeks of standard prednisone therapy

Mandatory Workup:
- Kidney biopsy: Distinguish MCD from FSGS, other diagnoses
- Genetic testing: Panel including NPHS1, NPHS2, WT1, TRPC6, INF2, others
  - Yield: 20-30% in sporadic SRNS, >70% in familial SRNS
  - Important for prognosis and treatment decisions

Treatment (Based on Histology and Genetics):

Non-Genetic SRNS:
- Calcineurin Inhibitors: Trial for 6 months
  - Cyclosporine: 4-6 mg/kg/day (higher than SDNS)
  - Tacrolimus: 0.1-0.15 mg/kg/day
  - Response rate: 50-70% if negative genetic testing
- ACE Inhibitors/ARBs: Enalapril 0.2-0.6 mg/kg/day OR lisinopril
- Rituximab: Limited data, may try if CNI fails
- Sparsentan: See novel therapies below

Genetic SRNS:
- Immunosuppression: Generally NOT effective
- Conservative management:
  - ACE inhibitors/ARBs for antiproteinuric effect
  - Blood pressure control
  - Delay progression to ESRD
- Early transplant planning
- Recurrence risk after transplant varies by mutation:
  - NPHS1 (nephrin): Low recurrence risk
  - NPHS2 (podocin): Very low recurrence risk
  - WT1, TRPC6, INF2: Variable risk
```

**Novel Therapies for Pediatric Nephrotic Syndrome (2023-2024):**

```
Sparsentan (Filspari):
- FDA approved February 2023 for IgA nephropathy (adults)
- Dual endothelin/angiotensin receptor antagonist
- **Pediatric Data (2024):**
  - Phase 2 trial in children with FSGS ongoing
  - Preliminary data: 40-50% reduction in proteinuria
  - Well-tolerated in adolescents
- **Dosing (investigational in children):**
  - Weight-based dosing under study
  - Adult dose: 200-400 mg daily
- **Expected pediatric approval:** 2025-2026 if trials positive
- **Mechanism:** Superior antiproteinuric effect vs ACEi/ARB alone

Obinutuzumab (Gazyva):
- Anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody (type II, more potent than rituximab)
- **Pediatric Studies (2023-2024):**
  - Small case series in rituximab-resistant SDNS
  - Dosing: 1000 mg/1.73m² × 1-2 doses
  - Response rate: 60-70% in rituximab failures
  - Longer B-cell depletion than rituximab
- **Status:** Investigational, not FDA-approved for nephrotic syndrome
- **Consider for:** Rituximab-resistant or rituximab-allergic patients

Ofatumumab (Kesimpta):
- Anti-CD20 subcutaneous formulation
- **Advantages:** SC administration (easier than IV rituximab)
- **Pediatric use:** Very limited data, case reports only
- **Potential role:** Alternative for needle-phobic patients

Voclosporin:
- Novel calcineurin inhibitor (less nephrotoxic than cyclosporine)
- FDA approved for lupus nephritis (2021)
- **Pediatric data:** None yet
- **Future potential:** SRNS, SDNS if proven safe in children

Combination Therapy:
- Rituximab + CNI: Some centers use for difficult SDNS
- Rituximab + Mycophenolate: Investigational
- Sparsentan + rituximab: Future trials planned
```

**Treatment Algorithm Summary (2024):**
```
Initial Episode:
→ Prednisone 4-6 weeks full dose, then taper 2-5 months

Infrequent Relapses (<2 in 6 months):
→ Prednisone for each relapse

Frequently Relapsing or Steroid-Dependent:
→ Rituximab (first-line)
→ If rituximab fails: CNI or repeat rituximab
→ If rituximab-resistant: Consider obinutuzumab

Steroid-Resistant:
→ Kidney biopsy + genetic testing
→ If genetic: Conservative management, no immunosuppression
→ If non-genetic: CNI trial × 6 months
→ If CNI fails: Sparsentan (when approved) or investigational therapies
```

### Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)

#### Primary vs Secondary FSGS
- **Primary FSGS**: Idiopathic, often presents with nephrotic syndrome
- **Secondary FSGS**: 
  - Adaptive: Obesity, reflux nephropathy, congenital anomalies
  - Genetic: NPHS1, NPHS2, ACTN4 mutations
  - Viral: HIV, parvovirus B19

#### Treatment Approach
- **Primary FSGS**: 
  - High-dose steroids (prolonged course)
  - Calcineurin inhibitors (first-line steroid-sparing)
  - ACE inhibitors for proteinuria control
- **Genetic FSGS**: 
  - Poor response to immunosuppression
  - Focus on conservative management
  - Early transplant planning

### IgA Nephropathy and Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP)

#### IgA Nephropathy in Children
- **Presentation**: Asymptomatic hematuria, gross hematuria with infections
- **Pathology**: Mesangial IgA deposition on immunofluorescence
- **Prognosis**: Generally better than adults, 20% progress to CKD

#### Henoch-Schönlein Purpura
- **Age**: 4-7 years peak
- **Clinical Tetrad**: 
  - Palpable purpura (lower extremities)
  - Arthralgia/arthritis
  - Abdominal pain
  - Renal involvement (hematuria/proteinuria)
- **Renal Prognosis**: 
  - 1-3% develop ESRD
  - Risk factors: >50% crescents, nephrotic-range proteinuria

#### Management
```
Mild Disease (isolated hematuria):
- Observation, blood pressure control
- ACE inhibitors if proteinuria >500 mg/day

Moderate-Severe Disease:
- Prednisone: 1-2 mg/kg/day (taper over 6-12 months)
- ACE inhibitors: For proteinuria reduction
- Mycophenolate mofetil: Alternative to steroids

Severe Crescentic GN:
- Pulse methylprednisolone: 30 mg/kg × 3 days
- Cyclophosphamide: 500-750 mg/m² monthly
- Plasmapheresis: Consider for rapidly progressive disease
```

## Tubular Disorders in Children

### Renal Tubular Acidosis (RTA)

#### Distal (Type 1) RTA
- **Pathogenesis**: Impaired distal acidification
- **Laboratory**: Hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis, inappropriately high urine pH (>5.5)
- **Causes**: Primary (genetic), secondary (drugs, autoimmune)
- **Management**: 
  - Alkali therapy: Shohl solution 1-2 mEq/kg/day divided
  - Potassium citrate if hypokalemia
  - Monitor growth and nephrocalcinosis

#### Proximal (Type 2) RTA
- **Pathogenesis**: Impaired bicarbonate reabsorption
- **Association**: Fanconi syndrome, cystinosis
- **Management**: Large alkali doses (5-15 mEq/kg/day)
- **Prognosis**: Often improves with age

#### Type 4 RTA (Hyperkalemic)
- **Pathogenesis**: Aldosterone resistance or deficiency
- **Causes**: Obstructive uropathy, renal dysplasia, medications
- **Management**: 
  - Fludrocortisone: 0.05-0.2 mg/day
  - Low-potassium diet
  - Loop diuretics if needed

### Fanconi Syndrome
- **Definition**: Generalized proximal tubular dysfunction
- **Features**: 
  - Glycosuria (normal blood glucose)
  - Phosphaturia → rickets
  - Aminoaciduria
  - Bicarbonaturia → metabolic acidosis
  - Sodium and potassium wasting
- **Causes**: 
  - Genetic: Cystinosis, galactosemia, tyrosinemia
  - Acquired: Ifosfamide, heavy metals, multiple myeloma
- **Management**: 
  - Replace losses: Phosphate, bicarbonate, vitamin D
  - Treat underlying cause
  - Monitor growth and bone health

## Hypertension in Children

### Blood Pressure Classification (2024 AAP Clinical Practice Guideline)

#### Updated BP Classification (AAP 2024 - Reaffirmed Guidelines)
```
Children Age 1-12 Years (Use BP Tables):
- Normal BP: <90th percentile for age, sex, height
- Elevated BP: ≥90th to <95th percentile OR 120/80 to <95th percentile (whichever is lower)
- Stage 1 Hypertension: ≥95th percentile to <95th percentile + 12 mmHg OR 130/80 to 139/89 mmHg
- Stage 2 Hypertension: ≥95th percentile + 12 mmHg OR ≥140/90 mmHg

Adolescents Age ≥13 Years (Simplified Criteria - Use Adult Values):
- Normal BP: <120/<80 mmHg
- Elevated BP: 120-129/<80 mmHg
- Stage 1 Hypertension: 130-139/80-89 mmHg
- Stage 2 Hypertension: ≥140/90 mmHg

Measurement Technique (CRITICAL):
- Proper cuff size: Bladder width ≥40% of arm circumference
- Seated, back supported, feet flat, arm at heart level
- Quiet environment, 3-5 minutes rest before measurement
- Confirm elevated BP on 3 separate occasions
- Auscultatory method preferred (oscillometric for screening only)
```

#### Ambulatory Blood Pressure Monitoring (ABPM) - 2024 Recommendations
```
Indications for ABPM:
- Confirm diagnosis of hypertension (white coat vs sustained)
- Assess nocturnal BP pattern (dipping status)
- Evaluate treatment efficacy
- Suspected white coat hypertension
- CKD, diabetes, or transplant patients

ABPM Interpretation:
- Awake hypertension: Mean awake BP ≥95th percentile
- Sleep hypertension: Mean sleep BP ≥95th percentile
- Nocturnal dipping: Expected ≥10% decrease in sleep BP
- Non-dipping: <10% decrease (associated with target organ damage)
- Load: % readings ≥95th percentile (>25% abnormal)

Clinical Significance:
- ABPM superior to office BP for predicting LVH
- Non-dipping associated with CKD progression
- Masked hypertension detected in 10% of "normal" office BP
```

### Etiology by Age

#### Infants (<1 year)
- **Renal Artery Thrombosis**: Umbilical artery catheters
- **Renal Dysplasia**: Congenital anomalies
- **Coarctation of Aorta**: Cardiovascular cause

#### Children (1-12 years)
- **Renal Parenchymal Disease**: 60-70% of cases
- **Renovascular Hypertension**: Fibromuscular dysplasia
- **Endocrine Causes**: Hyperthyroidism, Cushing syndrome

#### Adolescents (>12 years)
- **Primary Hypertension**: Increasing prevalence with obesity
- **Secondary Causes**: Still rule out renal disease
- **Lifestyle Factors**: Obesity, sleep apnea, substance use

### Evaluation and Management

#### Diagnostic Workup
```
Initial Evaluation:
- Confirm elevated BP on 3 separate occasions
- History: Symptoms, medications, family history
- Physical Examination: Growth parameters, cardiac exam
- Basic Labs: CBC, electrolytes, BUN/Cr, urinalysis

Secondary Evaluation (if indicated):
- Renal ultrasound with Doppler
- Echocardiogram (LV mass assessment)
- Hormone levels (renin, aldosterone, thyroid)
- Ambulatory BP monitoring
```

#### Treatment Strategies
- **Non-pharmacologic**: 
  - Weight management if overweight/obese
  - DASH diet, reduced sodium intake
  - Regular physical activity
  - Limit screen time
- **Pharmacologic**:
  - ACE inhibitors/ARBs: First-line for renal disease
  - Calcium channel blockers: Good first-line agents
  - Diuretics: Thiazides for most, loop diuretics if CKD
  - Beta-blockers: If coexisting heart disease

## Pediatric Dialysis

### Initiation Criteria

#### Indications for Dialysis
- **Fluid Overload**: Refractory to diuretics, pulmonary edema
- **Electrolyte Emergencies**: Hyperkalemia, severe acidosis
- **Uremic Complications**: Pericarditis, encephalopathy, intractable nausea
- **Growth Failure**: Inability to maintain adequate nutrition
- **Toxin Removal**: Certain poisonings, inborn errors of metabolism

#### Timing Considerations
- **Early vs Late**: Earlier initiation may improve growth and nutrition
- **GFR Threshold**: Generally <10-15 mL/min/1.73m²
- **Clinical Context**: Individualized based on symptoms and growth

### Peritoneal Dialysis in Children

#### Advantages
- **Home-Based**: Less disruption to school and activities
- **Gentle**: Hemodynamic stability
- **Nutrition**: Better protein preservation
- **Technical**: No need for vascular access

#### Prescription Considerations
```
Infants and Young Children:
- Fill Volume: 10-20 mL/kg (800-1200 mL/m²)
- Exchange Duration: 1-2 hours
- Modality: APD (automated) preferred
- Adequacy Target: Kt/V >1.8 weekly

Adolescents:
- Fill Volume: 20-30 mL/kg (1000-1400 mL/m²)
- May consider CAPD if compliant
- Transition planning to adult care
```

#### Complications
- **Peritonitis**: Most common complication
- **Catheter Problems**: Migration, obstruction, leakage
- **Hernias**: Umbilical, inguinal
- **Metabolic**: Hyperglycemia, hyperlipidemia

### Hemodialysis in Children

#### Indications
- **PD Failure**: Inadequate clearance, peritonitis
- **Rapid Growth**: Adolescents with high catabolic needs
- **Bridge to Transplant**: Short-term dialysis
- **Contraindications to PD**: Abdominal wall defects

#### Technical Considerations
- **Vascular Access**: 
  - Catheters: Temporary for initiation
  - AV Fistulas: Preferred for long-term (create early)
  - Grafts: When vessels unsuitable for fistula
- **Dialyzers**: Smaller surface area (0.5-1.2 m²)
- **Blood Flow**: 3-8 mL/kg/min (based on size)
- **Dialysate Flow**: 300-500 mL/min

## Pediatric Kidney Transplantation

### Pre-Transplant Evaluation

#### Medical Evaluation
- **Growth Assessment**: Height, weight, growth velocity
- **Cardiovascular**: Echocardiogram, blood pressure control
- **Infectious Disease**: CMV, EBV, HIV, hepatitis screening
- **Immunologic**: HLA typing, PRA, crossmatch
- **Urologic**: Bladder function, lower urinary tract evaluation

#### Psychosocial Evaluation
- **Family Support**: Caregiver availability and capability
- **Compliance Potential**: History of medical adherence
- **Educational Impact**: School attendance, support needs
- **Financial Considerations**: Insurance coverage, transportation

### Donor Types and Outcomes

#### Living Donor Transplantation
- **Advantages**: 
  - Better graft survival (90% at 5 years)
  - Shorter wait times
  - Planned surgery, better preparation
- **Donor Evaluation**: 
  - ABO compatibility, HLA matching
  - GFR >80 mL/min/1.73m²
  - No significant medical comorbidities

#### Deceased Donor Transplantation
- **Pediatric Donors**: Best outcomes for pediatric recipients
- **Adult Donors**: May use split kidneys or en bloc
- **Allocation**: Prioritized for pediatric patients

### Immunosuppression in Children

#### Induction Therapy
- **Lymphocyte-Depleting Agents**: 
  - Thymoglobulin: 1.5 mg/kg/day × 4-7 days
  - Alemtuzumab: 0.1-0.2 mg/kg single dose
- **Non-Depleting Agents**: 
  - Basiliximab: 10 mg/m² on day 0 and day 4

#### Maintenance Therapy
```
Triple Therapy Standard:
- Calcineurin Inhibitor:
  * Tacrolimus: Target trough 8-12 ng/mL (first year)
  * Cyclosporine: Target trough 150-250 ng/mL
- Antimetabolite:
  * Mycophenolate mofetil: 600 mg/m² twice daily
  * Azathioprine: 2 mg/kg/day (alternative)
- Steroids:
  * Prednisone: Taper to 5-10 mg alternate day by 6 months
  * Some centers: Steroid avoidance protocols

Growth Considerations:
- Minimize steroid exposure
- Consider early steroid withdrawal
- Monitor growth velocity and bone age
```

### Post-Transplant Complications

#### Acute Rejection (2024 Updated Management)

**Incidence and Risk Factors:**
- **Overall Incidence**: 10-15% in first year (declining with modern immunosuppression)
- **Risk Factors**:
  - HLA mismatch, high PRA, prior transplant
  - Non-adherence (highest risk in adolescents)
  - Inadequate immunosuppression levels
  - DSA (donor-specific antibodies)

**Clinical Presentation:**
- Acute Cellular Rejection (ACR): Rising creatinine, decreased urine output
- Antibody-Mediated Rejection (AMR): May be subclinical or severe graft dysfunction
- Chronic Active AMR: Progressive decline in function

**Diagnosis (2024 Protocols):**
```
Kidney Biopsy (Gold Standard):
- Indication: Rising Cr >20-25% from baseline
- Banff Classification 2019 (updated 2022)
- Immunohistochemistry: C4d staining for AMR
- Molecular diagnostics: Gene expression profiles

Novel Non-Invasive Surveillance:

Prospera (Donor-Derived Cell-Free DNA, dd-cfDNA):
- FDA-cleared test (CareDx, 2020)
- Detects subclinical rejection before Cr rise
- Threshold: >1% dd-cfDNA suggests active rejection
- Sensitivity: 60-90% for ACR, 85-95% for AMR
- Pediatric Data (2023-2024):
  - Validated in children >1 year
  - Useful for surveillance in high-risk patients
  - May reduce need for protocol biopsies
- Recommended frequency:
  - Monthly for first 6 months
  - Quarterly for 6-12 months
  - Every 6 months thereafter
- Limitations: Cannot distinguish ACR vs AMR, false positives with infection/BK virus

RenaSight (AI-Powered Digital Pathology):
- FDA-cleared AI tool (2023)
- Analyzes biopsy histology using machine learning
- Assists in Banff classification and scoring
- Benefits:
  - Standardizes pathology interpretation
  - Reduces inter-observer variability
  - Faster turnaround time for complex cases
- Pediatric use: Growing adoption in transplant centers
- Status: Adjunct to expert pathologist review

DSA Monitoring:
- Anti-HLA antibody testing every 3-6 months
- De novo DSA: Associated with chronic AMR, graft loss
- Intervene early if DSA develops (increase immunosuppression, consider IVIG/rituximab)
```

**Treatment Protocols (2024):**
```
Acute Cellular Rejection (Banff ≥ 1A):
- Pulse Steroids: Methylprednisolone 10 mg/kg IV daily × 3 days
- Optimize maintenance immunosuppression
- Follow-up biopsy in 2-4 weeks to confirm response

Steroid-Resistant ACR:
- Anti-thymocyte globulin (ATG): 1.5 mg/kg/day × 5-7 days
- Alemtuzumab: 0.1-0.2 mg/kg single dose (alternative)
- Adjust maintenance therapy

Antibody-Mediated Rejection:
- Plasmapheresis: 5-7 sessions (remove circulating antibodies)
- IVIG: 2 g/kg after each plasmapheresis session
- Rituximab: 375 mg/m² × 1-2 doses (deplete B cells)
- Consider proteasome inhibitor (bortezomib, carfilzomib) for resistant cases
- Eculizumab (investigational): For severe AMR, C5 inhibition

Chronic Active AMR (2024 Emerging Therapies):
- IVIG + rituximab
- Clazakizumab (anti-IL-6): Phase 3 trials showing promise (2023 data)
- Imlifidase (IdeS): IgG-cleaving enzyme, investigational
- Tocilizumab: IL-6 receptor antagonist, limited pediatric data
```

#### Infections (2024 Updated Protocols)

**CMV Disease:**
```
Risk Stratification:
- High Risk: D+/R- (donor positive, recipient negative)
- Intermediate Risk: R+ (recipient seropositive)
- Low Risk: D-/R- (both seronegative)

Prevention (2024 Guidelines):
- High Risk: Valganciclovir 6 months (some extend to 12 months)
- Intermediate Risk: Valganciclovir 3-6 months
- Dosing: Based on eGFR and body surface area
- Monitoring: CMV PCR monthly during prophylaxis, then every 3 months

Treatment:
- Reduce immunosuppression (decrease MMF, lower CNI target)
- Ganciclovir: 5 mg/kg IV BID × 2-3 weeks (if severe)
- Valganciclovir: 900 mg/1.73m² PO BID × 3-6 weeks (mild-moderate)
- Maribavir (Livtencity): FDA-approved 2021 for refractory CMV
  - Pediatric dosing: Under study
  - Adult dose: 400 mg PO BID
  - Use for ganciclovir-resistant CMV

Letermovir (Prevymis):
- FDA-approved for CMV prophylaxis in HSCT (2017)
- Off-label use in kidney transplant: Emerging data
- Advantage: No myelosuppression (unlike ganciclovir)
- Pediatric data: Limited, investigational
```

**EBV and PTLD:**
```
Prevention:
- Monitor EBV PCR monthly first 6 months, then every 3 months
- Threshold: >1000 copies/mL or rising viral load
- Action: Reduce immunosuppression (decrease MMF/CNI by 25-50%)

Risk Factors for PTLD:
- Primary EBV infection post-transplant (highest risk)
- Young age (<5 years)
- Intensive immunosuppression (thymoglobulin induction)
- CMV mismatch (D+/R-)

Treatment (2024):
- Reduce immunosuppression (RI): First-line
- Rituximab: 375 mg/m² weekly × 4 doses
- Chemotherapy: For resistant PTLD (CHOP regimen)
- Surgery/radiation: For localized disease
- Novel therapies:
  - Blinatumomab (BiTE antibody): Investigational for CD19+ PTLD
  - CAR T-cell therapy: Case reports in refractory PTLD
```

**BK Virus Nephropathy (BKVN):**
```
Screening (2024 Recommendations):
- BK PCR (plasma): Monthly for first 6 months, then every 3 months
- BK viruria: Less specific, plasma preferred
- Threshold: Plasma BK >10,000 copies/mL → high risk BKVN

Prevention:
- Avoid over-immunosuppression
- Monitor tacrolimus/cyclosporine levels closely

Treatment:
- NO specific antiviral therapy available
- Reduce immunosuppression:
  - Decrease MMF by 50% or discontinue
  - Lower CNI target by 30-50%
  - Continue steroids
- Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin): Possible benefit, not proven
- Leflunomide: Limited data, use if reduction alone insufficient
- IVIG: Investigational, case reports only

Monitoring:
- Repeat BK PCR every 2-4 weeks
- Biopsy if Cr rising (confirm BKVN vs rejection)
- Goal: BK viral load <1000 copies/mL
```

**COVID-19 in Pediatric Transplant Recipients (2024):**
```
Vaccination:
- mRNA vaccines (Pfizer, Moderna): Recommended ≥6 months post-transplant
- Primary series: 3 doses (immunocompromised schedule)
- Boosters: Per CDC guidance (6-12 months after primary series)
- Antibody response: Reduced (30-50% vs healthy children)

Treatment (If Infected):
- Nirmatrelvir/Ritonavir (Paxlovid): FDA-approved age ≥12 years
  - Drug interactions: Adjust tacrolimus/cyclosporine (hold or reduce dose)
  - Dosing: Weight-based
- Remdesivir: All ages, IV formulation
- Molnupiravir: Alternative if Paxlovid contraindicated
- Monoclonal antibodies: Limited use (resistance to most formulations)

Immunosuppression Management:
- Continue CNI (tacrolimus/cyclosporine)
- Reduce/hold MMF temporarily if severe COVID
- Continue steroids (do NOT abruptly stop)
```

#### Growth and Development Post-Transplant

**Catch-up Growth:**
- **Timeline**: Most rapid in first 2 years post-transplant
- **Predictors of Good Growth**:
  - Age at transplant <5 years
  - Low steroid dose
  - Good graft function
  - Adequate nutrition
- **Growth Hormone**: May resume 12+ months post-transplant if growth impaired

**Puberty and Sexual Maturation:**
- **Delayed Puberty**: Common (30-40% of adolescent recipients)
- **Tanner Staging**: Monitor every 6 months in peri-pubertal patients
- **Endocrine Referral**: If no pubertal development by age 14 (girls) or 15 (boys)
- **Fertility**: Generally preserved post-transplant, counsel on contraception

**Bone Health:**
- **Risk Factors**: Steroids, pre-transplant CKD-MBD, post-transplant hyperparathyroidism
- **Monitoring**:
  - Vitamin D levels every 6 months
  - DXA scan at 1 year post-transplant, then annually if abnormal
- **Management**:
  - Vitamin D supplementation (goal 25-OH vitamin D >30 ng/mL)
  - Weight-bearing exercise
  - Bisphosphonates if severe osteoporosis

**Neurocognitive Development:**
- **Outcomes**: Generally normal development post-transplant
- **Risk Factors for Impairment**:
  - Early-onset ESRD (<2 years)
  - Prolonged dialysis (>2 years)
  - Malnutrition
- **Monitoring**: Developmental screening, school performance assessment
- **Intervention**: Early intervention services, educational support if needed

## Growth, Nutrition, and Development

### Growth Assessment

#### Normal Growth Patterns
- **Infancy**: Rapid growth, 25 cm length gain first year
- **Early Childhood**: 5-7 cm/year height gain
- **Puberty**: Growth spurt, 8-12 cm/year
- **Final Height**: Target height based on mid-parental height

#### Growth Failure in CKD
- **Causes**: 
  - Protein-energy wasting
  - Growth hormone resistance
  - Metabolic acidosis
  - Renal osteodystrophy
- **Management**: 
  - Aggressive nutrition support
  - Growth hormone therapy: 0.05 mg/kg/day subcutaneously
  - Optimize dialysis adequacy
  - Early transplantation

### Nutritional Management

#### Energy and Protein Requirements
```
Energy Needs:
- Infants: 100-120 kcal/kg/day
- Children: 70-100 kcal/kg/day
- Adolescents: 30-60 kcal/kg/day

Protein Requirements:
- Pre-dialysis CKD: 0.8-1.1 g/kg/day
- Peritoneal dialysis: 1.2-1.5 g/kg/day
- Hemodialysis: 1.0-1.3 g/kg/day
- Post-transplant: 1.0-1.2 g/kg/day
```

#### Micronutrient Management
- **Vitamin D**: 
  - CKD stages 2-4: 400-1000 IU/day
  - Dialysis: Active vitamin D analogs
  - Post-transplant: Monitor levels, supplement as needed
- **Iron**: 
  - Prevent iron deficiency anemia
  - Ferritin target: 100-500 ng/mL
  - TSAT target: 20-50%
- **Zinc and Selenium**: 
  - May be deficient in dialysis patients
  - Supplement if levels low

### Developmental Considerations

#### Neurocognitive Development
- **Risk Factors**: Early onset ESRD, dialysis duration, malnutrition
- **Monitoring**: Developmental screening, school performance
- **Intervention**: Early intervention services, educational support

#### Psychosocial Development
- **Body Image**: Catheters, transplant scars, growth delay
- **Social Integration**: School attendance, peer relationships
- **Family Dynamics**: Parental stress, sibling adjustment
- **Transition to Adult Care**: 
  - Begin planning at age 14-16
  - Gradual transfer of responsibility
  - Adult care provider identification

## Growth Management in Pediatric CKD (2024 Update)

### Growth Failure in CKD: Pathophysiology

#### Multiple Contributing Factors
```
Hormonal Abnormalities:
- Growth hormone resistance (increased levels but decreased activity)
- IGF-1 resistance at tissue level
- Metabolic acidosis → protein catabolism
- Hyperparathyroidism → bone disease

Nutritional Deficiencies:
- Inadequate caloric intake (anorexia, dietary restrictions)
- Protein-energy wasting
- Micronutrient deficiencies (zinc, vitamin D, iron)

Medication Effects:
- Corticosteroids → growth plate suppression
- Calcineurin inhibitors → variable effects

Uremic Toxins:
- Accumulation of uremic solutes → anorexia
- Inflammation → cachexia
```

### Recombinant Human Growth Hormone (rhGH) Therapy

#### FDA-Approved Indications
- **CKD** (pre-dialysis): GFR 15-75 mL/min/1.73m²
- **Dialysis**: On maintenance PD or HD
- **Post-Transplant**: After graft function stable (with caution)

#### Eligibility Criteria (2024 Guidelines)
```
Must Meet ALL:
- Height <5th percentile OR Height velocity <25th percentile for age/sex
- Bone age <10 years (girls) or <12 years (boys)
- No malignancy or active malignant potential
- Open epiphyses (confirmed by bone age X-ray)
- Adequate nutrition and metabolic control

Additional Requirements:
- Corrected metabolic acidosis (serum CO2 >22 mEq/L)
- Optimal nutrition (adequate caloric/protein intake)
- Well-controlled secondary hyperparathyroidism
- Optimal blood pressure control
```

#### Dosing and Monitoring (2024 Protocols)
```
Standard Dosing:
- 0.05 mg/kg/day subcutaneous injection daily
- Given at bedtime (mimics physiologic secretion)
- Rotate injection sites to prevent lipohypertrophy

Monitoring Schedule:
- Height and weight monthly
- IGF-1 and IGFBP-3 every 3-6 months
- Bone age X-ray annually
- Fasting glucose, HbA1c every 6 months
- Thyroid function annually
- Monitor for slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE)

Expected Response:
- Increase in height velocity within 3-6 months
- Catch-up growth: 8-12 cm/year in first year
- Continued growth 4-6 cm/year in subsequent years
- Normalize height SDS over 3-5 years

Treatment Duration:
- Continue until near-final height OR transplantation
- Post-transplant: May resume after 1 year if growth remains impaired
```

#### Adverse Effects and Contraindications
```
Common Side Effects (Generally Mild):
- Injection site reactions
- Headache (usually transient)
- Peripheral edema
- Arthralgia
- Benign intracranial hypertension (rare)

Metabolic Effects:
- Insulin resistance (monitor glucose)
- Mild hyperglycemia (usually not requiring treatment)
- Slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE) - monitor hip/knee pain

Contraindications:
- Active malignancy or cancer history <2 years
- Acute critical illness
- Diabetic retinopathy
- Closed epiphyses
- Severe uncontrolled hyperparathyroidism

Special Considerations:
- Transplant recipients: Theoretical concern about rejection (monitor carefully)
- Cystic kidney diseases: No increased cyst growth observed
```

### Optimizing Growth Without rhGH (2024 Evidence-Based Strategies)

#### Nutritional Optimization
```
Caloric Requirements (2024 KDOQI Pediatric Nutrition Guidelines):
- CKD Stage 2-3: 100% of DRI for age
- CKD Stage 4-5: 100-140% of DRI (individualized)
- Dialysis: 100-120% of DRI + dialysis losses
- Infants/Young Children: May need up to 150% DRI

Protein Intake:
- Adequate protein CRITICAL for growth
- Pre-dialysis CKD: 100-140% of DRI for age
- Dialysis: 140-160% of DRI (account for dialytic losses)
- High biological value proteins preferred

Enteral Nutrition:
- Gastrostomy tube (G-tube) if unable to meet needs orally
- Indications: <80% of caloric goals for >1 month
- Nighttime feeds preferred (allow daytime oral intake)
- Evidence: G-tube supplementation improves growth velocity
```

#### Metabolic Optimization
```
Correct Metabolic Acidosis:
- Target: Serum bicarbonate >22 mEq/L (ideally 23-25)
- Sodium bicarbonate: 1-3 mEq/kg/day divided doses
- Citrate-based solutions (Bicitra, Shohl's solution) if tolerated
- Evidence: Acidosis correction improves growth by 30-40%

Manage Mineral Bone Disease:
- Target PTH (KDOQI):
  - CKD 2-3: Normal range
  - CKD 4: 35-70 pg/mL
  - CKD 5: 150-300 pg/mL
- Phosphate binders: Calcium-based if Ca normal, non-Ca-based if hypercalcemia
- Active vitamin D analogs: Calcitriol or paricalcitol
- Evidence: Uncontrolled HPT → growth plate abnormalities

Anemia Correction:
- Target Hgb: 10-12 g/dL (avoid >13 g/dL)
- Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs)
- Iron supplementation as needed
- Evidence: Anemia correction improves energy, appetite, growth

Optimize BP Control:
- Target <90th percentile (ideally <75th percentile)
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs preferred (additional antiproteinuric benefit)
- Evidence: Hypertension impairs growth hormone axis
```

#### Medication Strategies
```
Minimize Steroid Exposure:
- Steroid-sparing protocols post-transplant
- Alternate-day dosing when possible
- Early steroid withdrawal if feasible
- Deflazacort: Alternative with less growth suppression (not FDA-approved in US)

Optimize Immunosuppression:
- Tacrolimus vs cyclosporine: Similar growth outcomes
- Minimize CNI when possible
- Belatacept (investigational in children): May allow CNI reduction
```

### Post-Transplant Growth Management

#### Growth Patterns After Transplantation
```
Early Post-Transplant (0-12 months):
- Initial growth spurt common (catch-up growth)
- Most rapid growth if transplanted <5 years of age
- rhGH usually held first 6-12 months (allow graft stability)

Long-Term (>1 year):
- 30-40% remain below 3rd percentile for height
- Factors: Age at transplant, steroid dose, pre-transplant growth
- rhGH can be restarted if growth impaired and graft stable

Final Adult Height:
- Early transplant (<5 years): Often achieve near-normal height
- Late transplant (>12 years): Significant height deficit persists
- Pre-emptive transplant: BEST outcomes for growth
```

#### rhGH Use Post-Transplant (2024 Data)
```
Safety and Efficacy:
- Meta-analysis (2023): rhGH safe post-transplant, no increased rejection
- Improves height SDS by 1.0-1.5 over 3-5 years
- No increased risk of PTLD, malignancy, or graft loss
- Most centers comfortable starting 12 months post-transplant

Dosing:
- Same as pre-transplant: 0.05 mg/kg/day SC
- Monitor graft function closely (Cr, proteinuria)
- Some centers use lower dose initially (0.03-0.04 mg/kg)

Monitoring:
- Graft function (Cr, eGFR) monthly for 3 months, then quarterly
- Reject surveillance biopsies if center protocol
- IGF-1 levels every 3-6 months
- Glucose monitoring (transplant patients at higher DM risk)
```

### Novel and Future Therapies for Growth (2024)

#### IGF-1 Therapy
- **Rationale**: Bypass GH resistance, direct IGF-1 administration
- **Mecasermin (Increlex)**: FDA-approved for primary IGF-1 deficiency
- **CKD Application**: Investigational, small pilot studies
- **Challenges**: Hypoglycemia risk, limited data in CKD

#### GLP-1 Receptor Agonists (Emerging Data)
- **Background**: Used for obesity, type 2 diabetes in adults
- **Potential in Pediatric CKD**:
  - Improve appetite control in obesity-related CKD
  - Potential renal protective effects (adult data)
  - Weight management post-transplant
- **Status**: Very limited pediatric data, investigational only
- **Concern**: Weight loss could worsen malnutrition in some CKD patients

#### SGLT2 Inhibitors (Future Potential)
- **Adult CKD**: Proven renal protective (DAPA-CKD, EMPA-KIDNEY trials)
- **Pediatric Application**: Investigational
- **Trials**: Ongoing safety/efficacy studies in children with CKD
- **Growth Impact**: Unknown, may improve metabolic profile
- **Expected pediatric approval**: 2025-2027 if trials positive

### Growth Management Algorithm (2024)
```
All Children with CKD Stage 2-5:
1. Plot height and weight on WHO/CDC growth charts monthly
2. Calculate height velocity (cm/year) every 6 months
3. Optimize nutrition, acidosis, MBD, anemia, BP

If Height <5th percentile OR Height Velocity <25th percentile:
1. Assess nutritional intake (calorie count)
2. Check bone age, IGF-1, IGFBP-3, thyroid function
3. Correct metabolic acidosis (bicarb >22 mEq/L)
4. Ensure adequate protein/calorie intake
5. Consider G-tube if <80% of nutritional goals

If Growth Remains Impaired Despite Optimization:
1. Refer to pediatric endocrinology
2. Evaluate for rhGH therapy (if eligible)
3. Start rhGH 0.05 mg/kg/day SC at bedtime
4. Monitor height velocity, IGF-1, adverse effects

Post-Transplant:
1. Continue growth monitoring
2. Minimize steroid dose
3. Consider rhGH if growth impaired at 12+ months post-transplant
```

## Key Practice Points

### Clinical Pearls
1. **Neonatal AKI**: Serum creatinine reflects maternal values for first 48 hours
2. **Steroid Therapy**: Use weight-based dosing in children (mg/m² or mg/kg)
3. **Growth Monitoring**: Plot height and weight on appropriate growth charts MONTHLY
4. **Blood Pressure**: Use age, gender, and height-specific percentiles
5. **Transplant Timing**: Early transplantation improves growth and development
6. **rhGH Therapy**: Requires metabolic optimization FIRST (acidosis, nutrition, MBD)
7. **Rituximab for Nephrotic Syndrome**: Now first-line for FRNS/SDNS (2024 guidelines)
8. **Sparsentan**: Emerging therapy for pediatric FSGS (expected approval 2025-2026)

### Red Flags
- **Failure to Thrive**: Unexplained weight loss or poor weight gain
- **Severe Hypertension**: BP >99th percentile + symptoms
- **Gross Hematuria**: With edema or hypertension suggests glomerulonephritis
- **Proteinuria**: Nephrotic range (>40 mg/m²/hour) needs urgent evaluation
- **Developmental Delay**: May indicate underlying chronic kidney disease

### Prevention Strategies
- **Prenatal Screening**: Ultrasound for renal anomalies
- **Vaccinations**: Keep up-to-date, especially pneumococcal and influenza
- **Blood Pressure Screening**: Annual screening in children >3 years
- **Urinalysis Screening**: School-age screening for proteinuria/hematuria
- **Family Education**: Early recognition of warning signs
