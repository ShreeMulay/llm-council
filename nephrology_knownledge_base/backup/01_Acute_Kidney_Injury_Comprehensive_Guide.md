# Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) - Comprehensive Clinical Guide

## Metadata
**Core Domain:** Clinical Nephrology I  
**Difficulty Level:** Foundation-Advanced  
**Frequency of Use:** Daily  
**Clinical Impact:** High  
**Regulatory Requirements:** QIP reporting, CMS documentation  
**Prerequisites:** Basic renal physiology, fluid/electrolyte balance  
**Competency Development Time:** 6-12 months  
**Cross-references:** Electrolyte disorders (Domain I.2), CRRT (Domain II.6), ICU nephrology (Domain VI.19)

---

## 1. Definition & Pathophysiology

### Clinical Definition
Acute Kidney Injury is defined as a rapid decrease in kidney function occurring over hours to days, characterized by:
- Rise in serum creatinine
- Decrease in urine output
- Accumulation of metabolic waste products
- Disturbances in fluid, electrolyte, and acid-base balance

### KDIGO 2021 Definition & Staging
**Stage 1:**
- Serum creatinine rise ≥0.3 mg/dL (≥26.5 μmol/L) within 48 hours, OR
- Serum creatinine rise to 1.5-1.9 times baseline, OR
- Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/h for 6-12 hours

**Stage 2:**
- Serum creatinine rise to 2.0-2.9 times baseline, OR
- Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/h for ≥12 hours

**Stage 3:**
- Serum creatinine rise to ≥3.0 times baseline, OR
- Serum creatinine ≥4.0 mg/dL (≥353.6 μmol/L) with acute rise ≥0.5 mg/dL, OR
- Initiation of renal replacement therapy, OR
- Urine output <0.3 mL/kg/h for ≥24 hours, OR
- Anuria for ≥12 hours

### Pathophysiological Mechanisms
**Hemodynamic Alterations:**
- Renal autoregulation failure
- Afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction
- Efferent arteriolar vasodilation
- Glomerular hydrostatic pressure reduction
- Reduced renal blood flow (RBF)

**Tubular Injury Mechanisms:**
- Ischemic injury to proximal tubules
- Cytoskeletal disruption and cell polarity loss
- Tubular obstruction from cell debris
- Back-leak of glomerular filtrate
- Inflammatory cascade activation

**Inflammatory Response:**
- Endothelial activation and leukocyte adhesion
- Cytokine release (TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β)
- Complement activation
- Oxidative stress and reactive oxygen species
- Microvascular dysfunction

## 2. Etiology Classification

### Pre-renal Azotemia (Functional AKI)

#### Volume Depletion States
**Absolute Hypovolemia:**
- Hemorrhage (trauma, GI bleeding, surgical)
- Severe diarrhea/vomiting
- Burns (>20% BSA)
- Pancreatitis (third spacing)
- Diuretic overuse
- Osmotic diuresis (hyperglycemia, mannitol)

**Relative Hypovolemia:**
- Cirrhosis with ascites
- Nephrotic syndrome
- Sepsis with vasodilation
- Anaphylaxis
- Cardiac tamponade

#### Cardiogenic Causes
**Decreased Cardiac Output:**
- Acute decompensated heart failure
- Cardiogenic shock
- Myocardial infarction
- Severe arrhythmias
- Pulmonary embolism

**Cardiorenal Syndromes:**
- Type 1: Acute cardiac dysfunction leading to AKI
- Type 2: Chronic cardiac dysfunction exacerbating CKD
- Type 5: Systemic disorders affecting both heart and kidneys

#### Hepatorenal Syndrome
**HRS-AKI (Type 1):**
- Rapid rise in creatinine (≥2.5 mg/dL)
- Occurs in setting of acute liver injury
- No improvement after volume expansion
- Absence of shock
- No nephrotoxic drug exposure
- No parenchymal kidney disease

**HRS-CKD (Type 2):**
- Gradual creatinine rise
- Stable or slowly progressive
- Refractory ascites
- Sodium retention

### Intrinsic AKI (Structural)

#### Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)
**Ischemic ATN:**
- Prolonged hypotension (>60 minutes MAP <65 mmHg)
- Major surgery (cardiac, vascular, abdominal)
- Severe trauma
- Sepsis with hemodynamic compromise
- Burns

**Nephrotoxic ATN:**
**Contrast Media:**
- High-osmolar contrast > low-osmolar > iso-osmolar
- Risk factors: CKD, diabetes, dehydration, high contrast volume
- Prevention: IV isotonic saline 1 mL/kg/h for 12 hours pre/post
- N-acetylcysteine not proven effective

**Aminoglycosides:**
- Gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin
- Accumulation in proximal tubular cells
- Dose-dependent toxicity
- Therapeutic drug monitoring essential
- Once-daily dosing reduces toxicity

**Amphotericin B:**
- Liposomal formulations less toxic
- Vasoconstriction of afferent arterioles
- Direct tubular cell toxicity
- Electrolyte wasting (K+, Mg++)

**Calcineurin Inhibitors:**
- Cyclosporine, tacrolimus
- Afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction
- Chronic interstitial fibrosis
- Therapeutic drug monitoring

**Other Nephrotoxins:**
- Acyclovir (crystal nephropathy)
- Methotrexate (precipitation)
- Tenofovir (proximal tubulopathy)
- Ifosfamide (Fanconi syndrome)
- Cisplatin (magnesium wasting)

#### Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN)
**Drug-Induced AIN:**
**Common Culprits:**
- Antibiotics: β-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins), quinolones, sulfonamides, rifampin
- PPIs: Omeprazole, pantoprazole, esomeprazole (most common cause)
- NSAIDs: Ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac
- Diuretics: Thiazides, furosemide
- Allopurinol
- 5-ASA compounds

**Clinical Features:**
- Fever (70-80%)
- Rash (30-50%)
- Eosinophilia (60-80%)
- Arthralgias
- Timing: 7-14 days after drug exposure

**Laboratory Findings:**
- Sterile pyuria with WBC casts
- Eosinophiluria (Hansel's stain)
- Mild proteinuria (<1 g/day)
- Elevated ESR/CRP

**Infection-Related AIN:**
- Bacterial: Leptospirosis, Brucellosis
- Viral: EBV, CMV, HIV, hepatitis B/C
- Fungal: Histoplasmosis
- Parasitic: Toxoplasmosis

**Systemic Disease AIN:**
- Sarcoidosis (non-caseating granulomas)
- Sjögren's syndrome
- IgG4-related disease
- Tubulointerstitial nephritis with uveitis (TINU)

#### Glomerular Diseases
**Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN):**
- Anti-GBM disease (Goodpasture's)
- ANCA-associated vasculitis
- Immune complex GN (lupus, IgA, post-infectious)
- Pauci-immune GN

**Clinical Presentation:**
- Active urinary sediment (RBC casts)
- Nephritic syndrome
- Rapid decline in GFR
- Systemic symptoms (vasculitis)

#### Vascular Causes
**Thrombotic Microangiopathy (TMA):**
- Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP)
- Hemolytic uremic syndrome (STEC-HUS, atypical HUS)
- Drug-induced (calcineurin inhibitors, quinine)
- Pregnancy-associated (HELLP, pre-eclampsia)
- Shiga-toxin producing E. coli

**Atheroembolic Disease:**
- Recent vascular procedure (catheterization, surgery)
- Cholesterol crystal emboli
- Livedo reticularis
- Eosinophilia
- Low complement levels

**Renal Vein Thrombosis:**
- Nephrotic syndrome (membranous nephropathy)
- Hypercoagulable states
- Malignancy
- Dehydration

### Post-renal AKI (Obstructive)

#### Anatomical Obstruction
**Ureteral Obstruction:**
- Calculi (most common)
- Blood clots
- Tumors (transitional cell carcinoma)
- Strictures
- External compression (retroperitoneal fibrosis, lymphadenopathy)
- Papillary necrosis

**Bladder Outlet Obstruction:**
- Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH)
- Prostate cancer
- Urethral stricture
- Neurogenic bladder
- Phimosis

**Urethral Obstruction:**
- Catheter obstruction/kinking
- Meatal stenosis
- Foreign bodies

#### Functional Obstruction
- Neurogenic bladder
- Medications (anticholinergics, α-agonists)
- Spinal cord injury
- Diabetes mellitus (autonomic neuropathy)

## 3. Diagnostic Approach

### Initial Assessment

#### History Taking
**Timeline of Events:**
- Onset and duration of symptoms
- Recent procedures/surgeries
- New medication exposures
- Contrast administration
- Recent infections
- Volume status changes

**Symptoms Review:**
- Decreased urine output
- Edema (peripheral, pulmonary)
- Shortness of breath
- Fatigue, nausea, confusion
- Flank pain (obstruction)
- Rash, fever, arthralgias (AIN)

**Past Medical History:**
- Baseline kidney function
- Chronic kidney disease
- Diabetes, hypertension
- Heart failure, liver disease
- Autoimmune diseases

#### Physical Examination
**Volume Status Assessment:**
- Skin turgor, mucous membranes
- Jugular venous pressure
- Pulmonary exam (crackles, effusions)
- Peripheral edema
- Orthostatic vitals

**Cardiovascular Exam:**
- Heart sounds, murmurs
- Peripheral pulses
- Capillary refill

**Abdominal Exam:**
- Bladder distension
- Flank tenderness
- Costovertebral angle tenderness
- Organomegaly

**Skin Examination:**
- Rash (drug reactions)
- Livedo reticularis (atheroemboli)
- Purpura (vasculitis)
- Pallor (anemia)

### Laboratory Evaluation

#### Basic Metabolic Panel
**Creatinine Kinetics:**
- Baseline creatinine comparison
- Creatinine rise rate
- Fractional excretion calculations
- Cystatin C if muscle mass abnormal

**BUN/Creatinine Ratio:**
- >20:1 suggests pre-renal azotemia
- <10-15:1 suggests intrinsic AKI
- Exceptions: steroids, protein catabolism, GI bleeding

#### Urine Studies
**Urinalysis:**
- Specific gravity (>1.020 suggests pre-renal)
- pH (acidic in ATN, alkaline in RTA)
- Protein (nephrotic range in glomerular disease)
- Blood (RBCs vs myoglobin vs hemoglobin)

**Urine Microscopy:**
**Pre-renal:**
- Hyaline casts
- Few cells/crystals

**ATN:**
- Muddy brown granular casts
- Renal tubular epithelial cells
- WBC casts

**AIN:**
- WBC casts
- Eosinophils (Hansel's stain)
- RBC casts (less common)

**Glomerular Disease:**
- Dysmorphic RBCs
- RBC casts
- Lipiduria (nephrotic syndrome)

**Urine Electrolytes:**
**FENa (Fractional Excretion of Sodium):**
- <1%: Pre-renal azotemia
- >2%: Intrinsic AKI (ATN)
- Limitations: Diuretic use, CKD, contrast nephropathy

**FEUrea (Fractional Excretion of Urea):**
- <35%: Pre-renal azotemia
- >50%: Intrinsic AKI
- More reliable with diuretic use

**Urine Osmolality:**
- >500 mOsm/kg: Pre-renal
- <350 mOsm/kg: Intrinsic AKI

#### Advanced Biomarkers
**Damage Biomarkers:**
- NGAL (Neutrophil Gelatinase-Associated Lipocalin)
  - Rises within 2-6 hours
  - Predicts need for RRT
  - Distinguishes pre-renal vs ATN

- KIM-1 (Kidney Injury Molecule-1)
  - Proximal tubule injury marker
  - Specific for AKI

- IL-18 (Interleukin-18)
  - Inflammatory marker
  - Predicts mortality

- L-FABP (Liver-type Fatty Acid Binding Protein)
  - Tubular stress marker
  - Early detection

**Functional Biomarkers:**
- Cystatin C
- TIMP-2·IGFBP7 (NephroCheck)
  - Predicts moderate-severe AKI
  - Risk stratification

### Imaging Studies

#### Renal Ultrasound
**Indications:**
- All AKI patients without obvious cause
- Suspected obstruction
- Baseline kidney size assessment

**Findings:**
- Kidney size (normal 10-12 cm)
- Cortical echogenicity
- Hydronephrosis grading
- Renal artery/vein Doppler
- Bladder volume

**Limitations:**
- Operator dependent
- Limited in obesity
- Cannot differentiate AKI causes

#### CT Scanning
**Non-contrast CT:**
- Nephrolithiasis
- Retroperitoneal fibrosis
- Mass lesions

**Contrast CT:**
- Vascular abnormalities
- Mass characterization
- Contraindicated in AKI unless essential

#### MRI
**Indications:**
- Renal artery stenosis
- Renal vein thrombosis
- Complex cysts (Bosniak classification)
- Contrast allergy (gadolinium caution in GFR <30)

## 4. Management Strategies

### General Principles

#### Hemodynamic Optimization
**Fluid Resuscitation:**
- Isotonic crystalloids (0.9% NS, LR)
- Avoid hypotonic fluids
- Target MAP >65 mmHg
- Avoid fluid overload

**Vasopressor Support:**
- Norepinephrine first-line
- Vasopressin as adjunct
- Avoid excessive vasoconstriction
- Maintain renal perfusion pressure

**Diuretic Strategy:**
- Loop diuretics (furosemide 40-80 mg IV)
- Continuous infusion vs bolus
- Avoid in hypovolemia
- Monitor for ototoxicity

#### Nephrotoxin Avoidance
**Medication Review:**
- Hold nephrotoxic medications
- Adjust doses for GFR
- Avoid contrast when possible
- Alternative imaging modalities

**High-Risk Medications:**
- NSAIDs (hold all)
- ACEi/ARBs (hold in AKI)
- Diuretics (use cautiously)
- Aminoglycosides (avoid)
- Amphotericin B (avoid)

### Specific Etiology Management

#### Pre-renal Azotemia
**Volume Repletion:**
- Crystalloid bolus 250-500 mL
- Repeat based on response
- Monitor for pulmonary edema
- Consider albumin in cirrhosis

**Cardiac Support:**
- Inotropes if low cardiac output
- Afterload reduction
- Mechanical support if needed

**Hepatorenal Syndrome:**
- Vasoconstrictors (terlipressin, midodrine + octreotide)
- Albumin infusion 1 g/kg
- Avoid diuretics
- Liver transplant evaluation

#### Acute Tubular Necrosis
**Supportive Care:**
- Maintain euvolemia
- Electrolyte management
- Nutritional support
- Avoid further nephrotoxins

**Diuretic Challenge:**
- Furosemide 1-2 mg/kg IV
- Continuous infusion if responsive
- Goal: Prevent fluid overload
- Does not prevent RRT need

#### Acute Interstitial Nephritis
**Discontinue Offending Agent:**
- Immediate cessation
- All medications reviewed
- Rechallenge only if essential

**Corticosteroid Therapy:**
- Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day
- Duration: 4-6 weeks
- Taper gradually
- Consider if no improvement in 72 hours

**Supportive Care:**
- Fluid management
- Electrolyte correction
- Monitor for recovery

#### Glomerular Disease (RPGN)
**Urgent Treatment:**
- High-dose steroids (methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg daily × 3 days)
- Plasma exchange (anti-GBM, ANCA)
- Cyclophosphamide or rituximab
- Supportive care

**Diagnostic Workup:**
- Urgent kidney biopsy
- Autoimmune serologies
- Complement levels
- Infectious workup

#### Obstructive AKI
**Relieve Obstruction:**
- Foley catheter placement
- Urological consultation
- Stent placement if needed
- Percutaneous nephrostomy

**Post-Obstructive Diuresis:**
- Monitor electrolytes
- Replace losses
- Avoid dehydration
- May last 24-72 hours

### Renal Replacement Therapy Indications

#### Absolute Indications (AEIOU)
**A - Acidosis:**
- Severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.1-7.2)
- Refractory to bicarbonate therapy

**E - Electrolytes:**
- Severe hyperkalemia (>6.5 mEq/L)
- Refractory to medical therapy
- ECG changes

**I - Intoxication:**
- Toxic ingestions (lithium, metformin)
- Rhabdomyolysis
- Tumor lysis syndrome

**O - Overload:**
- Refractory pulmonary edema
- Severe fluid overload
- Unable to tolerate fluids

**U - Uremia:**
- Symptomatic uremia (pericarditis, encephalopathy)
- BUN >100 mg/dL with symptoms
- Intractable nausea/vomiting

#### Relative Indications
- Refractory hyperkalemia (>5.5-6.0 mEq/L)
- Moderate acidosis (pH 7.1-7.2)
- Fluid overload compromising oxygenation
- Need for nutrition/medications
- Temperature control

#### Modality Selection
**Intermittent Hemodialysis (IHD):**
- Hemodynamically stable patients
- Rapid solute clearance needed
- Drug intoxication
- Resource-limited settings

**Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy (CRRT):**
- Hemodynamically unstable
- ICP concerns
- Gradual solute removal
- 24/7 ICU availability

**Sustained Low-Efficiency Dialysis (SLED):**
- Intermediate option
- Hemodynamic stability
- Resource efficiency
- Anticoagulation flexibility

## 5. Special Populations

### Cardiac Surgery Patients

#### Risk Factors
- Pre-existing CKD
- Diabetes mellitus
- Advanced age
- Low cardiac output syndrome
- Cardiopulmonary bypass time >120 minutes
- Postoperative low cardiac output

#### Prevention Strategies
**Preoperative:**
- Optimize volume status
- Hold nephrotoxic medications
- Baseline creatinine/cystatin C
- Risk stratification

**Intraoperative:**
- Maintain MAP >65 mmHg
- Avoid excessive hemodilution
- Minimize bypass time
- Consider off-pump surgery

**Postoperative:**
- Optimize cardiac output
- Maintain euvolemia
- Early nephrology consultation
- Biomarker monitoring (NGAL)

#### Management
- Hemodynamic optimization
- Avoid hypotension
- Diuretic challenge if fluid overloaded
- Early RRT if indicated
- Consider remote ischemic preconditioning

### Sepsis-Associated AKI

#### Pathophysiology
- Global renal hypoperfusion
- Microcirculatory dysfunction
- Inflammatory mediator injury
- Metabolic reprogramming
- Cellular bioenergetic failure

#### Clinical Features
- Often non-oliguric initially
- High urinary output despite AKI
- Rapid creatinine rise
- Fluid overload common
- Multi-organ dysfunction

#### Management Principles
**Sepsis Management:**
- Early appropriate antibiotics
- Source control
- Hemodynamic support
- Vasopressor optimization

**Renal-Specific Care:**
- Conservative fluid strategy after initial resuscitation
- Avoid nephrotoxins
- Early biomarker monitoring
- Consider early RRT for fluid control

**RRT Timing:**
- No clear consensus
- Consider for severe fluid overload
- Earlier intervention may improve outcomes
- Individualized decision-making

### Contrast-Associated AKI

#### Risk Stratification
**High-Risk Patients:**
- eGFR <45 mL/min/1.73m²
- Diabetes mellitus
- Dehydration
- High contrast volume (>100 mL)
- Multiple contrast exposures

**Risk Scores:**
- Mehran risk score
- Chen risk score
- CIN risk calculator

#### Prevention Strategies
**Pre-Procedure:**
- IV isotonic saline 1 mL/kg/h for 12 hours
- Hold nephrotoxic medications
- Consider alternative imaging
- Use low-osmolar or iso-osmolar contrast

**Intra-Procedure:**
- Minimize contrast volume
- Use iso-osmolar contrast
- Avoid repeat injections
- Consider CO2 angiography

**Post-Procedure:**
- Continue hydration 6-12 hours
- Monitor creatinine 48-72 hours
- Avoid nephrotoxins
- Outpatient follow-up

#### Management
- Supportive care
- Monitor for progression
- Usually self-limited
- Rarely requires RRT
- Long-term follow-up for CKD development

### Liver Disease Patients

#### Hepatorenal Syndrome
**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- Cirrhosis with ascites
- Serum creatinine >1.5 mg/dL
- No improvement after albumin 1 g/kg × 2 days
- Absence of shock
- No nephrotoxic drugs
- No parenchymal kidney disease

**Treatment:**
- Terlipressin + albumin (first-line)
- Midodrine + octreotide + albumin (alternative)
- Avoid diuretics
- Consider TIPS
- Liver transplant evaluation

#### Other Causes
- Bacterial infections (SBP)
- GI bleeding
- Diuretic-induced
- Paracentesis-induced
- Nephrotoxic drugs

### Oncology Patients

#### Chemotherapy Nephrotoxicity
**Cisplatin:**
- Dose-dependent toxicity
- Magnesium wasting
- Prevent with hydration
- Monitor magnesium

**Ifosfamide:**
- Fanconi syndrome
- Glycosuria, phosphaturia
- Dose limitation
- Monitor tubular function

**Methotrexate:**
- Crystal nephropathy
- Alkalinize urine
- Leucovorin rescue
- Monitor levels

#### Tumor Lysis Syndrome
**Risk Stratification:**
- High tumor burden
- Rapid cell turnover
- Chemosensitive tumors
- Elevated LDH

**Prevention:**
- Aggressive hydration
- Allopurinol or rasburicase
- Urine alkalinization (controversial)
- Close monitoring

**Treatment:**
- Aggressive hydration
- Rasburicase for hyperuricemia
- Electrolyte management
- Early RRT if severe

### Pediatric Considerations

#### Unique Features
- Higher baseline GFR
- Different creatinine kinetics
- Age-specific normal ranges
- Growth and development concerns

#### Common Causes
- HUS (post-diarrheal)
- Dehydration
- Sepsis
- Congenital anomalies
- Glomerulonephritis

#### Management Differences
- Weight-based dosing
- Age-specific equipment
- Growth considerations
- Family-centered care

## 6. Prevention Strategies

### Risk Assessment

#### Pre-Procedure Evaluation
- Baseline kidney function
- Identify risk factors
- Medication review
- Volume status assessment
- Previous AKI episodes

#### Risk Stratification Tools
**KDIGO Risk Factors:**
- Hypotension
- Sepsis
- Nephrotoxin exposure
- Contrast media
- Advanced age
- CKD
- Diabetes
- Anemia

**Surgical Risk Scores:**
- Cleveland Clinic score
- AKI risk calculator
- Biomarker-enhanced models

### Pharmacologic Prevention

#### N-Acetylcysteine
- No proven benefit for contrast AKI
- May have antioxidant effects
- Low risk profile
- Not routinely recommended

#### Statins
- Pleiotropic effects
- Anti-inflammatory properties
- Some evidence for prevention
- Consider in high-risk patients

#### Diuretics
- Not preventive
- May worsen outcomes
- Use only for volume management
- Avoid in hypovolemia

#### Vasodilators
- Fenoldopam (dopamine agonist)
- Low-dose dopamine (not effective)
- Atrial natriuretic peptide
- Limited evidence

### Non-Pharmacologic Prevention

#### Fluid Management
- Isotonic crystalloids
- Goal-directed therapy
- Avoid hypervolemia
- Monitor dynamic parameters

#### Hemodynamic Optimization
- Maintain MAP >65 mmHg
- Avoid prolonged hypotension
- Optimize cardiac output
- Consider invasive monitoring

#### Nephrotoxin Avoidance
- Medication reconciliation
- Dose adjustments
- Alternative agents
- Therapeutic drug monitoring

#### Contrast Minimization
- Alternative imaging
- Low-volume protocols
- Pre- and post-procedure hydration
- Iso-osmolar contrast

## 7. Recovery & Follow-up

### Recovery Patterns

#### Time Course
- Pre-renal: 24-72 hours
- ATN: 7-21 days (average 10-14 days)
- AIN: 4-8 weeks after steroid initiation
- Obstruction: 24-72 hours post-relief
- Glomerular: Variable, may be incomplete

#### Predictors of Recovery
- Younger age
- Absence of comorbidities
- Shorter duration of AKI
- Lower peak creatinine
- Absence of need for chronic RRT

#### Non-Recovery Risk Factors
- Advanced age
- Baseline CKD
- Diabetes mellitus
- Heart failure
- Sepsis
- High severity of illness

### Long-Term Complications

#### CKD Development
- 30-50% develop CKD
- Risk proportional to AKI severity
- Monitor eGFR at 3, 6, 12 months
- Long-term nephrology follow-up

#### Cardiovascular Disease
- Increased risk after AKI
- Monitor blood pressure
- Cardiovascular risk assessment
- Preventive strategies

#### End-Stage Renal Disease
- 1-5% progress to ESRD
- Higher with recurrent AKI
- Early transplant evaluation
- Modality education

### Follow-up Protocol

#### Immediate Post-AKI (1-4 weeks)
- Serum creatinine trend
- Electrolyte panel
- Urinalysis
- Medication reconciliation
- Blood pressure control

#### Short-term (1-3 months)
- eGFR assessment
- Proteinuria screening
- Blood pressure optimization
- Cardiovascular risk assessment
- Vaccination status

#### Long-term (3-12 months)
- Annual eGFR monitoring
- Proteinuria management
- SGLT2 inhibitor consideration
- Cardiovascular prevention
- Lifestyle modification

#### Discharge Planning
- Medication list review
- Follow-up appointments
- Patient education
- Warning signs instruction
- Primary care communication

## 9. Clinical Algorithms

### Initial AKI Evaluation Algorithm

```
AKI Detected (↑Cr or ↓UO)
        ↓
Urgent Assessment (ABCs, Volume Status)
        ↓
Focused History & Physical
        ↓
Laboratory Evaluation:
- BMP, CBC, UA with microscopy
- Urine electrolytes (FENa, FEUrea)
- Medication review
        ↓
Imaging:
- Renal ultrasound (if no obvious cause)
- Consider CT/MRI if indicated
        ↓
Determine Etiology:
- Pre-renal? → Volume optimization
- Intrinsic? → Specific treatment
- Post-renal? → Relieve obstruction
        ↓
Monitor & Reassess
```

### RRT Initiation Algorithm

```
AKI Patient Assessment
        ↓
Check AEIOU Indications:
A - Acidosis (pH <7.1)
E - Electrolytes (K+ >6.5)
I - Intoxication
O - Overload (pulmonary edema)
U - Uremia (symptomatic)
        ↓
Assess Hemodynamic Status:
- Stable → IHD
- Unstable → CRRT
- Intermediate → SLED
        ↓
Vascular Access:
- IJ femoral, or subclavian
- Prefer right IJ for CRRT
- Consider tunneled catheter if >7 days
        ↓
Initiate RRT
        ↓
Daily Reassessment
```

## 10. Evidence-Based Guidelines

### KDIGO 2021 Recommendations

#### Prevention
1.1: Use isotonic crystalloids for volume expansion in high-risk patients
1.2: Avoid nephrotoxic medications in at-risk patients
1.3: Consider biomarkers for risk stratification
1.4: Use lowest effective contrast dose

#### Management
2.1: Optimize hemodynamics and perfusion pressure
2.2: Consider functional hemodynamic monitoring
2.3: Avoid diuretics for prevention/treatment
2.4: Individualized RRT timing decisions

#### Follow-up
3.1: Monitor renal function after AKI resolution
3.2: Screen for proteinuria
3.4: Manage cardiovascular risk factors

### Society Guidelines

#### American Society of Nephrology
- Focus on early detection
- Biomarker utilization
- Standardized definitions
- Quality improvement initiatives

#### Intensive Care Society
- CRRT modality selection
- Anticoagulation strategies
- Dose optimization
- Multidisciplinary care

## 12. Key Take-Home Points

1. **Early Recognition**: Use KDIGO criteria, monitor high-risk patients
2. **Systematic Approach**: Classify as pre-renal, intrinsic, or post-renal
3. **Rapid Evaluation**: Urgent assessment, focused labs, timely imaging
4. **Prevention Focus**: Avoid nephrotoxins, optimize hemodynamics
5. **Individualized Care**: Tailor treatment to etiology and patient factors
6. **RRT Decision**: Use AEIOU mnemonic, consider hemodynamic status
7. **Long-term Follow-up**: Monitor for CKD development, cardiovascular risk
8. **Quality Documentation**: Accurate coding, clear etiology documentation
9. **Multidisciplinary Care**: Collaborate with ICU, cardiology, urology
10. **Patient Education**: Warning signs, medication safety, follow-up
