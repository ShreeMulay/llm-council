## Chemotherapy-Induced Nephrotoxicity

### Platinum Compounds

#### Cisplatin Nephrotoxicity
- **Incidence**: 20-30% acute kidney injury, higher with cumulative doses
- **Mechanism**: Proximal tubular cell uptake via organic cation transporters, DNA adduct formation, oxidative stress
- **Risk Factors**: 
  - Cumulative dose >300 mg/m²
  - Pre-existing CKD, dehydration, concurrent nephrotoxins
  - Advanced age, hypertension, diabetes
- **Clinical Presentation**: 
  - Non-oliguric AKI 3-10 days post-administration
  - Magnesium wasting (hypomagnesemia)
  - Polyuria, salt wasting
  - Possible progression to CKD

#### Prevention and Management
```
Prevention Strategies:
- Aggressive hydration: 1-3 L/m² before and after cisplatin
- Magnesium supplementation: 12-24 mEq pre and post-treatment
- Amifostine: 200 mg/m² IV 30 minutes before cisplatin
- Avoid concurrent nephrotoxins: NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast

Management of AKI:
- Supportive care, maintain adequate hydration
- Correct electrolyte abnormalities
- Consider dose reduction or discontinuation
- Monitor for chronic kidney disease development
```

#### Carboplatin and Oxaliplatin
- **Carboplatin**: Lower nephrotoxicity risk, dose-adjusted by Calvert formula
- **Oxaliplatin**: Minimal nephrotoxicity, rare AKI cases reported

### Antimetabolites

#### Methotrexate
- **Mechanism**: Precipitation in renal tubules, direct tubular toxicity
- **Risk Factors**: High dose therapy, dehydration, acidic urine, drug interactions
- **Prevention Protocol**:
```
High-Dose Methotrexate (>1 g/m²):
- Pre-hydration: 2-3 L/m² starting 12 hours before
- Urine Alkalization: Sodium bicarbonate to maintain pH >7.0
- Post-MTX Monitoring: Levels at 24, 48, 72 hours
- Leucovorin Rescue: Based on MTX levels
- Hydration: Continue until MTX <0.1 μM
```

#### 5-Fluorouracil and Capecitabine
- **Rare AKI**: Usually due to dehydration or thrombotic microangiopathy
- **Monitoring**: Baseline and periodic renal function

#### Gemcitabine
- **TMA Risk**: 0.015-0.31% incidence, higher with combination therapy
- **Presentation**: MAHA, thrombocytopenia, renal dysfunction
- **Management**: Discontinue drug, consider plasma exchange

### Alkylating Agents

#### Ifosfamide
- **Incidence**: 5-30% AKI, dose-dependent
- **Mechanism**: Chloroacetaldehyde metabolite toxicity
- **Clinical Features**: 
  - Proximal tubular dysfunction (Fanconi syndrome)
  - Glycosuria, phosphaturia, aminoaciduria
  - Possible progression to CKD
- **Prevention**: 
  - Mesna protection (does not prevent renal toxicity)
  - Adequate hydration
  - Avoid cumulative doses >60 g/m²

#### Cyclophosphamide
- **Lower Nephrotoxicity**: Compared to ifosfamide
- **SIADH**: Can cause hyponatremia
- **Hemorrhagic Cystitis**: Primary urologic toxicity

### Targeted Therapies

#### Anti-VEGF Agents (Bevacizumab, Sunitinib, Sorafenib)
- **Mechanism**: Inhibition of VEGF signaling → glomerular endothelial injury
- **Clinical Syndromes**:
  - Proteinuria (30-40%)
  - Hypertension (20-30%)
  - Thrombotic microangiopathy
  - Minimal change disease/FSGS
- **Management**:
  - Monitor proteinuria and blood pressure every 2-3 weeks
  - ACE inhibitors/ARBs for proteinuria
  - Hold therapy for proteinuria >2 g/day or uncontrolled hypertension

#### BCR-ABL Inhibitors (Imatinib, Dasatinib)
- **Imatinib**: Generally well-tolerated, rare AKI
- **Dasatinib**: Higher risk of fluid retention, pleural effusions

#### Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors
- **PD-1/PD-L1 Inhibitors**: Pembrolizumab, nivolumab
- **CTLA-4 Inhibitors**: Ipilimumab
- **Renal Toxicity**: 
  - Acute interstitial nephritis (1-2%)
  - Minimal change disease/FSGS
  - Immune complex glomerulonephritis
- **Management**: 
  - Corticosteroids 0.5-1 mg/kg/day
  - Hold immunotherapy
  - Consider rechallenge after recovery

## Tumor-Related Kidney Diseases

### Paraneoplastic Glomerular Diseases

#### Membranous Nephropathy
- **Associated Tumors**: Lung, colon, prostate, breast cancers
- **Mechanism**: Tumor antigens → immune complex deposition
- **Clinical Features**: 
  - Nephrotic syndrome
  - Often precedes cancer diagnosis
  - May improve with tumor treatment
- **Evaluation**: Age-appropriate cancer screening in patients >50 years

#### Minimal Change Disease
- **Associated Tumors**: Hodgkin lymphoma, thymoma, carcinoid
- **Pathophysiology**: Cytokine-mediated podocyte injury
- **Treatment**: 
  - Treat underlying malignancy
  - Corticosteroids if needed
  - Often remits with cancer control

#### FSGS
- **Associated Tumors**: Various solid tumors
- **Mechanism**: Unknown, possibly circulating factors
- **Prognosis**: Variable, often treatment-resistant

### Cryoglobulinemic Glomerulonephritis

#### Types and Associations
- **Type I**: Monoclonal (multiple myeloma, Waldenström macroglobulinemia)
- **Type II**: Mixed (essential mixed cryoglobulinemia, hepatitis C)
- **Type III**: Polyclonal (autoimmune diseases, infections)

#### Clinical Features
- **Purpura**: Lower extremity palpable purpura
- **Arthralgias**: Migratory joint pain
- **Peripheral Neuropathy**: Sensory deficits
- **Renal Involvement**: 
  - Proteinuria (nephrotic range possible)
  - Hematuria
  - Renal insufficiency
  - Hypertension

#### Management
```
Treatment Approach:
- Treat underlying malignancy
- Plasmapheresis for severe disease
- Rituximab: 375 mg/m² weekly × 4 doses
- Corticosteroids: Pulse methylprednisolone followed by oral taper
- Avoid cold exposure
```

### Light Chain Associated Diseases

#### Multiple Myeloma Kidney Involvement
- **Myeloma Cast Nephropathy**: 
  - Light chain precipitation in distal tubules
  - Presents with AKI, Bence Jones proteinuria
  - Treatment: Hydration, chemotherapy, bortezomib-based regimens
- **AL Amyloidosis**: 
  - AL light chain deposition
  - Nephrotic syndrome, restrictive cardiomyopathy
  - Treatment: CyBorD regimen (cyclophosphamide, bortezomib, dexamethasone)
- **Light Chain Deposition Disease**: 
  - Non-amyloid light chain deposition
  - Proteinuria, progressive renal failure

#### Waldenström Macroglobulinemia
- **IgM Paraprotein**: Hyperviscosity, cryoglobulinemia
- **Renal Manifestations**: 
  - Cryoglobulinemic GN
  - IgM deposition disease
  - Fanconi syndrome (rare)

## Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation (HSCT) Complications

### Acute Kidney Injury Post-HSCT

#### Etiology and Timing
- **Pre-engraftment (Days 0-30)**: 
  - Sepsis, nephrotoxic antibiotics, calcineurin inhibitors
  - Tumor lysis syndrome, hepatic veno-occlusive disease
- **Early Post-engraftment (Days 30-100)**: 
  - Acute GVHD, infections, thrombotic microangiopathy
- **Late (>100 days)**: 
  - Chronic GVHD, radiation nephropathy, BK virus nephropathy

#### Risk Factors
- **Myeloablative Conditioning**: Higher risk than reduced-intensity
- **Unrelated Donor**: Increased GVHD risk
- **Calcineurin Inhibitors**: Tacrolimus, cyclosporine toxicity
- **Pre-existing CKD**: Baseline renal impairment

### Thrombotic Microangiopathy (TMA)

#### Pathogenesis
- **Endothelial Injury**: Conditioning regimens, calcineurin inhibitors, GVHD
- **Complement Activation**: Alternative pathway dysregulation
- **Clinical Features**: 
  - MAHA (schistocytes, elevated LDH)
  - Thrombocytopenia
  - Renal dysfunction (proteinuria, rising creatinine)
  - Hypertension

#### Management
```
TMA Treatment Algorithm:
1. Discontinue/Reduce Calcineurin Inhibitors
2. Control Hypertension (target <130/80 mmHg)
3. Consider Plasma Exchange (if severe)
4. Complement Inhibition (Eculizumab) for refractory cases
5. Supportive Care (transfusions as needed)
```

### Graft-versus-Host Disease (GVHD) and Kidney

#### Acute GVHD Renal Involvement
- **Incidence**: Rare, usually part of systemic GVHD
- **Pathology**: Acute interstitial nephritis
- **Treatment**: Optimize GVHD therapy, consider steroids

#### Chronic GVHD Renal Manifestations
- **Membranous Nephropathy**: Most common glomerular lesion
- **Minimal Change Disease**: Less common
- **Clinical**: Proteinuria, edema, potential progression to CKD
- **Treatment**: 
  - Optimize GVHD immunosuppression
  - ACE inhibitors/ARBs for proteinuria
  - Consider rituximab for refractory cases

### BK Virus Nephropathy

#### Epidemiology
- **Incidence**: 5-10% in HSCT, higher in kidney transplant
- **Risk Factors**: 
  - High-dose immunosuppression
  - Lymphopenia, older age
  - Bladder dysfunction

#### Clinical Course
- **Cystitis**: Hemorrhagic cystitis in early post-transplant period
- **Nephropathy**: Interstitial nephritis, tubular injury
- **Presentation**: Rising creatinine, BK viruria/viremia

#### Management
```
BK Virus Treatment:
1. Reduce Immunosuppression (primary intervention)
2. Monitor BK Viral Load Weekly
3. Consider Cidofovir (nephrotoxic, use with caution)
4. Leflunomide or Fluoroquinolones (limited evidence)
5. Supportive Care: Hydration, avoid nephrotoxins
```

## Electrolyte Disorders in Oncology

### Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS)

#### Risk Stratification (Cairo-Bishop Criteria)
```
High Risk Tumors:
- Acute leukemias (ALL, AML with high WBC)
- High-grade lymphomas (Burkitt, diffuse large B-cell)
- Solid tumors with high proliferative rate (small cell lung, germ cell)

Intermediate Risk:
- Low-grade lymphomas, CLL, AML with low WBC
- Breast, ovarian, neuroblastoma

Low Risk:
- Most solid tumors, indolent hematologic malignancies
```

#### Laboratory TLS Definition
- **Uric Acid**: >25% increase or >8 mg/dL
- **Potassium**: >25% increase or >6 mEq/L
- **Phosphate**: >25% increase or >4.5 mg/dL (adults)
- **Calcium**: <25% decrease or <7 mg/dL

#### Prevention and Treatment
```
TLS Prophylaxis:
- Aggressive Hydration: 2-3 L/m²/day starting 24-48 hours before chemotherapy
- Allopurinol: 300 mg PO daily (start 2-3 days before therapy)
- Rasburicase: 0.15-0.2 mg/kg IV single dose (high-risk patients)
- Urine Alkalinization: Controversial, consider if high uric acid

TLS Treatment:
- Aggressive IV hydration
- Rasburicase for hyperuricemia
- Treat hyperkalemia: Calcium gluconate, insulin/glucose, β-agonists
- Treat hyperphosphatemia: Phosphate binders
- Treat hypocalcemia: Only if symptomatic
- Consider renal replacement therapy for severe cases
```

### SIADH in Cancer

#### Etiology
- **Ectopic ADH Production**: Small cell lung cancer, neuroblastoma
- **Pulmonary Disease**: Pneumonia, atelectasis in cancer patients
- **Medications**: Vincristine, cyclophosphamide, opioids

#### Diagnostic Criteria
- **Hyponatremia**: <135 mEq/L
- **Serum Osmolality**: <275 mOsm/kg
- **Urine Osmolality**: >100 mOsm/kg
- **Urine Sodium**: >30 mEq/L
- **Euvolemic Status**: No edema, no dehydration
- **Normal Thyroid/Adrenal**: Exclude other causes

#### Management
```
SIADH Treatment:
Mild (Na >130):
- Fluid restriction 800-1000 mL/day
- Treat underlying malignancy

Moderate (Na 125-130):
- Fluid restriction
- Consider salt tablets or urea
- Demeclocycline 300 mg PO BID

Severe (Na <125) or Symptomatic:
- Hypertonic saline 3% (bolus 100 mL, then infusion)
- Consider vaptans (conivaptan, tolvaptan)
- Slow correction: <8-12 mEq/L in 24 hours
```

### Hypercalcemia of Malignancy

#### Mechanisms
- **Local Osteolysis**: Breast cancer, multiple myeloma
- **PTHrP Production**: Squamous cell cancers, renal cell carcinoma
- **Calcitriol Production**: Lymphomas
- **Ectopic PTH**: Rare (ovarian, neuroendocrine tumors)

#### Clinical Manifestations
- **GI Symptoms**: Nausea, vomiting, constipation, pancreatitis
- **Neurologic**: Fatigue, confusion, coma
- **Renal**: Polyuria, nephrolithiasis, AKI
- **Cardiovascular**: Arrhythmias, hypertension, shortened QT

#### Treatment Algorithm
```
Hypercalcemia Management:
1. Aggressive Hydration: 2-4 L/day normal saline
2. Bisphosphonates:
   - Zoledronic acid 4 mg IV over 15 minutes
   - Pamidronate 60-90 mg IV over 2-4 hours
3. Calcitonin: 4 IU/kg SQ/IM q12h (rapid onset, short duration)
4. Denosumab: 120 mg SC weekly (if refractory to bisphosphonates)
5. Glucocorticoids: For lymphoma-mediated hypercalcemia
6. Loop Diuretics: Only after volume repletion
7. Dialysis: For severe refractory cases or renal failure
```

## Cancer and Chronic Kidney Disease

### Epidemiology and Risk
- **Increased CKD Risk**: Cancer survivors have 1.5-2× higher CKD risk
- **Contributing Factors**: 
  - Direct nephrotoxicity from treatments
  - Indirect effects (dehydration, sepsis)
  - Paraneoplastic phenomena
  - Radiation nephropathy

### Management Considerations

#### Chemotherapy Dosing in CKD
- **Dose Adjustments**: Based on eGFR and drug pharmacokinetics
- **Monitoring**: More frequent renal function monitoring
- **Alternative Agents**: Choose less nephrotoxic regimens when possible

#### Dialysis and Cancer Treatment
- **Timing**: Coordinate dialysis with chemotherapy administration
- **Drug Clearance**: Consider dialytic clearance of chemotherapeutic agents
- **Supportive Care**: Balance cancer treatment with quality of life

#### Transplantation in Cancer Survivors
- **Waiting Period**: 
  - 2-5 years for most solid tumors
  - 5 years for melanoma, breast cancer
  - Variable for hematologic malignancies
- **Risk Assessment**: Tumor type, stage, response to therapy
- **Surveillance**: Regular cancer screening post-transplant

## Special Situations

### Renal Cell Carcinoma and VEGF Pathway
- **Paraneoplastic Hypertension**: Due to increased VEGF
- **Proteinuria**: May indicate tumor activity
- **Treatment**: Targeted therapies may improve renal parameters

### CAR T-Cell Therapy
- **Cytokine Release Syndrome**: Can cause AKI
- **Neurotoxicity**: May affect autonomic regulation
- **Monitoring**: Close renal function monitoring during therapy

### Immunotherapy and Autoimmunity
- **Immune-Related Adverse Events**: 
  - Interstitial nephritis
  - Glomerulonephritis
  - Tubulointerstitial disease
- **Management**: Steroids, immunosuppression, treatment hold

## Key Practice Points

### Clinical Pearls
1. **Prevention is Key**: Aggressive hydration before nephrotoxic chemotherapy
2. **Early Recognition**: Monitor renal function closely during high-risk treatments
3. **Drug Interactions**: Avoid concurrent nephrotoxins when possible
4. **Electrolyte Management**: Proactive management of TLS and SIADH
5. **Multidisciplinary Care**: Coordinate with oncology team for optimal outcomes

### Red Flags
- **Rising Creatinine**: During chemotherapy, evaluate all potential causes
- **Proteinuria**: New onset proteinuria with anti-VEGF therapy
- **Hypertension**: Severe or refractory hypertension with targeted therapy
- **Electrolyte Crashes**: Rapid changes in sodium, potassium, calcium
- **Hematuria**: Consider hemorrhagic cystitis or TMA

### Prevention Strategies
- **Risk Assessment**: Identify high-risk patients before therapy
- **Hydration Protocols**: Standardized pre and post-therapy hydration
- **Drug Selection**: Choose less nephrotoxic alternatives when possible
- **Monitoring Schedules**: Regular renal function and electrolyte monitoring
- **Patient Education**: Early reporting of symptoms
