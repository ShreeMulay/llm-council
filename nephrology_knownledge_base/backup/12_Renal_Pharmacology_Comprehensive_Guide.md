## Pharmacokinetic Principles in Kidney Disease

### Drug Absorption
- **Gastrointestinal Changes**: Uremic gastritis, delayed gastric emptying
- **Bioavailability**: May be increased due to reduced first-pass metabolism
- **Oral Bioavailability**: Variable for some drugs (e.g., furosemide 40-60% in CKD vs 50-70% normal)

### Drug Distribution
- **Volume of Distribution**: 
  - Increased for hydrophilic drugs (fluid overload)
  - Decreased for highly protein-bound drugs (hypoalbuminemia)
- **Protein Binding**: 
  - Reduced albumin increases free fraction of acidic drugs
  - Uremic toxins may displace drugs from binding sites
- **Tissue Binding**: Accumulation in tissues with impaired elimination

### Drug Metabolism
- **Hepatic Metabolism**: Generally preserved but may be altered
- **Phase I Reactions**: Oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis (CYP450)
- **Phase II Reactions**: Conjugation (glucuronidation, sulfation)
- **Non-renal Clearance**: May increase as renal clearance decreases

### Drug Elimination
- **Glomerular Filtration**: Primary route for many drugs and metabolites
- **Tubular Secretion**: Active transport (OAT, OCT systems)
- **Tubular Reabsorption**: Passive diffusion, pH-dependent
- **Renal Clearance**: CLr = (Cu × Vu) / Cp

## Dosing Adjustment Strategies

### Estimating Renal Function

#### Creatinine-Based Equations
```
Cockcroft-Gault Equation:
CrCl (mL/min) = [(140 - Age) × Weight (kg)] / [72 × SCr (mg/dL)]
× 0.85 (if female)

MDRD Equation (2006):
eGFR = 175 × (SCr)^-1.154 × (Age)^-0.203 × (0.742 if female) × (1.212 if African American)

CKD-EPI Equation (2009):
More accurate at higher GFR levels, preferred for staging
```

#### Cystatin C-Based Equations
- **Advantages**: Less affected by muscle mass, diet, inflammation
- **Indications**: Elderly, malnourished, amputees, muscle-wasting diseases
- **CKD-EPI Cystatin C**: Alternative when creatinine unreliable

### Dosing Adjustment Methods

#### Dose Reduction Method
- **Formula**: Adjusted Dose = Normal Dose × (Patient CrCl / Normal CrCl)
- **Use**: Drugs with concentration-dependent toxicity
- **Example**: Gentamicin, vancomycin, lithium

#### Dose Interval Extension
- **Formula**: Adjusted Interval = Normal Interval × (Normal CrCl / Patient CrCl)
- **Use**: Drugs with time-dependent efficacy
- **Example**: Beta-lactams, penicillins

#### Combined Method
- **Both dose reduction and interval extension**
- **Use**: Drugs with narrow therapeutic windows
- **Example**: Digoxin, aminoglycosides

### Loading Dose Considerations
- **Generally Unchanged**: Based on volume of distribution
- **Exceptions**: Significant volume of distribution changes
- **Formula**: Loading Dose = Target Cp × Vd

## Major Drug Classes & Renal Dosing

### Antibiotics

#### Beta-Lactams
```
Penicillins:
- Amoxicillin: CrCl <30: q12h dosing
- Amoxicillin-Clavulanate: CrCl <30: q12h dosing
- Piperacillin-tazobactam: CrCl <20: 2.25g q8h

Cephalosporins:
- Cefazolin: CrCl <30: q12h dosing
- Ceftriaxone: No adjustment needed (biliary excretion)
- Cefepime: CrCl <30: q24h dosing

Carbapenems:
- Imipenem-cilastatin: CrCl <30: q12h dosing
- Meropenem: CrCl <30: q24h dosing
- Ertapenem: CrCl <30: q24h dosing
```

#### Aminoglycosides
- **Gentamicin, Tobramycin**: 
  - Loading: 2 mg/kg ideal body weight
  - Maintenance: CrCl <30: q24-48h dosing
  - Monitoring: Peak 5-10 μg/mL, Trough <2 μg/mL
- **Amikacin**: Similar dosing, higher target levels

#### Vancomycin
- **Loading**: 25-30 mg/kg actual body weight
- **Maintenance**: 
  - CrCl 30-50: q24h dosing
  - CrCl 10-29: q48-72h dosing
  - CrCl <10: q72-96h dosing
- **Monitoring**: Trough 15-20 μg/mL (AUC/MC preferred)

#### Fluoroquinolones
- **Ciprofloxacin**: CrCl <30: q24h dosing
- **Levofloxacin**: CrCl <20: q48h dosing
- **Moxifloxacin**: No adjustment needed

#### Antifungals
- **Fluconazole**: CrCl <50: 50% dose reduction
- **Voriconazole**: No adjustment needed (hepatic metabolism)
- **Echinocandins**: No adjustment needed

### Cardiovascular Drugs

#### ACE Inhibitors & ARBs
```
ACE Inhibitors:
- Lisinopril: CrCl <30: Start 5mg q48h
- Enalapril: CrCl <30: 5mg q24h
- Ramipril: CrCl <30: 2.5mg q24h

ARBs:
- Losartan: CrCl <30: 25mg q24h
- Valsartan: CrCl <30: 80mg q24h
- Irbesartan: CrCl <30: 75mg q24h
```

#### Beta-Blockers
- **Metoprolol**: No adjustment needed (hepatic metabolism)
- **Atenolol**: CrCl <35: 50mg q24h
- **Carvedilol**: No adjustment needed

#### Calcium Channel Blockers
- **Amlodipine**: No adjustment needed
- **Diltiazem**: No adjustment needed
- **Verapamil**: No adjustment needed

#### Diuretics
```
Loop Diuretics:
- Furosemide: CrCl <20: Higher doses needed (40-80mg q6-8h)
- Torsemide: CrCl <30: 20-100mg daily
- Bumetanide: CrCl <20: 1-2mg q6-8h

Thiazide Diuretics:
- Hydrochlorothiazide: Ineffective when CrCl <30
- Metolazone: Can be used down to CrCl 10-20
- Chlorthalidone: CrCl <30: Avoid

Potassium-Sparing:
- Spironolactone: CrCl <30: Avoid or use 12.5mg q24h
- Eplerenone: CrCl <30: Avoid
- Amiloride: CrCl <30: Avoid
```

### Antidiabetic Drugs

#### Insulin
- **Clearance**: Reduced renal clearance
- **Dosing**: 20-50% dose reduction when eGFR <30
- **Monitoring**: Frequent glucose monitoring, avoid hypoglycemia

#### Oral Agents
```
Metformin:
- Contraindicated when eGFR <30
- Caution when eGFR 30-45
- Hold during acute illness, contrast procedures

Sulfonylureas:
- Glyburide: Avoid when eGFR <60
- Glipizide: No adjustment needed
- Glimepiride: CrCl <30: Start 1mg daily

DPP-4 Inhibitors:
- Sitagliptin: CrCl <30: 25mg q24h
- Saxagliptin: CrCl <30: 2.5mg q24h
- Linagliptin: No adjustment needed

SGLT2 Inhibitors:
- Generally ineffective when eGFR <30-45
- Canagliflozin: eGFR <30: Avoid
- Empagliflozin: eGFR <20: Avoid
- Dapagliflozin: eGFR <30: Avoid (except for HF/CKD indication)

GLP-1 Agonists:
- Exenatide: CrCl <30: Avoid
- Liraglutide: No adjustment needed
- Semaglutide: No adjustment needed
```

## Nephrotoxic Drugs

### Aminoglycosides
- **Mechanism**: Proximal tubular cell injury, mitochondrial dysfunction
- **Risk Factors**: High peak levels, prolonged therapy, concurrent nephrotoxins
- **Prevention**: 
  - Once-daily dosing
  - Therapeutic drug monitoring
  - Adequate hydration
  - Avoid concurrent NSAIDs, contrast

### Amphotericin B
- **Mechanism**: Tubular cell membrane damage, vasoconstriction
- **Risk Factors**: High cumulative dose, rapid infusion
- **Prevention**:
  - Lipid formulations (less nephrotoxic)
  - Sodium loading (0.9% saline 1L before and after)
  - Slow infusion over 2-6 hours
  - Monitor potassium and magnesium

### Contrast Media
- **Mechanism**: Renal vasoconstriction, direct tubular toxicity
- **Risk Factors**: Pre-existing CKD, diabetes, dehydration, high contrast volume
- **Prevention**:
  - Hydration: 0.9% saline 1mL/kg/hour 12 hours before and after
  - Low-osmolar or iso-osmolar contrast
  - Minimal effective dose
  - N-acetylcysteine: controversial benefit
  - Hold nephrotoxic medications

### NSAIDs
- **Mechanism**: Prostaglandin synthesis inhibition → afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction
- **Risk Factors**: Volume depletion, CKD, heart failure, cirrhosis
- **Prevention**:
  - Avoid in high-risk patients
  - Use lowest effective dose
  - Short duration only
  - Consider acetaminophen or COX-2 selective agents

### Calcineurin Inhibitors
- **Cyclosporine, Tacrolimus**: Afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction
- **Prevention**:
  - Therapeutic drug monitoring
  - Avoid high trough levels
  - Maintain adequate hydration
  - Consider CNI-sparing protocols

## Drug Interactions in Kidney Disease

### Pharmacokinetic Interactions

#### Altered Protein Binding
- **Displacement**: Highly protein-bound drugs displaced by uremic toxins
- **Examples**: Warfarin, phenytoin, valproic acid
- **Management**: Monitor free drug levels, adjust doses

#### Reduced Renal Clearance
- **Accumulation**: Drugs primarily renally excreted accumulate
- **Examples**: Digoxin, lithium, metformin
- **Management**: Dose adjustment, therapeutic monitoring

#### Competition for Tubular Secretion
- **Mechanism**: Competition for OAT/OCT transport systems
- **Examples**: Probenecid, cimetidine, trimethoprim
- **Management**: Monitor for increased levels of affected drugs

### Pharmacodynamic Interactions

#### Additive Nephrotoxicity
- **Combinations**: Aminoglycosides + NSAIDs, contrast + ACE inhibitors
- **Management**: Avoid combinations, monitor renal function closely

#### Enhanced Electrolyte Effects
- **Hyperkalemia**: ACE inhibitors + potassium-sparing diuretics + NSAIDs
- **Hyponatremia**: Thiazides + SSRIs
- **Management**: Monitor electrolytes, adjust doses

## Special Population Considerations

### Elderly Patients
- **Physiologic Changes**: Decreased GFR, reduced muscle mass
- **Dosing Principles**:
  - Start low, go slow
  - Use renal function estimates (not serum creatinine alone)
  - Monitor for adverse effects
  - Consider non-renal clearance changes

### Pediatric Patients
- **Dosing**: Weight-based dosing (mg/kg)
- **Renal Function**: Schwartz equation for eGFR estimation
- **Special Considerations**: 
  - Immature renal function in neonates
  - Volume of distribution differences
  - Long-term effects of nephrotoxic drugs

### Pregnancy
- **Physiologic Changes**: Increased GFR (50%), increased plasma volume
- **Dosing**: May need increased doses for renally excreted drugs
- **Safety**: Avoid known teratogens (ACE inhibitors, ARBs, warfarin)

### Liver Disease
- **Hepatorenal Syndrome**: Reduced renal perfusion
- **Drug Metabolism**: Impaired phase I/II reactions
- **Protein Binding**: Reduced albumin → increased free drug
- **Management**: Conservative dosing, monitor closely

## Dialysis and Drug Removal

### Hemodialysis

#### Drug Characteristics Affecting Removal
- **Molecular Weight**: <500 Da removed efficiently
- **Water Solubility**: Hydrophilic drugs removed better
- **Protein Binding**: <90% binding required for significant removal
- **Volume of Distribution**: <1 L/kg required for efficient removal

#### Dosing Adjustments
- **Supplemental Dosing**: Post-dialysis dosing for significantly removed drugs
- **Examples**: 
  - Gentamicin: 1-2 mg/kg after dialysis
  - Vancomycin: 500-1000 mg after dialysis
  - Phenytoin: Minimal removal, no adjustment needed

### Peritoneal Dialysis
- **Lower Clearance**: Generally less efficient than hemodialysis
- **Continuous Nature**: Steady-state concentrations
- **Drug-Specific**: Individual assessment required

### CRRT
- **High Clearance**: Similar to native kidney function
- **Dosing**: Often require increased doses or continuous infusion
- **Monitoring**: Frequent drug level monitoring essential

## Therapeutic Drug Monitoring

### Drugs Requiring Monitoring
- **Aminoglycosides**: Peak and trough levels
- **Vancomycin**: Trough or AUC/MIC
- **Digoxin**: Trough levels 0.5-0.9 ng/mL
- **Lithium**: Trough levels 0.6-1.2 mEq/L
- **Phenytoin**: Total and free levels
- **Calcineurin Inhibitors**: Trough levels

### Monitoring Principles
- **Timing**: 
  - Trough: Immediately before next dose
  - Peak: 30-60 minutes after IV infusion
- **Frequency**: 
  - Initial: 2-3 times weekly
  - Stable: Weekly to monthly
- **Sample Handling**: Proper collection and storage

## Key Practice Points

### Clinical Pearls
1. **eGFR vs CrCl**: Use eGFR for drug dosing when available
2. **Loading Doses**: Usually unchanged in renal failure
3. **Therapeutic Monitoring**: Essential for narrow therapeutic index drugs
4. **Hydration**: Key prevention strategy for nephrotoxic drugs
5. **Drug Interactions**: Consider additive nephrotoxicity and electrolyte effects

### Red Flags
- **Rising Creatinine**: Evaluate all medications for nephrotoxicity
- **Electrolyte Abnormalities**: Consider drug-induced causes
- **Drug Levels**: Toxic levels require immediate dose adjustment
- **Allergic Reactions**: May be more severe in renal failure
- **Bleeding**: Consider accumulation of anticoagulants, antiplatelets

### Prevention Strategies
- **Baseline Assessment**: Renal function before initiating nephrotoxic drugs
- **Regular Monitoring**: Creatinine, electrolytes, drug levels
- **Dose Adjustments**: Proactive adjustments based on eGFR
- **Hydration**: Adequate volume status before procedures
- **Drug Selection**: Choose less nephrotoxic alternatives when possible
