## Indications for Acute Renal Replacement Therapy

### Absolute Indications
- **Refractory Hyperkalemia**: K+ >6.5 mEq/L or ECG changes despite medical therapy
- **Severe Metabolic Acidosis**: pH <7.1-7.2 or bicarbonate <10-12 mEq/L
- **Volume Overload**: Refractory pulmonary edema, diuretic resistance
- **Uremic Complications**: Pericarditis, encephalopathy, coagulopathy, intractable nausea/vomiting
- **Certain Toxin Removal**: Lithium, ethylene glycol, methanol, salicylates

### Relative Indications
- **Non-oliguric AKI**: BUN >100 mg/dL, creatinine >10 mg/dL
- **Oliguria**: Urine output <0.3 mL/kg/hour >24 hours or anuria >12 hours
- **Severe Dysnatremia**: Na+ <120 or >160 mEq/L
- **Tumor Lysis Syndrome**: Preventive therapy in high-risk patients
- **Temperature Control**: Severe hyperthermia (>41°C)

### Timing Considerations
- **Early Initiation**: Within 12-24 hours of AKI diagnosis in critically ill
- **Delayed Initiation**: May be appropriate in stable patients
- **KDIGO Recommendations**: Consider emergent RRT for life-threatening changes
- **Individualized**: Based on clinical context, trajectory, comorbidities

## Modality Selection

### Intermittent Hemodialysis (IHD)

#### Indications
- **Hemodynamically Stable Patients**
- **Rapid Solute Removal Required**: Severe hyperkalemia, toxin ingestion
- **Limited ICU Resources**: Machine availability, nursing expertise
- **Chronic Dialysis Patients**: Acute on chronic kidney disease

#### Advantages
- **Rapid Clearance**: High efficiency for small solutes
- **Short Treatment Time**: 3-4 hours per session
- **Resource Efficient**: Lower nursing requirements post-initiation
- **Cost Effective**: Lower consumable costs

#### Disadvantages
- **Hemodynamic Instability**: Risk of hypotension
- **Limited Continuous Control**: Intermittent nature
- **Cerebral Edema Risk**: Rapid osmolar shifts
- **Limited in ICU**: Requires transport to dialysis unit

### Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy (CRRT)

#### Modalities

##### Slow Continuous Ultrafiltration (SCUF)
- **Mechanism**: Fluid removal only, no significant solute clearance
- **Indications**: Isolated volume overload, hemodynamically unstable
- **Flow Rates**: 100-200 mL/hour ultrafiltration
- **Limitations**: No significant solute clearance

##### Continuous Veno-Venous Hemofiltration (CVVH)
- **Mechanism**: Convection-based solute clearance
- **Replacement Fluid**: Pre-filter or post-filter
- **Flow Rates**: 20-35 mL/kg/hour effluent
- **Indications**: Sepsis, inflammatory states, middle molecule clearance

##### Continuous Veno-Venous Hemodialysis (CVVHD)
- **Mechanism**: Diffusion-based solute clearance
- **Dialysate Flow**: Counter-current to blood flow
- **Flow Rates**: 20-35 mL/kg/hour effluent
- **Indications**: Small solute clearance, electrolyte management

##### Continuous Veno-Venous Hemodiafiltration (CVVHDF)
- **Mechanism**: Combined diffusion and convection
- **Replacement + Dialysate**: Both modalities utilized
- **Flow Rates**: 20-35 mL/kg/hour effluent total
- **Indications**: Broad solute clearance, most versatile

#### Advantages
- **Hemodynamic Stability**: Gradual fluid/solute removal
- **Continuous Control**: Steady-state physiology
- **Cerebral Protection**: Avoids rapid osmolar shifts
- **Nutritional Support**: Allows aggressive nutrition

#### Disadvantages
- **Resource Intensive**: Continuous nursing supervision
- **Anticoagulation Required**: Circuit clotting risk
- **Limited Mobility**: Patient bed-bound
- **Higher Costs**: Consumables, nursing time

### Hybrid Therapies

#### Sustained Low-Efficiency Dialysis (SLED)
- **Duration**: 6-12 hours
- **Flow Rates**: 100-200 mL/min blood, 300 mL/min dialysate
- **Indications**: Intermediate stability, resource limitations
- **Advantages**: Balance between IHD and CRRT

#### Extended Daily Dialysis (EDD)
- **Duration**: 8-12 hours
- **Similar to SLED**: Longer conventional dialysis
- **ICU Friendly**: Can be performed at bedside

## Vascular Access for Acute Dialysis

### Catheter Types
- **Non-Tunneled Catheters**: Short-term use (<3 weeks)
- **Tunneled Catheters**: Longer-term use (>3 weeks)
- **Cuffed Catheters**: Reduced infection risk

### Catheter Sites

#### Preferred Sites
- **Right Internal Jugular**: Straight path to right atrium
- **Left Internal Jugular**: Acceptable alternative
- **Femoral Vein**: Bedside insertion, higher infection risk

#### Alternative Sites
- **External Jugular**: Limited success rate
- **Subclavian**: High stenosis risk, avoid if future AVF planned
- **Transhepatic**: Expertise required, specific indications

### Catheter Sizes
- **Double Lumen**: 11-14 French (adult)
- **Triple Lumen**: Additional monitoring port
- **Pediatric**: 7-9 French (size/age dependent)

### Insertion Complications
- **Immediate**: Arterial puncture, pneumothorax, arrhythmias
- **Early**: Catheter malfunction, infection, thrombosis
- **Late**: Vascular stenosis, catheter erosion

## Anticoagulation Strategies

### Unfractionated Heparin (UFH)

#### Protocol
```
Loading Dose: 2000-5000 units IV
Maintenance: 500-2000 units/hour infusion
Target: aPTT 1.5-2.5 times control or ACT 180-220 seconds
Monitoring: Every 4-6 hours initially, then daily
```

#### Advantages
- **Rapid Onset/Offset**: Protamine reversal available
- **Familiar Protocol**: Widely used, well understood
- **Cost Effective**: Inexpensive

#### Disadvantages
- **Bleeding Risk**: Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT)
- **Monitoring Required**: Regular laboratory testing
- **Variable Response**: Patient-specific metabolism

### Regional Citrate Anticoagulation

#### Protocol
```
Citrate Infusion: 3-4 mmol/L blood flow
Calcium Infusion: 1-1.5 mmol/L replacement fluid
Target Ionized Calcium: 0.8-1.2 mmol/L (systemic), 0.2-0.4 mmol/L (circuit)
Monitoring: Ionized calcium every 4-6 hours
```

#### Advantages
- **Bleeding Risk Minimal**: No systemic anticoagulation
- **Long Circuit Life**: Reduced clotting
- **Anti-inflammatory**: Calcium chelation effects

#### Disadvantages
- **Complex Protocol**: Requires expertise
- **Metabolic Complications**: Citrate accumulation, metabolic alkalosis
- **Monitoring Intensive**: Frequent laboratory testing

### No Anticoagulation
- **Indications**: High bleeding risk, coagulopathy
- **Strategies**: High blood flow, saline flushes, circuit coating
- **Limitations**: Short circuit life, frequent filter changes

### Alternative Anticoagulants
- **Low Molecular Weight Heparin**: Enoxaparin, dalteparin
- **Direct Thrombin Inhibitors**: Argatroban (HIT patients)
- **Prostacyclin**: Antiplatelet, vasodilatory effects

## Prescription & Dosing

### CRRT Dosing

#### Effluent Flow Rate
- **Standard Dose**: 20-25 mL/kg/hour
- **High Dose**: 30-35 mL/kg/hour (severe sepsis, high catabolism)
- **Low Dose**: 15-20 mL/kg/hour (stable patients, renal recovery)

#### Dose Calculation
```
Example: 70 kg patient, standard dose
Effluent Flow = 70 kg × 25 mL/kg/hour = 1750 mL/hour
Daily Volume = 1750 mL/hour × 24 hours = 42 L/day
```

#### Prescription Adjustments
- **Fluid Overload**: Increase ultrafiltration component
- **Hyperkalemia**: Increase dialysis component
- **Sepsis**: Increase hemofiltration component
- **Nutritional Needs**: Adjust for amino acid losses

### IHD Dosing

#### Standard Prescription
- **Blood Flow**: 250-400 mL/min
- **Dialysate Flow**: 500-800 mL/min
- **Duration**: 3-4 hours
- **Frequency**: Daily or every other day

#### Kt/V Target
- **Acute Dialysis**: Kt/V ≥1.2 per session
- **Sustained Therapy**: Weekly Kt/V ≥3.9

#### Solute Removal Goals
- **BUN Reduction**: 50-70% per session
- **Creatinine Clearance**: Individualized based on clinical status
- **Electrolyte Correction**: Gradual correction to avoid complications

## Fluid Management

### Fluid Balance Assessment
- **Daily Weights**: Accurate bed scales
- **Input/Output Monitoring**: Strict measurement
- **Physical Examination**: Edema, lung sounds, JVD
- **Hemodynamic Parameters**: Blood pressure, heart rate, CVP

### Ultrafiltration Strategies

#### CRRT Ultrafiltration
- **Net Removal**: 100-500 mL/hour based on needs
- **Adjustment**: Hourly based on hemodynamics
- **Monitoring**: Continuous weight tracking

#### IHD Ultrafiltration
- **Total Removal**: 1-4 L per session
- **Rate Limitation**: <10 mL/kg/hour to prevent hypotension
- **Blood Pressure Monitoring**: Every 15-30 minutes

### Complications of Fluid Removal
- **Hypotension**: Reduced preload, myocardial stunning
- **Electrolyte Shifts**: Rapid solute removal
- **Muscle Cramps**: Dialysis disequilibrium
- **Hypovolemia**: Tissue hypoperfusion

## Electrolyte & Acid-Base Management

### Potassium Management

#### CRRT Potassium Control
- **Dialysate/Replacement Potassium**: 2-4 mEq/L (adjust based on levels)
- **Monitoring**: Every 4-6 hours initially
- **Target**: 4.0-4.5 mEq/L

#### IHD Potassium Removal
- **Dialysate Potassium**: 1-3 mEq/L for hyperkalemia
- **Duration**: 2-4 hours based on initial level
- **Rebound**: Monitor 2-4 hours post-dialysis

### Sodium Management
- **Dialysate Sodium**: 140-145 mEq/L (avoid rapid shifts)
- **Hyponatremia Correction**: <8-10 mEq/L/24 hours
- **Hypernatremia Correction**: <10-12 mEq/L/24 hours

### Acid-Base Correction
- **Bicarbonate Dialysate**: 35-40 mEq/L
- **Citrate Effects**: Metabolic alkalosis potential
- **Lactate Clearance**: Monitor in liver failure

## CRRT Machine Management

### Common Machines
- **Prismaflex**: Baxter, widely used
- **Aquarius**: Edwards Lifesciences
- **CRRT-Ready**: Fresenius Medical Care
- **MultiFiltrate**: Fresenius

### Machine Setup
- **Priming**: 2-3 L saline with heparin
- **Air Detection**: Automatic air removal systems
- **Pressure Monitoring**: Pre-filter, post-filter, return pressures
- **Safety Features**: Blood leak detection, pressure limits

### Alarm Management

#### Common Alarms
- **High Pressure**: Catheter clotting, kinking
- **Low Pressure**: Disconnection, hypovolemia
- **Air Detection**: Air in circuit
- **Blood Leak**: Membrane rupture

#### Troubleshooting
- **Circuit Clotting**: Check anticoagulation, blood flow
- **Access Problems**: Reposition catheter, check patency
- **Pressure Issues**: Verify connections, check for kinks

## Complications Management

### Hemodynamic Complications

#### Hypotension
- **Causes**: Rapid fluid removal, vasodilation, myocardial depression
- **Prevention**: Gradual ultrafiltration, cool dialysate, sodium profiling
- **Treatment**: Fluid bolus, vasopressors, reduce ultrafiltration rate

#### Arrhythmias
- **Causes**: Electrolyte shifts, ischemia, acidosis
- **Prevention**: Gradual electrolyte correction, continuous monitoring
- **Treatment**: Specific antiarrhythmic therapy, electrolyte correction

### Bleeding Complications

#### Access Site Bleeding
- **Prevention**: Proper insertion technique, adequate pressure
- **Management**: Local pressure, reversal of anticoagulation
- **Considerations**: Ultrasound guidance for difficult insertions

#### Systemic Bleeding
- **Risk Factors**: Anticoagulation, coagulopathy, thrombocytopenia
- **Prevention**: Regional anticoagulation, minimal systemic heparin
- **Management**: Discontinue anticoagulation, blood product support

### Infectious Complications

#### Catheter-Related Infections
- **Prevention**: Aseptic technique, chlorhexidine preparation
- **Surveillance**: Regular exit site inspection
- **Management**: Antibiotics, catheter removal if indicated

#### Sepsis
- **Risk Factors**: Immunocompromised, prolonged catheter use
- **Prevention**: Early catheter removal when possible
- **Management**: Broad-spectrum antibiotics, source control

### Technical Complications

#### Membrane Clotting
- **Prevention**: Adequate anticoagulation, optimal blood flow
- **Early Detection**: Rising transmembrane pressure
- **Management**: Anticoagulation adjustment, circuit replacement

#### Catheter Malfunction
- **Causes**: Position, thrombosis, kinking
- **Management**: Repositioning, thrombolytic infusion, replacement

## Special Populations

### Sepsis and Septic Shock
- **Indications**: Cytokine clearance, fluid overload, AKI
- **Modality Preference**: CVVH or CVVHDF for middle molecule clearance
- **Dosing**: Higher intensity (25-35 mL/kg/hour)
- **Anticoagulation**: Citrate preferred to reduce inflammation

### Liver Failure
- **Indications**: Hepatorenal syndrome, ammonia clearance
- **Considerations**: Lactate clearance, coagulopathy
- **Modality**: CVVHDF with high convective component
- **Monitoring**: Frequent ammonia, lactate levels

### Cardiac Surgery Patients
- **Risk Factors**: Low cardiac output, inflammation, CPB-related AKI
- **Timing**: Early initiation may improve outcomes
- **Modality**: CRRT preferred for hemodynamic stability
- **Fluid Management**: Negative balance after initial resuscitation

### Trauma Patients
- **Indications**: Rhabdomyolysis, crush injury, massive transfusion
- **Modality**: High-flow CVVH for myoglobin clearance
- **Complications**: Hypercatabolism, multi-organ failure
- **Monitoring**: CK levels, potassium, compartment syndrome

### Pediatric Patients
- **Special Considerations**: Size-appropriate equipment, developmental needs
- **Dosing**: Higher mL/kg/hour requirements
- **Access**: Size-appropriate catheters, expertise required
- **Family Support**: Communication, education, involvement

## Nutrition Considerations

### Protein Requirements
- **CRRT Patients**: 2.0-2.5 g/kg/day (higher losses)
- **IHD Patients**: 1.2-1.5 g/kg/day
- **Monitoring**: Pre-albumin, nitrogen balance

### Amino Acid Losses
- **CRRT**: 10-15 g/day (convective losses)
- **IHD**: 5-8 g/day per session
- **Replacement**: Amino acid-enriched solutions, supplementation

### Caloric Needs
- **Hypermetabolism**: 25-30 kcal/kg/day in critically ill
- **Adjustments**: Based on nutritional status, tolerance
- **Monitoring**: Glycemic control, triglyceride levels

## Drug Dosing Adjustments

### Pharmacokinetic Considerations
- **Molecular Weight**: <500 Da cleared by diffusion, >500 Da by convection
- **Protein Binding**: Unbound fraction available for clearance
- **Volume of Distribution**: Tissue penetration affects removal
- **Renal Replacement**: Augmented clearance in CRRT

### Dosing Strategies

#### Antibiotics
- **Beta-lactams**: Increased dosing frequency, continuous infusion
- **Vancomycin**: Higher loading dose, therapeutic drug monitoring
- **Aminoglycosides**: Extended interval dosing, level monitoring
- **Antifungals**: Variable clearance, individualized dosing

#### Cardiovascular Drugs
- **Beta-blockers**: Minimal clearance, dose adjustment not needed
- **ACE Inhibitors**: Variable clearance, monitor for hypotension
- **Antiarrhythmics**: Individualized based on levels, clinical effect

#### Sedation/Analgesia
- **Propofol**: Minimal clearance, hepatic metabolism
- **Fentanyl**: Some clearance, monitor for accumulation
- **Midazolam**: Metabolites may accumulate, dose reduction

## Discontinuation & Recovery

### Recovery Assessment
- **Urine Output**: >0.5 mL/kg/hour sustained >6-12 hours
- **Creatinine**: Decreasing trend, <1.5 mg/dL
- **Hemodynamics**: Stable without vasopressors
- **Acid-Base**: Normal pH without dialysis

### Weaning Strategies

#### CRRT Weaning
- **Gradual Reduction**: Decrease effluent flow by 25% increments
- **Intermittent Trials**: 12-24 hour off periods
- **Monitoring**: Creatinine, potassium, fluid balance

#### IHD Transition
- **Frequency Reduction**: Daily to every other day
- **Duration Adjustment**: Based on clinical status
- **Access Planning**: Transition to chronic access if needed

### Discontinuation Criteria
- **Renal Function Recovery**: Sustained improvement
- **Goals of Care**: Palliative care decision
- **Futility**: No renal recovery after 2-3 weeks
- **Complications**: Unmanageable bleeding, infection

## Quality Assurance & Outcomes

### Quality Metrics
- **Timing**: <24 hours from AKI diagnosis to RRT initiation
- **Adequacy**: Delivered dose ≥80% of prescribed
- **Complications**: Circuit life >24 hours, bleeding rates
- **Outcomes**: ICU mortality, renal recovery rates

### Continuous Quality Improvement
- **Daily Review**: Prescription adequacy, complications
- **Weekly Analysis**: Outcomes, resource utilization
- **Monthly Assessment**: Protocol compliance, staff education

### Outcome Predictors
- **Early Initiation**: Improved survival in septic AKI
- **Adequate Dosing**: Better renal recovery rates
- **Hemodynamic Stability**: Reduced organ dysfunction
- **Nutritional Support**: Improved wound healing, recovery

## Key Practice Points

### Clinical Pearls
1. **Early Initiation**: Consider within 12 hours for septic AKI
2. **Citrate Anticoagulation**: Preferred for bleeding risk patients
3. **Dose Accuracy**: Account for downtime in CRRT prescriptions
4. **Drug Monitoring**: Essential for antibiotics, vancomycin levels
5. **Fluid Balance**: Negative balance after initial resuscitation phase

### Red Flags
- **Circuit Clotting**: Check anticoagulation, blood flow access
- **Hypotension**: Reduce ultrafiltration rate, assess volume status
- **Bleeding**: Discontinue anticoagulation, evaluate coagulation status
- **Electrolyte Crashes**: Monitor frequently during initiation
- **Access Problems**: Early intervention, consider replacement

### Prevention Strategies
- **AKI Prevention**: Adequate hydration, avoid nephrotoxins
- **Circuit Longevity**: Optimal anticoagulation, blood flow
- **Infection Control**: Aseptic technique, early catheter removal
- **Hemodynamic Stability**: Gradual fluid removal, monitoring
- **Nutritional Support**: Early enteral nutrition, protein supplementation
