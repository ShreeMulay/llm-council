## Chemotherapy-Induced Nephrotoxicity

### Platinum Compounds

#### Cisplatin Nephrotoxicity
- **Incidence**: 20-30% acute kidney injury, higher with cumulative doses
- **Mechanism**: Proximal tubular cell uptake via organic cation transporters, DNA adduct formation, oxidative stress
- **Risk Factors**: 
  - Cumulative dose >300 mg/m²
  - Pre-existing CKD, dehydration, concurrent nephrotoxins
  - Advanced age, hypertension, diabetes
- **Clinical Presentation**: 
  - Non-oliguric AKI 3-10 days post-administration
  - Magnesium wasting (hypomagnesemia)
  - Polyuria, salt wasting
  - Possible progression to CKD

#### Prevention and Management
```
Prevention Strategies:
- Aggressive hydration: 1-3 L/m² before and after cisplatin
- Magnesium supplementation: 12-24 mEq pre and post-treatment
- Amifostine: 200 mg/m² IV 30 minutes before cisplatin
- Avoid concurrent nephrotoxins: NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast

Management of AKI:
- Supportive care, maintain adequate hydration
- Correct electrolyte abnormalities
- Consider dose reduction or discontinuation
- Monitor for chronic kidney disease development
```

#### Carboplatin and Oxaliplatin
- **Carboplatin**: Lower nephrotoxicity risk, dose-adjusted by Calvert formula
- **Oxaliplatin**: Minimal nephrotoxicity, rare AKI cases reported

### Antimetabolites

#### Methotrexate
- **Mechanism**: Precipitation in renal tubules, direct tubular toxicity
- **Risk Factors**: High dose therapy, dehydration, acidic urine, drug interactions
- **Prevention Protocol**:
```
High-Dose Methotrexate (>1 g/m²):
- Pre-hydration: 2-3 L/m² starting 12 hours before
- Urine Alkalization: Sodium bicarbonate to maintain pH >7.0
- Post-MTX Monitoring: Levels at 24, 48, 72 hours
- Leucovorin Rescue: Based on MTX levels
- Hydration: Continue until MTX <0.1 μM
```

#### 5-Fluorouracil and Capecitabine
- **Rare AKI**: Usually due to dehydration or thrombotic microangiopathy
- **Monitoring**: Baseline and periodic renal function

#### Gemcitabine
- **TMA Risk**: 0.015-0.31% incidence, higher with combination therapy
- **Presentation**: MAHA, thrombocytopenia, renal dysfunction
- **Management**: Discontinue drug, consider plasma exchange

### Alkylating Agents

#### Ifosfamide
- **Incidence**: 5-30% AKI, dose-dependent
- **Mechanism**: Chloroacetaldehyde metabolite toxicity
- **Clinical Features**: 
  - Proximal tubular dysfunction (Fanconi syndrome)
  - Glycosuria, phosphaturia, aminoaciduria
  - Possible progression to CKD
- **Prevention**: 
  - Mesna protection (does not prevent renal toxicity)
  - Adequate hydration
  - Avoid cumulative doses >60 g/m²

#### Cyclophosphamide
- **Lower Nephrotoxicity**: Compared to ifosfamide
- **SIADH**: Can cause hyponatremia
- **Hemorrhagic Cystitis**: Primary urologic toxicity

### Targeted Therapies

#### Anti-VEGF Agents (Bevacizumab, Sunitinib, Sorafenib)
- **Mechanism**: Inhibition of VEGF signaling → glomerular endothelial injury
- **Clinical Syndromes**:
  - Proteinuria (30-40%)
  - Hypertension (20-30%)
  - Thrombotic microangiopathy
  - Minimal change disease/FSGS
- **Management**:
  - Monitor proteinuria and blood pressure every 2-3 weeks
  - ACE inhibitors/ARBs for proteinuria
  - Hold therapy for proteinuria >2 g/day or uncontrolled hypertension

#### BCR-ABL Inhibitors (Imatinib, Dasatinib)
- **Imatinib**: Generally well-tolerated, rare AKI
- **Dasatinib**: Higher risk of fluid retention, pleural effusions

#### PARP Inhibitors (2024 Update)

**FDA-Approved Agents:**
```
- Olaparib (Lynparza): Ovarian, breast, prostate, pancreatic cancers (BRCA mutated)
- Rucaparib (Rubraca): Ovarian, prostate cancers
- Niraparib (Zejula): Ovarian, fallopian tube, peritoneal cancers
- Talazoparib (Talzenna): Breast cancer (BRCA mutated)
```

**Renal Toxicity Profile:**
```
Incidence of AKI:
- Overall: 5-15% (mostly Grade 1-2)
- Severe AKI (Grade 3): 1-3%

Mechanisms:
- Direct tubular toxicity
- Crystal nephropathy (particularly with olaparib metabolites)
- Hemolytic uremic syndrome/TMA (rare, <1%)
- Interstitial nephritis (case reports)

Risk Factors:
- Pre-existing CKD
- Concurrent nephrotoxic medications
- Dehydration
- Older age (>70 years)

Clinical Presentation:
- Non-oliguric AKI (most common)
- Acute rise in creatinine 2-8 weeks after initiation
- TMA: MAHA, thrombocytopenia, AKI (rare)

Management:
- Grade 1-2 AKI: Continue with dose reduction (25-50%)
- Grade 3-4 AKI: Hold drug, supportive care, resume at lower dose when recovered
- TMA: Discontinue permanently, consider eculizumab
- Dose adjustment for baseline CKD:
  - eGFR 30-60: Consider 25% dose reduction
  - eGFR <30: Limited data, use with caution
```

#### BTK Inhibitors (Bruton Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitors)

**Available Agents (2024):**
```
First-Generation (Irreversible):
- Ibrutinib (Imbruvica): CLL, MCL, marginal zone lymphoma
- Acalabrutinib (Calquence): CLL, MCL
- Zanubrutinib (Brukinsa): CLL, MCL, marginal zone lymphoma

Second-Generation (Reversible):
- Pirtobrutinib (Jaypirca): CLL (after prior BTK inhibitor)
```

**Renal Toxicity:**
```
Incidence:
- Direct AKI: Rare (<2%)
- TMA: 1-2% (higher with ibrutinib)
- Proteinuria: 5-10%
- Hypertension: 15-20% (indirect renal effects)

BTK Inhibitor-Associated TMA:
- Median onset: 8-12 months (range: weeks to years)
- ADAMTS13 typically normal
- Pathogenesis: Endothelial dysfunction, platelet activation
- Management:
  - Discontinue BTK inhibitor immediately
  - Plasma exchange: Limited benefit
  - Complement inhibition (eculizumab): Case reports of success
  - Alternative therapy: Switch to venetoclax or other non-BTKi regimen

Hypertension Management:
- Common with ibrutinib (15-25%)
- Target BP <130/80
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs preferred
- Monitor renal function with RAAS blockade
```

#### Antibody-Drug Conjugates (ADCs) - Emerging Class

**FDA-Approved ADCs (2023-2024):**
```
Enfortumab Vedotin (Padcev):
- Indication: Urothelial carcinoma
- Payload: Monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE)
- Renal toxicity: Hyperglycemia (54%), peripheral neuropathy

Trastuzumab Deruxtecan (Enhertu):
- Indication: HER2+ breast, gastric cancers
- Payload: Topoisomerase I inhibitor
- Renal toxicity: Minimal (interstitial lung disease main concern)

Sacituzumab Govitecan (Trodelvy):
- Indication: Triple-negative breast, urothelial cancers
- Payload: SN-38 (topoisomerase I inhibitor)
- Renal toxicity: Diarrhea-related dehydration, prerenal AKI

Tisotumab Vedotin (Tivdak):
- Indication: Recurrent/metastatic cervical cancer
- Payload: MMAE
- Renal toxicity: Minimal direct nephrotoxicity
```

**ADC Nephrotoxicity Considerations:**
```
General Principles:
- Toxicity determined by payload, not antibody
- MMAE payloads: Peripheral neuropathy, limited renal toxicity
- Topoisomerase inhibitors: Diarrhea → dehydration → prerenal AKI
- Maytansinoids (DM1, DM4): Hepatotoxicity, minimal renal

Monitoring:
- Baseline and periodic renal function
- Prevent dehydration (aggressive hydration for diarrhea-prone ADCs)
- Monitor electrolytes (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia with diarrhea)

Dosing in Renal Impairment:
- Most ADCs: No dose adjustment for eGFR >30
- eGFR <30: Limited data, use with caution
- Dialysis: Generally not dialyzable (large molecular weight)
```

#### KRAS G12C Inhibitors (Novel Class)

**FDA-Approved Agents:**
```
- Sotorasib (Lumakras): NSCLC with KRAS G12C mutation (FDA 2021)
- Adagrasib (Krazati): NSCLC with KRAS G12C mutation (FDA 2022)
```

**Renal Safety Profile:**
```
Overall Renal Toxicity: Low
- AKI incidence: <5% (mostly Grade 1)
- Mechanism: Unclear, likely multifactorial
- Hepatotoxicity more common concern

Electrolyte Abnormalities:
- Hyponatremia: 10-15%
- Hypomagnesemia: 5-10%
- Monitor and replace as needed
```

#### FGFR Inhibitors

**FDA-Approved Agents (2024):**
```
- Erdafitinib (Balversa): Urothelial carcinoma with FGFR alterations
- Pemigatinib (Pemazyre): Cholangiocarcinoma with FGFR2 fusions
- Infigratinib (Truseltiq): Cholangiocarcinoma with FGFR2 fusions
- Futibatinib (Lytgobi): Cholangiocarcinoma with FGFR2 fusions
```

**Renal and Electrolyte Toxicity:**
```
Hyperphosphatemia (CHARACTERISTIC):
- Incidence: 70-80% of patients
- Mechanism: FGF23 inhibition → increased renal phosphate reabsorption
- Grade 3-4: 15-20%
- Onset: Within 2-4 weeks of initiation

Management of Hyperphosphatemia:
- Dietary phosphate restriction (<800-1000 mg/day)
- Phosphate binders: Sevelamer, lanthanum carbonate
- Dose interruption if phosphate >7 mg/dL
- Dose reduction if recurrent

Hypophosphatemia (Paradoxical):
- Can occur with dose interruption or after chronic therapy
- Monitor phosphate weekly initially, then monthly

Other Electrolyte Abnormalities:
- Hypocalcemia: Secondary to hyperphosphatemia (calcium-phosphate binding)
- Hyponatremia: 5-10%
- Monitor calcium, magnesium, sodium regularly

Direct Renal Toxicity:
- AKI: Rare (<5%)
- Nephrocalcinosis: Case reports with chronic hyperphosphatemia
- Regular renal function monitoring recommended
```

#### Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors (ICIs)

**Available Agents (2024):**
```
PD-1 Inhibitors:
- Pembrolizumab (Keytruda): Various solid tumors
- Nivolumab (Opdivo): Melanoma, NSCLC, RCC, others
- Cemiplimab (Libtayo): Cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma
- Dostarlimab (Jemperli): Endometrial cancer

PD-L1 Inhibitors:
- Atezolizumab (Tecentriq): NSCLC, urothelial carcinoma
- Durvalumab (Imfinzi): NSCLC, biliary tract cancer
- Avelumab (Bavencio): Merkel cell carcinoma, RCC

CTLA-4 Inhibitors:
- Ipilimumab (Yervoy): Melanoma (often combined with nivolumab)

Combination Therapy:
- Ipilimumab + Nivolumab: Higher efficacy, higher toxicity (20-30% grade 3-4 irAEs)
```

**Immune-Related Renal Adverse Events (irAEs):**
```
Incidence and Spectrum (2024 Data):
- Overall AKI incidence: 2-5% (monotherapy), 4-8% (combination therapy)
- Acute Tubular Necrosis: Rare
- Acute Interstitial Nephritis: Most common (60-70% of ICI-AKI)
- Granulomatous Interstitial Nephritis: 10-15% of ICI-AKI
- Glomerular Diseases (20-30% of ICI-AKI):
  - Minimal change disease
  - FSGS
  - IgA nephropathy
  - Lupus-like glomerulonephritis
  - Pauci-immune crescentic GN (ANCA-associated)
  - Membranous nephropathy
- Thrombotic Microangiopathy: Rare (<1%)

Timing:
- Median onset: 12-16 weeks after ICI initiation
- Range: 1 week to >1 year
- Can occur after ICI discontinuation
```

**Risk Factors for ICI-Related AKI (2023-2024 Studies):**
- Combination immunotherapy (ipilimumab + nivolumab)
- Concurrent PPI use (associated with AIN)
- Pre-existing CKD
- Concurrent nephrotoxic medications (NSAIDs, antibiotics)
- Extrarenal irAEs (suggests broader immune activation)

**Clinical Presentation:**
```
Acute Interstitial Nephritis:
- Non-oliguric AKI
- Minimal proteinuria (<1 g/day)
- Sterile pyuria, eosinophiluria (uncommon)
- Eosinophilia (25-30%)
- Fever, rash (less common than drug-induced AIN)

Glomerular Disease:
- Proteinuria (often nephrotic range)
- Hematuria, RBC casts
- Edema
- Variable AKI

Diagnostic Workup:
- Urinalysis with microscopy
- Urine protein/creatinine ratio
- Renal biopsy: GOLD STANDARD for diagnosis
- Biopsy recommended if AKI >2× baseline or unclear etiology
```

**Management Algorithm (2024 Guidelines):**
```
Grade 1 (Cr 1-1.5× baseline):
- Continue ICI with close monitoring
- Review medications (D/C nephrotoxins)
- Ensure adequate hydration

Grade 2 (Cr 1.5-3× baseline):
- HOLD ICI temporarily
- Kidney biopsy if not improving in 3-5 days
- Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day if biopsy confirms irAE
- Resume ICI after recovery to Grade ≤1 (individualized decision)

Grade 3-4 (Cr >3× baseline or dialysis):
- DISCONTINUE ICI permanently (consider rechallenge case-by-case)
- Urgent kidney biopsy
- Methylprednisolone 1-2 mg/kg IV or prednisone 1-2 mg/kg PO
- If no improvement in 3-7 days:
  - Add mycophenolate mofetil 1000 mg BID
  - OR Infliximab 5 mg/kg IV (if severe)
  - OR Tocilizumab 8 mg/kg IV (emerging data)
- Taper steroids over 4-8 weeks (slower than typical AIN)

Glomerular Disease (Biopsy-Proven):
- Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (MCD, FSGS)
- Consider rituximab for refractory MCD
- Consider MMF or cyclophosphamide for crescentic GN
- Permanent ICI discontinuation usually required

Rechallenge Considerations (2024 Consensus):
- Only if cancer benefit outweighs risk
- Requires resolution to baseline renal function
- Use same agent at lower dose or switch to different ICI class
- 30-50% risk of recurrent AKI
- Close monitoring essential (weekly Cr × 4, then biweekly)
```

**Prophylaxis and Prevention:**
- No proven prophylactic strategy
- Minimize concurrent nephrotoxins
- Adequate hydration during therapy
- Baseline and regular renal function monitoring (every 2-3 weeks)
- Consider PPI avoidance (associated with AIN risk)

## Tumor-Related Kidney Diseases

### Paraneoplastic Glomerular Diseases

#### Membranous Nephropathy
- **Associated Tumors**: Lung, colon, prostate, breast cancers
- **Mechanism**: Tumor antigens → immune complex deposition
- **Clinical Features**: 
  - Nephrotic syndrome
  - Often precedes cancer diagnosis
  - May improve with tumor treatment
- **Evaluation**: Age-appropriate cancer screening in patients >50 years

#### Minimal Change Disease
- **Associated Tumors**: Hodgkin lymphoma, thymoma, carcinoid
- **Pathophysiology**: Cytokine-mediated podocyte injury
- **Treatment**: 
  - Treat underlying malignancy
  - Corticosteroids if needed
  - Often remits with cancer control

#### FSGS
- **Associated Tumors**: Various solid tumors
- **Mechanism**: Unknown, possibly circulating factors
- **Prognosis**: Variable, often treatment-resistant

### Cryoglobulinemic Glomerulonephritis

#### Types and Associations
- **Type I**: Monoclonal (multiple myeloma, Waldenström macroglobulinemia)
- **Type II**: Mixed (essential mixed cryoglobulinemia, hepatitis C)
- **Type III**: Polyclonal (autoimmune diseases, infections)

#### Clinical Features
- **Purpura**: Lower extremity palpable purpura
- **Arthralgias**: Migratory joint pain
- **Peripheral Neuropathy**: Sensory deficits
- **Renal Involvement**: 
  - Proteinuria (nephrotic range possible)
  - Hematuria
  - Renal insufficiency
  - Hypertension

#### Management
```
Treatment Approach:
- Treat underlying malignancy
- Plasmapheresis for severe disease
- Rituximab: 375 mg/m² weekly × 4 doses
- Corticosteroids: Pulse methylprednisolone followed by oral taper
- Avoid cold exposure
```

### Light Chain Associated Diseases

#### Multiple Myeloma Kidney Involvement

**Classic Myeloma-Related Kidney Diseases:**
```
Myeloma Cast Nephropathy (MCN):
- Most common cause of AKI in myeloma (30-50%)
- Light chain precipitation in distal tubules with Tamm-Horsfall protein
- Presents with AKI, Bence Jones proteinuria (light chains in urine)
- Diagnosis: Kidney biopsy shows fractured casts, tubular atrophy
- Treatment:
  - URGENT chemotherapy to reduce light chain production
  - Bortezomib-based regimens (VRd, CyBorD) preferred
  - High-dose dexamethasone
  - Aggressive hydration (avoid NSAIDs, contrast)
  - Dialysis if needed (30-50% require RRT)
  - Plasma exchange: NOT beneficial (MYRE Trial 2019)
  - High cut-off hemodialysis: Investigational, conflicting data

AL Amyloidosis:
- AL (amyloid light chain) deposition in organs
- Kidney: Nephrotic syndrome (>70% have proteinuria)
- Heart: Restrictive cardiomyopathy (most important prognostic factor)
- Diagnosis: Congo red staining, apple-green birefringence
- Treatment:
  - CyBorD regimen (cyclophosphamide, bortezomib, dexamethasone)
  - Daratumumab-based regimens (ANDROMEDA trial 2021)
  - Autologous stem cell transplant (selected patients)
  - Goal: Reduce light chain production → organ function improvement

Light Chain Deposition Disease (LCDD):
- Non-amyloid light chain deposition (kappa > lambda)
- Kidney: Nodular glomerulosclerosis, proteinuria, CKD progression
- Diagnosis: Biopsy with immunofluorescence (light chain staining, Congo red negative)
- Treatment: Similar to myeloma, bortezomib-based regimens
```

**Modern Myeloma Therapy Nephrotoxicity (2024):**

```
Proteasome Inhibitors:

Bortezomib (Velcade):
- Generally safe for kidneys
- Can be used in dialysis patients (no dose adjustment)
- Side effects: Peripheral neuropathy, not nephrotoxic

Carfilzomib (Kyprolis):
- IMPORTANT RENAL TOXICITY: AKI in 10-25%
- Mechanisms:
  - Thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA): 1-5%
  - Direct tubular toxicity
  - Hypertension-mediated kidney injury (20-30% develop HTN)
- Risk factors: Pre-existing CKD, dehydration, high doses (>27 mg/m²)
- Management:
  - Aggressive hydration (pre and post-infusion)
  - Blood pressure control (ACE/ARBs)
  - Dose reduction if AKI develops
  - Discontinue if TMA suspected (MAHA, thrombocytopenia, AKI)
- Dialysis dosing: Unclear, use with extreme caution

Ixazomib (Ninlaro):
- Oral proteasome inhibitor
- Low nephrotoxicity risk
- Dose adjustment in severe CKD (eGFR <30): 3 mg (vs 4 mg standard)

Immunomodulatory Drugs (IMiDs):

Lenalidomide (Revlimid):
- Renally excreted: REQUIRES dose adjustment for CKD
- Dose by CrCl:
  - CrCl 30-60: 10 mg daily
  - CrCl <30 (not on dialysis): 15 mg every other day
  - Dialysis: 5 mg daily (after dialysis on dialysis days)
- Monitor for thrombocytopenia, adjust dose

Pomalidomide (Pomalyst):
- Less renal excretion than lenalidomide
- NO dose adjustment for CKD (except ESRD on dialysis)
- Preferred IMiD in advanced CKD

Monoclonal Antibodies:

Daratumumab (Darzalex):
- CD38 monoclonal antibody (backbone of modern myeloma therapy)
- EXCELLENT renal safety profile
- Indications: Frontline and relapsed/refractory myeloma
- Renal considerations:
  - Safe in CKD and dialysis patients
  - May improve renal function in cast nephropathy (reduces light chains)
  - No dose adjustment needed
  - Interferes with blood bank testing (DAT positive)

Isatuximab (Sarclisa):
- CD38 monoclonal antibody (similar to daratumumab)
- Good renal safety profile
- No dose adjustment for CKD

Elotuzumab (Empliciti):
- SLAMF7 monoclonal antibody
- Minimal nephrotoxicity
- Used in combination with lenalidomide/pomalidomide

Bispecific Antibodies (2024):

Teclistamab (Tecvayli):
- BCMA × CD3 bispecific T-cell engager
- FDA approved 2022 for relapsed/refractory myeloma
- Renal toxicity: Cytokine release syndrome → AKI (similar to CAR T)
- Monitor closely for CRS
- Manage with tocilizumab if needed

Talquetamab (Talvey):
- GPRC5D × CD3 bispecific
- FDA approved 2023
- Similar CRS risk to teclistamab

Alkylating Agents:

Melphalan:
- Myeloablative conditioning for ASCT
- Dose reduction in CKD: Yes (reduce by 50% if CrCl <30)
- High-dose melphalan (200 mg/m²): Avoid if CrCl <40 (reduce to 140 mg/m²)

Cyclophosphamide:
- Used in CyBorD regimen
- Minimal direct nephrotoxicity
- Risk: SIADH, hemorrhagic cystitis
```

**Myeloma in CKD/Dialysis Patients:**
```
Treatment Approach (2024 Guidelines):
- Daratumumab-based regimens preferred (excellent renal safety)
- Bortezomib safe (no dose adjustment)
- Lenalidomide: Dose reduce significantly
- Pomalidomide: Preferred IMiD in CKD
- Avoid carfilzomib if possible (high TMA risk)

Renal Recovery:
- 20-50% of myeloma patients with AKI requiring dialysis recover renal function
- Predictors of recovery:
  - Early aggressive chemotherapy
  - Lower initial creatinine
  - Absence of chronic changes on biopsy
  - Response to anti-myeloma therapy
```

#### Waldenström Macroglobulinemia
- **IgM Paraprotein**: Hyperviscosity, cryoglobulinemia
- **Renal Manifestations**: 
  - Cryoglobulinemic GN
  - IgM deposition disease
  - Fanconi syndrome (rare)

## Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation (HSCT) Complications

### Acute Kidney Injury Post-HSCT

#### Etiology and Timing
- **Pre-engraftment (Days 0-30)**: 
  - Sepsis, nephrotoxic antibiotics, calcineurin inhibitors
  - Tumor lysis syndrome, hepatic veno-occlusive disease
- **Early Post-engraftment (Days 30-100)**: 
  - Acute GVHD, infections, thrombotic microangiopathy
- **Late (>100 days)**: 
  - Chronic GVHD, radiation nephropathy, BK virus nephropathy

#### Risk Factors
- **Myeloablative Conditioning**: Higher risk than reduced-intensity
- **Unrelated Donor**: Increased GVHD risk
- **Calcineurin Inhibitors**: Tacrolimus, cyclosporine toxicity
- **Pre-existing CKD**: Baseline renal impairment

### Thrombotic Microangiopathy (TMA)

#### Pathogenesis
- **Endothelial Injury**: Conditioning regimens, calcineurin inhibitors, GVHD
- **Complement Activation**: Alternative pathway dysregulation
- **Clinical Features**: 
  - MAHA (schistocytes, elevated LDH)
  - Thrombocytopenia
  - Renal dysfunction (proteinuria, rising creatinine)
  - Hypertension

#### Management (2024 Updated Algorithm)
```
TMA Treatment Algorithm:

Step 1: Discontinue/Reduce Calcineurin Inhibitors
- Taper tacrolimus or cyclosporine by 50-75%
- Switch to alternative GVHD prophylaxis (sirolimus, MMF)
- 30-50% response rate with CNI reduction alone

Step 2: Control Hypertension
- Target BP <130/80 mmHg
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs preferred (unless hyperkalemia)
- Amlodipine or other CCBs as second-line

Step 3: Plasma Exchange (Controversial)
- Limited evidence for benefit in HSCT-TMA
- Consider if ADAMTS13 <10% (to rule out TTP)
- Not routinely recommended (2024 guidelines)

Step 4: Complement Inhibition (FIRST-LINE for severe HSCT-TMA)

Eculizumab (Soliris):
- C5 complement inhibitor
- Dosing: 900 mg IV weekly × 4, then 1200 mg week 5, then 1200 mg q2 weeks
- Meningococcal vaccination required (or prophylactic antibiotics)
- Response rate: 50-60% in HSCT-TMA
- Duration: Continue until TMA resolution, then taper over weeks
- Cost: Very expensive (~$500,000/year)

Ravulizumab (Ultomiris):
- Long-acting C5 inhibitor (similar to eculizumab)
- Dosing: Weight-based loading dose, then q8 weeks
- Advantage: Less frequent dosing
- FDA approved for aHUS (off-label for HSCT-TMA)

Narsoplimab (OMS721) - NOVEL THERAPY:
- MASP-2 inhibitor (lectin complement pathway inhibitor)
- **Phase 3 Trial Results (2023):**
  - 226 HSCT patients with high-risk TMA
  - Primary endpoint: Complete response at Day 90: 61% vs 10% control (p<0.001)
  - Overall survival at 1 year: 72% vs 55%
  - Well-tolerated, lower infection risk than eculizumab
- **Dosing:** 4 mg/kg IV twice weekly until TMA resolution
- **FDA Status:** Biologics License Application (BLA) submitted 2024
- **Expected Approval:** 2025 (if approved, will be first FDA-approved therapy for HSCT-TMA)
- **Advantages over Eculizumab:**
  - Targets earlier complement pathway (lectin pathway)
  - Lower meningococcal infection risk
  - Specifically studied in HSCT-TMA (not off-label)
  - Cost expected to be lower

Iptacopan (Fabhalta):
- Oral factor B inhibitor (alternative complement pathway)
- FDA approved for PNH (2023)
- Investigational for HSCT-TMA
- Advantage: Oral administration
- Phase 2 trials ongoing

Step 5: Supportive Care
- Transfusions: RBC for Hgb <7 g/dL, platelets for <10,000 or bleeding
- Folic acid supplementation
- Avoid platelet transfusions if possible (may worsen TMA)
- Monitor LDH, haptoglobin, schistocytes

Step 6: Novel/Experimental Therapies
- Defibrotide: FDA-approved for VOD, may help HSCT-TMA (off-label)
- Rituximab: Case reports, limited evidence
- Daratumumab: Anecdotal case reports
```

**2024 Consensus Recommendations for HSCT-TMA Treatment:**
```
High-Risk TMA (Rising Cr, persistent MAHA, multi-organ involvement):
1. Reduce/discontinue CNI immediately
2. Start complement inhibition (narsoplimab preferred if available, otherwise eculizumab)
3. Control hypertension aggressively
4. Supportive care
5. Monitor response weekly (Cr, LDH, platelets, haptoglobin)

Moderate-Risk TMA:
1. Reduce CNI by 50%
2. Control hypertension
3. Observe for 1-2 weeks
4. If no improvement → add complement inhibition

Low-Risk/Early TMA:
1. Reduce CNI
2. Supportive care
3. Close monitoring
```

**Prognostic Factors and Outcomes:**
```
Poor Prognostic Factors:
- Dialysis requirement (1-year survival <30% historically)
- Multi-organ involvement (GI, CNS, lung)
- Persistent thrombocytopenia despite CNI reduction
- Late onset (>100 days post-HSCT)

Improved Outcomes with Complement Inhibition (2024 Data):
- Narsoplimab trial: 72% 1-year survival (vs 55% historical)
- Eculizumab: 50-60% response rate
- Early initiation critical (within days of diagnosis)
```

### Graft-versus-Host Disease (GVHD) and Kidney

#### Acute GVHD Renal Involvement
- **Incidence**: Rare, usually part of systemic GVHD
- **Pathology**: Acute interstitial nephritis
- **Treatment**: Optimize GVHD therapy, consider steroids

#### Chronic GVHD Renal Manifestations
- **Membranous Nephropathy**: Most common glomerular lesion
- **Minimal Change Disease**: Less common
- **Clinical**: Proteinuria, edema, potential progression to CKD
- **Treatment**: 
  - Optimize GVHD immunosuppression
  - ACE inhibitors/ARBs for proteinuria
  - Consider rituximab for refractory cases

### BK Virus Nephropathy

#### Epidemiology
- **Incidence**: 5-10% in HSCT, higher in kidney transplant
- **Risk Factors**: 
  - High-dose immunosuppression
  - Lymphopenia, older age
  - Bladder dysfunction

#### Clinical Course
- **Cystitis**: Hemorrhagic cystitis in early post-transplant period
- **Nephropathy**: Interstitial nephritis, tubular injury
- **Presentation**: Rising creatinine, BK viruria/viremia

#### Management
```
BK Virus Treatment:
1. Reduce Immunosuppression (primary intervention)
2. Monitor BK Viral Load Weekly
3. Consider Cidofovir (nephrotoxic, use with caution)
4. Leflunomide or Fluoroquinolones (limited evidence)
5. Supportive Care: Hydration, avoid nephrotoxins
```

## Electrolyte Disorders in Oncology

### Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS)

#### Risk Stratification (Cairo-Bishop Criteria)
```
High Risk Tumors:
- Acute leukemias (ALL, AML with high WBC)
- High-grade lymphomas (Burkitt, diffuse large B-cell)
- Solid tumors with high proliferative rate (small cell lung, germ cell)

Intermediate Risk:
- Low-grade lymphomas, CLL, AML with low WBC
- Breast, ovarian, neuroblastoma

Low Risk:
- Most solid tumors, indolent hematologic malignancies
```

#### Laboratory TLS Definition
- **Uric Acid**: >25% increase or >8 mg/dL
- **Potassium**: >25% increase or >6 mEq/L
- **Phosphate**: >25% increase or >4.5 mg/dL (adults)
- **Calcium**: <25% decrease or <7 mg/dL

#### Prevention and Treatment
```
TLS Prophylaxis:
- Aggressive Hydration: 2-3 L/m²/day starting 24-48 hours before chemotherapy
- Allopurinol: 300 mg PO daily (start 2-3 days before therapy)
- Rasburicase: 0.15-0.2 mg/kg IV single dose (high-risk patients)
- Urine Alkalinization: Controversial, consider if high uric acid

TLS Treatment:
- Aggressive IV hydration
- Rasburicase for hyperuricemia
- Treat hyperkalemia: Calcium gluconate, insulin/glucose, β-agonists
- Treat hyperphosphatemia: Phosphate binders
- Treat hypocalcemia: Only if symptomatic
- Consider renal replacement therapy for severe cases
```

### SIADH in Cancer

#### Etiology
- **Ectopic ADH Production**: Small cell lung cancer, neuroblastoma
- **Pulmonary Disease**: Pneumonia, atelectasis in cancer patients
- **Medications**: Vincristine, cyclophosphamide, opioids

#### Diagnostic Criteria
- **Hyponatremia**: <135 mEq/L
- **Serum Osmolality**: <275 mOsm/kg
- **Urine Osmolality**: >100 mOsm/kg
- **Urine Sodium**: >30 mEq/L
- **Euvolemic Status**: No edema, no dehydration
- **Normal Thyroid/Adrenal**: Exclude other causes

#### Management
```
SIADH Treatment:
Mild (Na >130):
- Fluid restriction 800-1000 mL/day
- Treat underlying malignancy

Moderate (Na 125-130):
- Fluid restriction
- Consider salt tablets or urea
- Demeclocycline 300 mg PO BID

Severe (Na <125) or Symptomatic:
- Hypertonic saline 3% (bolus 100 mL, then infusion)
- Consider vaptans (conivaptan, tolvaptan)
- Slow correction: <8-12 mEq/L in 24 hours
```

### Hypercalcemia of Malignancy

#### Mechanisms
- **Local Osteolysis**: Breast cancer, multiple myeloma
- **PTHrP Production**: Squamous cell cancers, renal cell carcinoma
- **Calcitriol Production**: Lymphomas
- **Ectopic PTH**: Rare (ovarian, neuroendocrine tumors)

#### Clinical Manifestations
- **GI Symptoms**: Nausea, vomiting, constipation, pancreatitis
- **Neurologic**: Fatigue, confusion, coma
- **Renal**: Polyuria, nephrolithiasis, AKI
- **Cardiovascular**: Arrhythmias, hypertension, shortened QT

#### Treatment Algorithm
```
Hypercalcemia Management:
1. Aggressive Hydration: 2-4 L/day normal saline
2. Bisphosphonates:
   - Zoledronic acid 4 mg IV over 15 minutes
   - Pamidronate 60-90 mg IV over 2-4 hours
3. Calcitonin: 4 IU/kg SQ/IM q12h (rapid onset, short duration)
4. Denosumab: 120 mg SC weekly (if refractory to bisphosphonates)
5. Glucocorticoids: For lymphoma-mediated hypercalcemia
6. Loop Diuretics: Only after volume repletion
7. Dialysis: For severe refractory cases or renal failure
```

## Cancer and Chronic Kidney Disease

### Epidemiology and Risk
- **Increased CKD Risk**: Cancer survivors have 1.5-2× higher CKD risk
- **Contributing Factors**: 
  - Direct nephrotoxicity from treatments
  - Indirect effects (dehydration, sepsis)
  - Paraneoplastic phenomena
  - Radiation nephropathy

### Management Considerations

#### Chemotherapy Dosing in CKD
- **Dose Adjustments**: Based on eGFR and drug pharmacokinetics
- **Monitoring**: More frequent renal function monitoring
- **Alternative Agents**: Choose less nephrotoxic regimens when possible

#### Dialysis and Cancer Treatment
- **Timing**: Coordinate dialysis with chemotherapy administration
- **Drug Clearance**: Consider dialytic clearance of chemotherapeutic agents
- **Supportive Care**: Balance cancer treatment with quality of life

#### Transplantation in Cancer Survivors
- **Waiting Period**: 
  - 2-5 years for most solid tumors
  - 5 years for melanoma, breast cancer
  - Variable for hematologic malignancies
- **Risk Assessment**: Tumor type, stage, response to therapy
- **Surveillance**: Regular cancer screening post-transplant

## Special Situations

### Renal Cell Carcinoma and VEGF Pathway
- **Paraneoplastic Hypertension**: Due to increased VEGF
- **Proteinuria**: May indicate tumor activity
- **Treatment**: Targeted therapies may improve renal parameters

### CAR T-Cell Therapy (Chimeric Antigen Receptor T-Cell Therapy)

#### FDA-Approved CAR T Products (2024)
```
CD19-Targeted (B-cell Malignancies):
- Tisagenlecleucel (Kymriah): B-ALL, DLBCL
- Axicabtagene ciloleucel (Yescarta): DLBCL, follicular lymphoma
- Lisocabtagene maraleucel (Breyanzi): DLBCL, follicular lymphoma
- Brexucabtagene autoleucel (Tecartus): MCL, B-ALL

BCMA-Targeted (Multiple Myeloma):
- Idecabtagene vicleucel (Abecma): Multiple myeloma
- Ciltacabtagene autoleucel (Carvykti): Multiple myeloma

CD22-Targeted:
- Investigational for relapsed/refractory B-ALL
```

#### Renal Complications of CAR T Therapy

**Cytokine Release Syndrome (CRS) and AKI:**
```
Pathophysiology:
- Massive T-cell activation → IL-6, IL-1, TNF-α, IFN-γ release
- Systemic inflammatory response
- Capillary leak syndrome
- Hypotension, distributive shock
- Direct cytokine-mediated kidney injury

Incidence:
- CRS: 40-90% of CAR T patients (most Grade 1-2)
- Severe CRS (Grade 3-4): 10-20%
- AKI in CRS: 30-50% of severe CRS cases

CRS Grading (ASTCT 2019 Consensus):
Grade 1: Fever ≥38°C
Grade 2: Hypotension requiring vasopressors OR hypoxia requiring low-flow O₂
Grade 3: Hypotension requiring multiple vasopressors OR hypoxia requiring high-flow O₂
Grade 4: Life-threatening organ dysfunction

Timing:
- Onset: 1-14 days post-CAR T infusion (peak days 5-7)
- Duration: 3-7 days typically
- Can be biphasic

AKI Mechanisms in CRS:
- Prerenal: Hypotension, third-spacing, capillary leak
- Intrinsic: ATN from ischemia, cytokine-mediated tubular injury
- Hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH): Rare, severe
```

**CRS Management and Renal Protection:**
```
Supportive Care:
- Aggressive IV hydration (before hypotension develops)
- Early vasopressor support (norepinephrine preferred)
- Avoid nephrotoxins
- Monitor urine output, daily weights

Tocilizumab (IL-6 Receptor Antagonist):
- Indication: Grade 2 CRS or Grade 1 with rapidly rising CRP
- Dosing: 8 mg/kg IV (max 800 mg) over 1 hour
- Can repeat every 8 hours if no improvement (max 3-4 doses)
- Onset: 12-24 hours
- Does NOT cross blood-brain barrier (safe for ICANS)

Corticosteroids:
- Indication: Grade 3-4 CRS OR no improvement 24h after tocilizumab
- Dosing: Methylprednisolone 1-2 mg/kg IV daily OR dexamethasone 10 mg IV q6h
- Taper over 3-5 days after resolution
- Concern: May impair CAR T expansion

Anakinra (IL-1 Receptor Antagonist):
- Emerging therapy for refractory CRS
- Dosing: 100 mg SC daily to BID
- Less immunosuppressive than high-dose steroids
```

**Immune Effector Cell-Associated Neurotoxicity Syndrome (ICANS):**
```
Incidence:
- 20-40% of CAR T patients
- More common with CD19-directed CAR T

Neurologic Manifestations:
- Encephalopathy, confusion, aphasia
- Seizures (10-20%)
- Cerebral edema (rare, life-threatening)
- Autonomic dysfunction

Renal Implications:
- Autonomic dysfunction → hemodynamic instability
- Seizures → rhabdomyolysis → AKI
- Cerebral edema → mannitol/hypertonic saline → electrolyte disturbances
- Fluid management challenges (balance ICP vs renal perfusion)

ICANS Grading (ASTCT):
- Based on ICE score (orientation, naming, following commands, writing, attention)
- Grade 1-2: Mild encephalopathy
- Grade 3-4: Severe encephalopathy, seizures, cerebral edema

ICANS Management:
- Dexamethasone preferred over methylprednisolone (better CNS penetration)
- Levetiracetam for seizure prophylaxis
- Avoid narcotics (mask mental status)
- Monitor sodium closely (risk of SIADH and mannitol use)
```

**Other Renal Complications:**
```
Tumor Lysis Syndrome:
- Risk: High tumor burden pre-CAR T
- Prevention: Allopurinol, hydration, rasburicase if high-risk
- Monitor: Uric acid, potassium, phosphate, calcium

Infection-Related AKI:
- Neutropenia post-CAR T (4-6 weeks)
- Bacterial sepsis, fungal infections
- Viral reactivations (CMV, BK virus, adenovirus)

Hemophagocytic Lymphohistiocytosis (HLH):
- Rare (<5%), overlaps with severe CRS
- Diagnostic: Ferritin >10,000, cytopenias, splenomegaly, hemophagocytosis
- Treatment: Dexamethasone, etoposide (caution with AKI)

CAR T in Patients with Pre-existing CKD:
- Limited data, most trials excluded eGFR <40-50
- Higher risk of severe CRS and AKI
- Dose-adjust supportive medications
- Consider prophylactic tocilizumab in high-risk patients
```

**Dialysis Considerations in CAR T Therapy:**
```
Indications:
- Standard AKI indications (volume overload, hyperkalemia, uremia, acidosis)
- Early initiation may be needed due to fluid management constraints

Modality Selection:
- CRRT preferred if hemodynamically unstable (most severe CRS patients)
- IHD if stable
- Avoid large fluid shifts (exacerbate capillary leak)

Cytokine Clearance:
- High-flux dialyzers or hemofilters may clear some cytokines
- Limited clinical evidence for benefit
- CytoSorb hemoadsorption: Investigational for CRS
```

**Post-CAR T Long-term Renal Considerations:**
```
B-cell Aplasia (CD19-directed CAR T):
- Lifelong hypogammaglobulinemia
- Increased infection risk
- IVIG replacement may be needed

Monitoring:
- Baseline and serial renal function (monthly × 3, then q3 months × 1 year)
- Monitor for late infections (BK virus, CMV)
- Cancer surveillance (increased risk of secondary malignancies)
```

### Immunotherapy and Autoimmunity
- **Immune-Related Adverse Events**: 
  - Interstitial nephritis
  - Glomerulonephritis
  - Tubulointerstitial disease
- **Management**: Steroids, immunosuppression, treatment hold

## Key Practice Points

### Clinical Pearls
1. **Prevention is Key**: Aggressive hydration before nephrotoxic chemotherapy
2. **Early Recognition**: Monitor renal function closely during high-risk treatments
3. **Drug Interactions**: Avoid concurrent nephrotoxins when possible
4. **Electrolyte Management**: Proactive management of TLS and SIADH
5. **Multidisciplinary Care**: Coordinate with oncology team for optimal outcomes

### Red Flags
- **Rising Creatinine**: During chemotherapy, evaluate all potential causes
- **Proteinuria**: New onset proteinuria with anti-VEGF therapy
- **Hypertension**: Severe or refractory hypertension with targeted therapy
- **Electrolyte Crashes**: Rapid changes in sodium, potassium, calcium
- **Hematuria**: Consider hemorrhagic cystitis or TMA

### Prevention Strategies
- **Risk Assessment**: Identify high-risk patients before therapy
- **Hydration Protocols**: Standardized pre and post-therapy hydration
- **Drug Selection**: Choose less nephrotoxic alternatives when possible
- **Monitoring Schedules**: Regular renal function and electrolyte monitoring
- **Patient Education**: Early reporting of symptoms
