#### Task 10: Frailty Assessment Tools in Geriatric Nephrology
**Document**: 17_Geriatric_Nephrology_Comprehensive_Guide.md
**Lines Modified**: 426-1552 (1,000+ lines added)
**Completion Date**: November 23, 2025

**Enhancement**: Comprehensive frailty assessment tool coverage expanded from ~43 lines to over 1,000 lines

**Key Topics Added**:

1. **Fried Frailty Phenotype** (Most validated in CKD/dialysis)
   - Five components: weight loss, exhaustion, weakness, gait speed, physical activity
   - Scoring system (0-5 points): robust (0), pre-frail (1-2), frail (3-5)
   - CKD-specific cutoffs and outcomes data
   - Mortality risk: Frail patients 2.6× vs robust, Pre-frail 1.5× vs robust

2. **Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS)**
   - 9-point visual scale from very fit to terminally ill
   - CFS ≥7 threshold for considering conservative kidney management
   - Rapid bedside assessment tool (1-2 minutes)
   - Validated for dialysis decision-making

3. **FRAIL Scale**
   - Mnemonic-based 5-item questionnaire
   - Fatigue, Resistance, Ambulation, Illness, Loss of weight
   - Score 0-5: robust (0), pre-frail (1-2), frail (3-5)
   - Easy implementation in busy dialysis units

4. **Short Physical Performance Battery (SPPB)**
   - 12-point scale combining balance, gait speed, chair stands
   - Score <8 = high mortality risk in dialysis patients
   - Objective performance-based measure
   - Tracks intervention response

5. **Edmonton Frail Scale**
   - 17-point multidimensional assessment
   - Covers cognition, general health, functional independence, social support
   - More comprehensive but time-intensive (10-15 minutes)
   - Score ≥8 indicates frailty

6. **Standalone Measures**
   - Gait speed as "sixth vital sign" (<0.8 m/s = frail, <0.6 m/s = high mortality)
   - Grip strength measurement protocols and normative data
   - Timed Up and Go test (>12 seconds = falls risk)

**Clinical Applications Added**:
- Decision-making algorithms for conservative kidney management
- Intradialytic exercise programs (strongest evidence: +0.14 m/s gait speed)
- Nutrition interventions for frail dialysis patients
- Polypharmacy and deprescribing strategies
- Falls risk assessment and prevention
- Outcomes data: 50-75% of hemodialysis patients are frail vs 10-15% general elderly

**Board Exam High-Yield Concepts**:
- Fried Frailty Phenotype 5 components recognition
- CFS ≥7 threshold for conservative management consideration
- Gait speed <0.8 m/s as frailty indicator
- Intradialytic exercise as most evidence-based intervention

**Clinical Impact**: High - standardizes frailty assessment for dialysis decision-making and intervention targeting

#### Task 11: Postpartum Preeclampsia Management
**Document**: 13_Pregnancy_Kidney_Disease_Comprehensive_Guide.md
**Lines Modified**: 443-1167 (700+ lines added)
**Completion Date**: November 23, 2025

**Enhancement**: Expanded postpartum preeclampsia from minimal coverage (1 line) to comprehensive 700+ line section

**Key Topics Added**:

1. **Epidemiology and Timing**
   - Incidence: 3-5% of all deliveries
   - Timing: 50% within 48 hours, peak days 3-7, can occur up to 6 weeks postpartum
   - Leading cause of maternal mortality in developed countries
   - Risk highest in those with preeclampsia during pregnancy (15-20× risk)

2. **Diagnostic Criteria (ACOG 2020 Updated)**
   - BP ≥140/90 mmHg on two occasions 4 hours apart
   - Proteinuria ≥300 mg/24hr OR urine protein/creatinine ≥0.3
   - **KEY UPDATE**: Proteinuria NO LONGER REQUIRED if severe features present
   - Severe features: BP ≥160/110, platelets <100K, Cr >1.1 or doubling, pulmonary edema, cerebral symptoms

3. **Acute Severe Hypertension Management**
   - Goal: Reduce BP to <160/110 mmHg within 30-60 minutes (NOT normalization)
   - First-line agents (all breastfeeding-compatible):
     * IV labetalol: 20-40-80 mg escalation protocol
     * IV hydralazine: 5-10 mg q20min (max 30 mg)
     * PO nifedipine IR: 10-20 mg q20min (max 50 mg in 1 hour)

4. **Magnesium Sulfate Protocols**
   - Loading: 4-6 g IV over 15-20 minutes
   - Maintenance: 1-2 g/hour × 24 hours after symptom resolution
   - Monitoring: DTRs q1-2h, respiratory rate q1h (>12/min), urine output q1h (>25 mL/hr)
   - Toxicity levels: Loss of DTRs at 7-10 mEq/L, respiratory depression >10 mEq/L
   - Antidote: Calcium gluconate 1 g IV push over 3 minutes

5. **Chronic Outpatient Management**
   - Breastfeeding-compatible medications:
     * Labetalol 200-400 mg BID-TID (first-line)
     * Nifedipine ER 30-120 mg daily
     * Methyldopa 250-500 mg TID
     * ACE inhibitors: enalapril, captopril (per AAP compatible)
   - Contraindicated: atenolol (poor infant growth)
   - Target BP: <140/90 mmHg (<130/80 if high CV risk)

6. **Differential Diagnosis**
   - aHUS: Persists >72 hours, doesn't resolve (key differentiator)
   - TTP: ADAMTS13 <10%, fever, neurologic symptoms
   - HELLP syndrome: Hepatic involvement, hemolysis
   - Peripartum cardiomyopathy: EF <45%, dyspnea
   - Diagnostic workup: CBC, CMP, LDH, haptoglobin, peripheral smear

7. **Life-Threatening Complications**
   - Eclampsia: Seizures in setting of preeclampsia (0.1% incidence)
   - PRES (Posterior Reversible Encephalopathy Syndrome): MRI with vasogenic edema
   - Stroke: Hemorrhagic or ischemic (BP >160/110 major risk)
   - Pulmonary edema: Due to capillary leak and fluid overload
   - Acute kidney injury: ATN from severe vasospasm

8. **Long-Term Cardiovascular Risk**
   - 3-4× increased risk chronic hypertension
   - 2× increased risk CVD and stroke
   - Lifelong monitoring and preventive cardiology referral
   - Lifestyle modifications and statin consideration

**Patient Education Warning Signs**:
- Severe headache not relieved by acetaminophen
- Vision changes (blurry, seeing spots, light sensitivity)
- Upper abdominal/RUQ pain
- Shortness of breath or chest pain
- Seizures → call 911 immediately

**Board Exam High-Yield Concepts**:
- Proteinuria no longer required for diagnosis (ACOG 2020)
- Peak incidence days 3-7 postpartum
- Magnesium toxicity levels and calcium gluconate antidote
- aHUS persists >72 hours vs preeclampsia resolves
- Breastfeeding-compatible antihypertensives

**Clinical Impact**: High - leading cause of maternal mortality, requires prompt recognition and management

#### Task 12: HLA Epitope Matching in Kidney Transplantation
**Document**: 11_Kidney_Transplantation_Comprehensive_Guide.md
**Lines Modified**: 94-543 (430+ lines added)
**Completion Date**: November 23, 2025

**Enhancement**: Added comprehensive HLA epitope matching section (previously no coverage)

**Key Topics Added**:

1. **Traditional vs Epitope-Based Matching**
   - Traditional: Counts whole HLA antigen mismatches (0-6 scale for A, B, DR)
   - Epitope-based: Identifies small amino acid clusters (epitopes) that trigger immune response
   - Paradigm shift: Not all HLA mismatches are equal immunologically
   - Example: Some 6-antigen mismatches have lower dnDSA risk than some 2-antigen mismatches

2. **HLAMatchmaker Algorithm** (Most widely used)
   - Developer: René Duquesnoy, PhD (University of Pittsburgh)
   - Identifies "eplets" = polymorphic amino acid residue clusters
   - Catalog of ~200 eplets for HLA-A, -B, -C, -DR, -DQ, -DP
   - **Eplet Mismatch Load (EML)**: Count of donor eplets absent in recipient
   - Clinical thresholds:
     * EML Class II >20-25: High dnDSA risk (30-45%)
     * EML Class II <10: Low dnDSA risk (<10%)
     * EML Class I >15-20: High risk

3. **PIRCHE-II Algorithm**
   - Predicts T-cell recognition of donor HLA peptides
   - Score based on number of donor-derived peptides presented to recipient T cells
   - Clinical threshold: Score >150 = high dnDSA risk (>40%)
   - Incorporates both Class I and Class II HLA
   - More complex computational approach

4. **Clinical Evidence**
   - **De novo DSA formation**:
     * Meta-analysis (2023): 15 studies, >5,000 recipients
     * Low eplet mismatch (<10 Class II): 8-12% dnDSA incidence
     * High eplet mismatch (>20 Class II): 30-45% dnDSA incidence
     * HR 3.5 for dnDSA formation (high vs low eplet mismatch)

   - **Graft survival**:
     * 10-year graft survival: Low eplet (<15 total): 70-75%
     * 10-year graft survival: High eplet (>30 total): 55-60%
     * HR 1.6-2.0 for graft loss (high vs low eplet mismatch)

5. **Living Donor Selection Applications**
   - Scenario: Multiple potential living donors available
   - Example provided: Choosing between 3 potential donors
     * Donor A: 2-antigen mismatch, EML 25 (high risk)
     * Donor B: 4-antigen mismatch, EML 12 (low risk)
     * Recommendation: Choose Donor B despite more antigen mismatches
   - Most useful when traditional matching similar between donors

6. **Immunosuppression Tailoring**
   - High eplet mismatch (>25 total): Consider more intensive induction
   - High PIRCHE-II (>150): May warrant closer monitoring for dnDSA
   - Low eplet mismatch (<15 total): Potential for lower IS burden in future
   - **Note**: Not yet standard of care, research ongoing

7. **Technical and Clinical Limitations**
   - Requires high-resolution molecular HLA typing ($200-400 vs $50-100 serologic)
   - Not all epitopes equally immunogenic (some rarely cause dnDSA)
   - Algorithms still being refined and validated
   - Not incorporated into UNOS allocation system yet
   - Most useful for living donor selection, less applicable to deceased donor allocation

8. **Future Directions**
   - Incorporation into UNOS allocation algorithm (under consideration)
   - Machine learning/AI to predict immunogenicity of specific epitopes
   - Integration with donor-recipient age matching
   - Personalized immunosuppression protocols based on eplet load

**Practical Recommendations**:
- **Transplant centers**: Consider epitope matching for living donor evaluation when multiple donors available
- **Clinicians**: Use traditional matching for deceased donors (until UNOS adopts epitope matching)
- **Sensitized recipients**: Epitope matching may help identify "acceptable mismatches"
- **Research participation**: Encourage patients to enroll in epitope matching studies

**Board Exam High-Yield Concepts**:
- Epitope = small cluster of 3-8 amino acids on HLA molecule
- HLAMatchmaker identifies "eplets", PIRCHE-II predicts T-cell recognition
- High eplet mismatch → 3-4× higher dnDSA risk
- Best use: choosing between multiple living donors
- Not yet in UNOS deceased donor allocation

**Clinical Impact**: Medium-High - emerging tool for optimizing donor selection and predicting immunologic risk

**Verification Results**: COMPREHENSIVE COVERAGE CONFIRMED

   - FDA-approved indications: CKD (GFR 15-75), dialysis, post-transplant
   - Criteria: Height <5th percentile, bone age <10yr (girls) or <12yr (boys)
   - Open epiphyses confirmed on bone age X-ray

2. **Dosing and Monitoring** ✓
   - Standard dose: 0.05 mg/kg/day subcutaneous at bedtime
   - Monitoring schedule: Height/weight monthly, IGF-1 q3-6mo, bone age annually
   - Expected response: 8-12 cm/year first year, 4-6 cm/year subsequent

3. **Post-Transplant Safety** ✓
   - 2023 meta-analysis: No increased rejection risk
   - Improves height SDS by 1.0-1.5 over 3-5 years
   - Safe to start 12+ months post-transplant
   - No increased PTLD or malignancy risk

4. **Contraindications and Side Effects** ✓
   - Contraindications: Active malignancy, closed epiphyses, diabetic retinopathy
   - Side effects: Slipped capital femoral epiphysis, intracranial hypertension, glucose intolerance
   - Monitoring for complications included

5. **Evidence Base** ✓
   - Multiple RCTs demonstrating efficacy
   - Long-term outcome studies (normal adult height achievement)
   - Cost-effectiveness analysis
   - Quality of life improvements

**Board Exam Concepts Covered** ✓
- 0.05 mg/kg/day dosing
- Post-transplant safety (can resume 12mo post-tx)
- Monitoring: IGF-1, bone age, glucose
- SCFE as important complication
