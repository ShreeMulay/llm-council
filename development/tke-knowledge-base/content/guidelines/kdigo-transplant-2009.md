# KDIGO Clinical Practice Guideline for the Care of Kidney Transplant Recipients (2009) & Modern Supplements

## Overview
- **Scope:** This guideline covers the long-term outpatient management of adult kidney transplant recipients, focusing on immunosuppression strategies, allograft monitoring, infection prophylaxis, and management of cardiovascular and metabolic complications.
- **Why it matters for TKE:** Kidney transplant recipients represent a highly complex, high-risk population in outpatient nephrology. Proper management prevents allograft rejection, minimizes drug toxicity, and mitigates long-term risks such as cardiovascular disease, opportunistic infections, and malignancy.
- **Target Population:** Adult and pediatric kidney transplant recipients. (This reference focuses on adults).
- **Intended Audience:** Nephrologists, Advanced Practice Providers (APPs), transplant coordinators, and clinical pharmacists.
- **Note on Currency:** The base KDIGO guideline was published in 2009. Because transplant medicine has evolved, this reference incorporates essential modern updates (e.g., KDIGO 2021 BP guidelines, KDIGO 2022 Diabetes guidelines, and newer agents like Nulojix (belatacept)) to ensure safe, current practice at TKE.

## Key Recommendations

### 1. Immunosuppression: Maintenance Therapy
The goal is to prevent rejection while minimizing toxicity, infection, and malignancy.
- **First-Line Regimen (1A):** A combination of a calcineurin inhibitor (CNI) + an antiproliferative agent + corticosteroids.
- **Preferred CNI (2A):** Prograf / Envarsus XR / Astagraf XL (tacrolimus) is recommended over Gengraf / Neoral (cyclosporine) due to superior graft survival and lower rejection rates.
- **Preferred Antiproliferative (2B):** CellCept (mycophenolate mofetil - MMF) or Myfortic (mycophenolic acid - MPA) is recommended over Imuran (azathioprine).
- **Modern Alternative (Not in 2009 KDIGO):** Nulojix (belatacept) may be used as a CNI-free maintenance option to preserve eGFR and avoid CNI nephrotoxicity, provided the patient is Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) IgG positive.

**Dosing & Target Troughs (Tacrolimus):**
*Note: Targets vary by immunologic risk. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED] for specific local transplant center agreements.*
- **Months 1-3:** Target trough 8–10 ng/mL.
- **Months 4-12:** Target trough 6–8 ng/mL.
- **>12 Months:** Target trough 5–7 ng/mL.

### 2. Monitoring Allograft Function
Early detection of allograft dysfunction is critical for graft survival.
- **Serum Creatinine (SCr) & eGFR:** Measure at least monthly for months 1-6, every 2-3 months for months 7-12, and every 3-4 months thereafter (1B).
- **Proteinuria:** Monitor Urine Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio (UACR) or Urine Protein-to-Creatinine Ratio (UPCR) at least every 3 months for the first year, then annually (1C). Target UACR <30 mg/g.
- **Donor-Specific Antibodies (DSA):** Monitor for *de novo* DSA per transplant center protocol, typically if there is unexplained allograft dysfunction or non-adherence.
- **Allograft Biopsy:** The gold standard for diagnosing rejection, CNI toxicity, or recurrent glomerular disease.

### 3. Infection Prophylaxis & Monitoring
Immunosuppressed patients require targeted prophylaxis against opportunistic infections.
- **Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP) (1B):** 
  - *Drug:* Bactrim (sulfamethoxazole/trimethoprim) 400/80 mg (Single Strength) daily OR 800/160 mg (Double Strength) three times weekly.
  - *Duration:* 3 to 6 months post-transplant, and for 6 weeks during/after treatment for acute rejection.
  - *CKD Adjustment:* Reduce dose by 50% if eGFR 15-30 mL/min. Avoid if eGFR <15 mL/min. Alternative: Mepron (atovaquone) or Dapsone (dapsone).
- **Cytomegalovirus (CMV) (1B):**
  - *Drug:* Valcyte (valganciclovir).
  - *Duration:* 3 to 6 months based on Donor/Recipient serostatus (D+/R- is highest risk).
  - *CKD Adjustment:* Highly dependent on eGFR. If eGFR 40-59: 450 mg daily. If eGFR 25-39: 450 mg every 2 days. If eGFR 10-24: 450 mg twice weekly.
- **BK Polyomavirus (BKV) (1C):**
  - Screen plasma BKV DNA monthly for months 3-6, then every 3 months until year 1, then annually.
  - *Intervention:* If BKV DNA >10,000 copies/mL, reduce immunosuppression (typically lower antiproliferative first).

### 4. Cardiovascular & Metabolic Management
CVD is the leading cause of mortality in transplant recipients.
- **Blood Pressure:** KDIGO 2009 recommended <130/80 mmHg. *Modern Update (KDIGO 2021):* Target systolic BP <120 mmHg if tolerated.
- **Post-Transplant Diabetes Mellitus (PTDM):** Screen with fasting glucose, HbA1c, or OGTT at 1, 3, and 12 months, then annually. Target HbA1c 7.0-7.5%.
- **Lipids:** Treat with statins (e.g., Lipitor (atorvastatin) or Crestor (rosuvastatin)) based on cardiovascular risk, noting drug-drug interactions with CNIs.

## Practice Points

### Clinical Decision Trees
**Scenario 1: Rising Serum Creatinine (>20% from baseline)**
1. Rule out pre-renal causes (volume depletion, diuretics).
2. Rule out post-renal causes (ultrasound to check for hydronephrosis, lymphocele, or stricture).
3. Check Tacrolimus trough: Is it supratherapeutic (CNI toxicity) or subtherapeutic (risk of rejection)?
4. Check for infection: Urinalysis/culture, BKV PCR, CMV PCR.
5. If unexplained: Refer for allograft biopsy. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Biopsy referral workflow].

**Scenario 2: Leukopenia (WBC < 3.0 x 10^9/L)**
1. Rule out viral infection (CMV, BKV, Parvovirus B19).
2. Review medications: Valcyte (valganciclovir) and Bactrim (sulfamethoxazole/trimethoprim) are common culprits.
3. Escalate/Adjust: Reduce CellCept (mycophenolate mofetil) dose by 50% or hold temporarily. Do *not* reduce CNI without transplant center consultation.

### Patient Education Talking Points
- **Medication Adherence:** "Your anti-rejection medications must be taken at the exact same time every day. Missing even one dose increases the risk of losing your kidney."
- **OTC Avoidance:** "Never take NSAIDs like Advil (ibuprofen) or Aleve (naproxen). They can cause severe damage to your transplanted kidney. Use Tylenol (acetaminophen) instead."
- **Sun Protection:** "Your medications increase your risk of skin cancer. Wear SPF 50+ daily, wear long sleeves, and see a dermatologist annually."
- **Infection Signs:** "Call the clinic immediately for fever >100.4°F, burning with urination, or a new cough. Do not wait."

### ⚠️ Safety Concerns & Drug Interactions
- **CYP3A4 Inhibitors (Increase Tacrolimus levels):** Paxlovid (nirmatrelvir/ritonavir), Cardizem (diltiazem), Calan (verapamil), Diflucan (fluconazole), Biaxin (clarithromycin), Grapefruit juice. *Co-administration requires immediate empiric CNI dose reduction and frequent level monitoring.*
- **CYP3A4 Inducers (Decrease Tacrolimus levels):** Rifadin (rifampin), Dilantin (phenytoin), Tegretol (carbamazepine), St. John's Wort.
- **Live Vaccines:** Contraindicated post-transplant (e.g., MMR, Yellow Fever, live attenuated influenza).

## Evidence Summary

- **SYMPHONY Trial (2007):** Compared standard cyclosporine, MMF, and steroids vs. low-dose tacrolimus, low-dose cyclosporine, or low-dose sirolimus (all with daclizumab induction). 
  - *Result:* Low-dose tacrolimus + MMF + steroids showed the highest eGFR, best allograft survival, and lowest acute rejection rates at 1 year. This established the modern standard of care.
- **BENEFIT Trial (2010/2016):** Compared Nulojix (belatacept) to cyclosporine.
  - *Result:* Belatacept resulted in significantly higher patient and graft survival and higher eGFR at 7 years compared to CNI, though with a higher risk of early acute cellular rejection and PTLD (in EBV-negative patients).
- **Limitations of Evidence:** Many transplant trials rely on surrogate endpoints (1-year eGFR, acute rejection) rather than long-term (10+ year) graft survival.

## What's New / Changed (Modern Updates for TKE)

Since the 2009 KDIGO guideline, several critical shifts have occurred in nephrology that TKE providers must integrate:
1. **Extended-Release Tacrolimus:** Envarsus XR (tacrolimus extended-release) is now widely used. It requires a 20-30% dose reduction when converting from immediate-release Prograf (tacrolimus) and often results in fewer neurotoxic tremors.
2. **PTDM Management:** SGLT2 inhibitors (e.g., Farxiga (dapagliflozin)) and GLP-1 RAs (e.g., Ozempic (semaglutide)) are now standard of care for diabetic CKD (KDIGO 2022). While transplant patients were excluded from initial trials, real-world data supports their cautious use in stable transplant recipients (monitor for UTI/genital infections with SGLT2i).
3. **Blood Pressure Targets:** Shifted from <130/80 to <120 systolic (KDIGO 2021), though individualized based on patient tolerance and orthostasis.
4. **COVID-19:** Transplant patients require updated, aggressive vaccination schedules and rapid access to antivirals, requiring extreme caution with Paxlovid due to fatal CNI toxicity risks.
