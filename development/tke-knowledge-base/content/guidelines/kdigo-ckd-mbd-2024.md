## Overview

The KDIGO 2024 Clinical Practice Guideline Update for Chronic Kidney Disease-Mineral and Bone Disorder (CKD-MBD) provides the latest evidence-based framework for managing the complex interplay of phosphorus, calcium, parathyroid hormone (PTH), and vitamin D in patients with kidney disease. For nephrology practice, managing CKD-MBD is critical to preventing renal osteodystrophy, reducing severe vascular calcification, and mitigating cardiovascular mortality.

**Target Population and Intended Audience:**
This guideline applies to adult and pediatric patients with CKD stages G3a-G5D, as well as kidney transplant recipients (CKD G1T-G5T). The intended audience includes nephrologists, advanced practice providers (APPs), renal dietitians, and clinical pharmacists. 

**Why It Matters for TKE:**
In West Tennessee, where dietary habits often include high-phosphorus processed foods and regional rates of cardiovascular disease are elevated, aggressive and appropriate management of CKD-MBD is a cornerstone of our practice. The 2024 update emphasizes a shift away from strict biochemical normalization toward individualized, patient-centered trends, minimizing calcium loading, and integrating newer therapies that reduce pill burden.

## Key Recommendations

### Phosphorus Management
- **Target:** Lower elevated phosphorus levels *toward* the normal range (2.5–4.5 mg/dL) rather than strictly normalizing them (Grade 2C).
- **Dietary Interventions:** Restrict dietary phosphate, prioritizing the limitation of inorganic phosphate additives (found in processed foods/colas) over organic plant-based phosphates (Grade 1B). 
- **Pharmacotherapy:** 
  - Restrict the dose of calcium-based phosphate binders (e.g., PhosLo (calcium acetate), Tums (calcium carbonate)) across all CKD stages to prevent vascular calcification (Grade 1B).
  - **First-line non-calcium binders:** Renvela (sevelamer carbonate), Fosrenol (lanthanum carbonate), Velphoro (sucroferric oxyhydroxide), or Auryxia (ferric citrate).
  - *Note:* Auryxia (ferric citrate) also increases ferritin and TSAT; it is highly useful in concurrent CKD-MBD and anemia.

### Calcium Management
- **Target:** Avoid hypercalcemia (Grade 2C). Maintain serum corrected calcium within the normal laboratory reference range (typically 8.4–10.2 mg/dL).
- **Hypocalcemia:** Mild, asymptomatic hypocalcemia should generally *not* be treated with calcium loading. Treat symptomatic hypocalcemia or severe drops (< 7.5 mg/dL) with Rocaltrol (calcitriol) or careful calcium supplementation.
- **Dialysate Calcium:** Use a dialysate calcium concentration of 2.5 mEq/L (1.25 mmol/L) for most patients (Grade 2C).

### Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) Management
- **CKD G3a-G5 (Non-Dialysis):** Routine use of calcitriol or vitamin D analogs is *not* recommended unless severe and progressively rising hyperparathyroidism is present (Grade 2C). Target: Evaluate trends rather than a specific absolute number.
- **CKD G5D (Dialysis):** Maintain intact PTH (iPTH) levels in the range of approximately 2 to 9 times the upper limit of normal (ULN) for the assay used (typically 130–600 pg/mL) (Grade 2C).
- **Pharmacotherapy (SHPT):** 
  - Calcimimetics: Sensipar (cinacalcet) or Parsabiv (etelcalcetide).
  - Active Vitamin D Analogs: Zemplar (paricalcitol), Hectorol (doxercalciferol), or Rocaltrol (calcitriol).
  - *Decision Tree:* If PTH > 600 pg/mL AND Calcium > 9.5 mg/dL $\rightarrow$ Start Calcimimetic. If PTH > 600 pg/mL AND Calcium < 8.4 mg/dL $\rightarrow$ Start Active Vitamin D.

### Vitamin D Deficiency
- **Target:** Measure 25(OH)D (calcidiol) levels. Treat deficiency (< 20 ng/mL) or insufficiency (20-30 ng/mL) with nutritional vitamin D: Rayaldee (calcifediol), generic cholecalciferol (Vitamin D3), or ergocalciferol (Vitamin D2) (Grade 2C).

### Bone Quality and Osteoporosis
- **Assessment:** Perform bone mineral density (BMD) testing via DEXA if the results will impact treatment decisions (Grade 2B).
- **Treatment:** Antiresorptive therapy (e.g., Fosamax (alendronate), Prolia (denosumab)) can be used in CKD G3a-G3b. 
- *Safety Warning:* Severe, life-threatening hypocalcemia has been reported with Prolia (denosumab) in advanced CKD (G4-G5D). [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED] for mandatory calcium monitoring if denosumab is administered by rheumatology/endocrinology.

## Practice Points

**Practical Implementation for the Nephrology Clinic:**
- **Pill Burden:** Phosphate binders constitute up to 50% of the daily pill burden for ESRD patients. When adherence is poor, consider switching from TID Renvela (sevelamer carbonate) to chewable Velphoro (sucroferric oxyhydroxide) or Fosrenol (lanthanum carbonate) to reduce the number of tablets.
- **Dietitian Integration:** Always pair new binder prescriptions with a referral to the TKE renal dietitian. Emphasize reading labels for "PHOS" ingredients.
- **Timing of Labs:** Always draw PTH, calcium, and phosphorus at the same time to accurately assess the CKD-MBD axis. In hemodialysis, draw mid-week, pre-dialysis.

**Common Clinical Scenarios:**
- *Scenario:* Patient on hemodialysis with PTH 800 pg/mL, Calcium 10.5 mg/dL, Phos 6.5 mg/dL.
  - *Action:* Avoid active vitamin D (will worsen hypercalcemia and hyperphosphatemia). Start Sensipar (cinacalcet) 30 mg daily or Parsabiv (etelcalcetide) 5 mg IV TIW. Maximize non-calcium binders.
- *Scenario:* Patient with CKD G4, PTH 150 pg/mL, 25(OH)D 12 ng/mL.
  - *Action:* Replete nutritional vitamin D with cholecalciferol 50,000 IU weekly for 8 weeks. Do not start active vitamin D analogs.

**Grey Areas:**
- **Adynamic Bone Disease:** Suspect if PTH is consistently < 100 pg/mL in ESRD. The guideline suggests reducing or stopping calcimimetics and active vitamin D, and allowing PTH to rise to avoid adynamic bone disease, which paradoxically increases vascular calcification.

## Evidence Summary

- **EVOLVE Trial (Cinacalcet):** Evaluated Sensipar (cinacalcet) vs. placebo in hemodialysis patients with SHPT. *Primary endpoint:* Time to death or non-fatal cardiovascular events. *Result:* While the unadjusted primary endpoint was not statistically significant (p=0.11), age-adjusted analyses and secondary endpoints showed significant reductions in parathyroidectomy and severe fracture.
- **ADVANCE Study:** Evaluated Sensipar (cinacalcet) + low-dose vitamin D vs. flexible vitamin D alone. *Result:* Calcimimetic-based regimens attenuated the progression of vascular and cardiac valve calcification compared to vitamin D alone.
- **HiLo Trial (Ongoing/Recent Data):** Pragmatic trial comparing liberal (≥5.0 mg/dL) vs. strict (<5.5 mg/dL) phosphate targets in ESRD. Early data supports the KDIGO shift away from aggressive normalization, showing strict targets often lead to malnutrition and excessive pill burden without clear mortality benefit.
- **Limitations of Evidence:** Most CKD-MBD targets are based on observational data linking biochemical extremes to mortality. RCTs proving that treating to specific laboratory targets improves hard cardiovascular outcomes remain scarce.

## What's New / Changed

- **De-escalation of Calcium Binders:** The 2024 update takes a stronger stance against calcium-based binders across all stages of CKD, moving them firmly to second-line or adjunctive status due to cumulative evidence of vascular calcification.
- **Individualized PTH Targets:** Moving away from strict numerical targets in non-dialysis CKD, emphasizing the treatment of *progressively rising* PTH rather than isolated elevated values.
- **Integration of Parsabiv (etelcalcetide):** Formal inclusion of IV calcimimetics in the treatment algorithms, highlighting their utility in overcoming the GI intolerance and non-adherence associated with oral Sensipar (cinacalcet).
- **Denosumab Black Box Warning:** Incorporation of recent FDA warnings regarding severe hypocalcemia with Prolia (denosumab) in advanced CKD/ESRD.

### Patient Education Talking Points
- **The "Bone-Vascular" Connection:** Explain to patients that high phosphorus doesn't just weaken bones; it turns blood vessels into "bone-like" rigid pipes, increasing heart attack risk.
- **Binder Timing:** "Binders only work if they touch the food in your stomach. Taking them 2 hours after a meal is like wearing a seatbelt after the car crash."
- **Hidden Phosphorus:** Teach patients to look for "PHOS" in the ingredient list of dark sodas, processed meats, and fast food. Plant-based phosphorus (beans, nuts) is poorly absorbed and safer than chemical additives.
