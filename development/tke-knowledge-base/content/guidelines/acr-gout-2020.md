## Overview

### Scope and Nephrology Relevance
The 2020 ACR Guideline provides updated, evidence-based recommendations for the management of gout, focusing on indications for urate-lowering therapy (ULT), treat-to-target strategies, and flare management. 

For nephrology practice, this guideline is highly relevant because hyperuricemia and gout are ubiquitous in chronic kidney disease (CKD). Historically, providers under-dosed ULT in CKD due to fears of Allopurinol Hypersensitivity Syndrome (AHS). The 2020 ACR guideline explicitly supports a "start low, go slow" titration strategy for Zyloprim (allopurinol) in CKD, allowing nephrologists to safely achieve serum urate targets even in advanced renal impairment.

### Target Population and Audience
- **Population:** Adult patients with acute gout flares, intercritical gout, or chronic tophaceous gout, particularly those with comorbid CKD.
- **Audience:** Rheumatologists, Nephrologists, Primary Care Providers, and Advanced Practice Providers (APPs).

### Major Paradigm Shifts
- Strong endorsement of a **treat-to-target** strategy (Serum Urate < 6.0 mg/dL) over fixed-dose ULT.
- Strong recommendation for **Zyloprim (allopurinol)** as first-line ULT for *all* patients, including those with CKD stage $\ge$ 3.
- Expanded indications for HLA-B*5801 testing prior to starting Zyloprim (allopurinol).

## Key Recommendations

### 1. Indications for Urate-Lowering Therapy (ULT)
**Strongly Recommended (Initiate ULT):**
*   $\ge$ 1 subcutaneous tophi
*   Evidence of radiographic damage attributable to gout
*   Frequent gout flares ($\ge$ 2 annually)

**Conditionally Recommended (Initiate ULT):**
*   Patients who have experienced > 1 flare but have < 2 flares/year.
*   Patients experiencing their *first* gout flare IF they have:
    *   **CKD stage $\ge$ 3** (eGFR < 60 mL/min/1.73m²)
    *   Serum urate (SU) > 9.0 mg/dL
    *   Urolithiasis

**Conditionally Recommended AGAINST:**
*   Asymptomatic hyperuricemia (do not treat with ULT).
*   First gout flare without the above comorbidities.

### 2. First-Line ULT and Treat-to-Target Strategy
**Target Values:**
*   **Target SU:** < 6.0 mg/dL (Strongly recommended for all patients on ULT).
*   *Note:* Lower targets (e.g., < 5.0 mg/dL) may be needed for patients with extensive tophaceous disease to promote dissolution.

**Drug Recommendations & Dosing:**
*   **First-Line:** Zyloprim (allopurinol) is strongly recommended over all other ULTs, *including in patients with CKD stage $\ge$ 3*.
*   **Alternative:** Uloric (febuxostat) is recommended if Zyloprim (allopurinol) is contraindicated or not tolerated.
*   **Uricosurics:** Probalan (probenecid) is conditionally recommended against in patients with eGFR < 50 mL/min/1.73m².

**CKD-Specific Dosing Modifications (Zyloprim / allopurinol):**
*   **Normal Renal Function:** Start $\le$ 100 mg/day.
*   **CKD Stage $\ge$ 3:** Start $\le$ 50 mg/day.
*   **Titration:** Increase dose by 50-100 mg increments every 2-5 weeks until SU < 6.0 mg/dL. 
*   *Crucial ACR Point:* Zyloprim (allopurinol) **can** be titrated above 300 mg/day in CKD patients if accompanied by adequate patient education and close monitoring for toxicity (pruritus, rash, elevated LFTs).

### 3. HLA-B*5801 Testing
**Testing Indications:**
*   Conditionally recommended prior to starting Zyloprim (allopurinol) in patients of Southeast Asian descent (e.g., Han Chinese, Korean, Thai) and African American descent.
*   Universal testing is conditionally recommended against.

### 4. Flare Prophylaxis (When Initiating ULT)
Initiating ULT mobilizes urate stores and frequently precipitates flares.
*   **First-Line Prophylaxis:** Colcrys/Mitigare (colchicine) OR NSAIDs (e.g., Indocin/indomethacin) OR low-dose glucocorticoids (e.g., Deltasone/prednisone).
*   **Duration:** Continue prophylaxis for 3 to 6 months (Strongly recommended).
*   **CKD Context:** NSAIDs are generally contraindicated in CKD. Colcrys (colchicine) requires strict renal dosing. Deltasone (prednisone) $\le$ 5-10 mg/day is often the safest choice in advanced CKD.

### 5. Acute Flare Management
*   **First-Line:** Colcrys/Mitigare (colchicine), NSAIDs, or Glucocorticoids (oral, intra-articular, or intramuscular).
*   **Timing:** Start treatment as early as possible (within 12-24 hours of onset).
*   **During ULT:** If a flare occurs while on ULT, *do not stop* the ULT. Treat the flare concurrently.

## Practice Points

### Practical Implementation for Nephrology
*   **The "Allopurinol Ceiling" Myth:** Many providers stop Zyloprim (allopurinol) at 100-300 mg in CKD patients due to historical guidelines. ACR 2020 explicitly states that treat-to-target (SU < 6.0) is paramount, and doses can exceed 300 mg/day in CKD if titrated slowly and monitored closely.
*   **Colchicine Toxicity:** Colcrys (colchicine) toxicity (neuromyopathy, cytopenias) is highly prevalent in CKD. 
    *   *eGFR 30-59:* 0.3 mg daily or 0.6 mg every other day for prophylaxis.
    *   *eGFR < 30:* 0.3 mg twice weekly for prophylaxis (or avoid entirely).
    *   *Dialysis:* Avoid for prophylaxis; use extreme caution for acute flares (e.g., single 0.6 mg dose, do not repeat for 14 days).
*   **Steroids as the CKD Workhorse:** Given NSAID contraindications and Colcrys (colchicine) toxicity risks, oral Deltasone (prednisone) or Medrol (methylprednisolone) dose packs are often the safest, most effective flare management tools in the nephrology clinic.

### Decision Tree: ULT Initiation and Escalation
1.  **Assess Indication:** Does patient have $\ge$ 1 tophus, radiographic damage, $\ge$ 2 flares/year, OR 1 flare + CKD 3+?
    *   *No:* Monitor. Treat flares as they occur.
    *   *Yes:* Proceed to Step 2.
2.  **Pre-Screening:** Is patient African American or Southeast Asian?
    *   *Yes:* Order HLA-B*5801 test. If positive, use Uloric (febuxostat). If negative, proceed to Step 3.
    *   *No:* Proceed to Step 3.
3.  **Initiate ULT & Prophylaxis:**
    *   Start Zyloprim (allopurinol) 50 mg daily (if eGFR < 60).
    *   Start flare prophylaxis (e.g., Deltasone/prednisone 5 mg daily).
4.  **Titrate to Target:**
    *   Check SU every 2-4 weeks.
    *   If SU $\ge$ 6.0 mg/dL: Increase Zyloprim (allopurinol) by 50 mg.
    *   If SU < 6.0 mg/dL: Maintain dose.
5.  **Refractory Disease:** If SU remains > 6.0 despite maximum tolerated Zyloprim (allopurinol), switch to Uloric (febuxostat). If severe tophaceous gout remains refractory, consider Krystexxa (pegloticase) referral to Rheumatology.

### ⚠️ Safety Flags & Contraindications
*   **Allopurinol Hypersensitivity Syndrome (AHS):** High mortality. Risk factors include concurrent thiazide diuretic use, renal impairment, HLA-B*5801 positivity, and high starting doses. *Starting low mitigates this risk.*
*   **Uloric (febuxostat) CV Risk:** Carries an FDA Boxed Warning for increased risk of cardiovascular death compared to allopurinol (based on the CARES trial). Avoid in patients with a history of CVD if possible.
*   **Drug Interactions:** Zyloprim (allopurinol) and Uloric (febuxostat) severely interact with Imuran (azathioprine) and Purinethol (mercaptopurine), leading to fatal bone marrow suppression. **Absolute contraindication.**

## Evidence Summary

*   **STOP-GOUT Trial (2022 - post-guideline validation):** A multicenter, randomized, double-blind non-inferiority trial comparing allopurinol and febuxostat. Showed allopurinol was non-inferior to febuxostat in managing flares when using a treat-to-target strategy, reinforcing the ACR 2020 preference for allopurinol.
*   **CARES Trial (2018):** Evaluated CV safety of febuxostat vs. allopurinol. Found higher rates of all-cause and CV mortality with febuxostat. This drove the ACR recommendation to position allopurinol as the definitive first-line agent.
*   **Stamp et al. (2017):** Demonstrated that gradual dose escalation of allopurinol in CKD patients successfully achieved target SU without increasing the risk of AHS, directly informing the ACR 2020 dosing guidelines.

## What's New / Changed (from 2012 ACR Guidelines)

1.  **Treat-to-Target:** Upgraded to a *Strong* recommendation. Fixed-dose strategies are explicitly discouraged.
2.  **First-Line Agent:** Zyloprim (allopurinol) is now strongly recommended over Uloric (febuxostat) for *all* patients, including those with CKD.
3.  **CKD Dosing:** The previous hesitation to escalate Zyloprim (allopurinol) in CKD has been removed. The guideline now explicitly supports titrating past 300 mg/day in CKD if started low ($\le$ 50 mg) and monitored.
4.  **Vitamin C:** Previously considered a mild urate-lowering adjunct; now conditionally recommended *against* due to lack of meaningful clinical efficacy.

### One-Page Summary: Gout Management in CKD
1.  **Identify the Need:** Treat patients with tophi, joint damage, $\ge$ 2 flares/year, OR 1 flare + CKD stage 3+. Do not treat asymptomatic hyperuricemia.
2.  **Check Genetics:** Order HLA-B*5801 for African American and Southeast Asian patients before starting Zyloprim (allopurinol).
3.  **Start Low:** Initiate Zyloprim (allopurinol) at 50 mg daily for patients with eGFR < 60. 
4.  **Protect the Joints:** Start flare prophylaxis simultaneously. Use Deltasone (prednisone) 5-10 mg daily or strictly renally-dosed Colcrys (colchicine). Avoid NSAIDs.
5.  **Go Slow (Treat-to-Target):** Check SU every 2-4 weeks. Increase Zyloprim (allopurinol) by 50 mg increments until SU < 6.0 mg/dL. Do not stop at 300 mg if the target is not met, provided the patient is tolerating the drug.
6.  **Manage Flares Safely:** Treat acute flares early with oral or intra-articular steroids. **Never stop ULT during an acute flare.**
