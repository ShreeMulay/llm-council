# KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases

## Overview
The **KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases** represents a paradigm shift in nephrology, moving away from purely histopathologic descriptions (e.g., "idiopathic membranous nephropathy") toward pathogenesis- and biomarker-driven management (e.g., "PLA2R-associated membranous nephropathy"). 

**Why it matters for TKE practice:**
Glomerular diseases (GN) are a leading cause of end-stage kidney disease (ESKD). This guideline provides the evidence-based framework for diagnosing, risk-stratifying, and treating primary and secondary GN. For TKE clinicians, it standardizes the approach to immunosuppression, emphasizes maximizing supportive care before initiating cytotoxic therapies, and integrates novel biomarkers into routine monitoring.

**Target Population & Intended Audience:**
*   **Population:** Adults and children with biopsy-proven or biomarker-confirmed glomerular diseases (IgAN, Membranous, FSGS, Lupus Nephritis, ANCA Vasculitis, MCD, Infection-related GN).
*   **Audience:** Nephrologists, Advanced Practice Providers (APPs), and clinical pharmacists managing GN.

**What Changed from the 2012 Version:**
*   **Biomarker Integration:** Routine use of anti-PLA2R antibodies for Membranous Nephropathy diagnosis and monitoring.
*   **Risk Stratification:** Formal risk calculators (e.g., International IgAN Prediction Tool) are now required before initiating immunosuppression.
*   **Reduced Toxicity:** Lower-dose glucocorticoid regimens are now standard of care for ANCA vasculitis and IgA Nephropathy to minimize harm.
*   **Nomenclature:** "Primary/Secondary" FSGS is replaced with a pathogenesis-based classification (Primary/Podocytopathic vs. Adaptive/Secondary vs. Genetic).

## Key Recommendations

### 1. General Supportive Care for All Glomerular Diseases
Maximal supportive care is the mandatory first step for all GN patients before considering immunosuppression.
*   **Blood Pressure:** Target systolic BP < 120 mm Hg (per KDIGO 2021 BP guidelines) if tolerated (Grade 1B).
*   **Proteinuria Target:** Target proteinuria < 1.0 g/day for most GN; < 0.3 g/day (complete remission) is optimal.
*   **RAAS Inhibition:** Initiate *Cozaar (losartan)*, *Prinivil/Zestril (lisinopril)*, or *Diovan (valsartan)* at maximum tolerated doses for proteinuria > 0.5 g/day (Grade 1B).
*   **SGLT2 Inhibitors:** Initiate *Farxiga (dapagliflozin)* 10 mg daily or *Jardiance (empagliflozin)* 10 mg daily for patients with eGFR ≥ 20 mL/min/1.73m² and UACR ≥ 200 mg/g despite max RAASi (Grade 1A based on DAPA-CKD/EMPA-KIDNEY).

### 2. IgA Nephropathy (IgAN)
*   **Risk Assessment:** Calculate the International IgAN Prediction Tool score at biopsy to predict 50% eGFR decline or ESKD.
*   **Supportive Care:** Minimum 3-6 months of optimized RAASi + SGLT2i.
*   **Immunosuppression Decision Tree:**
    *   *Start:* If proteinuria remains > 0.75 - 1.0 g/day despite 3-6 months of max supportive care, consider a 6-month course of glucocorticoids.
    *   *Regimen:* *Rayos (prednisone)* 0.5–1 mg/kg/day (max 80 mg) tapering over 6 months.
    *   *Alternative (Post-2021 FDA Approval):* *Tarpeyo (budesonide)* targeted-release 16 mg daily for 9 months for patients at risk of rapid progression.
    *   *Stop/Avoid:* Avoid systemic steroids if eGFR < 30 mL/min/1.73m², diabetes, BMI > 30, or active peptic ulcer disease due to high toxicity risk (Grade 2B).

### 3. Membranous Nephropathy (MN)
*   **Diagnosis:** Anti-PLA2R antibody testing is mandatory. A kidney biopsy may not be required if PLA2R is positive and eGFR is stable without secondary signs (Grade 1B).
*   **Risk Stratification & Treatment:**
    *   *Low Risk* (Normal eGFR, proteinuria < 3.5 g/d): Supportive care only.
    *   *Moderate Risk* (Proteinuria > 3.5 g/d but stable eGFR, PLA2R < 50 RU/mL): Wait 6 months. If no remission, start *Rituxan (rituximab)* or Calcineurin Inhibitor (CNI) like *Prograf (tacrolimus)* or *Neoral (cyclosporine)*.
    *   *High Risk* (eGFR < 60, proteinuria > 8 g/d, PLA2R > 50 RU/mL): Start *Rituxan (rituximab)* 1g IV x 2 doses (days 1 and 15) OR *Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide)* + steroids (Modified Ponticelli regimen).
*   **Monitoring:** Check PLA2R levels every 3-6 months. Immunologic remission (PLA2R disappearance) precedes clinical remission (proteinuria drop) by months.

### 4. Lupus Nephritis (LN)
*   **Induction Therapy (Class III/IV):** 
    *   *Regimen A:* *CellCept (mycophenolate mofetil)* 2-3 g/day + *Rayos (prednisone)*.
    *   *Regimen B:* Low-dose IV *Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide)* (Euro-Lupus regimen: 500 mg IV every 2 weeks x 6 doses) + *Rayos (prednisone)*.
*   **Escalation/Add-on Therapies:** For severe or refractory cases, add *Benlysta (belimumab)* IV/SQ or *Lupkynis (voclosporin)* 23.7 mg BID to the *CellCept (mycophenolate)* backbone.
*   **Maintenance:** *CellCept (mycophenolate mofetil)* 1-2 g/day or *Imuran (azathioprine)* 1.5-2 mg/kg/day. Maintain for at least 36-60 months.

### 5. ANCA-Associated Vasculitis
*   **Induction:** *Rituxan (rituximab)* 375 mg/m² weekly x 4 doses OR 1g IV x 2 doses (days 1 and 15) + Glucocorticoids.
*   **Steroid Taper:** Use the reduced-dose PEXIVAS steroid taper to minimize toxicity (Grade 1B).
*   **Emerging Therapy:** *Tavneos (avacopan)* 30 mg BID can be used as a steroid-sparing adjunct in severe active disease.
*   **Maintenance:** *Rituxan (rituximab)* 500 mg IV every 6 months for 18-24 months.

### 6. Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)
*   **Differentiation:** Must distinguish Primary (podocytopathy, sudden onset nephrotic syndrome, diffuse foot process effacement) from Secondary (adaptive, slow onset, normal albumin).
*   **Primary FSGS Treatment:** High-dose *Rayos (prednisone)* 1 mg/kg/day (max 80 mg) for up to 16 weeks. 
*   **Secondary FSGS Treatment:** Supportive care ONLY (RAASi, SGLT2i, weight loss). Immunosuppression is contraindicated.

## Practice Points

**Practical Implementation for TKE Clinic:**
*   **The "Wait and See" Approach:** For MN and IgAN, immediate immunosuppression is rarely indicated. A 3-6 month run-in period of maximal supportive care (RAASi + SGLT2i + BP control) is standard.
*   **Biomarker Tracking:** Create flowsheets in the EHR to track PLA2R titers alongside UACR and serum albumin for MN patients.
*   **Vaccinations:** Administer Pneumococcal, Influenza, and COVID-19 vaccines *before* starting *Rituxan (rituximab)* or *Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide)*.

**Grey Areas & Clinical Judgment:**
*   **Advanced CKD:** Initiating heavy immunosuppression in patients with eGFR < 30 mL/min/1.73m² and extensive interstitial fibrosis on biopsy is controversial. The risk of fatal infections often outweighs the chance of kidney recovery.
*   **Steroid Toxicity:** Nephrologists must actively manage steroid side effects. Prescribe *Bactrim (sulfamethoxazole/trimethoprim)* for PCP prophylaxis if on > 20 mg prednisone for > 4 weeks. Monitor for steroid-induced diabetes and osteoporosis.

## Evidence Summary

*   **TESTING Trial (IgAN):** Evaluated oral methylprednisolone vs. placebo. *Result:* Steroids reduced kidney failure but caused severe infections. Led to the recommendation for lower-dose steroid regimens and strict contraindications.
*   **MENTOR Trial (Membranous):** Compared *Rituxan (rituximab)* vs. *Neoral (cyclosporine)*. *Result:* Rituximab was non-inferior at 12 months and superior at 24 months for maintaining remission. Established Rituximab as first-line.
*   **PEXIVAS Trial (ANCA):** Compared standard vs. reduced-dose glucocorticoids. *Result:* Reduced-dose steroids were non-inferior for ESKD/death but significantly reduced serious infections.
*   **BLISS-LN (Lupus):** Evaluated *Benlysta (belimumab)* + standard therapy. *Result:* Higher rates of primary efficacy renal response (PERR).
*   **AURORA 1 & 2 (Lupus):** Evaluated *Lupkynis (voclosporin)* + standard therapy. *Result:* Faster and higher rates of complete renal response.

**Limitations of Evidence:**
Many GN trials have small sample sizes and short follow-up periods. The definition of "remission" varies across studies. There is limited data on managing GN in non-white populations, particularly for IgAN.

## What's New / Changed

*   **Shift from Steroids:** Across all GN types, there is a massive push to reduce cumulative glucocorticoid exposure.
*   **New Drugs Included/Emerging:**
    *   *Farxiga (dapagliflozin)* / *Jardiance (empagliflozin)* for non-diabetic GN proteinuria.
    *   *Benlysta (belimumab)* and *Lupkynis (voclosporin)* for Lupus Nephritis.
    *   *Tavneos (avacopan)* for ANCA vasculitis (steroid-sparing).
    *   *Tarpeyo (budesonide)* and *Filspari (sparsentan)* for IgAN (FDA approved post-2021 but highly relevant to current guideline application).
*   **Changed Targets:** Stricter BP control (< 120 systolic) and lower thresholds for initiating SGLT2 inhibitors.

### Relevant ICD-10 & CPT Codes
*   **ICD-10 Codes:**
    *   N02.8 - Recurrent and persistent hematuria with other morphologic changes (IgAN)
    *   N04.2 - Nephrotic syndrome with diffuse membranous glomerulonephritis
    *   N04.1 - Nephrotic syndrome with focal and segmental glomerular lesions (FSGS)
    *   M32.14 - Glomerular disease in systemic lupus erythematosus
    *   M31.31 - Granulomatosis with polyangiitis (Wegener's) with renal involvement
*   **CPT Codes:**
    *   50200 - Renal biopsy; percutaneous, by trocar or needle
    *   96413, 96415 - Chemotherapy administration, intravenous infusion (used for Rituximab/Cyclophosphamide)

### Patient Education Talking Points
*   **The "Marathon, Not a Sprint":** Explain that GN treatment takes months to show results. Protein levels in the urine drop slowly, even if the immune system is controlled quickly.
*   **Infection Risk:** "The medications we use to save your kidneys lower your immune system. You must call the clinic for any fever > 100.4°F. Avoid sick contacts and ensure your vaccines are up to date before we start."
*   **Diet & Lifestyle:** Emphasize a low-sodium diet (< 2g/day) to help blood pressure medications work and reduce swelling.
*   **Medication Adherence:** "Do not stop your blood pressure pills or steroids abruptly. Stopping steroids suddenly can be life-threatening."

## Safety Flags & Contraindications

*   **🚨 INFECTION RISK:** Hold immunosuppression (except physiologic steroid doses) during active severe infections.
*   **🚨 CYTOXAN (Cyclophosphamide) TOXICITY:** Risk of hemorrhagic cystitis, infertility, and malignancy. Adjust dose for eGFR < 30 and age > 60. Always co-administer with Mesna if using high IV doses.
*   **🚨 LUPKYNIS (Voclosporin) / CNIs:** Can cause acute hemodynamic decreases in eGFR. Do not use if baseline eGFR < 45 mL/min/1.73m² (Voclosporin). Monitor BP closely.
*   **🚨 PREGNANCY:** *CellCept (mycophenolate)*, *Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide)*, and RAAS inhibitors are **TERATOGENIC**. Ensure highly effective contraception in women of childbearing age. Switch to *Imuran (azathioprine)* and *Prograf (tacrolimus)* if pregnancy is planned.

### One-Page Summary of Most Important Recommendations
1.  **Supportive Care is King:** Never rush to immunosuppression. Maximize *Cozaar (losartan)* / *Prinivil (lisinopril)*, add *Farxiga (dapagliflozin)* / *Jardiance (empagliflozin)*, and control BP < 120 systolic for 3-6 months first (unless rapidly progressive GN or nephrotic syndrome).
2.  **Biomarkers over Biopsies (Sometimes):** A positive anti-PLA2R antibody in a patient with nephrotic syndrome and normal kidney function is diagnostic for Membranous Nephropathy; a biopsy may be deferred.
3.  **Risk Stratify Everything:** Use the IgAN Prediction Tool for IgA, and PLA2R titers + proteinuria levels for Membranous Nephropathy to justify the risks of immunosuppression.
4.  **Lower Steroid Doses:** Use the PEXIVAS reduced-dose taper for ANCA vasculitis. Avoid steroids in IgAN if eGFR < 30 or high metabolic risk.
5.  **Modernize Lupus Care:** Standard induction is *CellCept (mycophenolate)* + steroids. Do not hesitate to add *Benlysta (belimumab)* or *Lupkynis (voclosporin)* for refractory proteinuria.
6.  **Primary vs. Secondary FSGS:** Only treat Primary FSGS (diffuse foot process effacement, sudden onset, low albumin) with steroids. Secondary FSGS gets supportive care only.
