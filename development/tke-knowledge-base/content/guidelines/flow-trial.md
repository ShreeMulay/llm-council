## Overview

### What this covers and why it matters for nephrology practice
The FLOW trial marks a paradigm shift in nephrology: it is the first dedicated renal outcomes trial to demonstrate that a Glucagon-Like Peptide-1 Receptor Agonist (GLP-1 RA)—specifically Ozempic (semaglutide)—significantly reduces the risk of major kidney disease events, cardiovascular events, and all-cause mortality in patients with Type 2 Diabetes (T2D) and Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD). 

Historically, GLP-1 RAs were recommended by KDIGO and ADA primarily for glycemic control, weight loss, and atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) risk reduction. Post-FLOW, Ozempic (semaglutide) is now recognized as a **primary disease-modifying therapy for CKD progression**, joining the ranks of RAS inhibitors, SGLT2 inhibitors (e.g., Farxiga (dapagliflozin), Jardiance (empagliflozin)), and non-steroidal MRAs (e.g., Kerendia (finerenone)).

### Target population and intended audience
*   **Target Population:** Patients with T2D and CKD (specifically those with eGFR 15–75 mL/min/1.73 m² and elevated Urine Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio [UACR] ≥100 mg/g).
*   **Intended Audience:** TKE Nephrologists, Advanced Practice Providers (APPs), and clinical pharmacists managing diabetic kidney disease.

## Key Recommendations

### 1. Patient Selection and Initiation
*   **Recommendation (KDIGO 1A / ADA A):** Initiate a GLP-1 RA with proven cardiovascular and kidney benefits in patients with T2D and CKD who have not achieved individualized glycemic targets, or as part of a comprehensive risk-reduction strategy independent of baseline A1c.
*   **FLOW-Specific Criteria:** Consider Ozempic (semaglutide) strongly for patients with T2D, eGFR ≥15 mL/min/1.73 m², and UACR >100 mg/g, regardless of whether they are already on an SGLT2 inhibitor.
*   **eGFR Thresholds:** 
    *   Ozempic (semaglutide) can be initiated down to an eGFR of 15 mL/min/1.73 m². 
    *   No dosage adjustment is required for any stage of CKD.
    *   *Caution:* Use in eGFR <15 mL/min/1.73 m² or dialysis is currently off-label and lacks robust safety data.

### 2. Dosing and Titration Schedule
*   **Drug:** Ozempic (semaglutide) subcutaneous injection.
*   **Standard Titration (to minimize GI adverse effects):**
    *   **Weeks 1-4:** 0.25 mg subcutaneously once weekly.
    *   **Weeks 5-8:** 0.5 mg subcutaneously once weekly.
    *   **Week 9 and beyond:** 1.0 mg subcutaneously once weekly (Target FLOW dose).
*   *Note:* Higher doses (e.g., 2.0 mg Ozempic or 2.4 mg Wegovy (semaglutide)) are approved for glycemic control and obesity, respectively, but 1.0 mg was the specific dose proven for primary kidney outcomes in FLOW.

### 3. Clinical and Laboratory Targets
*   **UACR:** Target UACR <30 mg/g (or at least a >30% reduction from baseline).
*   **HbA1c:** Individualized target, generally <6.5% to 8.0% depending on hypoglycemia risk and life expectancy.
*   **Weight:** Target >5-10% total body weight loss to reduce intraglomerular pressure and systemic inflammation.

## Practice Points

### Decision Tree: When to Start, Escalate, Switch, or Stop
1.  **START:** Patient has T2D + CKD (eGFR 15-75, UACR >100). 
    *   *Are they on max tolerated ACEi/ARB?* Yes.
    *   *Are they on an SGLT2i (e.g., Farxiga (dapagliflozin))?* Yes (or contraindicated).
    *   *Action:* Initiate Ozempic (semaglutide) 0.25 mg weekly.
2.  **ESCALATE:** Increase dose every 4 weeks per schedule above. If GI side effects occur, delay dose escalation for an additional 4 weeks.
3.  **SWITCH:** If a patient is on a DPP-4 inhibitor (e.g., Januvia (sitagliptin)), **STOP** the DPP-4i before starting Ozempic (semaglutide) due to overlapping mechanisms and lack of additive benefit.
4.  **STOP / HOLD:** 
    *   Hold for severe nausea/vomiting leading to volume depletion (risk of AKI).
    *   Hold 1 week prior to elective surgeries/endoscopy requiring general anesthesia or deep sedation (due to delayed gastric emptying and aspiration risk). [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]

### Practical Implementation Tips for Nephrology
*   **GI Mitigation:** Counsel patients to eat smaller meals, stop eating when they feel 80% full, and avoid high-fat or highly processed foods, which exacerbate delayed gastric emptying.
*   **Hydration:** Emphasize proactive hydration. The primary nephrology-specific risk of GLP-1 RAs is AKI secondary to volume depletion from vomiting or reduced oral intake.
*   **Polypharmacy:** When initiating Ozempic (semaglutide), proactively assess the need to reduce doses of sulfonylureas (e.g., Amaryl (glimepiride)) or insulin to prevent hypoglycemia.

### Safety Concerns & Contraindications 🚩
*   **Contraindications:** Personal or family history of Medullary Thyroid Carcinoma (MTC) or Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia syndrome type 2 (MEN 2).
*   **Warnings:** History of pancreatitis. Use with caution in patients with severe gastroparesis.
*   **Drug Interactions:** Delays gastric emptying; may impact absorption of concomitantly administered oral medications (e.g., narrow therapeutic index drugs like Prograf (tacrolimus) or Neoral (cyclosporine) in transplant patients).

## Evidence Summary

### The FLOW Trial (2024)
*   **Population:** 3,533 patients with T2D and CKD. Inclusion required eGFR 50–75 with UACR 300–5000 mg/g, OR eGFR 25–<50 with UACR 100–5000 mg/g. Patients were on max tolerated RAS inhibition.
*   **Intervention:** Ozempic (semaglutide) 1.0 mg weekly vs. Placebo.
*   **Primary Endpoint:** Composite of kidney failure (dialysis, transplant, or eGFR <15), ≥50% reduction in eGFR, or death from kidney or cardiovascular causes.
*   **Key Results:** 
    *   Trial stopped early for efficacy.
    *   **24% risk reduction** in the primary composite endpoint (Hazard Ratio 0.76; 95% CI, 0.66 to 0.88; P=0.0003).
    *   Slower rate of eGFR decline (-1.16 ml/min/1.73m² per year difference vs placebo).
    *   20% reduction in all-cause mortality.
*   **Number Needed to Treat (NNT):** ~20 patients treated over 3.4 years to prevent one primary endpoint event.
*   **Limitations:** Excluded non-diabetic CKD. Only 15% of patients were on baseline SGLT2 inhibitors (though subgroup analysis showed benefit regardless of SGLT2i use).

## What's New / Changed

*   **From Glycemic Agent to Kidney Protector:** Previously, GLP-1 RAs were second-line to SGLT2 inhibitors for kidney protection. Post-FLOW, Ozempic (semaglutide) has proven primary kidney outcome benefits, making it a foundational therapy alongside SGLT2 inhibitors.
*   **Additive Benefits:** The FLOW trial demonstrated that the kidney-protective benefits of Ozempic (semaglutide) are additive to RAS inhibitors and likely additive to SGLT2 inhibitors.
*   **Mortality Benefit:** FLOW is one of the few nephrology trials to show a significant reduction in *all-cause mortality* (HR 0.80) in a severe CKD population.
