## 2. ACUTE FLARE MANAGEMENT DECISION TREE
*Goal: Initiate treatment within 12-24 hours of symptom onset. Continue baseline Urate-Lowering Therapy (ULT) during acute flares; do not stop ULT.*

🚨 **SAFETY WARNING:** **AVOID NSAIDs** (e.g., Advil/Motrin (ibuprofen), Aleve (naproxen), Celebrex (celecoxib)) in **ALL** CKD patients due to risk of acute kidney injury and worsening CKD progression.

### Pathway by eGFR (mL/min/1.73m²)

#### eGFR ≥ 30
* **First-Line:** Colcrys (colchicine) 
  * *Dose:* 1.2 mg immediately, followed by 0.6 mg 1 hour later (low-dose regimen). Max 1.8 mg in 1 hour.
  * *Follow-up:* 0.6 mg once or twice daily starting 12 hours later until flare resolves.
* **Alternative:** Prednisone
  * *Dose:* 30-40 mg PO daily for 5-7 days (no taper needed if ≤ 7 days).
* **Localized Monoarticular Flare:** Intra-articular corticosteroid injection. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Referral workflow for joint injections vs. in-clinic administration].

#### eGFR 15 - 29
* **First-Line (Preferred):** Prednisone 
  * *Dose:* 30-40 mg PO daily for 5-7 days.
* **Alternative:** Colcrys (colchicine)
  * *Dose:* **REDUCE** to 0.3 mg single dose. 
  * *Warning:* Avoid repeated dosing; do not repeat course for at least 14 days. Avoid entirely in severe renal impairment if possible.
* **Localized Monoarticular Flare:** Intra-articular corticosteroid injection.

#### eGFR < 15 or Dialysis (ESRD)
* **First-Line (Preferred):** Prednisone
  * *Dose:* 30-40 mg PO daily for 5-7 days.
* **Contraindicated:** Colcrys (colchicine) — **AVOID** due to high risk of fatal neuromyopathy and bone marrow suppression.
* **Refractory Cases:** Kineret (anakinra) - IL-1 inhibitor
  * *Dose:* 100 mg SubQ daily for 3-5 days. (Off-label use; requires prior authorization).

## 3. URATE-LOWERING THERAPY (ULT) ALGORITHM

### Indications to Start ULT (ACR 2020)
Initiate ULT if the patient meets **any** of the following criteria:
* ≥ 2 gout flares per year
* Presence of subcutaneous tophi
* Evidence of urate arthropathy on imaging
* History of urolithiasis (uric acid kidney stones)

**Target Lab Value:** Serum Uric Acid (sUA) **< 6.0 mg/dL** *(Target < 5.0 mg/dL if severe tophaceous gout).*

### Flare Prophylaxis During ULT Initiation
*Initiating ULT mobilizes tissue urate and frequently triggers acute flares. Prophylaxis is mandatory.*
* **Duration:** 3 to 6 months (continue until sUA is at target and patient is flare-free for 3 months).
* **First-Line:** Colcrys (colchicine) 0.3 - 0.6 mg PO daily (Adjust for eGFR: use 0.3 mg every other day if eGFR 15-29; avoid if eGFR <15).
* **Alternative:** Prednisone 5 mg PO daily (if colchicine is contraindicated or eGFR <15).

### Step 1: First-Line Therapy
**Zyloprim (allopurinol)**
* **Pre-screening:** HLA-B*5801 testing is strongly recommended before starting, especially in patients of Southeast Asian, African American, or Korean descent, due to the risk of Allopurinol Hypersensitivity Syndrome (AHS).
* **Starting Dose:** 
  * eGFR ≥ 30: 100 mg PO daily.
  * eGFR < 30: 50 mg PO daily.
* **Titration:** Increase by 50-100 mg every 2-4 weeks until sUA target (<6.0 mg/dL) is reached.
* **Max Dose:** *ACR 2020 Paradigm Shift:* Maximum dose is **NOT** limited by eGFR. Titrate to target sUA (up to 800 mg/day) as long as the patient is monitored for pruritus, rash, and LFT abnormalities.

### Step 2: Second-Line Therapy
**Uloric (febuxostat)**
* **Indication:** Use if Zyloprim (allopurinol) is not tolerated, contraindicated (HLA-B*5801 positive), or fails to achieve sUA target at maximum tolerated dose.
* **Starting Dose:** 40 mg PO daily.
* **Titration:** Increase to 80 mg PO daily after 2-4 weeks if sUA target is not met.
* **CKD Adjustment:** NO dose adjustment required for mild-to-severe CKD.
* **Monitoring:** Baseline and periodic Liver Function Tests (LFTs).
* 🚨 **CARES Trial CV Warning:** Associated with a higher rate of cardiovascular death compared to allopurinol. Inform patient of risks; reserve for second-line use.

### Step 3: Refractory Chronic Gout
**Krystexxa (pegloticase)**
* **Indication:** Severe, refractory chronic gout with tophi/erosions failing oral ULT.
* **Dose:** 8 mg IV infusion every 2 weeks.
* **MIRROR Trial Protocol:** Co-treat with immunomodulation, typically Trexall (methotrexate) 15 mg weekly + folic acid, starting 4 weeks prior to Krystexxa. This significantly reduces anti-drug antibodies and improves efficacy.
* **Pre-medication:** Antihistamines, acetaminophen, and IV corticosteroids required prior to each infusion.
* **Contraindication:** G6PD deficiency (risk of hemolysis/methemoglobinemia).

## 4. COMPARISON: Zyloprim (allopurinol) vs. Uloric (febuxostat)

| Feature | Zyloprim (allopurinol) | Uloric (febuxostat) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Mechanism** | Xanthine oxidase inhibitor | Non-purine selective xanthine oxidase inhibitor |
| **Starting Dose** | 100 mg daily (50 mg if eGFR <30) | 40 mg daily |
| **Titration** | Increase 50-100 mg every 2-4 weeks | Increase to 80 mg after 2-4 weeks if needed |
| **Max Dose** | 800 mg daily | 80 mg daily |
| **CKD Adjustment** | Lower starting dose; titrate slowly | None required |
| **Key Warnings** | Allopurinol Hypersensitivity Syndrome (AHS), SJS/TEN | Cardiovascular death risk (CARES trial), Hepatic failure |
| **Pre-testing** | HLA-B*5801 (high-risk populations) | Baseline LFTs |
| **Cost/Access** | Generic, very low cost, Tier 1 | Generic available, but often requires Step Therapy/PA |

## 6. SAFETY CONCERNS & DRUG INTERACTIONS

* **Colcrys (colchicine) Interactions:** Fatal toxicity can occur when combined with strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (e.g., clarithromycin, ketoconazole) or P-glycoprotein inhibitors (e.g., cyclosporine). 
* **Zyloprim (allopurinol) + Imuran (azathioprine) / Purinethol (mercaptopurine):** **SEVERE INTERACTION.** Allopurinol inhibits the metabolism of these drugs, leading to severe bone marrow suppression. Reduce azathioprine/mercaptopurine dose by 75% if combination is unavoidable, or use alternative ULT.
* **Diuretics:** Thiazides (e.g., Microzide (hydrochlorothiazide)) and loop diuretics (e.g., Lasix (furosemide)) increase serum urate. Consider switching to alternative antihypertensives (e.g., Cozaar (losartan), which has mild uricosuric effects) if clinically appropriate.

## 7. PATIENT EDUCATION TALKING POINTS

* **The "Flare vs. Maintenance" Concept:** Explain that acute flare meds (like a fire extinguisher) are different from daily ULT meds (like fire prevention). 
* **Do Not Stop ULT During a Flare:** Emphasize that stopping Zyloprim (allopurinol) or Uloric (febuxostat) during an acute attack can actually make the flare worse by causing rapid shifts in uric acid levels.
* **Expect Flares When Starting ULT:** Warn the patient that starting a new uric acid medication often *causes* flares initially. This is why they are taking a daily preventative pill (colchicine or prednisone) for the first 3-6 months.
* **Diet & Lifestyle:** 
  * Avoid high-purine foods (organ meats, shellfish, red meat).
  * Strictly avoid High Fructose Corn Syrup (HFCS) and sugary sodas.
  * Limit alcohol intake, especially beer and liquor.
  * Maintain excellent hydration (tailored to their specific CKD fluid restrictions).
* **Action Plan:** Provide a clear plan on what to do at the first "twinge" of a flare (e.g., "Take your Colcrys immediately and call the clinic").
