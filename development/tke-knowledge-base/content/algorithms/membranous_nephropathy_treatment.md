# Membranous Nephropathy (MN): Clinical Decision Algorithm & Treatment Protocol

**Reference Guidelines:** KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases.

---

## 1. Initial Workup & Diagnosis

All patients presenting with suspected or biopsy-proven Membranous Nephropathy require a comprehensive evaluation to differentiate primary (autoimmune) from secondary causes.

### Diagnostic Labs & Imaging
*   **Anti-PLA2R antibody:** Present in ~70-80% of primary MN. Draw at baseline.
*   **Secondary Causes Workup:** 
    *   Autoimmune: ANA, anti-dsDNA, complement (C3, C4).
    *   Infectious: Hepatitis B (HBsAg, HBcAb), Hepatitis C, HIV, Syphilis.
    *   Malignancy Screen (Age-appropriate): Colonoscopy, mammogram, PSA, low-dose chest CT (if smoking history). *Note: Consider THSD7A or NELL-1 antibody testing if malignancy is highly suspected.*
    *   Drug Exposure History: NSAIDs, penicillamine, gold, captopril.
*   **Proteinuria Quantification:** 24-hour urine protein (gold standard) or spot Urine Protein-to-Creatinine Ratio (UPCR).
*   **Kidney Biopsy:** Required for definitive diagnosis unless patient has nephrotic syndrome + positive anti-PLA2R + high risk for biopsy complications. 
    *   *Pathology hallmark:* Subepithelial immune deposits, IgG subclass staining (IgG4 dominant in primary MN).
    *   **[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]:** Local preferred interventional radiology/nephrology biopsy referral workflow.

## 2. Risk Stratification (KDIGO 2021)

Risk of progressive loss of kidney function dictates the timing and aggressiveness of immunosuppression.

| Risk Category | Clinical & Laboratory Criteria | Treatment Approach |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Low Risk** | Normal eGFR AND Proteinuria <3.5 g/day | Supportive care only. |
| **Moderate Risk** | Normal eGFR AND Proteinuria 3.5–8.0 g/day AND Anti-PLA2R <50 RU/mL | Supportive care. Observe for 3-6 months. |
| **High Risk** | eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73m² OR Proteinuria >8.0 g/day OR Anti-PLA2R >50 RU/mL | Supportive care + Initiate Immunosuppression. |
| **Very High Risk** | Life-threatening nephrotic syndrome OR Rapid, unexplained eGFR decline | Immediate Immunosuppression (often cyclophosphamide-based). |

## 3. Treatment Algorithm

### STEP 1: Maximized Supportive Care (All Patients)
*Initiate immediately. For Moderate Risk patients, observe for 3-6 months on optimized supportive care before considering immunosuppression, as up to 30% may undergo spontaneous remission.*

*   **RAAS Blockade:** Maximize ACEi or ARB therapy. Target BP <120 mm Hg systolic.
*   **SGLT2 Inhibitor:** Add Farxiga (dapagliflozin) 10 mg daily (if eGFR >25 mL/min) OR Jardiance (empagliflozin) 10 mg daily (if eGFR >20 mL/min).
*   **Edema Management:** 
    *   Sodium restriction (<2 g/day).
    *   Loop diuretics: Lasix (furosemide) 40-120 mg daily or Bumex (bumetanide) 1-3 mg daily.
    *   Sequential nephron blockade: Add Zaroxolyn (metolazone) 2.5-5 mg PO 30 mins prior to loop diuretic for refractory edema.
*   **Lipid Management:** High-intensity statin (e.g., Lipitor (atorvastatin) 40-80 mg or Crestor (rosuvastatin) 20-40 mg).
*   **DVT Prophylaxis:** Initiate prophylactic anticoagulation (e.g., Lovenox (enoxaparin) or Eliquis (apixaban)) if **serum albumin <2.5 g/dL AND proteinuria >10 g/day** (or high bleeding risk score per KDIGO). **[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]:** Preferred DOAC vs LMWH dosing for prophylactic indications in nephrotic syndrome.

### STEP 2: Immunosuppressive Therapy (IST)
*Indicated for High/Very High Risk, or Moderate Risk with persistent nephrotic-range proteinuria after 3-6 months of Step 1.*

#### First-Line: Rituxan (rituximab)
*Preferred for most High and Moderate-progressing risk patients due to favorable toxicity profile.*
*   **Dosing:** 1000 mg IV on Day 1 and Day 15.
*   **Pre-medication:** Tylenol (acetaminophen) 650 mg PO, Benadryl (diphenhydramine) 50 mg PO/IV, and Solu-Medrol (methylprednisolone) 100 mg IV.
*   **Follow-up Dosing:** Repeat at 6 months if anti-PLA2R remains positive or partial clinical remission.

> **Clinical Evidence: The MENTOR Trial (NEJM 2019)**
> *   **Design:** Rituxan (rituximab) vs. Neoral/Sandimmune (cyclosporine) in MN.
> *   **12-Month Results:** Rituximab was non-inferior to cyclosporine for complete/partial remission (60% vs. 52%).
> *   **24-Month Results:** Rituximab demonstrated **SUPERIOR sustained remission** (60% vs. 20%). Cyclosporine had a massive relapse rate after discontinuation.
> *   **Conclusion:** Rituximab provides durable immunologic and clinical remission without the nephrotoxicity or relapse rates of CNIs.

#### Alternative First-Line: Modified Ponticelli Regimen
*Consider for Very High Risk, rapidly declining eGFR, or if Rituxan (rituximab) is unavailable/failed.*
*   **Schedule (6-month alternating cycle):**
    *   **Months 1, 3, 5 (Corticosteroids):** Solu-Medrol (methylprednisolone) 1g IV daily for 3 days, followed by Deltasone (prednisone) 0.5 mg/kg/day PO for 27 days.
    *   **Months 2, 4, 6 (Alkylating Agent):** Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) 2.0-2.5 mg/kg/day PO for 30 days.
*   **Dose Adjustment:** Reduce Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) dose by 25-50% if eGFR <45 mL/min or age >60.

#### Second-Line / Bridge Therapy: Calcineurin Inhibitors (CNIs)
*Useful as bridge therapy or in women desiring pregnancy. High relapse rate (50-60%) upon discontinuation.*
*   **Prograf (tacrolimus):** 0.05 mg/kg/day PO divided BID. Target 12-hour trough: 3-5 ng/mL.
*   **Neoral / Gengraf (cyclosporine):** 3.5 mg/kg/day PO divided BID. Target 12-hour trough: 100-150 ng/mL.

## 4. Anti-PLA2R Guided Treatment & Monitoring

**Crucial Concept:** Immunologic remission (clearance of anti-PLA2R antibodies) precedes clinical remission (reduction in proteinuria) by weeks to months. Do NOT escalate therapy based on proteinuria alone if anti-PLA2R is dropping.

### Anti-PLA2R Decision Matrix

| Timeline | Anti-PLA2R Status | Proteinuria Status | Clinical Decision / Next Step |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Baseline** | Positive (>2 RU/mL) | Nephrotic (>3.5g) | Assess risk. Start Step 1. Plan Step 2 if High Risk. |
| **3 Months (Post-IST)** | Decreasing (>50% drop) | Unchanged | **Wait.** Immunologic response is occurring. Continue current plan. |
| **6 Months (Post-IST)** | Negative (<2 RU/mL) | Decreasing | **Success.** Immunologic remission achieved. Monitor UPCR monthly. |
| **6 Months (Post-IST)** | Persistently Positive | Unchanged/Worse | **Treatment Failure.** Repeat Rituxan (rituximab) OR switch to Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide). |

### Routine Monitoring Schedule
*   **Anti-PLA2R levels:** Baseline, then every 2-3 months during active treatment.
*   **Proteinuria (UPCR):** Monthly. *Target: Complete remission <300 mg/g; Partial remission <3.5 g/day with >50% reduction from baseline.*
*   **Renal Function (CMP):** Monthly (eGFR, albumin, potassium).
*   **Immunosuppression Labs:**
    *   *Rituximab:* CD20 count (target 0), quantitative immunoglobulins (IgG) prior to repeat dosing.
    *   *Cyclophosphamide:* CBC with diff every 1-2 weeks (monitor for leukopenia; target WBC >3.5k).
    *   *CNIs:* Trough levels monthly, monitor eGFR closely for CNI nephrotoxicity.

## 5. ⚠️ Safety, Contraindications & Drug Interactions

*   **Rituxan (rituximab):** 
    *   **Hepatitis B Reactivation:** Fatal fulminant hepatitis can occur. MUST check HBsAg and HBcAb prior to infusion.
    *   **Infusion Reactions:** High risk on first dose. Ensure strict adherence to pre-medication protocol.
*   **Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide):**
    *   **Toxicity:** Hemorrhagic cystitis (ensure adequate hydration), bone marrow suppression, increased malignancy risk (bladder, skin, hematologic), and infertility/gonadal toxicity.
*   **Prograf (tacrolimus) / Neoral (cyclosporine):**
    *   **Nephrotoxicity:** Can cause acute hemodynamic eGFR decline and chronic interstitial fibrosis.
    *   **Interactions:** CYP3A4 inhibitors (e.g., diltiazem, verapamil, azole antifungals, grapefruit) will drastically increase CNI levels.
*   **Farxiga (dapagliflozin):**
    *   **Safety:** Hold for 3 days prior to surgery or during acute illness/fasting to prevent euglycemic diabetic ketoacidosis (euDKA). Risk of mycotic genital infections.

## 6. Patient Education Talking Points

*   **The "Lag Time":** "Your immune system is attacking the kidney filters. The medications (like Rituxan) stop the attack, but it takes months for the kidney filters to heal and stop leaking protein. We will see your antibody levels drop before your urine protein drops."
*   **Edema & Diet:** "Swelling is driven by salt. You must restrict sodium to less than 2,000 mg per day. Diuretics (water pills) will not work if you eat too much salt."
*   **Infection Risk:** "Immunosuppressants lower your ability to fight infections. Call the clinic immediately for fevers >100.4°F, chills, or severe respiratory symptoms."
*   **Medication Adherence:** "Do not stop your blood pressure medications (ACEi/ARB) or Farxiga even if your blood pressure is normal; we are using them to protect the kidney filters, not just to lower blood pressure."
