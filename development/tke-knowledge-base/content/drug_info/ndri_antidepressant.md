## Wellbutrin, Zyban (bupropion)

### Mechanism of Action
Wellbutrin, Zyban (bupropion) is a Norepinephrine-Dopamine Reuptake Inhibitor (NDRI). It works by weakly inhibiting the neuronal uptake of dopamine and norepinephrine. Unlike SSRIs or SNRIs, it does not inhibit monoamine oxidase or the reuptake of serotonin, and it lacks clinically significant affinity for histamine, alpha-adrenergic, or muscarinic receptors. This unique mechanism explains its lack of sexual dysfunction and weight gain side effects.

### Indications in Nephrology
Depression and smoking are highly prevalent in the Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) and End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD) populations, accelerating CKD progression and cardiovascular mortality.

*   **FDA-Approved Indications:**
    *   Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) (Wellbutrin IR, SR, XL)
    *   Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD) (Wellbutrin XL)
    *   Smoking Cessation (Zyban)
*   **Off-Label Uses Common in Nephrology:**
    *   Antidepressant-induced sexual dysfunction (as an adjunct or replacement for SSRIs)
    *   Fatigue and lethargy associated with CKD/ESRD
    *   Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)
*   **Key Supporting Clinical Data:**
    *   *ASCEND Trial (2010):* Demonstrated that treating depression in CKD improves quality of life, though bupropion specifically was evaluated in broader MDD trials.
    *   *ZYBAN Smoking Cessation Trials:* Demonstrated a 1-year continuous abstinence rate of ~23% for Zyban (bupropion) vs. 12% for placebo. Smoking cessation is a Class 1 recommendation in KDIGO and ACC/AHA guidelines to reduce cardiovascular risk and slow CKD progression.

### Dosing
*Note: Wellbutrin (bupropion) is available in Immediate-Release (IR), Sustained-Release (SR), and Extended-Release (XL) formulations. XL is generally preferred for compliance in polypharmacy CKD patients.*

**Standard Dosing (Normal Renal Function):**
*   **Wellbutrin XL (bupropion extended-release):** Start 150 mg PO once daily in the morning. May titrate to 300 mg once daily after 4 days. Maximum dose: 450 mg/day.
*   **Wellbutrin SR (bupropion sustained-release):** Start 150 mg PO once daily in the morning. May titrate to 150 mg BID after 3 days. Maximum dose: 400 mg/day (200 mg BID).
*   **Zyban (bupropion SR for smoking cessation):** Start 150 mg PO once daily for 3 days, then increase to 150 mg BID for 7-12 weeks. Target quit date should be set for week 2 of therapy.

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range (CRITICAL):**
Bupropion and its active metabolites (hydroxybupropion, threohydroxybupropion) are renally cleared. Accumulation significantly lowers the seizure threshold.
*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m²:** Standard dosing.
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min/1.73 m²:** Reduce dose and/or frequency. Maximum 150 mg/day (Wellbutrin SR or XL).
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min/1.73 m²:** 150 mg every other day (Wellbutrin XL) OR 100 mg once daily (Wellbutrin SR).
*   **eGFR <15 mL/min/1.73 m² / Dialysis:** 150 mg every other day to every 3 days (Wellbutrin XL). Hemodialysis does not significantly clear bupropion or its active metabolites. *[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Standardize ESRD dosing to Wellbutrin XL 150 mg PO Monday/Wednesday/Friday post-dialysis for ease of adherence].*

**Hepatic Adjustments:**
*   Mild to moderate: Use caution; reduce frequency.
*   Severe hepatic impairment: Maximum dose is 150 mg every other day.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs/Vitals:**
    *   Blood Pressure and Heart Rate (can cause dose-dependent hypertension).
    *   Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP) to establish baseline SCr/eGFR for dosing.
    *   Liver Function Tests (LFTs).
*   **Follow-up Schedule:**
    *   Assess BP at every clinic visit (Target BP <120/80 mmHg per KDIGO 2024, or <130/80 mmHg depending on patient-specific factors).
    *   Assess mental status, suicidality, and PHQ-9 scores at 2-4 weeks, then every 3 months. *[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Frequency of PHQ-9 administration in clinic].*
*   **Therapeutic Drug Monitoring:** Not routinely required.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common Side Effects (>5%):** Insomnia, dry mouth, headache, nausea, weight loss, agitation, tachycardia, diaphoresis.
*   **Serious/Rare Side Effects:**
    *   **Seizures:** Dose-dependent risk (~0.1% at 300 mg/day; ~0.4% at 400 mg/day). Risk increases exponentially with renal impairment if not dose-adjusted.
    *   Hypertension (sometimes severe).
    *   Angle-closure glaucoma.
    *   Neuropsychiatric symptoms (hallucinations, paranoia).
*   **Black Box Warning (BBW):** Increased risk of suicidal thoughts and behaviors in children, adolescents, and young adults (up to age 24) taking antidepressants.
*   **Contraindications:**
    *   *Absolute:* Active seizure disorder; current or prior diagnosis of bulimia or anorexia nervosa; abrupt discontinuation of alcohol, benzodiazepines, barbiturates, or antiepileptic drugs; use of MAOIs within 14 days.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:**
    *   **Metabolite Accumulation:** Hydroxybupropion accumulates up to 3-fold in ESRD, drastically increasing seizure risk. Strict adherence to renal dosing is mandatory.

### Drug Interactions
*   **CYP2D6 Inhibitor:** Wellbutrin (bupropion) is a strong inhibitor of CYP2D6. It can significantly increase levels of CYP2D6 substrates (e.g., metoprolol, carvedilol, flecainide, propafenone, haloperidol, risperidone). *Nephrology pearl: Watch for profound bradycardia if a patient is on metoprolol and starts bupropion.*
*   **Seizure Threshold Lowering Agents:** Extreme caution when combining with other drugs that lower the seizure threshold (e.g., tramadol, theophylline, systemic corticosteroids, antipsychotics).
*   **Food Interactions:** None significant; can be taken with or without food.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Historically Category C. Generally considered safe, but SSRIs (like sertraline) are often preferred as first-line in pregnancy.
*   **Elderly in CKD:** High risk for accumulation and neurotoxicity. Start at the lowest possible dose (e.g., 100 mg SR daily or 150 mg XL every other day) and titrate very slowly.
*   **Transplant Considerations:**
    *   *Calcineurin Inhibitors (CNIs):* Tacrolimus and cyclosporine are known to cause neurotoxicity and lower the seizure threshold. Combining CNIs with Wellbutrin (bupropion) requires extreme caution and close neurological monitoring due to the compounded seizure risk.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Administration:** Take in the morning to avoid insomnia. If taking SR twice daily, ensure doses are at least 8 hours apart, with the second dose no later than 4:00 PM.
*   **Do Not Crush/Chew:** Swallow SR and XL tablets whole. Crushing causes rapid release and increases seizure risk.
*   **Warning Signs:** Report any worsening depression, suicidal thoughts, severe agitation, or sudden changes in vision (glaucoma risk) immediately.
*   **Substance Use:** Strictly avoid alcohol. Alcohol use (and abrupt cessation) while on this medication significantly increases the risk of seizures.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **Common ICD-10 Codes:**
    *   F32.A - Major depressive disorder, single episode, unspecified
    *   F33.1 - Major depressive disorder, recurrent, moderate
    *   F17.200 - Nicotine dependence, unspecified, uncomplicated
    *   Z71.6 - Tobacco abuse counseling
*   **CPT Codes for Smoking Cessation:**
    *   99406: Smoking and tobacco use cessation counseling visit; intermediate, greater than 3 minutes up to 10 minutes.
    *   99407: Intensive, greater than 10 minutes.
*   **PA Tips:** Generic bupropion is almost universally covered on Tier 1 without Prior Authorization. Brand name Wellbutrin XL or Forfivo XL will require step therapy through generic bupropion and SSRIs.

### Clinical Decision Tree: When to Choose an NDRI

**1. Consider STARTING Wellbutrin (bupropion) if the CKD patient has depression AND:**
*   Concomitant tobacco dependence (Use Zyban protocol).
*   Severe fatigue, lethargy, or hypersomnia.
*   Obesity or strong desire to avoid weight gain.
*   History of SSRI-induced sexual dysfunction.

**2. Consider AVOIDING or STOPPING Wellbutrin (bupropion) and switching to an SSRI if:**
*   The patient has a history of seizures or is on high-dose tramadol/CNIs.
*   The patient has severe concomitant anxiety, panic disorder, or agitation (bupropion is activating and can worsen anxiety).
*   The patient has uncontrolled hypertension (BP >140/90 mmHg).
*   The patient is non-compliant with complex renal dosing schedules (eGFR <30 requiring every-other-day dosing).

**3. Switching Therapies:**
*   *From SSRI to NDRI:* Cross-tapering is generally safe. Gradually reduce the SSRI over 1-2 weeks while initiating bupropion at a low dose.
*   *Adding NDRI to SSRI:* Bupropion is frequently added to SSRIs (like Lexapro or Zoloft) to counteract sexual dysfunction or residual fatigue. Ensure the SSRI dose is stable and monitor for CYP2D6 interactions (bupropion can increase levels of fluoxetine and paroxetine).
