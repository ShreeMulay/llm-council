# Vitamin D Analogs Clinical Monograph

## Zemplar (paricalcitol)

### Mechanism of Action
Zemplar (paricalcitol) is a synthetic, biologically active vitamin D analog (19-nor-1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D2). It binds to and activates the Vitamin D Receptor (VDR) in the parathyroid gland to suppress parathyroid hormone (PTH) synthesis and secretion. 
*   **Receptor Specificity:** Paricalcitol is a "selective" VDR activator. Compared to non-selective agents like Rocaltrol (calcitriol), it has less up-regulating effect on VDRs in the intestines and bone. This selectivity results in effective PTH suppression with a significantly lower risk of inducing hypercalcemia and hyperphosphatemia.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved Indications:** 
    *   Prevention and treatment of secondary hyperparathyroidism (SHPT) in Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) Stages 3 and 4.
    *   Prevention and treatment of SHPT in CKD Stage 5 (ESRD) on hemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis.
*   **Off-Label Uses:** Proteinuria reduction (adjunctive therapy), though routine use for this indication is not recommended due to hypercalcemia risks.
*   **Key Clinical Trials & Guidelines:**
    *   **KDIGO 2017 CKD-MBD Guidelines:** Recommends *against* the routine use of calcitriol or vitamin D analogs in non-dialysis CKD (ND-CKD) G3a-G5, reserving them only for severe and progressive SHPT. For CKD G5D (dialysis), calcitriol or vitamin D analogs are considered standard of care for elevated PTH.
    *   **PRIMO Trial (2012):** Evaluated Zemplar (paricalcitol) for reducing left ventricular mass in CKD. *Outcomes:* Did not significantly alter LV mass index but did significantly increase the risk of hypercalcemia (22.6% vs 0.9% in placebo).
    *   **VITAL Trial (2010):** Evaluated Zemplar (paricalcitol) addition to RAAS blockade for proteinuria. *Outcomes:* Showed a modest reduction in albuminuria but with increased risk of hypercalcemia.

### Dosing
Dosing is primarily determined by the patient's baseline intact PTH (iPTH) level and dialysis status, rather than strictly by eGFR. However, per TKE formatting requirements, dosing is mapped to eGFR ranges below.

#### CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range
*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min:** Not indicated.
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min (CKD Stage 3):** 
    *   *Oral only.* 
    *   Baseline iPTH ≤500 pg/mL: 1 mcg orally daily OR 2 mcg orally 3 times weekly.
    *   Baseline iPTH >500 pg/mL: 2 mcg orally daily OR 4 mcg orally 3 times weekly.
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min (CKD Stage 4):**
    *   *Oral only.* Dosing is identical to eGFR 30-59 mL/min.
*   **eGFR <15 mL/min / Dialysis (CKD Stage 5/5D):**
    *   *IV (Preferred for Hemodialysis):* Initial dose = baseline iPTH / 80 (in mcg). Administer IV bolus no more frequently than every other day during dialysis. Maximum initial dose is 32 mcg.
    *   *Oral (Preferred for Peritoneal Dialysis/Home HD):* Initial dose = baseline iPTH / 80 (in mcg). Administer orally 3 times weekly (no more frequently than every other day).

#### Titration Schedule & Decision Tree
Adjust dose every 2 to 4 weeks based on iPTH, Calcium (Ca), and Phosphorus (P) levels.
*   **If iPTH is decreasing by < 30%:** Increase dose by 1 mcg/day (or 2 mcg 3x/week).
*   **If iPTH is decreasing by 30-60%:** Maintain current dose.
*   **If iPTH decreases by > 60% OR iPTH falls below target:** Decrease dose by 1 mcg/day (or 2 mcg 3x/week).
*   **If corrected serum Calcium > 10.5 mg/dL OR Phosphorus > 5.5 mg/dL:** STOP therapy until levels normalize, then restart at a lower dose.

#### Hepatic Adjustments
*   Mild to moderate hepatic impairment: No adjustment necessary.
*   Severe hepatic impairment: Not studied; use with caution.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs Required:** Intact PTH, serum Calcium (corrected for albumin), serum Phosphorus, 25-OH Vitamin D (replete nutritional vitamin D before starting analogs).
*   **Follow-up Lab Schedule:**
    *   *Initiation/Titration:* Check Ca and P twice weekly (IV) or weekly (Oral) for the first 2-4 weeks. Check iPTH every 2-4 weeks.
    *   *Maintenance:* Check Ca and P monthly. Check iPTH every 1-3 months.
    *   *[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]:* Confirm local TKE standard lab draw frequency for in-center hemodialysis patients (typically monthly for all CKD-MBD labs).
*   **Therapeutic Targets (KDIGO 2017):**
    *   *Dialysis (CKD 5D):* Target iPTH 2 to 9 times the upper limit of normal (approx. 130-600 pg/mL).
    *   *ND-CKD (CKD 3-5):* Target iPTH is not strictly defined; treat only if iPTH is progressively rising and remains persistently above the upper limit of normal.
    *   *Calcium:* Maintain within normal range (avoid hypercalcemia).
    *   *Phosphorus:* Lower toward the normal range.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common Side Effects (>5%):** Nausea, vomiting, edema, dizziness, joint pain, diarrhea.
*   **Serious/Rare Side Effects:** Severe hypercalcemia, adynamic bone disease (from oversuppression of PTH), vascular/soft tissue calcification, calciphylaxis.
*   **Black Box Warnings:** None.
*   **Contraindications:** 
    *   Absolute: Hypercalcemia, vitamin D toxicity.
    *   Relative: Hyperphosphatemia (must be controlled prior to initiation).
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** 
    *   Oversuppression of PTH (< 100 pg/mL) can lead to adynamic bone disease, increasing the risk of fractures and vascular calcification.
    *   Concurrent use of calcium-based phosphate binders (e.g., PhosLo, Tums) exponentially increases the risk of hypercalcemia.

### Drug Interactions
*   **CYP3A4 Inhibitors (Major):** Zemplar (paricalcitol) is partially metabolized by CYP3A4. Strong inhibitors (e.g., ketoconazole, clarithromycin, ritonavir) can increase paricalcitol AUC by up to 50%. Dose reduction may be required.
*   **Digitalis Toxicity:** Hypercalcemia induced by paricalcitol can precipitate fatal digoxin toxicity. Monitor calcium meticulously in patients on digoxin.
*   **Thiazide Diuretics:** Co-administration increases the risk of hypercalcemia.
*   **Phosphate Binders:** 
    *   *Calcium-based:* Increases hypercalcemia risk.
    *   *Aluminum-based:* Vitamin D analogs increase intestinal absorption of aluminum, risking aluminum toxicity. Avoid concomitant use.
*   **Food Interactions:** Oral Zemplar (paricalcitol) can be taken with or without food.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Category C. No adequate human studies. Use only if potential benefit justifies the potential risk to the fetus.
*   **Lactation:** Unknown if excreted in human milk. Consider discontinuing nursing or the drug.
*   **Elderly:** No overall differences in safety or efficacy, but greater sensitivity to hypercalcemia cannot be ruled out.
*   **Transplant:** Not routinely used post-transplant unless severe SHPT persists after resolution of allograft function. Monitor calcium closely as transplant patients are prone to hypercalcemia.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Administration:** Take exactly as prescribed. Do not take extra doses if a dose is missed.
*   **Diet & Supplements:** Strictly adhere to your low-phosphorus diet. **DO NOT** take over-the-counter calcium supplements, Vitamin D, or antacids (like Tums) without explicit approval from your nephrologist.
*   **Warning Signs to Report:** Contact the clinic immediately if you experience signs of high calcium: unexplained nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite, severe constipation, confusion, or unusual weakness.
*   **Storage:** Store at room temperature away from moisture and heat.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **Common ICD-10 Codes:**
    *   N25.81 (Secondary hyperparathyroidism of renal origin)
    *   N18.30 - N18.6 (Chronic kidney disease, stages 3 through ESRD)
    *   E83.52 (Hypercalcemia - *use to justify switching from calcitriol to paricalcitol*)
*   **CPT Codes:** 
    *   J2501 (Injection, paricalcitol, 1 microgram)
    *   *Note:* For in-center hemodialysis, IV paricalcitol is typically bundled into the ESRD Prospective Payment System (PPS) bundle and not billed separately.
*   **PA Tips:** For oral Zemplar (paricalcitol) in ND-CKD, PAs often require documentation of a progressively rising iPTH despite optimization of nutritional Vitamin D (25-OH D > 30 ng/mL) and failure of/intolerance to generic calcitriol.
*   **Cost/Assistance:** Generic oral paricalcitol is available but remains moderately expensive. Manufacturer patient assistance programs (e.g., AbbVie) may be available for eligible patients.

### Decision Tree: Choosing and Switching Agents

**1. Initial Selection:**
*   **First-line for ND-CKD (if indicated):** Rocaltrol (calcitriol) is often utilized first due to cost, but Zemplar (paricalcitol) is preferred if baseline calcium is high-normal (>9.5 mg/dL).
*   **First-line for Dialysis:** [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED] - Select based on clinic formulary/bundle agreements. Zemplar (paricalcitol) or Hectorol (doxercalciferol) are generally preferred over calcitriol due to lower hypercalcemia risk.

**2. When to Escalate / Switch:**
*   *Scenario A (Hypercalcemia on Calcitriol):* If a patient on Rocaltrol (calcitriol) develops hypercalcemia (Ca > 10.2 mg/dL) but still has uncontrolled PTH, **SWITCH** to Zemplar (paricalcitol).
    *   *Conversion:* 1 mcg of calcitriol ≈ 3 to 4 mcg of paricalcitol.
*   *Scenario B (Hepatic Impairment):* If a patient on Hectorol (doxercalciferol) develops acute liver injury or severe hepatic impairment, **SWITCH** to Zemplar (paricalcitol) or Rocaltrol (calcitriol), as doxercalciferol is a prodrug requiring hepatic activation.
*   *Scenario C (Refractory SHPT):* If PTH remains > 9x ULN despite maximum tolerated doses of Vitamin D analogs (due to hypercalcemia/hyperphosphatemia), **ESCALATE** by adding a calcimimetic (e.g., Sensipar/cinacalcet or Parsabiv/etelcalcetide) and reduce the Vitamin D analog dose by 50%.

**3. When to Stop:**
*   Stop all Vitamin D analogs if corrected Calcium > 10.5 mg/dL, Phosphorus > 5.5 mg/dL, or if iPTH falls below 100 pg/mL (to prevent adynamic bone disease). Resume at 50% of the previous dose once labs normalize.
