# Steroidal Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists (MRAs)

Steroidal MRAs are foundational therapies in nephrology and cardiology, primarily utilized for resistant hypertension, heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), and management of volume overload (e.g., cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome). While KDIGO 2024 guidelines preferentially recommend non-steroidal MRAs (e.g., finerenone) for diabetic kidney disease, steroidal MRAs remain critical tools for blood pressure control, heart failure management, and off-label proteinuria reduction.

---

## Aldactone (spironolactone)

### Mechanism of Action
Aldactone (spironolactone) is a synthetic, steroidal, non-selective mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist. It competitively binds to receptors at the aldosterone-dependent sodium-potassium exchange site in the distal convoluted renal tubule. This action increases sodium and water excretion while retaining potassium. Because it is non-selective, it also binds to androgen and progesterone receptors, which is responsible for its endocrine side effects.

### Indications in Nephrology
**FDA-Approved Indications:**
*   Primary hyperaldosteronism
*   Edematous conditions (cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, congestive heart failure)
*   Essential hypertension
*   Heart failure (HFrEF)

**Off-Label Nephrology Uses:**
*   Resistant hypertension (AHA/ACC guidelines recommend as 4th-line agent)
*   Proteinuria reduction in CKD (when non-steroidal MRAs are unavailable/contraindicated)

**Key Clinical Trials:**
*   **RALES (1999):** Demonstrated a 30% reduction in all-cause mortality when Aldactone (spironolactone) 25 mg was added to standard therapy in severe HFrEF.
*   **PATHWAY-2 (2015):** Established Aldactone (spironolactone) as the most effective add-on drug for resistant hypertension, reducing systolic BP by an average of 8.7 mmHg more than placebo.

### Dosing
*   **Resistant Hypertension:** Start 12.5 to 25 mg PO daily. Titrate every 2-4 weeks to a maximum of 100 mg/day (doses >50 mg/day significantly increase hyperkalemia risk with minimal added BP benefit).
*   **Heart Failure:** Start 12.5 to 25 mg PO daily. Max 50 mg/day.
*   **Edema (Nephrotic Syndrome/Cirrhosis):** 50 to 100 mg PO daily (often combined with loop diuretics). Max 400 mg/day for severe cirrhotic ascites.

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min:** Standard dosing.
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min:** Start 12.5 mg PO daily or every other day. Maximum dose 25 mg/day.
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min:** Use with extreme caution. Start 12.5 mg PO every other day. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Many providers avoid use in this range unless managing severe HFrEF under strict cardiology/nephrology co-management].
*   **eGFR <15 mL/min / Dialysis:** Generally contraindicated due to severe hyperkalemia risk. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: May be used in anuric ESRD patients for severe heart failure or ascites, as hyperkalemia risk is mitigated by anuria and dialysis, but requires strict monitoring].

**Hepatic Adjustments:** No specific dose adjustments, but use caution in severe hepatic impairment due to risk of hepatic encephalopathy from fluid/electrolyte shifts.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs:** BMP (specifically K+ and SCr/eGFR). **Do not initiate if baseline K+ > 5.0 mEq/L.**
*   **Follow-up Schedule:** 
    *   Check BMP at 1 week and 4 weeks after initiation or dose titration.
    *   Thereafter, check every 3-6 months depending on eGFR and concomitant medications (e.g., ACEi/ARB).
    *   [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Standardize local lab frequency for high-risk patients].
*   **Therapeutic Targets:** Target K+ ≤ 5.0 mEq/L. If used for proteinuria, target UACR < 30 mg/g or > 30% reduction from baseline.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Hyperkalemia, gynecomastia (up to 10% in men at 50 mg/day), breast tenderness/mastodynia in women, menstrual irregularities, impotence, lethargy.
*   **Serious/Rare:** Severe hyperkalemia leading to arrhythmias, acute kidney injury (AKI), Stevens-Johnson Syndrome.
*   **Black Box Warning:** Aldactone (spironolactone) has been shown to be a tumorigen in chronic toxicity studies in rats. Use should be restricted to approved indications.
*   **Contraindications:** Addison's disease, hyperkalemia (K+ > 5.0 mEq/L), concomitant use with eplerenone.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** The risk of life-threatening hyperkalemia increases exponentially as eGFR declines, especially when combined with RAAS inhibitors (ACEi/ARB/ARNI).

### Drug Interactions
*   **ACEi / ARB / ARNI:** Additive risk of hyperkalemia. Requires vigilant monitoring.
*   **Bactrim (trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole):** *CRITICAL NEPHROLOGY PEARL.* Trimethoprim acts as an epithelial sodium channel (ENaC) blocker (similar to amiloride). Co-administration with Aldactone (spironolactone) can cause sudden, life-threatening hyperkalemia. Avoid combination if possible.
*   **NSAIDs:** May reduce the diuretic/antihypertensive effect and increase AKI and hyperkalemia risk.
*   **Potassium Supplements / Salt Substitutes:** High risk of hyperkalemia.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Not recommended (anti-androgenic effects can feminize a male fetus).
*   **Elderly:** Higher risk of hyperkalemia and AKI due to age-related decline in eGFR. Start at lowest possible dose.
*   **Transplant:** Use with extreme caution. Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, cyclosporine) already cause hyperkalemia and Type 4 RTA; adding an MRA compounds this risk significantly.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Diet:** Avoid salt substitutes, as most are made of potassium chloride. Maintain a consistent, moderate potassium diet.
*   **Warning Signs:** Report muscle weakness, palpitations, or irregular heartbeat immediately (signs of high potassium).
*   **Side Effects:** Men should be warned about the potential for breast enlargement/tenderness (gynecomastia). Inform the patient that this is reversible if the medication is stopped or switched.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **Common ICD-10 Codes:** I15.0 (Renovascular HTN), I15.1 (Secondary HTN), I11.0 (Hypertensive heart disease with HF), I50.20 (Unspecified systolic HF), E26.01 (Conn's syndrome).
*   **Cost/PA:** Generic spironolactone is a Tier 1 drug, widely available on $4 generic lists. Prior authorization is rarely required.

## Inspra (eplerenone)

### Mechanism of Action
Inspra (eplerenone) is a highly selective steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist. Unlike Aldactone (spironolactone), it has a very low binding affinity for androgen and progesterone receptors. This selectivity provides the cardiovascular and renal benefits of aldosterone blockade without the endocrine side effects (gynecomastia, impotence, menstrual irregularities).

### Indications in Nephrology
**FDA-Approved Indications:**
*   Heart failure post-myocardial infarction (improving survival in stable patients with LV dysfunction).
*   Hypertension (alone or in combination).

**Off-Label Nephrology Uses:**
*   Resistant hypertension (especially in patients who developed gynecomastia on spironolactone).
*   Proteinuria reduction.

**Key Clinical Trials:**
*   **EPHESUS (2003):** Showed a 15% reduction in all-cause mortality when Inspra (eplerenone) was given to patients with acute MI complicated by LV dysfunction and heart failure.
*   **EMPHASIS-HF (2011):** Demonstrated significant reductions in cardiovascular death and hospitalization in patients with mild HFrEF.

### Dosing
*   **Hypertension:** Start 50 mg PO daily. If BP is not controlled after 4 weeks, increase to 100 mg PO daily (given as 50 mg BID or 100 mg daily).
*   **Heart Failure:** Start 25 mg PO daily. Titrate to 50 mg PO daily within 4 weeks as tolerated by potassium levels.

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min:** Standard dosing.
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min:** 
    *   *For Heart Failure:* Start 25 mg every other day. Max 25 mg daily.
    *   *For Hypertension:* **FDA Contraindicated** if CrCl < 50 mL/min or in patients with Type 2 Diabetes and microalbuminuria (due to hyperkalemia risk).
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min:** Contraindicated.
*   **eGFR <15 mL/min / Dialysis:** Contraindicated.

**Hepatic Adjustments:** No adjustment needed for mild-to-moderate impairment. Not studied in severe hepatic impairment.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs:** BMP (K+ and SCr). **Do not initiate if baseline K+ > 5.0 mEq/L.**
*   **Follow-up Schedule:** 
    *   Check K+ at 3 days, 1 week, and 4 weeks after initiation or dose change.
    *   Monthly for the first 3 months, then quarterly.
*   **Therapeutic Targets:** Target K+ ≤ 5.0 mEq/L.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Hyperkalemia, dizziness, diarrhea.
*   **Serious/Rare:** Severe hyperkalemia. *Note: Incidence of gynecomastia is <1% (comparable to placebo).*
*   **Black Box Warning:** None.
*   **Contraindications:** 
    *   Serum K+ > 5.5 mEq/L at initiation.
    *   CrCl < 30 mL/min (all indications).
    *   CrCl < 50 mL/min (for hypertension indication).
    *   Concomitant use of strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (e.g., ketoconazole, clarithromycin, ritonavir).
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** Inspra (eplerenone) carries stricter FDA eGFR cutoffs than spironolactone, primarily due to trial design and hyperkalemia risks.

### Drug Interactions
*   **CYP3A4 Inhibitors (MAJOR):** Inspra (eplerenone) is heavily metabolized by CYP3A4. 
    *   *Strong inhibitors* (ketoconazole, clarithromycin, ritonavir) are **contraindicated**.
    *   *Moderate inhibitors* (verapamil, diltiazem, fluconazole, grapefruit juice) require dose reduction of Inspra (eplerenone) to a maximum of 25 mg daily.
*   **ACEi / ARB / ARNI:** Additive risk of hyperkalemia.
*   **Bactrim (trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole):** High risk of severe hyperkalemia.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Category B (generally considered safer than spironolactone, but use only if clearly needed).
*   **Elderly:** No specific dose adjustment, but age-related eGFR decline necessitates careful monitoring.
*   **Transplant:** Extreme caution due to interaction with calcineurin inhibitors (hyperkalemia) and potential CYP3A4 interactions (cyclosporine/tacrolimus are also CYP3A4 substrates).

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Diet:** Avoid grapefruit and grapefruit juice, as it can increase drug levels in the blood. Avoid salt substitutes.
*   **Warning Signs:** Report muscle weakness, numbness, or tingling (hyperkalemia signs).
*   **Adherence:** Take exactly as prescribed; do not double up if a dose is missed.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **Common ICD-10 Codes:** I15.0, I15.1, I50.20, I50.22.
*   **Cost/PA:** Generic eplerenone is more expensive than spironolactone but generally affordable. Some Medicare Part D or commercial plans may require Step Therapy (trial of spironolactone first) before approving eplerenone for hypertension.

## Class Comparison & Decision Trees

### Steroidal MRA Comparison Table

| Feature | Aldactone (spironolactone) | Inspra (eplerenone) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Receptor Selectivity** | Non-selective | Highly selective |
| **Half-life** | 1.4 hours (active metabolites 16.5 hrs) | 4-6 hours (no active metabolites) |
| **Gynecomastia Risk** | High (~10%) | Very Low (<1%) |
| **eGFR Cutoff (FDA)** | Caution if < 30 mL/min | Contraindicated if < 30 mL/min (or < 50 for HTN) |
| **CYP450 Interactions** | Minimal | Major (CYP3A4 substrate) |
| **Key Trials** | RALES, PATHWAY-2 | EPHESUS, EMPHASIS-HF |
| **Relative Cost** | $ (Very low) | $$ (Moderate) |

### Decision Tree: When to Choose Which Agent
1.  **First-Line Steroidal MRA:** **Aldactone (spironolactone)** is the preferred first-line agent for resistant hypertension, primary hyperaldosteronism, and cirrhosis due to its robust evidence base, high potency, and low cost.
2.  **When to choose Inspra (eplerenone):**
    *   Patient develops gynecomastia, severe breast tenderness, or impotence on spironolactone.
    *   Patient has post-MI HFrEF (specifically matching EPHESUS trial criteria).
    *   Female patients experiencing severe menstrual irregularities on spironolactone.
3.  **When to AVOID Inspra (eplerenone):**
    *   Patient is on strong CYP3A4 inhibitors.
    *   Patient consumes large amounts of grapefruit juice.
    *   eGFR is < 50 mL/min and the primary indication is hypertension.

### Protocol for Switching Between Agents
If a patient develops endocrine side effects on Aldactone (spironolactone) and requires a switch to Inspra (eplerenone):
*   **Conversion Ratio:** Spironolactone is roughly twice as potent as eplerenone on a milligram-per-milligram basis for blood pressure control.
*   **Standard Switch:** Aldactone (spironolactone) 25 mg daily $\approx$ Inspra (eplerenone) 50 mg daily.
*   **Action:** Stop spironolactone. Start eplerenone the next day at the equivalent dose. Recheck BMP (K+ and SCr) 1 week after the switch. 

### Decision Tree: Managing Hyperkalemia on MRAs
*   **K+ 5.1 - 5.4 mEq/L:** Maintain MRA dose. Reinforce low-potassium diet. Review other meds (NSAIDs, Bactrim). Consider adding a daily loop or thiazide diuretic if volume status allows.
*   **K+ 5.5 - 5.9 mEq/L:** Hold MRA. Recheck K+ in 48-72 hours. Once K+ < 5.0, restart MRA at 50% of the previous dose. Alternatively, initiate a potassium binder (e.g., Lokelma, Veltassa) to maintain MRA therapy [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Consult local guidelines on binder use for MRA optimization].
*   **K+ ≥ 6.0 mEq/L:** Discontinue MRA immediately. Treat hyperkalemia acutely. Re-initiation requires attending nephrologist approval.
