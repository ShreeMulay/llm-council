# Clinical Drug Monograph: Statins in Nephrology

**Guideline Foundations:** 
This monograph is based on the **KDIGO 2013 Clinical Practice Guideline for Lipid Management in CKD**, the **2018 ACC/AHA/AACVPR/AAPA/ABC/ACPM/ADA/AGS/APhA/ASPC/NLA/PCNA Guideline on the Management of Blood Cholesterol**, and the **KDIGO 2024 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of CKD**. 

**Key KDIGO Principle for Nephrology:** 
*   **Initiation:** Recommend statin therapy for all adults aged ≥50 years with eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m² but *not* treated with chronic dialysis or kidney transplantation. For adults 18-49 years with CKD, initiate if they have known coronary disease, diabetes, prior ischemic stroke, or estimated 10-year ASCVD risk >10%.
*   **Dialysis:** Do **NOT** initiate statins in patients with dialysis-dependent CKD (based on 4D and AURORA trials). If a patient is already receiving a statin at the time of dialysis initiation, it should be continued.
*   **Targets:** KDIGO 2013 recommends a "fire and forget" strategy (prescribe a specific intensity based on risk, without titrating to specific LDL-C targets). However, ACC/AHA 2018 recommends targeting LDL-C <70 mg/dL for very high-risk ASCVD patients. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Confirm local TKE preference for treat-to-target vs. fire-and-forget approach].

## Lipitor (atorvastatin)

### Mechanism of Action
Lipitor (atorvastatin) is a selective, competitive inhibitor of HMG-CoA reductase, the rate-limiting enzyme that converts 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-coenzyme A to mevalonate, a precursor of sterols, including cholesterol. It increases the number of hepatic LDL receptors on the cell surface to enhance uptake and catabolism of LDL. It is a lipophilic statin.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Primary and secondary prevention of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD); treatment of primary hyperlipidemia and mixed dyslipidemia.
*   **Nephrology Context:** First-line agent for cardiovascular risk reduction in non-dialysis CKD patients due to its lack of renal dose adjustment.
*   **Key Clinical Trials:**
    *   **4D Trial (Die Deutsche Diabetes Dialyse Studie):** Evaluated Lipitor (atorvastatin) 20 mg vs. placebo in 1,255 hemodialysis patients with Type 2 Diabetes. *Outcome:* No significant reduction in the primary composite endpoint of cardiovascular death, nonfatal MI, or stroke. Led to the KDIGO recommendation against initiating statins in dialysis.
    *   **TNT (Treating to New Targets):** Included a subgroup of patients with CKD. *Outcome:* High-dose Lipitor (atorvastatin) 80 mg reduced major cardiovascular events by 32% compared to 10 mg in patients with mild-to-moderate CKD.
    *   **CARDS:** Demonstrated significant reduction in major cardiovascular events in patients with Type 2 Diabetes, including those with mild CKD.

### Dosing
*   **Standard Dosing:** 
    *   Moderate intensity: 10–20 mg PO once daily.
    *   High intensity: 40–80 mg PO once daily.
*   **CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
    *   **eGFR ≥60:** 10–80 mg daily (No adjustment)
    *   **eGFR 30-59:** 10–80 mg daily (No adjustment)
    *   **eGFR 15-29:** 10–80 mg daily (No adjustment)
    *   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** 10–80 mg daily (No adjustment). *Note: Do not initiate in dialysis-dependent patients; continue only if previously prescribed.*
*   **Hepatic Adjustments:** Contraindicated in active liver disease or unexplained persistent elevations of hepatic transaminases.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs:** Fasting lipid panel, ALT/AST, CK (creatine kinase), TSH (to rule out secondary dyslipidemia), HbA1c.
*   **Follow-up Schedule:** 
    *   Lipid panel 4–12 weeks after initiation or dose adjustment to assess adherence and response.
    *   Annually thereafter.
    *   Routine monitoring of LFTs or CK is *not* recommended unless the patient develops symptoms (e.g., severe myalgia, dark urine, jaundice).
*   **Therapeutic Targets:** While KDIGO does not specify LDL targets, ACC/AHA guidelines suggest LDL-C <70 mg/dL for high-risk ASCVD patients.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Myalgia, arthralgia, diarrhea, nasopharyngitis, urinary tract infection.
*   **Serious/Rare:** Myopathy, rhabdomyolysis (rare but severe, risk increased with interacting drugs), immune-mediated necrotizing myopathy (IMNM), new-onset diabetes mellitus, hepatotoxicity.
*   **Contraindications:** Active liver disease, pregnancy, lactation.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** CKD is an independent risk factor for statin-induced myopathy. Monitor closely for muscle symptoms, especially in advanced CKD, despite no formal dose adjustments being required.

### Drug Interactions
*   **CYP450 Interactions:** Lipitor (atorvastatin) is a major substrate of **CYP3A4**.
*   **Major Nephrology Interactions:**
    *   **Cyclosporine:** Significantly increases atorvastatin levels. *Max Lipitor (atorvastatin) dose: 10 mg/day.*
    *   **Tacrolimus:** Weak interaction, but monitor for myopathy.
    *   **Colchicine:** Increased risk of myopathy/rhabdomyolysis, especially in CKD.
    *   **Amiodarone / Diltiazem / Verapamil:** Increases atorvastatin exposure. *Consider max dose of 20 mg/day.*
    *   **Azole Antifungals / Macrolides (e.g., clarithromycin):** Avoid concurrent use or temporarily suspend statin.
*   **Food Interactions:** Grapefruit juice (>1.2 liters/day) significantly increases drug levels. Advise against excessive consumption.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Contraindicated. Discontinue 1-2 months before attempting conception.
*   **Elderly (>65 years):** Higher risk of myopathy. Consider starting at lower doses (10-20 mg) even if high-intensity is indicated.
*   **Transplant:** [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED] Due to calcineurin inhibitor (CNI) interactions, statin selection and dosing must be carefully managed. Pravastatin or low-dose Crestor (rosuvastatin) are often preferred over Lipitor (atorvastatin) in cyclosporine-treated patients.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Administration:** Can be taken at any time of day, with or without food (long half-life of ~14 hours).
*   **Warning Signs:** Immediately report unexplained muscle pain, tenderness, or weakness, especially if accompanied by malaise, fever, or dark "tea-colored" urine.
*   **Lifestyle:** Medication is an adjunct to, not a replacement for, a heart-healthy diet and exercise.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **ICD-10 Codes:** 
    *   E78.00 (Pure hypercholesterolemia, unspecified)
    *   E78.5 (Hyperlipidemia, unspecified)
    *   I25.10 (Atherosclerotic heart disease of native coronary artery without angina pectoris)
*   **Cost:** Generic is highly affordable (Tier 1 on most formularies). PA is rarely required unless requesting brand-name Lipitor.

## Crestor (rosuvastatin)

### Mechanism of Action
Crestor (rosuvastatin) is a selective, competitive inhibitor of HMG-CoA reductase. Unlike atorvastatin, rosuvastatin is relatively **hydrophilic**, which theoretically reduces its penetration into extrahepatic tissues (like skeletal muscle), potentially lowering the risk of myopathy in susceptible patients.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Primary and secondary prevention of ASCVD; treatment of primary hyperlipidemia and mixed dyslipidemia.
*   **Nephrology Context:** Highly potent statin. Often preferred when CYP3A4 drug interactions are a concern. Requires strict dose adjustment in advanced CKD.
*   **Key Clinical Trials:**
    *   **AURORA (A Study to Evaluate the Use of Rosuvastatin in Subjects on Regular Hemodialysis):** Evaluated Crestor (rosuvastatin) 10 mg vs. placebo in 2,776 hemodialysis patients. *Outcome:* No significant reduction in the primary composite cardiovascular endpoint. Reinforced KDIGO guidelines against initiating statins in dialysis.
    *   **JUPITER:** Showed significant reduction in major cardiovascular events in patients with normal LDL but elevated CRP. Included a large cohort of mild-to-moderate CKD patients.
    *   **PLANET I:** Showed that high-dose Crestor (rosuvastatin) (40 mg) did not reduce proteinuria in diabetic nephropathy and was associated with a higher risk of renal adverse events compared to atorvastatin.

### Dosing
*   **Standard Dosing:** 
    *   Moderate intensity: 5–10 mg PO once daily.
    *   High intensity: 20–40 mg PO once daily.
*   **CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range (CRITICAL):**
    *   **eGFR ≥60:** 5–40 mg daily.
    *   **eGFR 30-59:** 5–40 mg daily.
    *   **eGFR 15-29:** **Start 5 mg once daily. Maximum dose: 10 mg once daily.**
    *   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** **Start 5 mg once daily. Maximum dose: 10 mg once daily.** *Note: Do not initiate in dialysis-dependent patients; continue only if previously prescribed.*
*   **Hepatic Adjustments:** Contraindicated in active liver disease.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs:** Fasting lipid panel, ALT/AST, CK, TSH, HbA1c. Baseline urinalysis (to document pre-existing proteinuria/hematuria).
*   **Follow-up Schedule:** 
    *   Lipid panel 4–12 weeks after initiation or dose adjustment.
    *   Annually thereafter.
    *   **Renal Monitoring:** High doses (40 mg) have been associated with transient proteinuria and microscopic hematuria. If unexplained proteinuria/hematuria occurs, consider dose reduction or switching agents.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Myalgia, arthralgia, headache, nausea.
*   **Serious/Rare:** Myopathy, rhabdomyolysis, hepatotoxicity, new-onset diabetes.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** 
    *   **Renal Toxicity:** The 40 mg dose is associated with an increased risk of proteinuria and hematuria. The FDA recommends dose reduction if unexplained proteinuria persists.
    *   **Accumulation:** Because rosuvastatin is partially excreted by the kidneys (~10%), drug accumulation occurs in severe CKD, necessitating the strict 10 mg maximum for eGFR <30.

### Drug Interactions
*   **CYP450 Interactions:** Minor substrate of CYP2C9 and CYP2C19. **Not** significantly metabolized by CYP3A4 (major advantage over atorvastatin).
*   **Major Nephrology Interactions:**
    *   **Cyclosporine:** Significantly increases rosuvastatin levels via OATP1B1 transporter inhibition. *Max Crestor (rosuvastatin) dose: 5 mg/day.*
    *   **Gemfibrozil:** Avoid concurrent use (increases rosuvastatin levels and myopathy risk).
    *   **Antacids (Aluminum/Magnesium):** Decreases rosuvastatin absorption by ~50%. *Administer antacids at least 2 hours after Crestor (rosuvastatin).*
    *   **Colchicine:** Increased risk of myopathy.

### Special Populations
*   **Asian Descent:** Pharmacokinetic studies show a 2-fold increase in median exposure to rosuvastatin in Asian subjects compared to Caucasians. **Start at 5 mg daily; maximum dose is 20 mg daily.**
*   **Pregnancy:** Contraindicated.
*   **Elderly (>65 years):** Start at 5 mg daily due to increased risk of myopathy and age-related decline in renal function.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Administration:** Can be taken at any time of day, with or without food (half-life ~19 hours).
*   **Antacids:** If taking over-the-counter antacids or phosphate binders containing aluminum/magnesium, take them at least 2 hours *after* Crestor (rosuvastatin).
*   **Warning Signs:** Report unexplained muscle pain or dark urine immediately.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **ICD-10 Codes:** E78.00, E78.5, I25.10.
*   **Cost:** Generic is widely available and inexpensive.

## Class Comparison: Atorvastatin vs. Rosuvastatin

| Feature | Lipitor (atorvastatin) | Crestor (rosuvastatin) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Intensity / Potency** | High (40-80mg) / Moderate (10-20mg) | High (20-40mg) / Moderate (5-10mg) |
| **Solubility** | Lipophilic | Hydrophilic |
| **Renal Excretion** | <2% | ~10% |
| **CKD Dosing (eGFR <30)** | **No adjustment needed** (Max 80 mg) | **Max 10 mg daily** |
| **Metabolism** | Major CYP3A4 substrate | Minor CYP2C9; NOT CYP3A4 |
| **Key Dialysis Trial** | 4D (Negative) | AURORA (Negative) |
| **Cyclosporine Max Dose** | 10 mg daily | 5 mg daily |
| **Asian Patient Dosing** | Standard dosing | Max 20 mg daily |

### Decision Tree: When to Choose Which Agent
*   **Choose Lipitor (atorvastatin) when:**
    *   The patient has advanced CKD (eGFR <30) and requires high-intensity statin therapy (since rosuvastatin is capped at 10 mg, which is only moderate intensity).
    *   The patient has a history of transient proteinuria or hematuria that you wish to avoid exacerbating.
*   **Choose Crestor (rosuvastatin) when:**
    *   The patient is on concurrent CYP3A4 inhibitors (e.g., amiodarone, diltiazem, certain azoles).
    *   The patient has experienced myalgias on lipophilic statins (atorvastatin, simvastatin); the hydrophilic nature of rosuvastatin is sometimes better tolerated.

### Switching Between Agents
If switching due to formulary changes or adverse effects, use the following approximate dose equivalencies to maintain similar LDL-lowering efficacy:
*   Crestor (rosuvastatin) 5 mg ≈ Lipitor (atorvastatin) 10 mg
*   Crestor (rosuvastatin) 10 mg ≈ Lipitor (atorvastatin) 20 mg
*   Crestor (rosuvastatin) 20 mg ≈ Lipitor (atorvastatin) 40 mg
*   Crestor (rosuvastatin) 40 mg ≈ Lipitor (atorvastatin) 80 mg

*(Note: If switching a patient with eGFR <30 from atorvastatin 40mg to rosuvastatin, you **cannot** use the equivalent rosuvastatin 20mg dose due to the 10mg renal cap. In this scenario, the patient will experience a step-down in statin intensity).*
