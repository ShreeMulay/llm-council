# Alkalinizing Agents Clinical Monograph
**Target Audience:** Nephrologists, Advanced Practice Providers (APPs), and Clinical Staff at The Kidney Experts (TKE)  
**Guidelines Referenced:** KDIGO 2024 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of Chronic Kidney Disease  

---

## Generic (sodium bicarbonate)
**Aliases:** Bicarb, NaHCO3, baking soda (household)  
*Note: While branded versions like Neut exist for IV use, oral formulations are almost exclusively prescribed and dispensed as Generic (sodium bicarbonate).*

### Mechanism of Action
- **Target/Receptor:** Systemic and urinary alkalinizer.
- **How it works:** Dissociates in water to provide sodium and bicarbonate ions. Bicarbonate neutralizes hydrogen ions, buffering systemic acidemia and raising blood pH. It is subsequently excreted in the urine, raising urinary pH. 

### Indications in Nephrology
#### FDA-Approved Indications
- Metabolic acidosis 
- Urinary alkalinization

#### Common Off-Label / Nephrology Uses
- **CKD-associated metabolic acidosis:** Standard of care to slow CKD progression and prevent bone demineralization/muscle wasting.
- **Uric acid nephropathy / Nephrolithiasis:** Alkalinization of urine to prevent uric acid or cystine stone formation.
- **Renal Tubular Acidosis (RTA):** Type 1 (distal), Type 2 (proximal), and Type 4 (hyperkalemic) RTA management.

#### Key Supporting Clinical Trials
- **UBI (Use of Bicarbonate in Chronic Renal Insufficiency) Trial (2019):** Multicenter RCT showing that targeting serum bicarbonate to 24-28 mEq/L in CKD stage 3-5 patients significantly slowed creatinine doubling and reduced all-cause mortality compared to standard care.
- **BICAR Trial (2019):** Demonstrated that sodium bicarbonate therapy in CKD patients with severe metabolic acidosis improved renal survival and nutritional parameters.

### Dosing
*Note: 1 tablet of 650 mg sodium bicarbonate = 7.7 mEq of bicarbonate AND 7.7 mEq (178 mg) of sodium.*

- **Target Lab Value:** Serum bicarbonate (total CO2) **22–26 mEq/L** (per KDIGO 2024 guidelines).
- **Standard Starting Dose:** 650 mg PO BID or TID.
- **Titration Schedule:** Increase by 650 mg/day every 2–4 weeks until serum bicarbonate is within the target range. Maximum practical dose is usually 3,900 mg/day (2 tabs TID) due to sodium load and GI intolerance.

#### CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range
*Sodium bicarbonate is not cleared by eGFR in a way that requires traditional renal dose adjustments; however, the **sodium load** requires strict adjustment based on eGFR-related volume tolerance.*
- **eGFR ≥60:** Standard dosing (650 mg PO BID to TID).
- **eGFR 30-59:** Standard dosing. Monitor closely for new-onset hypertension or peripheral edema.
- **eGFR 15-29:** Start low (650 mg PO QD or BID). High risk for volume overload and exacerbation of hypertension due to sodium retention. Consider concurrent loop diuretic adjustment.
- **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** 
  - *Pre-dialysis:* Use with extreme caution due to severe volume overload risk. 
  - *On Dialysis:* Generally discontinued, as metabolic acidosis is managed via the dialysate bicarbonate buffer. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Standard dialysate bicarb bath settings for HD/PD].

#### Hepatic Adjustments
- No specific adjustments required, but use with caution in cirrhosis with ascites due to the sodium load.

### Monitoring
#### Baseline Labs Required
- BMP (specifically Serum CO2/Bicarbonate, Sodium, Potassium, Calcium)
- Blood pressure and baseline weight

#### Follow-up Schedule
- **2-4 weeks post-initiation/titration:** BMP to check serum CO2, K+, and Na+.
- **Maintenance:** BMP every 3-6 months alongside routine CKD labs.
- **Clinical Monitoring:** Assess for peripheral edema, weight gain, and worsening hypertension at every visit.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
#### Common Side Effects (>5%)
- Gastrointestinal: Bloating, flatulence, belching, abdominal cramping (due to CO2 gas production in the stomach).
- Fluid retention and peripheral edema.
- Weight gain.

#### Serious/Rare Side Effects
- Exacerbation of congestive heart failure (CHF).
- Severe hypertension.
- Hypokalemia (alkalemia drives potassium intracellularly).
- Hypocalcemia/Tetany (alkalemia decreases ionized calcium levels).
- Milk-alkali syndrome (if taken with high doses of calcium).

#### Contraindications
- **Absolute:** Metabolic or respiratory alkalosis, hypernatremia, severe pulmonary edema, hypocalcemia, unknown abdominal pain.
- **Relative:** Uncontrolled hypertension, decompensated heart failure, severe edema.

#### CKD-Specific Safety Concerns
- **The Sodium Trap:** A patient taking 650 mg TID is consuming an extra **534 mg of sodium daily**. This directly antagonizes the low-sodium diet recommended for CKD and can blunt the efficacy of RAAS inhibitors and diuretics.

### Drug Interactions
- **Gastric pH Alteration:** Decreases the absorption of drugs requiring an acidic environment (e.g., oral iron supplements, ketoconazole, atazanavir, cefpodoxime). *Separate by at least 2 hours.*
- **Urinary pH Alteration:** 
  - *Increases clearance* of weak acids (e.g., aspirin, methotrexate).
  - *Decreases clearance* of weak bases (e.g., amphetamines, pseudoephedrine, flecainide), risking toxicity.
- **Calcium Supplements:** Concurrent use increases the risk of milk-alkali syndrome (hypercalcemia, alkalosis, AKI).
- **Potassium-Wasting Diuretics:** Additive risk of hypokalemia.

### Special Populations
- **Pregnancy:** Category C. Generally considered safe if clinically indicated, but monitor for fluid retention.
- **Elderly:** Higher risk of volume overload, CHF exacerbation, and hypertension. Start at lower doses and titrate slowly.
- **Transplant:** Highly relevant. Calcineurin inhibitors (Tacrolimus, Cyclosporine) frequently cause Type 4 RTA. Sodium bicarbonate is routinely used post-transplant to maintain serum CO2 >22 mEq/L.

### Patient Counseling Points
- **Administration:** Take 1 to 2 hours apart from other medications, especially iron pills or thyroid medications.
- **Side Effects:** You may experience burping or a bloated feeling shortly after taking this; this is normal as the pill releases gas in your stomach.
- **Warning Signs:** Call the clinic immediately if you notice sudden weight gain, swelling in your legs/ankles, or shortness of breath.
- **Household Baking Soda:** **DO NOT** substitute household baking soda for your prescription without exact instructions from our clinic. One teaspoon of baking soda contains about 1,200 mg of sodium—a massive amount that can cause severe swelling and high blood pressure.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
- **ICD-10 Codes:** 
  - E87.2 (Acidosis)
  - N18.x (Chronic kidney disease, stage x)
- **CPT Codes:** Standard E&M codes (99213, 99214, 99215) apply for medical decision making involving prescription management.
- **Cost/PA:** Extremely low cost. Usually available over-the-counter (OTC), but writing a prescription allows patients to use FSA/HSA funds and ensures the medication appears accurately on the pharmacy fill record. No PA required.

### TKE Clinical Decision Tree: Management of CKD Metabolic Acidosis
*[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Review and adjust thresholds based on local clinic preferences]*

**1. WHEN TO START:**
- Serum Total CO2/Bicarbonate is **< 22 mEq/L** on two consecutive BMPs.
- Ensure the patient does not have decompensated CHF or uncontrolled severe hypertension.

**2. AGENT SELECTION:**
- **First Line:** Generic (sodium bicarbonate) 650 mg PO BID.
- **Alternative (GI Intolerance):** If patient cannot tolerate gas/bloating from tablets, switch to Bicitra (sodium citrate) 10-15 mL PO BID mixed in water.
- **Alternative (Household):** If cost is an absolute barrier, 1/2 teaspoon of baking soda dissolved in water daily (~20 mEq bicarb). *Requires strict patient education on measuring.*

**3. WHEN TO ESCALATE:**
- If repeat BMP at 4 weeks shows CO2 < 22 mEq/L:
  - Increase dose by 650 mg/day (e.g., move from BID to TID).
  - Assess BP and edema. If BP rises, consider adding/increasing a loop diuretic (e.g., Torsemide) to manage the sodium load.

**4. WHEN TO STOP / REDUCE:**
- Serum CO2 > 26 mEq/L (reduce dose to avoid alkalosis and hypocalcemia).
- Development of refractory edema, new-onset heart failure, or uncontrolled hypertension (stop agent, reassess risk/benefit, consider dietary acid reduction via plant-based diet).
- Patient initiates maintenance dialysis (stop PO agent, manage via dialysate).
