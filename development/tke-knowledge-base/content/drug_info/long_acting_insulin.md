# Clinical Drug Monograph: Long-Acting Insulins (Basal)

**Disclaimer:** *This document is a clinical reference for healthcare professionals at The Kidney Experts (TKE). It is based on the ADA 2024 Standards of Medical Care in Diabetes and the KDIGO 2022 Clinical Practice Guideline for Diabetes Management in CKD. It does not replace clinical judgment. Patient-specific factors must always be considered.*

---

*Editorial Note: Because Lantus, Basaglar, and Toujeo share the same active pharmaceutical ingredient (insulin glargine), they are presented in a combined monograph to avoid redundancy, with critical differences in concentration (U-100 vs. U-300), pharmacokinetics, and dosing explicitly delineated.*

## Lantus, Basaglar, Toujeo (insulin glargine)

### Mechanism of Action
- **Primary MOA:** Insulin glargine is a recombinant human insulin analog. It binds to insulin receptors on muscle and adipose tissue to facilitate cellular glucose uptake, and it inhibits hepatic gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis.
- **Pharmacokinetics/Target Specificity:** 
  - **Lantus/Basaglar (U-100):** Formulated at an acidic pH (4.0). Upon subcutaneous injection into physiologic pH (7.4), it forms microprecipitates that slowly release insulin over ~24 hours, providing a relatively peakless basal profile.
  - **Toujeo (U-300):** Contains 3 times the concentration of insulin glargine. The subcutaneous depot has a smaller surface area, resulting in a more gradual, prolonged release (up to 36 hours) and less intra-day glycemic variability compared to U-100 formulations.

### Indications in Nephrology
- **FDA-Approved Indications:** 
  - Treatment of Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus (T1DM) in adults and pediatric patients.
  - Treatment of Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM) in adults and pediatric patients.
- **Off-Label/Nephrology-Specific Uses:**
  - Post-Transplant Diabetes Mellitus (PTDM): Basal insulin is often first-line for managing steroid-induced fasting hyperglycemia in renal transplant recipients.
- **Key Supporting Clinical Trials:**
  - **ORIGIN Trial (2012):** Evaluated Lantus (insulin glargine) vs. standard care in patients with CV risk factors and IFG/IGT or early T2DM. *Outcomes:* Neutral cardiovascular outcomes (HR 1.02, p=0.63); no increased risk of cancer; established long-term CV safety of glargine.
  - **EDITION 1, 2, and 3 Trials (2014-2015):** Compared Toujeo (insulin glargine U-300) to Lantus (insulin glargine U-100) in T2DM. *Outcomes:* Toujeo demonstrated comparable A1c reduction but with a **21-31% lower risk of nocturnal hypoglycemia** compared to Lantus.

### Dosing
**General Principles:** Insulin is renally cleared. As kidney function declines, insulin half-life prolongs. The phenomenon of "burnt-out diabetes" in advanced CKD/ESRD requires aggressive down-titration of basal insulin to prevent fatal hypoglycemia.

#### Standard Dosing & Titration (T2DM)
- **Starting Dose:** 0.1 to 0.2 units/kg/day OR 10 units subcutaneously once daily.
- **Titration Schedule [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]:** Increase by 2 units every 3 days until Fasting Blood Glucose (FBG) target (typically 80–130 mg/dL) is reached without hypoglycemia.

#### CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range (CRITICAL)
*Note: Adjustments are based on total daily insulin requirements. Frequent FBG monitoring is mandatory when eGFR <30.*
- **eGFR ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m²:** Standard dosing. No empirical adjustment required.
- **eGFR 30-59 mL/min/1.73 m²:** Standard starting dose, but anticipate a **25% reduction** in maintenance dose requirements. Titrate cautiously.
- **eGFR 15-29 mL/min/1.73 m²:** Reduce starting and maintenance doses by **25% to 50%**. High risk of prolonged hypoglycemia.
- **eGFR <15 mL/min/1.73 m² / Dialysis:** Reduce dose by **50% or more**. 
  - *Hemodialysis Patients:* Insulin requirements may vary on dialysis vs. non-dialysis days. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED] Consider reducing basal insulin by an additional 10-20% on the night before or morning of hemodialysis to prevent intra-dialytic hypoglycemia.

#### Hepatic Adjustments
- No specific quantitative adjustments, but hepatic impairment reduces gluconeogenesis, further increasing hypoglycemia risk. Use conservative dosing.

### Monitoring
- **Baseline Labs:** A1c, CMP (specifically eGFR and Potassium), FBG.
- **Follow-up Schedule:**
  - **Patient Self-Monitoring:** FBG daily every morning.
  - **Clinical Labs:** A1c every 3 months (if not at goal) or every 6 months (if stable at goal). CMP every 3-6 months to monitor eGFR and potassium.
- **Lab Targets (per ADA 2024 / KDIGO 2022):**
  - **FBG:** 80–130 mg/dL.
  - **A1c:** Individualized. <7.0% for early CKD with low hypoglycemia risk. **<8.0%** for advanced CKD (Stage 4/5), ESRD, or patients with a history of severe hypoglycemia.
- **Therapeutic Drug Monitoring:** Not applicable (no serum trough levels drawn).

### Adverse Effects & Safety
- **Common Side Effects (>5%):** Hypoglycemia, weight gain, injection site reactions (erythema, pruritus), lipohypertrophy (if sites are not rotated).
- **Serious/Rare Side Effects:** Severe, refractory hypoglycemia; hypokalemia (insulin shifts potassium intracellularly); anaphylaxis.
- **Black Box Warnings:** None.
- **Contraindications:** 
  - *Absolute:* During episodes of hypoglycemia; documented hypersensitivity to insulin glargine.
- **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** 
  - **Uremic Hypoglycemia:** Uremia decreases both insulin clearance (prolonging action) and hepatic gluconeogenesis. Patients transitioning to ESRD often require drastic reductions or complete cessation of insulin.
  - **Fluid Overload:** Insulin causes mild sodium retention, which can exacerbate edema in nephrotic syndrome or advanced CKD.

### Drug Interactions
- **Major Interactions:**
  - **SGLT2 inhibitors (e.g., Farxiga (dapagliflozin), Jardiance (empagliflozin)) & GLP-1 RAs:** Synergistic glucose lowering. *Decision Tree:* When initiating an SGLT2i or GLP-1 RA in a patient on insulin glargine, preemptively decrease the glargine dose by **10-20%** to prevent hypoglycemia.
  - **Thiazolidinediones (TZDs - e.g., Actos (pioglitazone)):** Co-administration increases the risk of severe fluid retention and heart failure. Avoid combination in advanced CKD.
- **Other Interactions:**
  - **Beta-blockers (e.g., Coreg (carvedilol), Toprol XL (metoprolol)):** Mask the sympathetic warning signs of hypoglycemia (tachycardia, tremors). *Sweating is not masked.*
  - **ACEi/ARBs & Bactrim (sulfamethoxazole/trimethoprim):** Can enhance the hypoglycemic effect of insulin.

### Special Populations
- **Pregnancy:** Historically Category C. Levemir (insulin detemir) is often preferred due to more robust pregnancy data, but insulin glargine is frequently used and generally considered safe if required for glycemic control.
- **Elderly in CKD:** High risk for hypoglycemia unawareness and falls. Target A1c <8.0% to <8.5%. Prefer Toujeo (U-300) over Lantus/Basaglar (U-100) if nocturnal hypoglycemia is a concern.
- **Transplant:** Safe and effective for PTDM. Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus) impair insulin secretion; basal insulin effectively bridges this defect.

### Patient Counseling Points
- **Hypoglycemia Management:** Teach the "Rule of 15" (consume 15g of fast-acting carbs, wait 15 minutes, recheck). Warn that kidney disease makes low blood sugar last longer.
- **Injection Technique:** Administer subcutaneously in the abdomen, thigh, or upper arm. **Must rotate sites** daily to prevent lipohypertrophy (hard lumps), which severely impairs insulin absorption.
- **Storage:** 
  - Unopened pens/vials: Store in the refrigerator (36°F to 46°F).
  - Opened/In-use pens: Store at room temperature (below 86°F). 
  - **Discard after:** 28 days for Lantus/Basaglar; **56 days for Toujeo**.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
- **Common ICD-10 Codes:** 
  - E11.22 (Type 2 diabetes mellitus with diabetic chronic kidney disease)
  - E11.9 (Type 2 diabetes mellitus without complications)
  - N18.4 (CKD Stage 4) / N18.5 (CKD Stage 5) / N18.6 (ESRD)
- **CPT Codes:** 98960 (Education and training for patient self-management).
- **PA Tips:** Basaglar is often the preferred formulary U-100 glargine for many Medicare Part D and commercial plans. If Toujeo is required, document a history of nocturnal hypoglycemia on U-100 glargine.
- **Cost/Assistance:** Sanofi (Lantus/Toujeo) and Lilly (Basaglar) offer patient assistance programs and $35/month insulin copay caps under the Inflation Reduction Act for Medicare patients.

### Decision Tree: Choosing and Switching Agents

**When to choose one agent over another:**
- **Start with Basaglar (U-100):** For most new insulin starts due to favorable formulary tiering and cost.
- **Switch to Toujeo (U-300):** 
  1. If the patient experiences nocturnal hypoglycemia on U-100 glargine.
  2. If the patient requires large doses of basal insulin (>60-80 units/day) to reduce injection volume.
  3. If the patient has erratic eating/sleeping schedules (Toujeo allows a flexible dosing window of +/- 3 hours from the usual injection time).

**Protocol for Switching Between Agents [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]:**
- **Lantus to Basaglar (or vice versa):** Switch unit-for-unit (1:1). No dose adjustment needed.
- **U-100 Glargine (Lantus/Basaglar) to U-300 Glargine (Toujeo):** 
  - Switch unit-for-unit (1:1) initially. 
  - *Clinical Pearl:* Because Toujeo has a slower absorption profile, patients often require a **10% to 20% higher daily dose** of Toujeo eventually. Titrate up every 3-4 days based on FBG.
- **U-300 Glargine (Toujeo) to U-100 Glargine (Lantus/Basaglar):**
  - **Decrease the dose by 20%** to prevent hypoglycemia, as U-100 absorbs faster and has a higher peak effect. Titrate up as needed based on FBG.
