# Clinical Drug Monograph: ACE Inhibitors (ACEi)

**Guideline Foundations:** 
- **KDIGO 2024 CKD Guidelines:** Recommends Renin-Angiotensin-System inhibitors (RASi) as first-line therapy for patients with CKD, hypertension, and a Urine Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio (UACR) ≥30 mg/g (Strong recommendation for UACR ≥300 mg/g).
- **KDIGO 2022 Diabetes Management in CKD:** Recommends ACEi or ARB for patients with diabetes, hypertension, and albuminuria.
- **ACC/AHA 2017 Hypertension Guidelines:** First-line agents for hypertension, particularly in patients with CKD or heart failure.

## Prinivil, Zestril (lisinopril)

### Mechanism of Action
Prinivil, Zestril (lisinopril) is a competitive inhibitor of angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE). It prevents the conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II, a potent vasoconstrictor. In the kidney, it preferentially vasodilates the efferent arteriole, reducing intraglomerular pressure and subsequent proteinuria. It also decreases aldosterone secretion (reducing sodium/water retention) and inhibits the degradation of bradykinin.

### Indications in Nephrology
- **FDA-Approved:** Hypertension, Heart Failure with reduced Ejection Fraction (HFrEF), Acute Myocardial Infarction.
- **Off-Label (Nephrology Standard of Care):** Proteinuric Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD), Diabetic Nephropathy.
- **Key Clinical Trials:** 
  - **ALLHAT (2002):** Demonstrated lisinopril was equally effective to amlodipine and chlorthalidone in preventing fatal CHD and nonfatal MI.
  - **EUCLID (1997):** Showed lisinopril reduced albuminuria progression in normotensive patients with Type 1 Diabetes (baseline UACR decreased by ~50% vs placebo).

### Dosing
- **Standard Dosing:** Initial 5–10 mg orally once daily. Titrate by 5–10 mg increments every 2–4 weeks. Maximum dose: 40 mg once daily.
- **CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR range:**
  - **eGFR ≥30 mL/min:** Initial 10 mg once daily.
  - **eGFR 10–29 mL/min:** Initial 5 mg once daily.
  - **eGFR <10 mL/min / Dialysis:** Initial 2.5 mg once daily. *Note: Lisinopril is dialyzable (up to 50% removed); administer post-hemodialysis on dialysis days.*
- **Hepatic Adjustments:** None required (lisinopril is not a prodrug and does not require hepatic activation).

### Monitoring
- **Baseline Labs:** Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP) specifically for Serum Creatinine (SCr) and Potassium (K+), and UACR.
- **Follow-up Schedule [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]:** Repeat BMP 1–2 weeks after initiation or any dose titration. 
- **Targets:** 
  - Potassium < 5.0 mEq/L.
  - Permissive SCr increase of up to 30% from baseline is acceptable and indicates hemodynamic efficacy.
  - Target UACR < 30 mg/g, or at least a >30% reduction from baseline.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
- **Common (>5%):** Dry cough (bradykinin-mediated), dizziness, hypotension, hyperkalemia.
- **Serious/Rare:** Angioedema (higher risk in Black patients), acute kidney injury (AKI), cholestatic jaundice.
- **Black Box Warning:** **Fetal Toxicity.** Discontinue immediately if pregnancy is detected.
- **Contraindications:** History of ACEi-induced angioedema, hereditary/idiopathic angioedema, concurrent use with aliskiren in patients with diabetes, bilateral renal artery stenosis (relative/absolute depending on severity).
- **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** High risk for hyperkalemia in advanced CKD (Stage 4-5). Risk of AKI during volume depletion.

### Drug Interactions
- **Major:** ARBs, Direct Renin Inhibitors (aliskiren), Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists (MRAs - e.g., spironolactone), Potassium supplements/salt substitutes (severe hyperkalemia risk).
- **NSAIDs:** Concurrent use causes "double hit" to glomerulus (afferent constriction + efferent dilation), significantly increasing AKI risk.
- **Lithium:** ACEi can reduce lithium clearance, leading to toxicity.
- **CYP450:** No significant CYP450 interactions.

### Special Populations
- **Pregnancy:** Category D (Contraindicated). Teratogenic (oligohydramnios, fetal renal failure).
- **Elderly:** Start at lower end of dosing range (2.5–5 mg) due to increased risk of orthostatic hypotension and falls.

### Patient Counseling Points
- **What to expect:** You may develop a dry, hacking cough. If it becomes intolerable, let us know; do not stop abruptly without calling the clinic.
- **Warning Signs:** Seek immediate emergency care for swelling of the lips, tongue, or throat (angioedema).
- **Sick Day Rules [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]:** Hold this medication if you are experiencing severe vomiting, diarrhea, or inability to drink fluids to prevent acute kidney injury.
- **Diet:** Avoid salt substitutes, as they are often made of potassium chloride and can cause dangerously high potassium levels.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
- **ICD-10 Codes:** I12.9 (Hypertensive CKD stage 1-4), E11.21 (Type 2 DM with diabetic nephropathy), N18.3x (CKD Stage 3), N18.4 (CKD Stage 4).
- **CPT Codes:** 99213/99214 (E&M visits), 99490 (Chronic Care Management).
- **Cost:** Tier 1 generic. Very low cost ($4-$10/month). PA rarely required.

## Vasotec (enalapril)

### Mechanism of Action
Vasotec (enalapril) is a prodrug that is hydrolyzed in the liver to its active form, enalaprilat. It shares the same ACE inhibition mechanism as lisinopril.

### Indications in Nephrology
- **FDA-Approved:** Hypertension, Symptomatic Heart Failure, Asymptomatic Left Ventricular Dysfunction.
- **Off-Label:** Proteinuric CKD, Diabetic Nephropathy.
- **Key Clinical Trials:**
  - **SOLVD (1991):** Demonstrated significant reduction in mortality and hospitalization in patients with HFrEF.
  - **CONSENSUS (1987):** Showed a 40% reduction in 6-month mortality in severe heart failure.

### Dosing
- **Standard Dosing:** Initial 2.5–5 mg orally once daily. Can be dosed BID for better 24-hour blood pressure control. Maximum dose: 40 mg/day (usually divided BID).
- **CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR range:**
  - **eGFR ≥30 mL/min:** Initial 5 mg once daily.
  - **eGFR <30 mL/min:** Initial 2.5 mg once daily.
  - **Dialysis:** Initial 2.5 mg on dialysis days (administer post-HD).
- **Hepatic Adjustments:** May have delayed conversion to active enalaprilat in severe hepatic impairment; monitor closely.

### Monitoring
- **Baseline Labs:** BMP (SCr, K+), UACR.
- **Follow-up Schedule:** Repeat BMP 1–2 weeks post-initiation/titration.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
- *Identical to lisinopril.* (Cough, hyperkalemia, angioedema, AKI).
- **Black Box Warning:** Fetal Toxicity.

### Drug Interactions
- *Identical to lisinopril.* (Avoid NSAIDs, ARBs, MRAs without close monitoring, potassium supplements).

### Special Populations
- **Pregnancy:** Contraindicated.
- **Elderly:** Start at 2.5 mg daily.

### Patient Counseling Points
- *Identical to lisinopril.* Emphasize sick day rules and avoidance of potassium-based salt substitutes.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
- **Cost:** Tier 1 generic. Very low cost. PA rarely required.

## Altace (ramipril)

### Mechanism of Action
Altace (ramipril) is a highly lipophilic prodrug converted in the liver to ramiprilat. Its high tissue affinity provides strong tissue ACE inhibition (in vascular endothelium and kidneys) in addition to circulating ACE inhibition.

### Indications in Nephrology
- **FDA-Approved:** Hypertension, Heart Failure post-MI, Reduction in risk of MI, stroke, and death from CV causes.
- **Off-Label:** Proteinuric CKD (diabetic and non-diabetic).
- **Key Clinical Trials:**
  - **HOPE (2000):** Showed a 22% reduction in the primary composite endpoint of CV death, MI, or stroke in high-risk patients. Also showed a 24% reduction in overt nephropathy.
  - **AASK (2002):** African American Study of Kidney Disease. Demonstrated ramipril was superior to amlodipine and metoprolol in slowing CKD progression in Black patients with hypertensive nephrosclerosis.
  - **REIN (1997):** Demonstrated ramipril safely reduced proteinuria and slowed GFR decline in non-diabetic nephropathy.

### Dosing
- **Standard Dosing:** Initial 2.5 mg orally once daily. Titrate every 2–4 weeks. Maximum dose: 20 mg/day (often split BID for HTN, daily for CV risk).
- **CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR range:**
  - **eGFR ≥40 mL/min:** Initial 2.5 mg once daily.
  - **eGFR <40 mL/min:** Initial 1.25 mg once daily. Maximum dose 5 mg daily.
  - **Dialysis:** Initial 1.25 mg daily.
- **Hepatic Adjustments:** Max dose 2.5 mg daily in severe hepatic impairment.

### Monitoring
- **Baseline Labs:** BMP (SCr, K+), UACR.
- **Follow-up Schedule:** Repeat BMP 1–2 weeks post-initiation/titration.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
- *Identical to class.* (Cough, hyperkalemia, angioedema, AKI).
- **Black Box Warning:** Fetal Toxicity.

### Drug Interactions
- *Identical to class.*

### Special Populations
- **Pregnancy:** Contraindicated.
- **Swallowing difficulties:** Altace capsules can be opened and the contents sprinkled on a small amount of applesauce (consume immediately, do not chew).

### Patient Counseling Points
- *Identical to class.* Plus: "If you have trouble swallowing pills, you can open this capsule and sprinkle it on applesauce."

### Prior Authorization & Coding
- **Cost:** Tier 1 generic. Low cost. PA rarely required.

### When to Choose One Agent Over Another
- **Prinivil, Zestril (lisinopril):** The default choice for most CKD patients due to once-daily dosing, lack of hepatic metabolism requirement, and extensive clinical familiarity.
- **Vasotec (enalapril):** Preferred when a patient has poorly controlled hypertension on once-daily regimens, as enalapril is easily and effectively dosed BID for smoother 24-hour BP coverage.
- **Altace (ramipril):** Preferred in patients with high cardiovascular risk (per HOPE trial), non-diabetic proteinuric CKD (per REIN trial), or Black patients with hypertensive nephrosclerosis (per AASK trial). Also useful for patients with dysphagia (capsule can be opened).

### Switching Between Agents
If switching due to formulary issues or preference (NOT due to angioedema or cough, which are class effects), use approximate dose equivalents:
- **Lisinopril 10 mg** ≈ **Enalapril 5 mg** ≈ **Ramipril 2.5 mg**

### Clinical Decision Tree: ACEi Management in CKD

**1. INITIATION**
- **Criteria:** Patient has CKD + HTN + UACR ≥30 mg/g (Strongly recommended if UACR ≥300 mg/g or Diabetic).
- **Action:** Check baseline BMP. If K+ < 5.0 and BP tolerates, start low-dose ACEi based on eGFR.

**2. ESCALATION**
- **Criteria:** 1-2 weeks post-initiation, SCr increase is <30%, K+ is <5.5 mEq/L, and BP tolerates.
- **Action:** Double the dose every 2-4 weeks until UACR drops by >30%, UACR is <30 mg/g, or maximum tolerated dose is reached.

**3. TROUBLESHOOTING & HOLDING**
- **If SCr rises >30%:** Rule out volume depletion, NSAID use, or bilateral renal artery stenosis. Reduce dose or hold until stabilized.
- **If K+ rises to 5.1 - 5.4 mEq/L:** Initiate low-potassium diet, consider adding a thiazide/loop diuretic (if volume overloaded) or a potassium binder (e.g., Lokelma, Veltassa). Do *not* stop ACEi immediately if proteinuria benefit is high.
- **If K+ ≥ 5.5 mEq/L despite interventions:** Reduce dose or discontinue ACEi.
- **If patient develops a dry cough:** Switch to an Angiotensin Receptor Blocker (ARB) (e.g., losartan, valsartan).
- **If patient develops angioedema:** **STOP immediately.** Add allergy to chart. Do NOT switch to an ARB (cross-reactivity risk). Switch to a non-RASi antihypertensive.
