# Clinical Drug Monograph: Xanthine Oxidase Inhibitors (XOIs)

**Class Overview:** Xanthine oxidase inhibitors (XOIs) are the first-line urate-lowering therapies (ULT) for the management of gout and hyperuricemia. In nephrology, managing hyperuricemia is common, but recent high-quality trials have shifted the paradigm away from treating asymptomatic hyperuricemia solely to delay chronic kidney disease (CKD) progression. 

**Guideline Basis:** 2020 American College of Rheumatology (ACR) Guideline for the Management of Gout; KDIGO 2012/2024 Clinical Practice Guidelines for CKD.

---

## Zyloprim (allopurinol)

### Mechanism of Action
Zyloprim (allopurinol) and its active metabolite, oxypurinol, are purine analogs that competitively inhibit xanthine oxidase, the enzyme responsible for converting hypoxanthine to xanthine, and xanthine to uric acid. This reduces both serum and urine uric acid concentrations.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Management of gout; management of patients with leukemia, lymphoma, and malignancies receiving cancer therapy (Tumor Lysis Syndrome); management of recurrent calcium oxalate calculi in patients with hyperuricosuria.
*   **Off-Label / Nephrology Context:** Prevention of uric acid nephropathy. 
*   **Clinical Pearl (Asymptomatic Hyperuricemia):** Routine use of Zyloprim (allopurinol) to delay CKD progression in *asymptomatic* hyperuricemia is **NOT** recommended. 
    *   *CKD-FIX Trial (2020):* Showed no significant difference in eGFR decline between allopurinol and placebo in stage 3/4 CKD.
    *   *PERL Trial (2020):* Showed no benefit of allopurinol in preventing eGFR decline in Type 1 Diabetics with early to moderate CKD.

### Dosing
**Target:** Serum urate (SU) < 6.0 mg/dL (or < 5.0 mg/dL in severe tophaceous gout).
*   **Standard Starting Dose:** 100 mg PO daily.
*   **Titration:** Increase by 50–100 mg every 2–5 weeks until SU target is met. 
*   **Maximum Dose:** 800 mg/day (doses >300 mg should be divided BID). *Note: ACR 2020 guidelines support titrating above traditional eGFR-based maximums if the patient is tolerating the drug and SU target is not met, provided there is intensive monitoring for toxicity.*

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range (Starting Doses):**
*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min:** Start 100 mg daily.
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min:** Start 50–100 mg daily.
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min:** Start 50 mg daily.
*   **eGFR <15 mL/min / Dialysis:** Start 50 mg daily or 50 mg every other day. 
    *   *Hemodialysis:* Oxypurinol is dialyzable. Administer dose *after* hemodialysis on dialysis days.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline:** Serum urate, CBC, CMP (SCr, BUN, LFTs).
*   **Genetic Testing:** HLA-B\*5801 testing is **strongly recommended** prior to initiation in high-risk populations (Han Chinese, Korean, Thai descent, and African Americans) due to high risk of severe cutaneous adverse reactions (SCAR). *   **Follow-up:** Serum urate every 2–5 weeks during titration, then every 6 months once at target. Monitor SCr and LFTs periodically.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Skin rash, maculopapular rash, nausea, diarrhea, acute gout flares (upon initiation).
*   **Serious/Rare:** 
    *   **Allopurinol Hypersensitivity Syndrome (AHS):** Fever, rash, hepatitis, eosinophilia, acute renal failure. High mortality rate (up to 20-25%).
    *   Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN).
    *   Bone marrow suppression (especially in renal impairment).
*   **Contraindications:** Prior severe reaction to allopurinol.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** The active metabolite (oxypurinol) is renally cleared. Accumulation in CKD increases the risk of AHS and bone marrow suppression. **Start low and go slow.**

### Drug Interactions
*   **Azathioprine (Imuran) / Mercaptopurine (6-MP):** **MAJOR INTERACTION.** Allopurinol inhibits the metabolism of these drugs, leading to fatal bone marrow suppression. *Action:* Avoid combination if possible. If required, reduce Azathioprine/6-MP dose by 70-75% and monitor CBC closely.
*   **ACE Inhibitors / Thiazide Diuretics:** May increase the risk of allopurinol hypersensitivity and toxicity, particularly in CKD.
*   **Warfarin:** Allopurinol may prolong prothrombin time. Monitor INR closely upon initiation/titration.
*   **Amoxicillin/Ampicillin:** Increased risk of skin rash.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Category C. Use only if potential benefit justifies the risk.
*   **Elderly:** Start at lower doses due to age-related decline in renal function.
*   **Transplant:** Extreme caution due to azathioprine interaction. Safe to use with mycophenolate (CellCept/Myfortic) and calcineurin inhibitors (Tacrolimus/Cyclosporine).

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Gout Flares:** "This medication lowers uric acid, but starting it can actually *cause* a gout flare. Do not stop taking it if you have a flare. We will prescribe a second medication (like colchicine) to prevent flares for the first 3-6 months."
*   **Warning Signs:** "Stop the medication and call the clinic immediately if you develop any skin rash, fever, or sores in your mouth."
*   **Administration:** Take after meals to minimize stomach upset. Maintain adequate hydration (if fluid status allows).

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **ICD-10 Codes:** M10.9 (Gout, unspecified), E84.0 (Hyperuricemia without signs of inflammatory arthritis), N20.0 (Calculus of kidney).
*   **Cost:** Generic is highly cost-effective (Tier 1 on most formularies). PA is rarely required.

## Uloric (febuxostat)

### Mechanism of Action
Uloric (febuxostat) is a non-purine, selective inhibitor of xanthine oxidase. It forms a highly stable complex with the enzyme, blocking the active site and preventing uric acid production.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Chronic management of hyperuricemia in patients with gout who have an inadequate response to a maximally titrated dose of allopurinol, who are intolerant to allopurinol, or for whom treatment with allopurinol is not advisable.
*   **Clinical Trials:** 
    *   *CARES Trial (2018):* Showed higher rates of cardiovascular (CV) death and all-cause mortality with febuxostat compared to allopurinol, leading to a Black Box Warning.
    *   *FAST Trial (2020):* A European trial that showed febuxostat was non-inferior to allopurinol regarding the primary CV endpoint, and its use was not associated with an increased risk of death. (Despite FAST, the FDA Black Box Warning remains).

### Dosing
**Target:** Serum urate (SU) < 6.0 mg/dL.
*   **Standard Starting Dose:** 40 mg PO daily.
*   **Titration:** If SU is not < 6.0 mg/dL after 2 weeks, increase to 80 mg PO daily.
*   **Maximum Dose:** 120 mg/day (rarely used, 80 mg is standard max in US).

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
*   **eGFR ≥30 mL/min:** No dose adjustment necessary.
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min:** Maximum dose is 40 mg PO daily.
*   **eGFR <15 mL/min / Dialysis:** Insufficient data; use with caution. Not efficiently removed by hemodialysis.
*   **Hepatic Impairment:** Mild-to-moderate (Child-Pugh A/B): No adjustment. Severe: Use with caution.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline:** Serum urate, LFTs, CMP.
*   **Follow-up:** Serum urate 2 weeks after initiation or dose change. LFTs at 2 months, 4 months, and periodically thereafter.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Black Box Warning:** **Cardiovascular Death.** In the CARES trial, Uloric (febuxostat) showed a higher rate of CV death compared to allopurinol. It should only be used in patients who have failed or cannot tolerate allopurinol.
*   **Common (>5%):** Liver function abnormalities, nausea, arthralgia, rash, acute gout flares.
*   **Serious/Rare:** Hepatic failure, severe skin reactions (SJS/TEN/DRESS), cardiovascular events (MI, stroke).
*   **Contraindications:** Patients being treated with azathioprine or mercaptopurine.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** Safer alternative to allopurinol in patients with severe CKD who are HLA-B\*5801 positive or have a history of AHS, as it is primarily metabolized by the liver.

### Drug Interactions
*   **Azathioprine (Imuran) / Mercaptopurine (6-MP):** **CONTRAINDICATED.** Febuxostat severely inhibits their metabolism, leading to fatal hematologic toxicity.
*   **Theophylline:** Febuxostat may increase theophylline levels; monitor closely.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Category C.
*   **Elderly:** No specific dose adjustment based on age, but consider baseline CV risk.
*   **Transplant:** **Absolute contraindication** if the patient is on azathioprine. Safe with mycophenolate/calcineurin inhibitors.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Cardiovascular Risk:** "This medication carries a risk for cardiovascular events. Seek immediate emergency care if you experience chest pain, shortness of breath, rapid heartbeat, or numbness/weakness on one side of your body."
*   **Gout Flares:** "Starting this medication can trigger a gout flare. Take your prescribed flare-prevention medication (e.g., colchicine) as directed."
*   **Liver Health:** "We will need to draw blood periodically to ensure this medication is not irritating your liver."

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **ICD-10 Codes:** M10.9 (Gout, unspecified), E84.0 (Hyperuricemia).
*   **PA Tips:** Almost all payers require a Prior Authorization for Uloric (febuxostat). [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Standard PA template]. You must document:
    1. Patient has symptomatic gout.
    2. Patient had an inadequate response to maximally titrated allopurinol OR has a documented contraindication/intolerance to allopurinol (e.g., severe rash, HLA-B\*5801 positive).

### Decision Tree: Initiating Urate-Lowering Therapy (ULT)

1.  **Does the patient meet indications for ULT?**
    *   *Yes:* ≥1 subcutaneous tophi, radiographic damage from gout, OR frequent flares (≥2/year).
    *   *Conditional:* >1 flare/year, first flare with CKD stage ≥3, SU >9.0, or urolithiasis.
    *   *No:* Asymptomatic hyperuricemia (Do not treat solely for CKD progression).
2.  **Is the patient of Han Chinese, Korean, Thai, or African American descent?**
    *   *Yes:* Check HLA-B\*5801.
        *   If Positive: Start **Uloric (febuxostat)**.
        *   If Negative: Start **Zyloprim (allopurinol)**.
    *   *No:* Start **Zyloprim (allopurinol)**.
3.  **Does the patient have high cardiovascular risk / history of CVD?**
    *   Strongly prefer **Zyloprim (allopurinol)**.
4.  **Is the patient on Azathioprine?**
    *   Avoid both if possible. If ULT is mandatory, use **Zyloprim (allopurinol)** with a 75% AZA dose reduction and extreme CBC monitoring. **Uloric (febuxostat)** is strictly contraindicated.

### Decision Tree: Escalation and Switching

*   **Escalation:** If SU remains > 6.0 mg/dL after 2-5 weeks, titrate XOI dose up. 
    *   *Allopurinol:* Increase by 50-100 mg increments. (Ensure flare prophylaxis is active).
    *   *Febuxostat:* Increase from 40 mg to 80 mg.
*   **When to Switch:**
    *   Switch from Allopurinol to Febuxostat if: Patient develops a rash/hypersensitivity, or fails to reach SU target despite maximum tolerated allopurinol dose.
    *   Switch from Febuxostat to Allopurinol if: Patient develops LFT elevations >3x ULN, experiences a CV event, or cannot afford febuxostat.
*   **How to Switch:** Direct switch is acceptable. Stop Drug A one day, start Drug B the next day. **Ensure the patient is on flare prophylaxis (colchicine 0.6 mg daily/BID or NSAID) during the switch.**
