# Biguanides Clinical Monograph

## Glucophage (metformin)

### Mechanism of Action
Glucophage (metformin) is an antihyperglycemic agent belonging to the biguanide class. It lowers both basal and postprandial plasma glucose via three primary mechanisms:
1. **Hepatic:** Decreases hepatic glucose production (gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis) via activation of AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK).
2. **Intestinal:** Decreases intestinal absorption of glucose.
3. **Peripheral:** Improves insulin sensitivity by increasing peripheral glucose uptake and utilization, primarily in skeletal muscle.
*Note: Glucophage (metformin) does not stimulate insulin secretion and therefore rarely causes hypoglycemia when used as monotherapy.*

### Indications in Nephrology
**FDA-Approved Indications:**
*   Adjunct to diet and exercise to improve glycemic control in adults and pediatric patients (10 years and older) with Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM).

**Nephrology/CKD Context & Guidelines:**
*   **KDIGO 2022 Clinical Practice Guideline for Diabetes Management in CKD:** Recommends Glucophage (metformin) combined with an SGLT2 inhibitor as **first-line therapy** for patients with T2DM, CKD, and an eGFR ≥30 mL/min/1.73 m².
*   **ADA 2024 Standards of Care:** Aligns with KDIGO, emphasizing Glucophage (metformin) as foundational therapy for T2DM, provided eGFR is ≥30 mL/min/1.73 m². Target HbA1c is generally <7.0% (or individualized based on hypoglycemia risk/life expectancy), and target UACR is <30 mg/g.

**Off-Label Uses:**
*   Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)
*   Prevention of T2DM in high-risk patients (prediabetes)
*   Gestational diabetes

### Dosing
Glucophage (metformin) is available in Immediate-Release (IR), Extended-Release (ER/XR), and oral solution formulations. 

**Standard Dosing & Titration (Normal Renal Function):**
*   **IR Formulation:** Start 500 mg PO once or twice daily with meals. Titrate by 500 mg weekly. Maximum clinical dose: 2000–2550 mg/day (given in divided doses).
*   **ER Formulation:** Start 500 mg PO once daily with the evening meal. Titrate by 500 mg weekly. Maximum dose: 2000 mg/day.

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range (CRITICAL):**
*Glucophage (metformin) dosing is strictly dictated by eGFR, not serum creatinine.*

| eGFR Range (mL/min/1.73 m²) | Dosing Recommendation & Action |
| :--- | :--- |
| **eGFR ≥60** | **No adjustment necessary.** Max dose: 2000–2550 mg/day. |
| **eGFR 45–59** | **Continue use.** Max dose: 2000 mg/day. Monitor eGFR every 3–6 months. |
| **eGFR 30–44** | **DO NOT INITIATE new therapy.** <br>If already taking: **Reduce dose by 50%** (Max dose: 1000 mg/day). Monitor eGFR every 3 months. Assess risk/benefit of continuing. |
| **eGFR <30** | **CONTRAINDICATED.** Discontinue immediately. |
| **Dialysis (HD/PD)** | **CONTRAINDICATED.** Discontinue immediately. |

**Hepatic Adjustments:**
*   Avoid use in patients with clinical or laboratory evidence of hepatic disease due to a significantly increased risk of lactic acidosis.

### Monitoring
**Baseline Labs Required:**
*   eGFR and Serum Creatinine
*   Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c)
*   Liver Function Tests (LFTs)
*   Hemoglobin/Hematocrit
*   Vitamin B12 levels (especially in patients with peripheral neuropathy or anemia)

**Follow-up Lab Schedule:**
*   **eGFR:** Annually if normal; every 3–6 months if eGFR 45-59; every 3 months if eGFR 30-44.
*   **HbA1c:** Every 3 months until at target, then every 6 months.
*   **Vitamin B12:** Every 1–2 years, or if peripheral neuropathy/anemia develops.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
**Common Side Effects (>5%):**
*   Gastrointestinal: Diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, flatulence, abdominal discomfort (occurs in up to 30% of patients; usually transient and dose-dependent).
*   Asthenia/fatigue.
*   Metallic taste.

**Serious/Rare Side Effects:**
*   Vitamin B12 deficiency (long-term use interferes with B12 absorption).
*   Hypoglycemia (rare, usually only when combined with sulfonylureas or insulin).

**BLACK BOX WARNING:**
*   **Lactic Acidosis:** Postmarketing cases of Glucophage (metformin)-associated lactic acidosis have resulted in death, hypothermia, hypotension, and resistant bradyarrhythmias. 
*   *Risk Factors:* Renal impairment, concomitant use of certain drugs (e.g., carbonic anhydrase inhibitors), age ≥65, radiological studies with contrast, surgery, hypoxic states, excessive alcohol intake, and hepatic impairment.

**Contraindications:**
*   Severe renal impairment (eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73 m²).
*   Known hypersensitivity to metformin.
*   Acute or chronic metabolic acidosis, including diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).

**CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:**
*   **"Sick Day" Rules:** Patients must be instructed to HOLD Glucophage (metformin) during acute illnesses that cause dehydration (vomiting, severe diarrhea) to prevent Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) and subsequent lactic acidosis. ### Drug Interactions
*   **Iodinated Contrast Media:** Increases risk of AKI and subsequent metformin accumulation/lactic acidosis. 
    *   *Protocol:* **Hold Glucophage (metformin)** at the time of or prior to imaging IF the patient has an eGFR 30-60, a history of hepatic disease, alcoholism, heart failure, or will receive intra-arterial contrast. Re-evaluate eGFR 48 hours post-procedure; restart only if renal function is stable. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Contrast hold orders for outpatient imaging].
*   **OCT2 / MATE1 Inhibitors (e.g., dolutegravir, cimetidine, ranolazine, vandetanib):** Can decrease renal clearance of metformin, increasing plasma concentrations and lactic acidosis risk. Consider metformin dose reduction.
*   **Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors (e.g., topiramate, zonisamide, acetazolamide):** May decrease serum bicarbonate and induce non-anion gap, hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis. Concomitant use increases the risk of lactic acidosis.
*   **Food Interactions:** Take with food to decrease GI upset. Alcohol increases the risk of lactic acidosis and should be minimized/avoided.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Generally considered safe (historically Category B), but insulin is the preferred treatment for T2DM and gestational diabetes in pregnancy per ACOG/ADA guidelines. Metformin crosses the placenta.
*   **Elderly:** Aging is associated with declining renal function. Do not titrate to maximum doses in patients ≥65 years old without carefully assessing eGFR. Avoid initiation in patients ≥80 years old unless normal renal function is documented.
*   **Transplant:** Can be used in kidney transplant recipients if eGFR >30. However, extreme caution is required due to Calcineurin Inhibitor (CNI)-induced hemodynamic fluctuations in eGFR. Monitor eGFR frequently.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Administration:** Always take this medication with meals to reduce stomach upset. If taking the Extended-Release (ER) tablet, swallow it whole—do not crush, chew, or cut it.
*   **GI Symptoms:** Nausea and diarrhea are very common when starting but usually go away after 1-2 weeks. If severe vomiting or diarrhea occurs, stop taking the medication and call the clinic.
*   **Ghost Pills:** If taking the ER formulation, you may notice the empty shell of the pill in your stool. This is normal and the medicine has already been absorbed.
*   **Sick Day Management:** Stop taking Glucophage (metformin) if you are sick and cannot keep fluids down, or if you are scheduled for a CT scan with IV contrast dye.
*   **Warning Signs to Report:** Seek immediate emergency care if you experience unusual muscle pain, trouble breathing, unexplained stomach pain, unusual sleepiness, or feeling cold/dizzy (signs of lactic acidosis).

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **Common ICD-10 Codes:**
    *   E11.22 (Type 2 diabetes mellitus with diabetic chronic kidney disease)
    *   E11.9 (Type 2 diabetes mellitus without complications)
    *   N18.30 - N18.32 (CKD Stage 3)
*   **CPT Codes:** 99213, 99214, 99215 (Standard E&M codes for CKD/DM management).
*   **PA & Cost Considerations:** Glucophage (metformin) is universally a Tier 1 generic medication. Prior authorization is almost never required. It is frequently available on $4 generic lists at major pharmacies.

## Class Comparison: Biguanide Formulations

Because Glucophage (metformin) is the only biguanide currently available for clinical use, the primary clinical decision involves choosing between the Immediate-Release (IR) and Extended-Release (ER) formulations, or determining when to switch.

| Feature | Glucophage (metformin IR) | Glucophage XR (metformin ER) | Riomet (metformin oral solution) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Starting Dose** | 500 mg PO QD or BID | 500 mg PO QD (with evening meal) | 500 mg (5 mL) PO QD or BID |
| **Max Dose** | 2000–2550 mg/day (divided) | 2000 mg/day (single or divided) | 2550 mg/day (25.5 mL) |
| **eGFR Thresholds** | Stop if <30; Max 1000mg if 30-44 | Stop if <30; Max 1000mg if 30-44 | Stop if <30; Max 1000mg if 30-44 |
| **GI Tolerability** | Moderate (up to 30% experience GI upset) | High (significantly lower rates of diarrhea/nausea) | Moderate |
| **Cost** | Extremely Low ($) | Low ($) | High ($$$) |
| **Pill Burden** | Usually BID | Usually QD | N/A (Liquid) |

### Decision Tree: Choosing and Switching Formulations

**1. When to Start Therapy:**
*   *Patient has T2DM + eGFR ≥45:* Start Glucophage (metformin IR) 500 mg daily with largest meal. Titrate slowly over 4 weeks to target 1000 mg BID.
*   *Patient has T2DM + eGFR 30-44:* Do NOT initiate. Choose alternative agent (e.g., SGLT2i, GLP-1 RA, or DPP4i).

**2. When to Switch to Extended-Release (ER):**
*   *Indication for Switch:* Patient experiences persistent nausea, diarrhea, or abdominal cramping on IR formulation after 2 weeks of therapy, OR patient has poor adherence due to BID dosing.
*   *How to Switch:* Convert at the same total daily dose. (e.g., If taking IR 500 mg BID, switch to ER 1000 mg once daily with the evening meal).

**3. When to Stop or De-escalate Therapy:**
*   *eGFR drops to 30-44 mL/min:* Reduce total daily dose to a maximum of 1000 mg/day (e.g., 500 mg BID or 1000 mg ER QD).
*   *eGFR drops <30 mL/min:* Discontinue immediately. Transition to renal-safe alternatives (e.g., Tradjenta (linagliptin), GLP-1 RA, or insulin).
*   *Patient develops advanced heart failure or severe liver disease:* Discontinue due to elevated lactic acidosis risk.
