# Clinical Drug Monograph: Uricase Enzymes

**Target Audience:** Nephrologists, Advanced Practice Providers (APPs), and Clinical Staff at The Kidney Experts (TKE).
**Reference Standard:** American College of Rheumatology (ACR) 2020 Guidelines for the Management of Gout; KDIGO 2024 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of CKD.

---

## Krystexxa (pegloticase)

### Mechanism of Action
- **Target/Receptor:** Recombinant, PEGylated uricase (urate oxidase) enzyme.
- **Action:** Humans lack the uricase enzyme. Krystexxa (pegloticase) catalyzes the oxidation of uric acid into allantoin, a highly water-soluble, inert metabolite that is easily excreted by the kidneys. This rapidly and profoundly lowers serum uric acid (sUA) levels and dissolves existing urate crystal deposits (tophi).

### Indications in Nephrology
- **FDA-Approved Indication:** Treatment of chronic gout in adult patients refractory to conventional therapy (failure to normalize sUA and control symptoms with maximum medically appropriate doses of xanthine oxidase inhibitors).
- **Off-Label Uses:** Occasionally utilized in solid organ transplant (e.g., kidney transplant) recipients with severe, tophaceous gout who cannot tolerate standard therapies due to drug interactions (e.g., allopurinol/febuxostat interacting with azathioprine).
- **Key Clinical Trials:**
  - **MIRROR RCT (2022):** Evaluated Krystexxa (pegloticase) co-administered with methotrexate (MTX) vs. Krystexxa monotherapy. **Outcomes:** 71% of patients on co-therapy achieved sustained sUA <6 mg/dL at month 6, compared to 38.5% on monotherapy. Infusion reactions were significantly lower in the co-therapy group (4.2% vs. 30.6%).

### Dosing
**Standard Dosing & Administration:**
- **Dose:** 8 mg given as an intravenous (IV) infusion over no less than 120 minutes, every 2 weeks.
- **Co-Therapy (Standard of Care per MIRROR trial):** Methotrexate 15 mg orally once weekly + Folic Acid 1 mg daily. *Note: See CKD adjustments for MTX below.*
- **Premedication (Mandatory):** Antihistamine (e.g., fexofenadine), acetaminophen, and IV corticosteroid (e.g., hydrocortisone 200 mg) immediately prior to infusion.
- **Gout Flare Prophylaxis:** Initiate NSAID or colchicine at least 1 week prior to starting Krystexxa (pegloticase) and continue for at least 6 months.

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range (CRITICAL):**
*Note: Krystexxa (pegloticase) itself does NOT require renal dose adjustment. However, the required co-therapies (Methotrexate and Colchicine) DO require strict adjustments.*

| eGFR Range (mL/min/1.73m²) | Krystexxa (pegloticase) Dose | Co-Therapy (Methotrexate) Adjustment | Prophylaxis (Colchicine) Adjustment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **≥ 60** | 8 mg IV q2 weeks | 15 mg PO weekly | 0.6 mg PO daily or BID |
| **30 - 59** | 8 mg IV q2 weeks | Reduce MTX dose by 50% (7.5 mg weekly) | 0.3 mg PO daily |

### Monitoring
**Baseline Labs Required:**
- **G6PD Level:** **MANDATORY.** Must be resulted before the first infusion.
- Serum Uric Acid (sUA)
- CBC, CMP (specifically assessing baseline hepatic and renal function for co-therapies)

**Follow-Up Lab Schedule:**
- **sUA:** Must be drawn within 48 hours *prior to every single infusion*.
- **Target:** sUA < 6.0 mg/dL (often drops to < 1.0 mg/dL on therapy).
- **Decision Tree (STOP Therapy):** If sUA is > 6.0 mg/dL on **two consecutive measurements**, DISCONTINUE Krystexxa (pegloticase). A rising sUA indicates the development of anti-pegloticase antibodies, which drastically increases the risk of anaphylaxis and renders the drug ineffective.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
**Black Box Warnings (BBW):**
1. **Anaphylaxis and Infusion Reactions:** Occurs in ~6.5% of patients. Highest risk is in patients whose sUA rises > 6 mg/dL. Must be administered in a healthcare setting prepared to manage anaphylaxis.
2. **G6PD Deficiency Associated Hemolysis and Methemoglobinemia:** Absolute contraindication. Screen all patients at baseline.

**Common Side Effects (>5%):**
- Gout flares (up to 74% in early treatment due to rapid mobilization of tissue urate)
- Infusion reactions (urticaria, dyspnea, chest discomfort)
- Nausea, vomiting, constipation
- Contusion/ecchymosis

**Contraindications:**
- G6PD deficiency (Absolute)
- History of severe hypersensitivity to Krystexxa (pegloticase) (Absolute)

**CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:**
- **Fluid Overload:** The infusion volume and required normal saline flushes may pose a volume risk in anuric ESRD or severe CHF patients.
- **Allantoin Accumulation:** Allantoin is renally excreted. In severe CKD/ESRD, allantoin will accumulate. While currently believed to be non-toxic and dialyzable, long-term effects of massive allantoin accumulation in ESRD are not fully characterized.
- **Co-therapy Toxicity:** Methotrexate toxicity (myelosuppression, mucositis) is highly likely if used in eGFR <30.

### Drug Interactions
- **Oral Urate-Lowering Therapies (ULTs):** **CONTRAINDICATED.** Allopurinol, Uloric (febuxostat), and probenecid MUST be discontinued before starting Krystexxa (pegloticase).
  - *Why?* Oral ULTs will artificially lower the sUA, masking the rise in sUA that serves as the critical warning sign for anti-pegloticase antibody development and impending anaphylaxis.
- **Pegylated Drugs:** Potential for cross-reactive anti-PEG antibodies.

### Special Populations
- **Pregnancy:** Category C (under old system). No adequate data. Generally avoid unless absolutely necessary.
- **Elderly:** No dose adjustment needed for Krystexxa (pegloticase), but extreme caution required with MTX and colchicine co-therapies due to age-related renal decline.
- **Transplant Recipients:** Often already on anti-rejection medications (mycophenolate, tacrolimus) which naturally suppress anti-pegloticase antibodies, similar to MTX. Co-administration with MTX is usually unnecessary and contraindicated due to over-immunosuppression. Coordinate closely with the transplant center.

### Patient Counseling Points
- **The "Gout Flare" Paradox:** Explain that gout attacks will likely get *worse* before they get better during the first 3-6 months. This means the drug is working to dissolve deep crystal deposits. Do not stop the infusion if a flare occurs.
- **Medication Adherence:** Emphasize the critical importance of taking the weekly Methotrexate (if prescribed) to prevent allergic reactions to the infusion.
- **Warning Signs:** Instruct patients to report any rash, wheezing, lip swelling, or chest pain immediately during or after the infusion.
- **Discontinuation of Old Meds:** Remind patients to completely stop their allopurinol or Uloric (febuxostat).

### Prior Authorization & Coding
- **Common ICD-10 Codes:**
  - M1A.0--- (Idiopathic chronic gout)
  - M1A.3--- (Chronic gout due to renal impairment)
  - *Note: Must specify with/without tophi and specific joint involvement for highest specificity.*
- **CPT Code:** J2507 (Injection, pegloticase, 1 mg). *Billing note: An 8 mg dose equals 8 billing units.*
- **Tips for PA Success:**
  - Document baseline sUA > 8 mg/dL.
  - Document presence of symptomatic gout (≥3 flares/year, or ≥1 tophus, or chronic gouty arthritis).
  - Explicitly document failure, contraindication, or intolerance to maximum medically appropriate doses of BOTH allopurinol and Uloric (febuxostat).
  - Include the negative G6PD lab result in the PA packet.

## Class Comparison: Uricase Enzymes

While Krystexxa (pegloticase) is the primary uricase enzyme used in outpatient nephrology/rheumatology for chronic gout, Elitek (rasburicase) is another uricase enzyme used strictly in the inpatient setting for Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS). 

| Feature | Krystexxa (pegloticase) | Elitek (rasburicase) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Indication** | Chronic refractory tophaceous gout | Hyperuricemia associated with malignancy / Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS) |
| **Setting** | Outpatient Infusion Center | Inpatient / ICU |
| **Structure** | PEGylated recombinant uricase (longer half-life) | Non-PEGylated recombinant uricase (short half-life) |
| **Standard Dosing** | 8 mg IV every 2 weeks | 0.2 mg/kg IV daily for up to 5 days |
| **eGFR Adjustments** | None required | None required |
| **Duration of Therapy** | Chronic (months to years) | Acute (1 to 5 days) |
| **Immunogenicity** | High (requires MTX co-therapy to prevent antibodies) | High (but short duration of use limits clinical impact) |
| **G6PD Screening** | Mandatory prior to start | Mandatory prior to start |

### Clinical Decision Making
- **When to choose Krystexxa (pegloticase):** Use in advanced CKD patients with severe, debilitating, tophaceous gout who continue to have flares and sUA > 6.0 mg/dL despite maximized xanthine oxidase inhibitors (or in those who cannot take XOIs due to severe allergy/HLA-B*5801 positivity).
- **Switching/Stopping:** If Krystexxa (pegloticase) fails (sUA rises > 6.0 mg/dL on two consecutive checks), the drug must be permanently discontinued. The patient should be transitioned back to the highest tolerated dose of a xanthine oxidase inhibitor (allopurinol or febuxostat) in combination with a uricosuric (if eGFR > 30) or anti-inflammatory prophylaxis.
