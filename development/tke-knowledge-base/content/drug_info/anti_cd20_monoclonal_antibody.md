## Rituxan (rituximab)

**Aliases:** RTX, anti-CD20

### Mechanism of Action
Rituxan (rituximab) is a chimeric murine/human monoclonal IgG1 kappa antibody directed against the CD20 antigen found on the surface of pre-B and mature B lymphocytes. It mediates B-cell lysis through complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC), antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC), and induction of apoptosis. It does not affect hematopoietic stem cells or plasma cells (which lack CD20).

### Indications in Nephrology
**FDA-Approved Indications:**
*   **ANCA-Associated Vasculitis (AAV):** Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA) and Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA) in combination with glucocorticoids.

**Off-Label Uses (Common in Nephrology per KDIGO 2021/2024):**
*   **Primary Membranous Nephropathy (MN):** First-line therapy for moderate to high-risk patients.
*   **Minimal Change Disease (MCD) / Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS):** Frequently relapsing or steroid-dependent disease.
*   **Lupus Nephritis (LN):** Refractory disease or as part of a multi-target regimen.
*   **Antibody-Mediated Rejection (AMR):** Kidney transplant rejection.

**Key Supporting Clinical Trials:**
*   **RAVE Trial (2010):** Demonstrated Rituxan (rituximab) is non-inferior to daily cyclophosphamide for remission induction in severe AAV, and superior in relapsing disease (64% vs 39% remission at 6 months).
*   **MENTOR Trial (2019):** Rituxan (rituximab) was non-inferior to cyclosporine at 12 months and superior at 24 months for maintaining remission in Membranous Nephropathy (60% vs 20% complete/partial remission at 24 months).

### Dosing
*Note: Premedication with acetaminophen, an antihistamine (e.g., diphenhydramine), and a glucocorticoid (e.g., methylprednisolone 100 mg IV) is required 30-60 minutes prior to each infusion. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED] for specific infusion center premedication orders.*

**Standard Dosing:**
*   **AAV Induction:** 375 mg/m² IV weekly for 4 weeks.
*   **AAV Maintenance:** 500 mg IV every 6 months (starting 16-24 weeks after induction).
*   **Membranous Nephropathy / Glomerular Diseases (Rheumatoid Arthritis protocol):** 1000 mg IV on Days 1 and 15. Repeat at 6 months if B-cells repopulate or based on clinical response (e.g., PLA2R antibody levels).

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
Rituxan (rituximab) is a large protein molecule cleared via the reticuloendothelial system, not renally excreted.
*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min:** No adjustment
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min:** No adjustment
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min:** No adjustment
*   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** No adjustment (Not removed by hemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis)

### Monitoring
**Baseline Labs (Required before starting):**
*   Hepatitis B panel (HBsAg, HBcAb) — *Absolute requirement due to BBW*
*   Hepatitis C antibody
*   Tuberculosis screening (QuantiFERON-TB Gold)
*   Quantitative Immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM)
*   CBC with differential
*   Disease-specific biomarkers (e.g., ANCA titers, anti-PLA2R, anti-dsDNA)

**Follow-up Lab Schedule:**
*   **CD19/CD20 B-cell counts:** Check 1-2 months post-infusion to confirm depletion (Target: <5 cells/microL or <1% of lymphocytes). Monitor every 3-6 months to guide re-dosing.
*   **Quantitative IgG:** Check every 6 months. Monitor for hypogammaglobulinemia (IgG <400 mg/dL increases severe infection risk).
*   **CBC with diff:** Monthly for 3-6 months, then every 3 months (monitor for late-onset neutropenia).

### Adverse Effects & Safety
**Common Side Effects (>5%):**
*   Infusion-related reactions (fever, chills, rigors, pruritus) — most common during the first infusion.
*   Infections (URI, UTI).
*   Asthenia/fatigue.

**Serious/Rare Side Effects:**
*   Late-onset neutropenia (weeks to months after therapy).
*   Hypogammaglobulinemia requiring IVIG replacement.

**Black Box Warnings (BBW):**
1.  **Fatal Infusion Reactions:** Usually occur within 24 hours of the first infusion.
2.  **Severe Mucocutaneous Reactions:** Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS), Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN).
3.  **Hepatitis B Virus (HBV) Reactivation:** Can result in fulminant hepatitis, hepatic failure, and death.
4.  **Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy (PML):** Rare, fatal JC virus CNS infection.

**Contraindications:**
*   Absolute: Known Type I hypersensitivity/anaphylaxis to murine proteins or rituximab. Active severe infections. Active HBV infection.
*   Relative: Severe baseline hypogammaglobulinemia (IgG <400 mg/dL).

**CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:**
*   Patients with nephrotic syndrome may excrete the monoclonal antibody in the urine, potentially lowering serum half-life and requiring closer monitoring of CD19 counts and earlier re-dosing.

### Drug Interactions
*   **Live Vaccines:** Contraindicated during and for at least 6 months after therapy. Administer required live vaccines ≥4 weeks prior to starting.
*   **Other Immunosuppressants:** Additive risk of fatal infections.
*   **Antihypertensives:** May exacerbate hypotension during infusion reactions. Consider holding BP meds 12 hours prior to infusion [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED].

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Avoid unless life-threatening. Crosses the placenta and causes B-cell depletion in the neonate. Use effective contraception during and for 12 months after the last dose.
*   **Elderly:** Higher risk of severe infections and cardiac arrhythmias during infusion.
*   **Transplant:** Used in desensitization and AMR; requires strict opportunistic infection prophylaxis (PJP, CMV).

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Infusion Reactions:** The first infusion takes the longest (4-6 hours) and has the highest risk of reactions (chills, fever, rash). Subsequent infusions are usually faster and better tolerated.
*   **Infection Risk:** Wash hands frequently, avoid sick contacts. Report any fever, chills, or persistent cough immediately.
*   **Vaccines:** Do not receive any "live" vaccines (e.g., MMR, yellow fever). Non-live vaccines (flu, COVID, pneumococcal) should ideally be given 4 weeks before the infusion, as the drug blunts vaccine response for ~6 months.
*   **Warning Signs:** Report any new neurological symptoms (confusion, dizziness, vision changes, weakness) immediately, as this could be a sign of a rare brain infection (PML).

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **CPT/HCPCS Codes:** J9312 (Rituxan 10 mg). *Note: J9311 is for Rituxan Hycela (subcutaneous), which is generally restricted to oncology.*
*   **Common ICD-10 Codes:** M31.3 (GPA), M31.7 (MPA), N04.x (Nephrotic syndrome), M32.14 (Lupus nephritis).
*   **PA Tips:** For off-label uses (MN, MCD, LN), explicitly cite KDIGO guidelines and document failure, intolerance, or contraindication to standard therapies (e.g., cyclophosphamide, calcineurin inhibitors).

## Gazyva (obinutuzumab)

**Aliases:** anti-CD20

### Mechanism of Action
Gazyva (obinutuzumab) is a humanized, Type II anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody. Compared to Rituxan (a Type I antibody), Gazyva binds to a different epitope on the CD20 molecule, resulting in greater direct cell death and enhanced antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC), with less complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC). This makes it highly effective in depleting B-cells, even in patients who have developed anti-rituximab antibodies or have rituximab-resistant disease.

### Indications in Nephrology
**FDA-Approved Indications:**
*   Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia (CLL) and Follicular Lymphoma (Oncology only).

**Off-Label Uses (Emerging in Nephrology):**
*   **Refractory Lupus Nephritis (LN):** For patients failing standard therapy or rituximab.
*   **Refractory Primary Membranous Nephropathy (MN):** For patients with persistent anti-PLA2R antibodies despite rituximab.
*   **Rituximab Intolerance:** Patients who developed anti-chimeric antibodies (human anti-chimeric antibodies - HACA) to rituximab.

**Key Supporting Clinical Trials:**
*   **NOBILITY Trial (2022):** Phase 2 trial in proliferative Lupus Nephritis. Gazyva (obinutuzumab) + standard of care (MMF/steroids) showed superior complete renal response at 104 weeks compared to placebo + SOC (41% vs 23%).

### Dosing
*Note: Premedication is strictly required and more intensive than rituximab due to higher rates of severe infusion reactions. Requires IV glucocorticoid, acetaminophen, and antihistamine. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED] for specific Gazyva premedication and step-up infusion rates.*

**Standard Dosing (Off-label Nephrology Protocols):**
*   **Lupus Nephritis / Membranous Nephropathy:** 1000 mg IV on Days 1 and 15.
*   *Note: Oncology dosing is different (1000 mg on days 1, 8, 15 of cycle 1), but nephrology typically uses the Day 1/Day 15 rheumatoid-style dosing.*

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
Like rituximab, it is cleared via the reticuloendothelial system.
*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min:** No adjustment
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min:** No adjustment
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min:** No adjustment
*   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** No adjustment

### Monitoring
**Baseline Labs (Required before starting):**
*   Hepatitis B panel (HBsAg, HBcAb) — *Absolute requirement*
*   Hepatitis C, QuantiFERON-TB Gold
*   Quantitative Immunoglobulins (IgG)
*   CBC with differential

**Follow-up Lab Schedule:**
*   **CD19/CD20 B-cell counts:** Check 1-2 months post-infusion. Gazyva often achieves deeper tissue B-cell depletion than rituximab.
*   **CBC with diff:** Monitor closely for neutropenia (higher incidence than rituximab). Check weekly for the first month, then monthly.
*   **Quantitative IgG:** Every 6 months.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
**Common Side Effects (>5%):**
*   **Infusion-related reactions (IRRs):** Very common (up to 65% in oncology trials), especially during the first 1000 mg dose. Can include hypotension, tachycardia, dyspnea.
*   Neutropenia (can be prolonged).
*   Upper respiratory tract infections.

**Serious/Rare Side Effects:**
*   Tumor Lysis Syndrome (rare in non-oncology settings).
*   Thrombocytopenia (acute).

**Black Box Warnings (BBW):**
1.  **Hepatitis B Virus (HBV) Reactivation:** Can be fatal.
2.  **Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy (PML):** Rare, fatal JC virus CNS infection.

**Contraindications:**
*   Absolute: Known hypersensitivity to obinutuzumab. Active HBV. Active severe infection.

**CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:**
*   Fluid overload during management of infusion reactions (which require IV fluids) must be carefully managed in oliguric/anuric ESRD patients.

### Drug Interactions
*   **Live Vaccines:** Contraindicated.
*   **Immunosuppressants:** Additive infection risk.
*   **Antihypertensives:** High risk of hypotension during the first infusion. Hold BP medications 12 hours prior to infusion [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED].

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Avoid. Causes fetal B-cell depletion. Use contraception during and for 6 months after therapy.
*   **Elderly:** Increased risk of severe adverse events and infections.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **First Infusion Warning:** The first infusion has a very high chance of causing a reaction (fever, chills, drop in blood pressure). You will be monitored very closely, and the infusion will start very slowly.
*   **Infection Risk:** This drug suppresses the immune system deeply. Report fevers, chills, or unusual symptoms immediately.
*   **Vaccines:** No live vaccines. Get necessary vaccines 4 weeks before starting therapy.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **CPT/HCPCS Codes:** J9301 (Gazyva 10 mg).
*   **Common ICD-10 Codes:** M32.14 (Lupus nephritis), N04.x (Nephrotic syndrome).
*   **PA Tips:** Gazyva is almost exclusively off-label in nephrology. PA success requires detailed documentation of Rituxan (rituximab) failure, development of anti-chimeric antibodies, or refractory Lupus Nephritis citing the NOBILITY trial. Peer-to-peer review is frequently required.

## Class Comparison & Decision Tree

### Anti-CD20 Agents Comparison Table

| Feature | Rituxan (rituximab) | Gazyva (obinutuzumab) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Antibody Type** | Chimeric (Murine/Human) Type I | Humanized Type II |
| **Mechanism Focus** | CDC > ADCC | ADCC > CDC (Deeper tissue depletion) |
| **Standard Nephrology Dosing** | 1000 mg IV Days 1 & 15 <br>*OR* 375 mg/m² weekly x 4 | 1000 mg IV Days 1 & 15 |
| **eGFR Thresholds** | No adjustments needed | No adjustments needed |
| **Key Nephrology Trials** | MENTOR (MN), RAVE (AAV) | NOBILITY (Lupus Nephritis) |
| **Infusion Reactions** | Common (~30% first dose) | Very Common (>60% first dose) |
| **Cost / Access** | Moderate (Biosimilars available) | High (Brand only, difficult PA) |

### Decision Tree: When to Choose, Escalate, or Switch

**1. Initial Therapy (When to Start):**
*   **Rituxan (rituximab)** is the **first-line** anti-CD20 agent for all nephrology indications (AAV, MN, MCD, LN).
*   Always utilize rituximab first due to guideline support (KDIGO), extensive safety data, and easier prior authorization (availability of biosimilars like Ruxience, Truxima).

**2. When to Escalate/Re-dose:**
*   Monitor CD19 counts every 3-6 months.
*   If CD19 >5 cells/microL (or >1%) AND clinical disease is active (e.g., rising anti-PLA2R, rising proteinuria, rising ANCA), **re-dose Rituxan**.
*   If CD19 remains depleted but disease is active, consider switching drug classes (e.g., add CNI, cyclophosphamide, or voclosporin for LN).

**3. When to Switch to Gazyva (obinutuzumab):**
*   **Anti-Drug Antibodies:** If a patient develops severe allergic reactions to rituximab or loses efficacy rapidly, check for Human Anti-Chimeric Antibodies (HACA). If positive, switch to the humanized **Gazyva**.
*   **Refractory Disease:** If a patient has persistent CD19 B-cells or persistent clinical disease (e.g., refractory Lupus Nephritis) despite adequate rituximab dosing, switch to **Gazyva** for deeper tissue B-cell depletion.

**4. When to Stop Therapy:**
*   Severe, recurrent infections or profound, persistent hypogammaglobulinemia (IgG <400 mg/dL) requiring IVIG.
*   Development of Hepatitis B reactivation or PML.
*   Pregnancy.
