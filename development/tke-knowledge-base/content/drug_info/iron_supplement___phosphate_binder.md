# Iron-Based Phosphate Binders & Iron Supplements

This clinical monograph provides comprehensive reference information for the use of iron-based phosphate binders, specifically focusing on **Auryxia (ferric citrate)**, in the management of hyperphosphatemia and iron deficiency anemia (IDA) in chronic kidney disease (CKD). 

All recommendations align with the **KDIGO 2017 Clinical Practice Guideline Update for the Diagnosis, Evaluation, Prevention, and Treatment of Chronic Kidney Disease–Mineral and Bone Disorder (CKD-MBD)** and the **KDIGO 2012 Clinical Practice Guideline for Anemia in Chronic Kidney Disease**.

---

## Auryxia (ferric citrate)

### Mechanism of Action
Auryxia (ferric citrate) serves a dual mechanism of action depending on the patient's CKD stage and indication:
1. **Phosphate Binder:** In the gastrointestinal tract, the ferric iron binds to dietary phosphate to form ferric phosphate, an insoluble complex that is excreted in the feces, thereby reducing serum phosphorus levels.
2. **Iron Supplement:** The citrate complex allows for the dissociation and absorption of ferric iron in the GI tract. Once absorbed, it incorporates into transferrin and increases systemic iron stores (ferritin) and transferrin saturation (TSAT), promoting erythropoiesis.

### Indications in Nephrology
**FDA-Approved Indications:**
*   **Hyperphosphatemia:** Control of serum phosphorus levels in adult patients with CKD on dialysis (DD-CKD).
*   **Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA):** Treatment of IDA in adult patients with CKD not on dialysis (NDD-CKD).

**Off-Label Uses:**
*   Management of hyperphosphatemia in NDD-CKD (Note: KDIGO 2017 suggests restricting calcium-based binders across all CKD stages, making non-calcium binders like Auryxia (ferric citrate) a frequent off-label choice for NDD-CKD patients with concurrent hyperphosphatemia and IDA).

**Key Supporting Clinical Trials:**
*   **Phase 3 NDD-CKD Trial (Fishbane et al., 2017):** Demonstrated efficacy in NDD-CKD patients with IDA. 52% of patients receiving Auryxia (ferric citrate) achieved a ≥1 g/dL increase in hemoglobin (Hgb) compared to 19% on placebo.
*   **Phase 3 DD-CKD Trial (Lewis et al., 2015):** Showed non-inferiority to active controls (Renvela [sevelamer carbonate] and PhosLo [calcium acetate]) for phosphorus lowering in ESRD. Additionally, patients on Auryxia (ferric citrate) required significantly less IV iron and erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs).

### Dosing
Auryxia (ferric citrate) is supplied as 1 gram tablets (containing 210 mg of ferric iron). **Must be taken with meals.**

**Standard Dosing & Titration:**
*   **For Hyperphosphatemia (Dialysis):** 
    *   *Starting Dose:* 2 tablets orally three times daily (TID) with meals (6 tablets/day).
    *   *Titration:* Adjust by 1 to 2 tablets per day at intervals of 1 week or longer based on serum phosphorus levels.
    *   *Maximum Dose:* 12 tablets/day.
*   **For IDA (NDD-CKD):**
    *   *Starting Dose:* 1 tablet orally TID with meals (3 tablets/day).
    *   *Titration:* Adjust dose as needed based on Hgb, TSAT, and ferritin.
    *   *Maximum Dose:* 12 tablets/day.

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
*   **eGFR ≥60:** 1 tablet TID with meals (Indicated for IDA).
*   **eGFR 30-59:** 1 tablet TID with meals (Indicated for IDA).
*   **eGFR 15-29:** 1 tablet TID with meals (Indicated for IDA).
*   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** 2 tablets TID with meals (Indicated for Hyperphosphatemia). 
    *   *[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]: Determine local clinic threshold for transitioning NDD-CKD IDA dosing (3 tabs/day) to ESRD Hyperphosphatemia dosing (6 tabs/day) as patients initiate dialysis.*

**Hepatic Adjustments:**
*   No specific dose adjustments provided in the manufacturer labeling.

### Monitoring
**Baseline Labs Required:**
*   Serum phosphorus, corrected calcium, intact PTH (iPTH).
*   CBC (specifically Hemoglobin and Hematocrit).
*   Iron Panel (TSAT, Ferritin).

**Follow-up Lab Schedule:**
*   **Phosphorus:** Check every 1-4 weeks during initial titration, then every 1-3 months once stable.
    *   *Target:* Lower elevated phosphorus toward the normal range (2.5 - 4.5 mg/dL) per KDIGO 2017.
*   **Iron Panel & CBC:** Check monthly during initial therapy, then every 3 months.
    *   *Target (NDD-CKD):* TSAT >20%, Ferritin >100 ng/mL.
    *   *Target (DD-CKD):* TSAT >20%, Ferritin >200 ng/mL. 
    *   *Safety Threshold:* Hold or reduce dose if Ferritin >800-1000 ng/mL or TSAT >50% to prevent iron overload.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
**Common Side Effects (>5%):**
*   Discolored feces (dark/black) (>20%) - *Expected pharmacological effect.*
*   Diarrhea (21%)
*   Constipation (19%)
*   Nausea (11%)
*   Vomiting (7%)

**Serious/Rare Side Effects:**
*   Iron overload (hemosiderosis), particularly in dialysis patients receiving concurrent IV iron therapy.
*   Gastrointestinal bleeding/ulceration (rare, but iron can be caustic to GI mucosa).

**Black Box Warnings:**
*   None for adults. (Standard warning for all iron products: Accidental overdose of iron-containing products is a leading cause of fatal poisoning in children under 6).

**Contraindications:**
*   **Absolute:** Iron overload syndromes (e.g., hemochromatosis).
*   **Relative:** Active severe gastrointestinal inflammatory conditions (e.g., severe IBD, active peptic ulcer disease).

**CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:**
*   **Dialysis Iron Overload:** Dialysis patients are frequently on IV iron and ESAs. The systemic absorption of iron from Auryxia (ferric citrate) requires proactive reduction or cessation of IV iron to prevent toxic iron accumulation.

### Drug Interactions
Auryxia (ferric citrate) can bind to other oral medications in the GI tract, reducing their absorption.
*   **Doxycycline:** Take Auryxia (ferric citrate) at least 1 hour *before* doxycycline.
*   **Ciprofloxacin:** Take Auryxia (ferric citrate) at least 2 hours *before or after* ciprofloxacin.
*   **Levothyroxine (Synthroid):** Iron significantly decreases levothyroxine absorption. Separate administration by at least 4 hours.
*   **Food Interactions:** Must be taken *with meals* to effectively bind dietary phosphate and to minimize GI upset.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** No well-controlled studies. Iron requirements increase during pregnancy, but maternal iron overload must be avoided. Use only if clearly needed.
*   **Elderly:** No specific dose adjustments required. Monitor for constipation, which is more prevalent in this population.
*   **Transplant:** Not specifically studied. If used off-label for post-transplant hyperphosphatemia or anemia, monitor iron indices closely and separate from immunosuppressants (e.g., mycophenolate, tacrolimus) by at least 2 hours due to theoretical binding risks. *[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]: Standardize separation times for transplant medications.*

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Administration:** "Take this medication exactly as prescribed, strictly *with your meals*. If you skip a meal, skip the dose."
*   **Expected Side Effects:** "Your stool will turn dark or black. This is a normal effect of the iron leaving your body and does not mean you are bleeding internally."
*   **Medication Timing:** "Do not take your thyroid medication (levothyroxine) at the same time as this drug. Separate them by at least 4 hours."
*   **Warning Signs:** "Report severe stomach pain, persistent nausea, severe constipation, or diarrhea that does not go away."

### Prior Authorization & Coding
**Common ICD-10 Codes:**
*   **E83.39:** Other disorders of phosphorus metabolism (Hyperphosphatemia)
*   **D50.9:** Iron deficiency anemia, unspecified
*   **D63.1:** Anemia in chronic kidney disease
*   **N18.4:** Chronic kidney disease, stage 4 (severe)
*   **N18.5:** Chronic kidney disease, stage 5
*   **N18.6:** End-stage renal disease

**Prior Authorization Tips:**
*   For Hyperphosphatemia: Most payers require documented failure, contraindication, or intolerance to generic Renvela (sevelamer carbonate) and/or PhosLo (calcium acetate).
*   For IDA in NDD-CKD: Document failure or severe GI intolerance to generic over-the-counter oral iron supplements (e.g., ferrous sulfate).
*   **Cost Considerations:** High cost tier. Utilize the AkebiaCares patient assistance program for eligible patients.

## Class Comparison: Iron-Based Phosphate Binders

While Auryxia (ferric citrate) is unique in its dual FDA indications, it is clinically compared to **Velphoro (sucroferric oxyhydroxide)**, the other iron-based phosphate binder used in nephrology.

| Feature | Auryxia (ferric citrate) | Velphoro (sucroferric oxyhydroxide) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Mechanism** | Binds phosphate AND absorbs systemic iron | Binds phosphate (minimal systemic iron absorption) |
| **FDA Indications** | Hyperphosphatemia (DD-CKD)<br>IDA (NDD-CKD) | Hyperphosphatemia (DD-CKD) |
| **Iron Content** | 210 mg ferric iron per tablet | 500 mg iron per chewable tablet |
| **Systemic Iron Absorption** | **Yes** (Increases TSAT/Ferritin) | **No** (Clinically insignificant absorption) |
| **Starting Dose (Dialysis)** | 2 tabs TID with meals (6/day) | 1 chewable tab TID with meals (3/day) |
| **Pill Burden / Formulation** | Higher pill burden; Swallow whole | Lower pill burden; Must be chewed |
| **Key Trials** | Fishbane et al. (2017), Lewis et al. (2015) | Floege et al. (2014) |
| **Cost / Access** | High (PA usually required) | High (PA usually required) |

### Decision Tree: Choosing, Escalating, and Switching

**1. When to Choose Auryxia (ferric citrate):**
*   **NDD-CKD:** Patient has eGFR <60 with Iron Deficiency Anemia (TSAT <20%, Ferritin <100) who cannot tolerate OTC oral iron (ferrous sulfate/gluconate) due to GI upset.
*   **DD-CKD:** Patient has hyperphosphatemia AND requires frequent IV iron or high ESA doses. Auryxia can reduce the need for IV iron and ESAs.
*   **Avoid if:** Patient has baseline Ferritin >800 ng/mL or TSAT >50%.

**2. When to Choose Velphoro (sucroferric oxyhydroxide) over Auryxia:**
*   Patient has hyperphosphatemia but already has high iron stores (Ferritin >800 ng/mL) or is at risk for iron overload.
*   Patient struggles with high pill burdens (Velphoro requires fewer pills per day).
*   Patient cannot swallow large pills (Velphoro is chewable).

**3. Escalation & De-escalation:**
*   **Escalate:** If serum phosphorus remains >4.5 mg/dL after 2 weeks, increase dose by 1-2 tablets/day.
*   **De-escalate/Stop:** If Ferritin exceeds 1000 ng/mL or TSAT >50%, hold Auryxia (ferric citrate) and switch to a non-calcium, non-iron binder like Renvela (sevelamer carbonate) or Fosrenol (lanthanum carbonate). *[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]: Define exact ferritin threshold for mandatory Auryxia cessation at TKE clinics.*
