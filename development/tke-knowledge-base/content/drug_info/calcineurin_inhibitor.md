# Calcineurin Inhibitors (CNIs) — Clinical Monograph

Calcineurin inhibitors (CNIs) are potent immunosuppressive agents that form the backbone of solid organ transplant rejection prophylaxis and are increasingly utilized in the management of proteinuric glomerular diseases (e.g., Lupus Nephritis, Membranous Nephropathy, FSGS, Minimal Change Disease). 

---

## Prograf, Envarsus XR, Astagraf XL (tacrolimus)
**Aliases:** FK506, CNI, tac

### Mechanism of Action
Tacrolimus binds to the intracellular protein FKBP-12. This complex inhibits calcineurin, a calcium-dependent phosphatase. Inhibition of calcineurin prevents the dephosphorylation and nuclear translocation of Nuclear Factor of Activated T-cells (NFAT), thereby blocking the transcription of Interleukin-2 (IL-2) and other cytokines required for T-cell activation and proliferation. In podocytes, CNIs also stabilize the actin cytoskeleton by preventing synaptopodin degradation, directly reducing proteinuria independent of immunosuppression.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Prophylaxis of organ rejection in kidney transplant (in combination with other immunosuppressants).
*   **Off-Label (Guideline Supported):** 
    *   Lupus Nephritis (KDIGO 2024: Alternative/combination therapy for active Class III/IV/V).
    *   Primary Membranous Nephropathy (KDIGO 2021: Alternative first-line or second-line therapy).
    *   Minimal Change Disease (MCD) / Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS) (KDIGO 2021: Frequently used for steroid-dependent or steroid-resistant cases).
*   **Key Trials:** 
    *   *MENTOR (2019):* Rituximab vs. Cyclosporine in Membranous Nephropathy (established CNIs have high relapse rates upon discontinuation compared to B-cell depletion).
    *   *AURA-LV (2016):* Multi-target therapy (Tacrolimus + MMF + steroids) showed superior complete remission rates in Lupus Nephritis compared to IV cyclophosphamide.

### Dosing
*Note: Prograf (immediate-release), Envarsus XR (extended-release), and Astagraf XL (extended-release) are **NOT** interchangeable on a 1:1 basis. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED] for specific conversion ratios if switching formulations.*

*   **Glomerular Diseases (Off-label, Prograf IR):** Start 0.05 mg/kg/day divided BID (every 12 hours). Titrate to target trough.
*   **Kidney Transplant (Prograf IR):** Start 0.1 to 0.2 mg/kg/day divided BID.

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
Tacrolimus is hepatically cleared (CYP3A4/5); therefore, baseline eGFR does not dictate the *pharmacokinetic* clearance dose. However, because tacrolimus causes afferent arteriole vasoconstriction (acute nephrotoxicity), dosing is strictly guided by eGFR *changes*.
*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min/1.73m²:** Standard weight-based dosing.
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min/1.73m²:** Standard dosing. Monitor SCr closely.
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min/1.73m²:** Use with extreme caution in native kidney disease. Consider alternative agents. If used, target lower trough levels.
*   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** No supplemental dose required for hemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis. 
*   **Hepatic Impairment:** Reduce initial dose by 50% in severe hepatic impairment (Child-Pugh C).

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline:** SCr, eGFR, K+, Mg2+, fasting lipid panel, HbA1c/fasting glucose, BP, baseline UACR.
*   **Follow-up Schedule:** 
    *   Check SCr, K+, Mg2+, and trough level 3-5 days after initiation or dose change.
    *   Once stable: Monthly for 3 months, then every 2-3 months.
*   **Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM):** 
    *   **Timing:** Draw 12-hour trough (C0) exactly 30-60 minutes *before* the morning dose.
    *   **Targets (Glomerular Disease):** 4–8 ng/mL (KDIGO 2021).
    *   **Targets (Transplant):** Varies by time post-transplant and protocol (typically 8-12 ng/mL early, 5-8 ng/mL maintenance). [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED] for transplant center-specific targets.
    *   **CPT Code:** 80197 (Tacrolimus level).

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Tremor, headache, diarrhea, nausea, hypertension, insomnia.
*   **Serious/Rare:** Posterior Reversible Encephalopathy Syndrome (PRES), BK virus nephropathy (transplant), opportunistic infections, malignancy (skin/lymphoma).
*   **Black Box Warnings:** Increased risk of developing serious infections and malignancies. Astagraf XL has a mortality warning in female liver transplant patients.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** 
    *   **Acute Nephrotoxicity:** Reversible afferent vasoconstriction. *Action:* If SCr rises >30% from baseline, reduce dose by 25-50% or hold.
    *   **Chronic Nephrotoxicity:** Irreversible interstitial fibrosis/tubular atrophy (IFTA) with prolonged use.
    *   **Electrolytes:** Hyperkalemia (Type IV RTA) and Hypomagnesemia (renal wasting) are extremely common.
    *   **Metabolic:** New-Onset Diabetes After Transplant (NODAT) / hyperglycemia. Tacrolimus is highly diabetogenic.

### Drug Interactions
*Tacrolimus is a major CYP3A4/5 and P-glycoprotein (P-gp) substrate.*
*   **Increase Tacrolimus Levels (CYP3A4 Inhibitors):** Non-DHP CCBs (diltiazem, verapamil), azole antifungals (fluconazole, ketoconazole), macrolides (clarithromycin, erythromycin), Paxlovid (nirmatrelvir/ritonavir - *absolute contraindication/requires holding tacrolimus*).
*   **Decrease Tacrolimus Levels (CYP3A4 Inducers):** Rifampin, phenytoin, carbamazepine, St. John's Wort.
*   **Food Interactions:** High-fat meals decrease absorption. Grapefruit/grapefruit juice inhibits intestinal CYP3A4, drastically increasing levels (avoid completely).

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Category C. Crosses placenta. Generally continued in transplant recipients (risk of rejection > risk of drug). In glomerular disease, avoid if possible, but may be used if benefits outweigh risks.
*   **Elderly:** Higher risk of neurotoxicity (tremor, confusion) and nephrotoxicity.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   Take exactly 12 hours apart (e.g., 8 AM and 8 PM) for IR formulations.
*   Take consistently with or without food (empty stomach preferred for consistent absorption).
*   **Do not eat grapefruit or drink grapefruit juice.**
*   On lab days, **DO NOT** take your morning dose until *after* your blood is drawn.
*   Report severe headaches, vision changes, tremors, or significant changes in urine output immediately.

## Neoral, Sandimmune, Gengraf (cyclosporine)
**Aliases:** CsA, CNI

### Mechanism of Action
Cyclosporine binds to the intracellular protein cyclophilin. This complex inhibits calcineurin, preventing NFAT dephosphorylation and IL-2 transcription, thereby halting T-cell activation. Like tacrolimus, it also stabilizes podocyte actin cytoskeletons.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Kidney transplant rejection prophylaxis, Rheumatoid Arthritis, severe Psoriasis.
*   **Off-Label (Guideline Supported):** MCD, FSGS, Membranous Nephropathy, Lupus Nephritis (KDIGO 2021/2024).

### Dosing
*Note: Sandimmune (non-modified) has erratic absorption and is rarely used today. Neoral and Gengraf (modified) have improved bioavailability. **Modified and non-modified formulations are NOT interchangeable.***
*   **Glomerular Diseases (Modified):** Start 3-5 mg/kg/day divided BID (every 12 hours).
*   **Kidney Transplant (Modified):** Start 7-9 mg/kg/day divided BID.

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
Hepatically cleared (CYP3A4). Dosing is guided by nephrotoxicity, not pharmacokinetic clearance.
*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min/1.73m²:** Standard weight-based dosing.
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min/1.73m²:** Standard dosing. Monitor SCr closely.
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min/1.73m²:** Avoid in native kidney disease if possible.
*   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** No supplemental dose required.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline:** SCr, eGFR, K+, Mg2+, fasting lipid panel, BP, LFTs, UACR.
*   **Follow-up Schedule:** SCr, K+, Mg2+, and trough 3-5 days after initiation/change.
*   **Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM):**
    *   **Timing:** 12-hour trough (C0) or 2-hour post-dose peak (C2). C0 is most common in GN.
    *   **Targets (Glomerular Disease):** C0 100–200 ng/mL (KDIGO 2021).
    *   **CPT Code:** 80158 (Cyclosporine level).

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Hypertension (more severe than tacrolimus), gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism (hair growth), hyperlipidemia.
*   **Serious/Rare:** Hepatotoxicity, PRES, malignancy, opportunistic infections.
*   **Black Box Warnings:** Renal toxicity, increased risk of malignancies and serious infections. Bioequivalence warning (do not switch brands without monitoring).
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** 
    *   **Nephrotoxicity:** Afferent vasoconstriction. Reduce dose by 25-50% if SCr rises >30% above baseline.
    *   **Hyperkalemia / Hypomagnesemia:** Common.

### Drug Interactions
*Major CYP3A4 and P-gp substrate AND inhibitor.*
*   **Statins:** Cyclosporine inhibits OATP1B1 and CYP3A4, drastically increasing statin levels. **Simvastatin, lovastatin, and pitavastatin are contraindicated.** Max dose of rosuvastatin is 5 mg/day; max atorvastatin is 10 mg/day.
*   **CYP3A4 Inhibitors/Inducers:** Same as tacrolimus (diltiazem, azoles, rifampin, etc.).

### Patient Counseling Points
*   Maintain excellent dental hygiene to prevent gum overgrowth (gingival hyperplasia).
*   May cause increased hair growth (hirsutism).
*   Do not drink grapefruit juice.
*   Hold morning dose on lab days until after blood is drawn.

## Lupkynis (voclosporin)
**Aliases:** CNI

### Mechanism of Action
Voclosporin is a novel, structurally modified calcineurin inhibitor. It binds to cyclophilin to inhibit calcineurin. The structural modification increases its potency and provides a more predictable pharmacokinetic profile, **eliminating the need for routine therapeutic drug monitoring (trough levels).**

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Active Lupus Nephritis (LN) in adult patients (in combination with background immunosuppressive therapy, typically MMF and steroids).
*   **Key Trials:** 
    *   *AURORA 1 (2021):* Voclosporin + MMF + steroids vs. Placebo + MMF + steroids. Complete Renal Response at 52 weeks was significantly higher in the voclosporin group (40.8% vs. 22.5%).
    *   *AURORA 2 (2023):* 3-year extension study proving long-term safety and sustained eGFR stability.

### Dosing
*   **Standard Dose:** 23.7 mg (three 7.9 mg capsules) orally BID.

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range (CRITICAL):**
Voclosporin has strict, FDA-mandated eGFR parameters.
*   **Baseline eGFR ≥45 mL/min/1.73m²:** Start 23.7 mg BID.
*   **Baseline eGFR <45 mL/min/1.73m²:** Not recommended unless benefit clearly outweighs risk. If used, start at 23.7 mg BID but monitor extremely closely.
*   **Dose Modifications for eGFR Declines (During Therapy):**
    *   If eGFR drops **>20% but <30%** from baseline: Reduce dose to 15.8 mg (two capsules) BID.
    *   If eGFR drops **≥30%** from baseline: Discontinue Lupkynis.
    *   *Note: If eGFR recovers to within 20% of baseline, the dose may be increased back to 23.7 mg BID.*
*   **Hepatic Impairment:** Mild/Moderate (Child-Pugh A/B): Start 15.8 mg BID. Severe (Child-Pugh C): Contraindicated.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline:** Accurate baseline eGFR, BP, UACR.
*   **Follow-up Schedule:** 
    *   Check eGFR every 2 weeks for the first month.
    *   Then every 4 weeks for the first year.
    *   Then quarterly thereafter.
*   **Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM):** **NOT REQUIRED.** Do not check trough levels.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Decreased eGFR, hypertension, diarrhea, headache, anemia, cough, UTI.
*   **Serious/Rare:** QTc prolongation (rare, but noted in labeling).
*   **Black Box Warnings:** Increased risk of serious infections and malignancies (class effect).
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** Acute hemodynamic eGFR decline is expected but must be managed strictly according to the >20% / >30% drop rules above.

### Drug Interactions
*CYP3A4 Substrate.*
*   **Strong CYP3A4 Inhibitors:** (e.g., ketoconazole, itraconazole, clarithromycin) - **Contraindicated.**
*   **Moderate CYP3A4 Inhibitors:** (e.g., diltiazem, verapamil, fluconazole) - Reduce Lupkynis dose to 15.8 mg BID.
*   **Strong/Moderate CYP3A4 Inducers:** (e.g., rifampin) - Avoid use (decreases voclosporin efficacy).
*   **Grapefruit:** Avoid.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   Take capsules whole; do not crush or chew.
*   Take consistently 12 hours apart, preferably on an empty stomach.
*   You will need frequent blood tests (every 2 weeks initially) to check your kidney function, but you do not need "drug level" checks like older medications in this class.
*   Monitor blood pressure at home.

### Prior Authorization & Coding (Lupkynis)
*   **ICD-10 Codes:** M32.14 (Glomerular disease in systemic lupus erythematosus), M32.15 (Tubulo-interstitial nephropathy in SLE).
*   **PA Tips:** Must document active Lupus Nephritis (usually via biopsy within last 6-12 months). Must document concurrent use of MMF (CellCept) and steroids. Must document baseline eGFR >45.
*   **Cost/Assistance:** Very high cost (specialty pharmacy). Aurinia Pharma has a patient support program (Aurinia Alliance) for copay assistance and bridge programs. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Route to specialty pharmacy coordinator].

### Decision Tree: Choosing a CNI in Glomerular Disease

1.  **Is the diagnosis Active Lupus Nephritis?**
    *   *Yes:* Consider **Voclosporin (Lupkynis)** as add-on to MMF/steroids if eGFR >45 and patient has commercial insurance/PA approval. It has superior trial data for rapid proteinuria reduction without the need for TDM.
    *   *If Voclosporin is unaffordable/denied:* Use **Tacrolimus** (multi-target therapy per AURA-LV trial).
2.  **Is the diagnosis Primary Membranous Nephropathy, FSGS, or MCD?**
    *   **Tacrolimus** is generally preferred over Cyclosporine as first-line CNI due to a more favorable cosmetic side effect profile (avoids gum hypertrophy and hair growth) and less severe hyperlipidemia.
    *   *Exception:* If the patient has pre-existing poorly controlled diabetes or high risk for diabetes, **Cyclosporine** may be preferred, as Tacrolimus is highly diabetogenic.
3.  **When to STOP a CNI:**
    *   If SCr rises >30% from baseline and does not recover after a 50% dose reduction.
    *   If target proteinuria reduction (e.g., UACR < 300 mg/g or >50% reduction from baseline) is not achieved after 6 months of therapeutic trough levels (indicates CNI resistance).
    *   If severe, recurrent infections occur.

### Switching Between Agents
*   **Tacrolimus to Cyclosporine (or vice versa):** Stop the first agent. Wait 12-24 hours. Start the new agent at the standard starting dose. Check trough of the *new* agent in 3-5 days. **Never overlap Tacrolimus and Cyclosporine** due to synergistic nephrotoxicity.
*   **Tacrolimus/Cyclosporine to Voclosporin:** Stop the older CNI. Start Voclosporin 23.7 mg BID the next day. Ensure baseline eGFR is documented prior to the switch.
