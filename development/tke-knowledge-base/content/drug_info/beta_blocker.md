# Clinical Drug Monograph: Beta-Blockers (Evidence-Based for HFrEF)

**Class Overview:** 
In nephrology, beta-blockers are primarily utilized for patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) who have compelling cardiovascular indications, most notably Heart Failure with reduced Ejection Fraction (HFrEF), coronary artery disease (CAD), and atrial fibrillation. According to the **2021 KDIGO Blood Pressure Guidelines** and the **2022 ACC/AHA/HFSA Heart Failure Guidelines**, beta-blockers are *not* first-line for primary hypertension in CKD without these compelling indications. 

The three agents detailed below—Coreg (carvedilol), Toprol-XL (metoprolol succinate), and Zebeta (bisoprolol)—are the **only** beta-blockers with proven mortality benefits in HFrEF.

---

## Coreg (carvedilol)

### Mechanism of Action
Coreg (carvedilol) is a non-selective beta-adrenergic (beta-1 and beta-2) and alpha-1 adrenergic receptor antagonist. The alpha-1 blockade provides additional vasodilation, making it highly effective for blood pressure control compared to cardioselective agents.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), hypertension, left ventricular dysfunction following myocardial infarction (MI).
*   **Off-Label:** Portal hypertension/variceal bleeding prophylaxis (relevant in hepatorenal syndrome patients), prevention of anthracycline-induced cardiomyopathy.
*   **Key Trials:** 
    *   **COPERNICUS (2001):** 35% reduction in mortality in severe HF.
    *   **CAPRICORN (2001):** 23% reduction in all-cause mortality in post-MI patients with LV dysfunction.

### Dosing
*   **Standard Dosing (HFrEF):** Start 3.125 mg PO BID. Double the dose every 2 weeks as tolerated.
*   **Target Dose:** 25 mg PO BID (or 50 mg PO BID if weight >85 kg).
*   **Standard Dosing (Hypertension):** Start 6.25 mg PO BID, titrate up to max 25 mg PO BID.
*   **CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR:**
    *   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min:** No adjustment.
    *   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min:** No adjustment.
    *   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min:** No adjustment.
    *   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** No adjustment. Not significantly removed by hemodialysis (highly protein-bound).
*   **Hepatic Adjustments:** Contraindicated in severe hepatic impairment.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs:** Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP) to assess baseline potassium and renal function; ECG to rule out high-degree AV block.
*   **Follow-up:** 
    *   Check BP and HR 1-2 weeks after each dose titration.
    *   Target HR: 55-60 bpm (per ACC/AHA HF guidelines).
    *   Target BP: Systolic <120 mmHg (per KDIGO 2021 guidelines).
*   **Therapeutic Drug Monitoring:** None required.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Dizziness, fatigue, hypotension, bradycardia, weight gain, diarrhea.
*   **Serious/Rare:** Worsening heart failure (initially), bronchospasm, masking of hypoglycemia symptoms.
*   **Black Box Warnings:** Avoid abrupt cessation (can precipitate angina, MI, or ventricular arrhythmias). Taper over 1-2 weeks.
*   **Contraindications:** Bronchial asthma/reactive airway disease, 2nd or 3rd-degree AV block, sick sinus syndrome, severe bradycardia, decompensated HF requiring IV inotropes.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** Non-selective beta-blockade inhibits beta-2 mediated cellular potassium uptake, which can cause mild **hyperkalemia** (usually +0.2 to +0.5 mEq/L), especially when combined with RAAS inhibitors in advanced CKD.

### Drug Interactions
*   **Major:** Non-dihydropyridine CCBs (diltiazem, verapamil), amiodarone, and digoxin (additive AV nodal blockade and bradycardia).
*   **CYP450:** Substrate of CYP2D6. Inhibitors (e.g., fluoxetine, paroxetine, bupropion) can increase Coreg (carvedilol) levels.
*   **Food:** Taking with food slows absorption and reduces the risk of orthostatic hypotension.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Avoid if possible. Can cause fetal/neonatal bradycardia, hypotension, and hypoglycemia.
*   **Elderly:** Start at the lowest dose (3.125 mg BID) due to increased risk of orthostasis and falls.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   Take with food to prevent sudden drops in blood pressure and dizziness.
*   Do not stop taking this medication suddenly; doing so can cause a heart attack or severe heart rhythm issues.
*   If you have diabetes, this medication may hide the symptoms of low blood sugar (like a fast heartbeat), though sweating will still occur.
*   Report severe dizziness, fainting, or weight gain of >3 lbs in a day or >5 lbs in a week.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **ICD-10:** I50.20 (Unspecified systolic heart failure), I50.22 (Chronic systolic heart failure), I11.0 (Hypertensive heart disease with heart failure), I12.9 (Hypertensive CKD stage 1-4).
*   **CPT:** 99213-99215 (E&M), 93000 (ECG routine).
*   **Cost:** Generic is widely available and inexpensive (Tier 1 on most formularies).

## Toprol-XL (metoprolol succinate)

### Mechanism of Action
Toprol-XL (metoprolol succinate) is a cardioselective beta-1 adrenergic receptor antagonist. At higher doses, it loses its selectivity and can block beta-2 receptors. *Note: Metoprolol tartrate (Lopressor) is immediate-release and does NOT have proven mortality benefits in HFrEF.*

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** HFrEF, hypertension, angina pectoris.
*   **Key Trials:** 
    *   **MERIT-HF (1999):** 34% reduction in all-cause mortality in patients with HFrEF.

### Dosing
*   **Standard Dosing (HFrEF):** Start 12.5 to 25 mg PO daily. Double the dose every 2 weeks as tolerated.
*   **Target Dose:** 200 mg PO daily.
*   **Standard Dosing (Hypertension):** Start 25-50 mg PO daily, titrate up to max 400 mg daily.
*   **CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR:**
    *   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min:** No adjustment.
    *   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min:** No adjustment.
    *   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min:** No adjustment.
    *   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** No adjustment. Not significantly removed by hemodialysis.
*   **Hepatic Adjustments:** Initiate at lower doses and titrate slowly in severe hepatic impairment.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs:** ECG to evaluate for AV block.
*   **Follow-up:** Check BP and HR 1-2 weeks post-titration. Target HR 55-60 bpm.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Fatigue, dizziness, depression, bradycardia, sexual dysfunction.
*   **Serious/Rare:** Heart block, bronchospasm (at high doses), Raynaud's phenomenon exacerbation.
*   **Black Box Warnings:** Ischemic heart disease exacerbated following abrupt cessation. Taper over 1-2 weeks.
*   **Contraindications:** Severe bradycardia, 2nd/3rd-degree AV block, sick sinus syndrome, decompensated HF.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** Less risk of hyperkalemia compared to non-selective agents like Coreg (carvedilol).

### Drug Interactions
*   **Major:** Digoxin, amiodarone, diltiazem, verapamil (additive bradycardia/AV block).
*   **CYP450:** Major substrate of CYP2D6.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Frequently used in pregnancy for maternal arrhythmias or hypertension, but monitor neonate for beta-blockade symptoms.
*   **Elderly:** Well tolerated; start at 12.5 mg daily.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   Swallow the pill whole or break it in half at the score line. **Do not crush or chew** the tablet, as this destroys the extended-release mechanism.
*   Take once daily, preferably with or immediately following a meal.
*   Do not stop abruptly to avoid chest pain or heart attacks.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **ICD-10:** I50.22 (Chronic systolic heart failure), I20.9 (Angina pectoris).
*   **Cost:** Generic is widely available and inexpensive.

## Zebeta (bisoprolol)

### Mechanism of Action
Zebeta (bisoprolol) is a highly cardioselective beta-1 adrenergic receptor antagonist. It is one of the most beta-1 selective agents available, making it a preferred choice in patients with reactive airway disease (asthma/COPD).

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Hypertension.
*   **Off-Label (Guideline-Directed):** HFrEF (Class I recommendation per ACC/AHA).
*   **Key Trials:** 
    *   **CIBIS-II (1999):** 34% reduction in all-cause mortality in HFrEF patients.

### Dosing
*   **Standard Dosing (HFrEF):** Start 1.25 mg PO daily. Titrate slowly (every 2-4 weeks) to 2.5 mg, 3.75 mg, 5 mg, 7.5 mg, and finally 10 mg daily.
*   **Target Dose:** 10 mg PO daily.
*   **CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR:** *(Requires adjustment due to 50% renal clearance)*
    *   **eGFR ≥40 mL/min:** Standard dosing.
    *   **eGFR <40 mL/min:** Start 2.5 mg PO daily (for HTN). For HFrEF, start at 1.25 mg and titrate with extreme caution. Maximum dose is often capped at 2.5 - 5 mg daily depending on tolerability.
    *   **Dialysis:** Start 1.25 - 2.5 mg daily. Not significantly dialyzable. Administer after dialysis on hemodialysis days to avoid intradialytic hypotension.
*   **Hepatic Adjustments:** Max 10 mg daily in severe hepatic impairment.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs:** BMP (renal function dictates max dose), ECG.
*   **Follow-up:** BP, HR, and serum creatinine.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Fatigue, headache, dizziness, bradycardia.
*   **Serious/Rare:** AV block, heart failure exacerbation.
*   **Black Box Warnings:** Abrupt cessation may exacerbate angina or cause MI. Taper over 1-2 weeks.
*   **Contraindications:** Cardiogenic shock, overt cardiac failure, 2nd/3rd-degree AV block, severe bradycardia.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** Accumulates in severe CKD. Close monitoring for profound bradycardia is required if eGFR <40 mL/min.

### Drug Interactions
*   **Major:** Amiodarone, digoxin, non-DHP CCBs.
*   **CYP450:** Minor substrate of CYP3A4; no major CYP interactions.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Avoid if possible; potential for intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR).
*   **Elderly:** Start at 1.25 mg daily.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   Take in the morning.
*   Do not stop taking this medication suddenly.
*   Report a heart rate less than 50 beats per minute or severe dizziness.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **ICD-10:** I10 (Essential hypertension), I50.22 (Chronic systolic heart failure).
*   **Cost:** Generic available, inexpensive.

## Class Comparison & Decision Trees

### Beta-Blocker Comparison Table

| Feature | Coreg (carvedilol) | Toprol-XL (metoprolol succinate) | Zebeta (bisoprolol) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Receptor Selectivity** | Non-selective (β1, β2, α1) | Cardioselective (β1) | Highly Cardioselective (β1) |
| **HFrEF Target Dose** | 25-50 mg BID | 200 mg Daily | 10 mg Daily |
| **eGFR Adjustment** | None | None | **Yes (if eGFR <40)** |
| **Dialyzable?** | No | No | No |
| **BP Lowering Effect** | ++++ (due to α1 block) | ++ | ++ |
| **Asthma/COPD Safety** | Avoid | Moderate | Best |
| **Key HFrEF Trial** | COPERNICUS / CAPRICORN | MERIT-HF | CIBIS-II |

### Decision Tree: When to Choose Which Agent
1.  **Does the patient have HFrEF?**
    *   *Yes:* MUST use one of these three agents. Do not use metoprolol tartrate or atenolol.
2.  **Does the patient have poorly controlled hypertension alongside HFrEF?**
    *   *Choose:* **Coreg (carvedilol)**. The alpha-1 blockade provides superior BP reduction.
3.  **Does the patient have severe asthma or COPD?**
    *   *Choose:* **Zebeta (bisoprolol)** or **Toprol-XL (metoprolol succinate)**. Avoid Coreg.
4.  **Does the patient have a history of medication non-adherence?**
    *   *Choose:* **Toprol-XL** or **Zebeta** (once-daily dosing).
5.  **Does the patient have advanced CKD (eGFR <30) and frequent hyperkalemia?**
    *   *Choose:* **Toprol-XL** (avoids the renal accumulation of bisoprolol and the beta-2 mediated hyperkalemia of carvedilol).

### Decision Tree: Escalation and Stopping
*   **Escalate:** Double dose every 2-4 weeks if HR >60 bpm and Systolic BP >100 mmHg.
*   **Hold/Reduce Dose:** If HR <50 bpm, symptomatic hypotension, or 2nd/3rd-degree AV block develops. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Standard clinic parameters for holding beta-blockers (e.g., hold if SBP < 90 or HR < 55)].
*   **Stop:** Decompensated heart failure requiring IV inotropes (dobutamine/milrinone). *Always taper over 1-2 weeks when discontinuing permanently to avoid rebound tachycardia/ischemia.*

### Switching Between Agents
If switching due to side effects or formulary issues, use the following approximate dose equivalents for HFrEF:
*   Coreg (carvedilol) 3.125 mg BID ≈ Toprol-XL (metoprolol succinate) 12.5 - 25 mg Daily ≈ Zebeta (bisoprolol) 1.25 mg Daily.
*   *Note:* When switching from immediate-release metoprolol tartrate to Toprol-XL, the total daily dose is the same (e.g., metoprolol tartrate 25 mg BID = Toprol-XL 50 mg Daily).
