# Alkylating Agents in Nephrology

## Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide)

**Aliases:** CYC, CTX

### Mechanism of Action
Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) is a cell cycle phase-nonspecific alkylating agent. It is an inactive prodrug that requires hepatic conversion by cytochrome P450 enzymes (primarily CYP2B6) into active metabolites, including phosphoramide mustard and acrolein. Phosphoramide mustard cross-links DNA strands, preventing cell division and leading to apoptosis. In nephrology, its profound cytotoxic effect on rapidly dividing B-lymphocytes and T-lymphocytes provides powerful immunosuppression to halt autoantibody production and immune-mediated glomerular damage. 

### Indications in Nephrology
*Note: All nephrology indications are guided by the **KDIGO 2021 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Glomerular Diseases**.*

**FDA-Approved Indications Relevant to Nephrology:**
*   Minimal Change Disease (MCD) / Nephrotic Syndrome (pediatric and adult, steroid-dependent or frequently relapsing)

**Common Off-Label Uses in Nephrology:**
*   **ANCA-Associated Vasculitis (AAV):** Induction therapy for severe/organ-threatening disease (Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis [GPA] and Microscopic Polyangiitis [MPA]).
*   **Lupus Nephritis (LN):** Class III, IV, or V induction therapy.
*   **Primary Membranous Nephropathy (MN):** High-risk disease (Modified Ponticelli regimen).
*   **Anti-GBM Disease (Goodpasture's):** Standard induction therapy alongside plasmapheresis and corticosteroids.

**Key Supporting Clinical Trials:**
*   **CYCLOPS Trial (2009):** Compared daily oral vs. pulsed IV Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) in AAV. Showed IV pulse had lower cumulative dose and fewer leukopenic episodes with equal remission rates, though long-term follow-up showed a slightly higher relapse rate with IV.
*   **Euro-Lupus Trial (2002):** Compared low-dose IV Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) (500 mg every 2 weeks x 6 doses) vs. high-dose NIH protocol in Lupus Nephritis. Showed equal efficacy (renal remission) with significantly lower toxicity in the low-dose group.
*   **ALMS Trial (2009):** Compared CellCept (mycophenolate mofetil) to IV Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) for LN induction. Demonstrated non-inferiority, establishing MMF as a first-line alternative to CYC for many patients.

### Dosing
Dosing depends heavily on the specific glomerular disease and the chosen protocol (Oral vs. Intravenous). 

#### Standard Protocols
*   **ANCA Vasculitis (Induction):**
    *   *IV Pulse:* 15 mg/kg IV every 2 weeks for 3 doses, then every 3 weeks for 3-6 doses. Max dose 1.2 g per infusion.
    *   *Oral Daily:* 1.5 to 2 mg/kg/day PO for 3-6 months. Max dose 200 mg/day.
*   **Lupus Nephritis (Euro-Lupus Protocol - Preferred for Caucasian/European descent):**
    *   500 mg IV every 2 weeks for a total of 6 doses.
*   **Lupus Nephritis (NIH Protocol - Often reserved for severe/crescentic disease):**
    *   0.5 to 1.0 g/m² IV monthly for 6 months.
*   **Membranous Nephropathy (Modified Ponticelli):**
    *   Months 2, 4, and 6: Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) 2 to 2.5 mg/kg/day PO for 30 days (alternating with IV/PO corticosteroids in months 1, 3, and 5).

#### CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range
*Active metabolites are renally excreted. Dose reduction is CRITICAL to prevent severe myelosuppression in CKD.*

*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min/1.73m²:** 100% of standard dose.
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min/1.73m²:** 100% of standard dose (monitor WBC closely; some protocols recommend a 25% reduction if patient is elderly or frail).
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min/1.73m²:** Reduce dose by 25% to 30%.
    *   *Oral:* 1 to 1.5 mg/kg/day.
    *   *IV:* Reduce calculated dose by 25% (e.g., 10-12.5 mg/kg for AAV).
*   **eGFR <15 mL/min/1.73m² / Dialysis:** Reduce dose by 25% to 50%. 
    *   *Hemodialysis:* Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) is dialyzable. Administer the dose **AFTER** the hemodialysis session.

#### Hepatic Adjustments
*   Bilirubin 3.1 to 5.0 mg/dL or AST/ALT >3x ULN: Reduce dose by 25%.
*   Bilirubin > 5.0 mg/dL: Omit dose or avoid use.

### Monitoring
#### Baseline Labs Required
*   CBC with differential
*   CMP (SCr, eGFR, LFTs)
*   Urinalysis (document baseline hematuria)
*   Infectious screening: Hepatitis B (HBsAg, HBcAb), Hepatitis C, HIV, QuantiFERON-TB Gold
*   Serum hCG (pregnancy test) in women of childbearing potential

#### Follow-Up Lab Schedule
*   **CBC with diff:** Every 1-2 weeks for the first 2 months, then monthly. 
    *   *Target Nadir:* WBC > 3,500 - 4,000/mm³ and ANC > 1,500/mm³.
    *   *Action:* If WBC < 3,500 or ANC < 1,500, hold dose until recovery, then resume at a 25% reduced dose.
*   **CMP:** Monthly to monitor SCr/eGFR and LFTs.
*   **Urinalysis:** Monthly. Monitor for new-onset non-glomerular hematuria (warning sign for hemorrhagic cystitis).
*   **UACR / UPCR:** Monthly to quarterly to assess therapeutic response (Target UACR < 30 mg/g or >50% reduction from baseline).

#### Therapeutic Drug Monitoring
*   Routine serum trough levels are NOT utilized for Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide). Dosing is titrated based on clinical response and WBC/ANC nadir.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
#### Common Side Effects (>5%)
*   Alopecia (hair thinning/loss)
*   Nausea, vomiting, anorexia (highly emetogenic, especially IV)
*   Leukopenia, neutropenia, anemia

#### Serious/Rare Side Effects
*   **Hemorrhagic Cystitis:** Caused by the accumulation of the toxic metabolite *acrolein* in the bladder.
*   **Infertility:** Premature ovarian failure (POF) in women and azoospermia in men. Risk is cumulative dose-dependent and age-dependent (higher risk in older women).
*   **Secondary Malignancies:** Bladder cancer, acute myeloid leukemia (AML), myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS). Risk increases with cumulative doses > 36 grams.
*   **Opportunistic Infections:** Especially *Pneumocystis jirovecii* pneumonia (PJP).

#### Black Box Warnings
1.  Severe myelosuppression
2.  Immunosuppression leading to fatal infections
3.  Hemorrhagic cystitis and severe urotoxicity
4.  Cardiotoxicity (usually at high oncologic doses, rare in nephrology)
5.  Secondary malignancies

#### Contraindications
*   **Absolute:** Severe bone marrow depression, active life-threatening infection, known hypersensitivity, pregnancy (unless life-threatening maternal disease).
*   **Relative:** Severe hepatic impairment, active urinary tract infection (increases cystitis risk).

#### CKD-Specific Safety Concerns
*   Prolonged half-life of active metabolites in CKD increases the risk of profound, prolonged neutropenia. Strict adherence to eGFR-based dose reductions is mandatory.

### Drug Interactions
*   **Zyloprim (allopurinol):** *Major Interaction.* Concomitant use significantly increases the risk of severe bone marrow suppression. Avoid combination if possible, or monitor CBC twice weekly.
*   **CYP450 Inducers/Inhibitors:** Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) is a prodrug. CYP inducers (e.g., Dilantin (phenytoin), Rifadin (rifampin)) may increase toxic metabolites. CYP inhibitors (e.g., Diflucan (fluconazole), Cordarone (amiodarone)) may decrease efficacy.
*   **Other Immunosuppressants:** Additive risk of fatal infections.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Category D. Known teratogen. Causes fetal malformations. Effective contraception is required during therapy and for up to 1 year after stopping.
*   **Elderly:** Higher risk of severe infections and myelosuppression. Consider starting at 25% reduced dose regardless of eGFR.
*   **Fertility:** **[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]** All patients of childbearing age MUST be counseled on fertility preservation (sperm banking, oocyte/embryo cryopreservation) PRIOR to the first dose. Consider concurrent use of Lupron (leuprolide) in premenopausal women to suppress ovarian function during therapy.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Hydration is Key:** Drink 2 to 3 liters of fluid daily and urinate frequently (every 2-3 hours), including right before bed. This flushes the toxic byproduct (acrolein) out of the bladder to prevent bleeding.
*   **Timing of Dose:** Take oral Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) in the morning. *Never take it at bedtime*, as the drug will sit in the bladder overnight.
*   **Infection Risk:** Report any fever > 100.4°F, chills, sore throat, or cough immediately. Avoid crowds and sick contacts.
*   **Warning Signs to Report:** Blood in the urine, painful urination, unexplained bruising, or severe nausea/vomiting.
*   **Hair Loss:** Hair thinning or loss is common but usually reversible after stopping the medication.

## Class Comparison: Alkylating Agents in Nephrology

While Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) is the dominant alkylating agent in modern nephrology, the route of administration (IV vs. PO) functions as a distinct therapeutic choice. Historically, Leukeran (chlorambucil) was also used but has largely been replaced due to higher toxicity profiles.

| Feature | Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) - **IV Pulse** | Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) - **Oral Daily** | Leukeran (chlorambucil) - *Historical/Rare* |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Use** | Lupus Nephritis (Euro-Lupus/NIH), AAV | AAV, Membranous (Ponticelli), MCD | Membranous Nephropathy (Legacy Ponticelli) |
| **Dosing Schedule** | Every 2 to 4 weeks | Daily | Daily (alternating months) |
| **eGFR <30 Adjustment** | Reduce dose by 25-30% | Reduce dose by 25-30% | Reduce dose / use with extreme caution |
| **Cumulative Toxicity** | Lower cumulative dose | Higher cumulative dose | High cumulative toxicity |
| **Bladder Toxicity Risk** | Lower (flushed out rapidly with IV fluids) | Higher (constant bladder exposure) | Low (different metabolic pathway) |
| **Key Trials** | Euro-Lupus, CYCLOPS | CYCLOPS, Ponticelli | Original Ponticelli |
| **Cost** | High (requires infusion center, nursing) | Low (generic oral tablets) | High (specialty pharmacy) |

### Decision Tree: Choosing and Switching Therapy

**1. When to choose IV over Oral Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide):**
*   **Lupus Nephritis:** IV is strongly preferred (Euro-Lupus protocol) due to proven efficacy with significantly lower cumulative toxicity and lower risk of premature ovarian failure compared to oral.
*   **Compliance Concerns:** IV guarantees adherence as it is administered in a controlled infusion center.
*   **Fertility Preservation:** IV pulse results in a lower cumulative lifetime dose, reducing the risk of permanent infertility.

**2. When to choose Oral over IV Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide):**
*   **Membranous Nephropathy:** The Modified Ponticelli regimen specifically utilizes oral Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) alternating with steroids. IV is not standard for this indication.
*   **Logistical Barriers:** If the patient cannot access an infusion center or lacks transportation.

**3. When to STOP or SWITCH therapy:**
*   **Max Cumulative Dose Reached:** Switch to maintenance therapy (e.g., Imuran (azathioprine) or Rituxan (rituximab)) once induction is complete (usually 3-6 months) to avoid exceeding a lifetime cumulative dose of 36 grams (high risk of malignancy).
*   **Severe Leukopenia:** If WBC < 3,500 or ANC < 1,500, hold therapy. If recurrent despite dose reduction, switch to alternative induction (e.g., CellCept (mycophenolate mofetil) for LN, or Rituxan (rituximab) for AAV).
*   **Hemorrhagic Cystitis:** STOP Cytoxan (cyclophosphamide) immediately. Do not rechallenge. Switch to non-alkylating agent.
*   **Refractory Disease:** If no response after 3 months of adequate dosing, switch class (e.g., switch to Rituxan (rituximab) for AAV, or add/switch to voclosporin/belimumab for LN per KDIGO guidelines).
