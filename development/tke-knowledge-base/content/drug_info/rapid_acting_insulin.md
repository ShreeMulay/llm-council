# Clinical Drug Monograph: Rapid-Acting Insulins
**Document Owner:** The Kidney Experts (TKE) Clinical Committee  
**Guidelines Referenced:** KDIGO 2022 Clinical Practice Guideline for Diabetes Management in CKD; ADA 2024 Standards of Care in Diabetes

## NovoLog, Fiasp (insulin aspart)

### Mechanism of Action
NovoLog (insulin aspart) and Fiasp (insulin aspart) are recombinant, rapid-acting human insulin analogs. They lower blood glucose by stimulating peripheral glucose uptake (especially by skeletal muscle and fat) and inhibiting hepatic glucose production. 
*   **NovoLog (insulin aspart):** Homologous to regular human insulin except for a single substitution of the amino acid proline by aspartic acid in position B28, which reduces hexamer formation, allowing for faster absorption.
*   **Fiasp (insulin aspart):** Formulated with niacinamide (vitamin B3) to increase the speed of initial absorption, making it an "ultra-rapid-acting" insulin.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Treatment of diabetes mellitus (Type 1 and Type 2) to improve glycemic control in adults and pediatric patients.
*   **Nephrology Context:** Management of mealtime (prandial) hyperglycemia and correction of acute hyperglycemia in patients with CKD or ESRD. 
*   **Key Guidelines:** KDIGO 2022 and ADA 2024 recommend insulin therapy for patients with advanced CKD (eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73 m²) or on dialysis, as many oral agents (e.g., metformin, SGLT2 inhibitors) are contraindicated or less effective.

### Dosing
*Note: Insulin requirements decrease significantly as kidney function declines due to reduced renal insulin clearance and decreased renal gluconeogenesis.*

**Standard Dosing & Titration:**
*   **Type 1 DM:** Total Daily Dose (TDD) is typically 0.4 to 1.0 units/kg/day. 50% is given as basal insulin, and 50% is given as rapid-acting insulin divided equally before meals.
*   **Type 2 DM:** Typically started at 4-6 units or 10% of basal dose before the largest meal. Titrate by 1-2 units or 10-15% twice weekly until post-prandial targets are met.

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m²:** 100% of standard dose.
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min/1.73 m²:** 75-100% of standard dose. Monitor closely for hypoglycemia.
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min/1.73 m²:** 75% of standard dose.
*   **eGFR <15 mL/min/1.73 m² / Dialysis:** 50% of standard dose. High risk of prolonged hypoglycemia. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Standardize post-dialysis mealtime dosing adjustments for HD days vs. non-HD days].

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs:** CMP (specifically baseline potassium, BUN, SCr, eGFR), HbA1c.
*   **Follow-up Schedule:** 
    *   HbA1c every 3 months. 
    *   **Targets (KDIGO 2022):** Individualized HbA1c target ranging from <6.5% to <8.0%. For advanced CKD/ESRD with high hypoglycemia risk, target <8.0%.
*   **Therapeutic Monitoring:** 
    *   Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) is highly recommended in CKD. 
    *   **CGM Targets:** Time in Range (TIR 70-180 mg/dL) >70%; Time Below Range (TBR <70 mg/dL) <4%. *In severe CKD/ESRD, prioritize TBR <1% to avoid fatal hypoglycemia.*
    *   Self-Monitoring of Blood Glucose (SMBG): Check before meals and at bedtime.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Hypoglycemia, weight gain, lipodystrophy at the injection site, localized allergic reactions.
*   **Serious/Rare:** Severe, life-threatening hypoglycemia; hypokalemia (insulin shifts potassium intracellularly); anaphylaxis.
*   **Contraindications:** During episodes of hypoglycemia; documented hypersensitivity.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** Uremia induces insulin resistance, but the loss of renal insulin clearance and renal gluconeogenesis ultimately leads to a net *increase* in hypoglycemia risk. Hypoglycemia unawareness is highly prevalent in ESRD.

### Drug Interactions
*   **Thiazolidinediones (TZDs):** Co-administration increases the risk of fluid retention and heart failure. Avoid in advanced CKD/ESRD.
*   **Beta-blockers, Clonidine:** May mask the signs of hypoglycemia (tachycardia, tremors) and impair glycogenolysis.
*   **Other Hypoglycemics (Sulfonylureas, GLP-1s):** Additive hypoglycemia risk. Reduce sulfonylurea dose or discontinue when starting prandial insulin.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Safe for use. Tight glycemic control is required.
*   **Elderly in CKD:** Extreme caution. Relax A1c targets (<8.0% or <8.5%) to prioritize avoidance of hypoglycemia.
*   **Transplant:** Corticosteroid-induced post-transplant diabetes mellitus (PTDM) often requires rapid-acting insulin to manage pronounced post-prandial glucose spikes.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Administration:** 
    *   **NovoLog:** Inject subcutaneously 5-10 minutes before a meal.
    *   **Fiasp:** Inject at the start of a meal or within 20 minutes after starting a meal.
*   **Hypoglycemia Warning Signs:** Sweating, shakiness, confusion, dizziness. Teach the "Rule of 15" (consume 15g of fast-acting carbs, recheck in 15 mins).
*   **Site Rotation:** Rotate injection sites within the abdomen, thigh, or upper arm to prevent lipohypertrophy, which causes erratic insulin absorption.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **ICD-10 Codes:** E11.22 (Type 2 DM with diabetic CKD), E11.9 (Type 2 DM without complications), E10.22 (Type 1 DM with diabetic CKD), N18.x (CKD stage).
*   **CPT Codes:** 95250 (CGM sensor placement), 95251 (CGM interpretation).
*   **PA Tips:** Document failure of or contraindication to oral agents (e.g., eGFR <30 for metformin).

## Humalog, Lyumjev (insulin lispro)

### Mechanism of Action
Humalog (insulin lispro) and Lyumjev (insulin lispro-aabc) are recombinant, rapid-acting human insulin analogs. 
*   **Humalog (insulin lispro):** The amino acids at positions B28 (proline) and B29 (lysine) are reversed compared to human regular insulin, resulting in reduced hexamer formation and faster absorption.
*   **Lyumjev (insulin lispro-aabc):** Formulated with treprostinil (a local vasodilator) and sodium citrate (increases vascular permeability) to accelerate absorption, making it an "ultra-rapid-acting" insulin.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Treatment of diabetes mellitus (Type 1 and Type 2) to improve glycemic control.
*   **Nephrology Context:** Management of mealtime hyperglycemia. Often preferred in ESRD patients on parenteral nutrition or high-carbohydrate intradialytic snacks.

### Dosing
*Note: Follows the same physiologic principles as insulin aspart. Renal dose adjustments are mandatory.*

**Standard Dosing & Titration:**
*   **Initiation (Type 2 DM):** 4 units or 10% of basal dose before the largest meal.
*   **Titration:** Increase by 1-2 units twice weekly based on 2-hour post-prandial SMBG or CGM data.

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m²:** 100% of standard dose.
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min/1.73 m²:** 75-100% of standard dose.
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min/1.73 m²:** 75% of standard dose.
*   **eGFR <15 mL/min/1.73 m² / Dialysis:** 50% of standard dose. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Protocol for holding prandial insulin if pre-dialysis glucose is <150 mg/dL].

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs:** CMP (Potassium, BUN, SCr, eGFR), HbA1c.
*   **Follow-up Schedule:** HbA1c every 3 months. 
*   **Therapeutic Monitoring:** CGM is preferred. Target TIR >70%, TBR <4% (or <1% in frail/ESRD patients). Monitor potassium closely if using large doses, as insulin drives potassium intracellularly.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Hypoglycemia, weight gain, injection site reactions. *Note: Lyumjev has a higher rate of injection site pain/reactions due to the treprostinil and citrate additives.*
*   **Serious/Rare:** Severe hypoglycemia, hypokalemia, hypersensitivity.
*   **Contraindications:** Hypoglycemia, hypersensitivity.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** Dialysis patients have erratic gastric emptying (diabetic gastroparesis is common), which can cause a mismatch between the rapid peak of insulin lispro and delayed carbohydrate absorption, leading to severe post-prandial hypoglycemia.

### Drug Interactions
*   **TZDs:** Increased risk of fluid retention/HF.
*   **ACEi/ARBs:** May enhance the blood-glucose-lowering effect of insulins. Monitor closely when initiating RAAS blockade in a patient on insulin.
*   **Beta-blockers:** Mask hypoglycemic symptoms.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Safe for use.
*   **Elderly in CKD:** High risk for fatal hypoglycemia. Use conservative dosing.
*   **Transplant:** Safe and effective for PTDM.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Administration:** 
    *   **Humalog:** Inject within 15 minutes before a meal or immediately after a meal.
    *   **Lyumjev:** Inject at the start of a meal or within 20 minutes after starting a meal.
*   **Gastroparesis Consideration:** If the patient has known delayed gastric emptying (common in advanced diabetic kidney disease), advise them to inject *after* they are sure they can keep the meal down, to prevent insulin-induced hypoglycemia without carbohydrate intake.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **ICD-10 Codes:** E11.22, E10.22, N18.x.
*   **Cost Considerations:** Humalog and NovoLog both have unbranded generic versions (Insulin Lispro and Insulin Aspart) which are significantly cheaper and should be utilized for Medicare Part D patients in the donut hole.

## Class Comparison: Rapid-Acting Insulins

### Comparison Table

| Feature | NovoLog (insulin aspart) | Fiasp (ultra-rapid aspart) | Humalog (insulin lispro) | Lyumjev (ultra-rapid lispro) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Onset of Action** | 10 - 20 minutes | ~2.5 minutes | 15 minutes | ~1 minute |
| **Peak Effect** | 1 - 3 hours | 1 - 2 hours | 1 - 2 hours | 0.5 - 1.5 hours |
| **Duration of Action** | 3 - 5 hours | 3 - 5 hours | 3 - 5 hours | 3 - 5 hours |
| **Timing of Injection** | 5-10 mins prior to meal | Start of meal (up to 20 mins after) | 15 mins prior to meal | Start of meal (up to 20 mins after) |
| **eGFR <15 / ESRD Dose** | Reduce by 50% | Reduce by 50% | Reduce by 50% | Reduce by 50% |
| **Formulary / Cost** | Moderate (Generic available) | High (Brand only) | Moderate (Generic available) | High (Brand only) |
| **Injection Site Pain** | Low | Low | Low | Moderate/High (due to additives) |

### Decision Tree: Selection, Escalation, and Switching

**1. When to Start Rapid-Acting Insulin in CKD:**
*   Patient is already on optimized basal insulin (e.g., glargine, degludec) but HbA1c remains above target.
*   CGM shows post-prandial glucose excursions (>180 mg/dL) despite basal insulin.
*   Patient has advanced CKD (eGFR <30) where GLP-1 RAs or SGLT2is are either insufficient, not tolerated, or contraindicated.

**2. Choosing Between Agents:**
*   **NovoLog vs. Humalog:** Clinically interchangeable. Choice is almost entirely driven by patient insurance formulary and cost. Always prescribe the unbranded generic (Insulin Aspart or Insulin Lispro) if cost is a barrier.
*   **Standard vs. Ultra-Rapid (Fiasp/Lyumjev):** 
    *   *Choose Ultra-Rapid if:* The patient struggles to remember to inject 15 minutes *before* meals and prefers to inject right as they sit down to eat, or if CGM shows extreme, immediate post-prandial spikes.
    *   *Avoid Ultra-Rapid if:* The patient has severe diabetic gastroparesis (common in ESRD). The ultra-rapid onset will peak before the delayed food absorption occurs, causing severe hypoglycemia followed by late hyperglycemia.

**3. Switching Between Agents:**
*   **Within Class (e.g., NovoLog to Humalog):** Switch unit-for-unit (1:1 conversion). No dose adjustment is typically necessary.
*   **Standard to Ultra-Rapid (e.g., NovoLog to Fiasp):** Switch unit-for-unit (1:1 conversion), but strictly counsel the patient on the new timing of administration (at the start of the meal, not 15 minutes prior).

**4. When to Stop/De-escalate:**
*   If eGFR declines rapidly (e.g., progressing from Stage 3b to Stage 5), insulin clearance drops precipitously. Proactively reduce prandial doses by 25-50%.
*   If CGM shows TBR (<70 mg/dL) >4%, reduce the prandial dose of the offending meal by 10-20%.
*   [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Standardize de-escalation protocol for patients initiating dialysis, as the initiation of HD often drastically alters insulin sensitivity and clearance].
