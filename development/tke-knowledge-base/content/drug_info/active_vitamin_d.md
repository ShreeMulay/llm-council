# Active Vitamin D & Analogs

This monograph covers the active vitamin D class, focusing on **Rocaltrol (calcitriol)**, the foundational active vitamin D therapy used in nephrology for the management of secondary hyperparathyroidism (SHPT) and hypocalcemia.

## Rocaltrol (calcitriol)
**Aliases:** 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D, 1,25(OH)₂D₃

### Mechanism of Action
Rocaltrol (calcitriol) is the synthetically manufactured, biologically active form of vitamin D3. In normal physiology, 25-hydroxyvitamin D is converted to 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D by the enzyme 1-alpha-hydroxylase in the kidneys. In CKD, the loss of functional renal mass leads to a deficiency of this active hormone. 
*   **Receptor Target:** Binds directly to the Vitamin D Receptor (VDR) in the parathyroid gland, intestines, and bone.
*   **Effect:** Directly suppresses parathyroid hormone (PTH) gene transcription and secretion. Indirectly suppresses PTH by increasing intestinal absorption of calcium and phosphorus, thereby raising serum calcium levels.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved Indications:**
    *   Management of secondary hyperparathyroidism (SHPT) and resultant metabolic bone disease in patients with moderate to severe CKD (Stages 3-5) not yet on dialysis.
    *   Management of hypocalcemia and SHPT in patients on chronic hemodialysis.
*   **Off-Label Uses:**
    *   Management of severe post-parathyroidectomy hypocalcemia ("hungry bone syndrome").
    *   Management of hypocalcemia secondary to cinacalcet or etelcalcetide therapy.
*   **Key Guidelines & Evidence:**
    *   **KDIGO 2017 CKD-MBD Guidelines:** Routine use of Rocaltrol (calcitriol) in non-dialysis CKD G3a-G5 is **no longer recommended** as first-line therapy due to the increased risk of hypercalcemia and lack of proven patient-level outcome benefits. It should be reserved for patients with severe and progressively rising intact PTH (iPTH) despite correction of modifiable factors (hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia, and 25-OH vitamin D deficiency).
    *   In ESRD/Dialysis (CKD G5D), calcitriol or vitamin D analogs are recommended to maintain iPTH within 2 to 9 times the upper limit of normal.

### Dosing
*Note: Dosing must be highly individualized based on serum calcium, phosphorus, and iPTH levels.*

**Standard Dosing & Titration:**
*   **Initial:** 0.25 mcg orally once daily or every other day.
*   **Titration:** Increase by 0.25 mcg increments every 2 to 4 weeks based on iPTH and calcium response.
*   **Maximum Oral Dose:** Typically 0.5 to 1 mcg/day (higher doses increase the risk of hypercalcemia).
*   **Pulse Dosing (Dialysis):** 0.5 to 2 mcg orally 3 times weekly (post-dialysis) is often preferred over daily dosing to maximize VDR activation while minimizing sustained intestinal calcium absorption.

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
| eGFR Range (mL/min/1.73m²) | Recommended Dosing |
| :--- | :--- |
| **eGFR ≥60** | Not typically indicated for SHPT. Treat nutritional vitamin D deficiency with cholecalciferol/ergocalciferol. |
| **eGFR 30-59** | 0.25 mcg daily or every other day. *Reserve for severe/progressive SHPT per KDIGO 2017.* |
| **eGFR 15-29** | 0.25 mcg daily or every other day. *Reserve for severe/progressive SHPT per KDIGO 2017.* |
| **eGFR <15 / Dialysis** | 0.25 mcg daily OR 0.5–2 mcg orally 3x/week. Titrate every 2-4 weeks. |

*   **Hepatic Impairment:** No dosage adjustment necessary (calcitriol is already the active form and does not require hepatic activation).

### Monitoring
**Baseline Labs Required:**
*   Serum Calcium (corrected or ionized)
*   Serum Phosphorus
*   Intact PTH (iPTH)
*   25-OH Vitamin D (to rule out/treat nutritional deficiency first)

**Follow-up Lab Schedule [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]:**
*   **Initiation/Titration:** Check Calcium and Phosphorus 1 to 2 weeks after starting or changing the dose.
*   **Maintenance (Non-Dialysis):** Calcium, Phosphorus, and iPTH every 3 months.
*   **Maintenance (Dialysis):** Calcium and Phosphorus monthly; iPTH every 3 months.

**Therapeutic Targets (KDIGO 2017):**
*   **Calcium:** Maintain within the normal reference range (avoid hypercalcemia >10.2 mg/dL).
*   **Phosphorus:** Lower toward the normal range (avoid hyperphosphatemia >5.5 mg/dL).
*   **iPTH (Non-Dialysis):** Baseline trend; treat only if progressively rising.
*   **iPTH (Dialysis):** Target 2 to 9 times the upper limit of normal (approx. **150 - 600 pg/mL**).

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Hypercalcemia, hyperphosphatemia, headache, nausea, vomiting, constipation.
*   **Serious/Rare:** Vascular calcification, calciphylaxis (calcific uremic arteriolopathy), adynamic bone disease (if PTH is over-suppressed <100 pg/mL).
*   **Black Box Warnings:** None.
*   **Contraindications:** 
    *   Absolute: Hypercalcemia, evidence of vitamin D toxicity.
    *   Relative: Uncontrolled hyperphosphatemia (due to risk of elevated Ca x P product and metastatic calcification).
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** Rocaltrol (calcitriol) increases intestinal absorption of both calcium and phosphorus. In CKD patients who cannot excrete phosphorus, this frequently exacerbates hyperphosphatemia, increasing the risk of severe vascular calcification.

### Drug Interactions
*   **Thiazide Diuretics:** Co-administration increases the risk of hypercalcemia.
*   **Digitalis (Digoxin):** Hypercalcemia induced by calcitriol can precipitate fatal cardiac arrhythmias in patients taking digoxin.
*   **Phosphate Binders:** 
    *   *Calcium-based binders (calcium acetate/carbonate):* Increased risk of hypercalcemia.
    *   *Magnesium-based antacids/binders:* Risk of hypermagnesemia.
    *   *Sevelamer / Lanthanum:* May bind oral calcitriol in the gut. Administer Rocaltrol (calcitriol) at least 1 hour before or 3 hours after these binders.
*   **CYP450 Interactions:** Ketoconazole and other strong CYP3A4 inhibitors may inhibit the enzymes responsible for calcitriol breakdown, potentially increasing calcitriol levels.
*   **Food Interactions:** Can be taken with or without food.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Category C. Use only if the potential benefit justifies the potential risk to the fetus. Hypercalcemia during pregnancy may produce congenital disorders in the offspring.
*   **Elderly:** Start at the lower end of the dosing range due to decreased renal, hepatic, and cardiac function.
*   **Transplant:** Frequently used in the immediate post-renal transplant period to manage hypocalcemia or persistent SHPT, but must be monitored closely as graft function returns and native 1-alpha-hydroxylase activity recovers.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Administration:** Take exactly as prescribed. Do not increase the dose without provider instruction.
*   **Diet & Supplements:** Strictly adhere to your prescribed low-phosphorus diet. **Do not** take over-the-counter calcium supplements, vitamin D, or magnesium-containing antacids without consulting the nephrology team.
*   **Warning Signs:** Report symptoms of high calcium immediately: profound weakness, bone pain, metallic taste, severe constipation, nausea/vomiting, confusion, or increased thirst/urination.
*   **Storage:** Store at room temperature away from light and moisture.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **Common ICD-10 Codes:**
    *   N25.81 (Secondary hyperparathyroidism of renal origin)
    *   E83.51 (Hypocalcemia)
    *   N18.30 - N18.6 (CKD Stages 3-5, ESRD)
*   **CPT Codes:** Standard E&M codes (99213, 99214, 99215) apply for management.
*   **PA Tips:** Rocaltrol (calcitriol) is generally available as a low-cost generic and rarely requires prior authorization. If required, document baseline iPTH > normal limit and normal/low serum calcium.

## Class Comparison & Decision Trees

While Rocaltrol (calcitriol) is the endogenous active hormone, synthetic analogs were developed to provide PTH suppression with theoretically less intestinal calcium and phosphorus absorption.

### Clinical Decision Tree: Managing SHPT with Active Vitamin D

**1. When to START Active Vitamin D:**
*   **Non-Dialysis (CKD G3-G5):** 
    *   Is 25-OH Vit D <30 ng/mL? → *Treat with nutritional Vit D (cholecalciferol) first.*
    *   Is Phosphorus >5.5 mg/dL? → *Treat with diet/binders first.*
    *   Is iPTH progressively rising despite the above? → *Consider starting low-dose Rocaltrol (calcitriol) 0.25 mcg daily.*
*   **Dialysis (ESRD):**
    *   Is iPTH >600 pg/mL? → *Start active Vitamin D (calcitriol, paricalcitol, or doxercalciferol).*

**2. When to ESCALATE Therapy:**
*   If iPTH remains above target (e.g., >600 pg/mL in ESRD) after 4 weeks AND Calcium is <9.5 mg/dL AND Phosphorus is <5.5 mg/dL:
    *   *Action:* Increase Rocaltrol (calcitriol) dose by 0.25 mcg/day or 0.5 mcg/pulse.

**3. When to SWITCH Agents:**
*   If patient develops hypercalcemia (Ca >10.2 mg/dL) on Rocaltrol (calcitriol) but iPTH remains uncontrolled:
    *   *Action:* Switch to a Vitamin D analog (Zemplar/paricalcitol) OR switch class to a calcimimetic (Sensipar/cinacalcet) [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED for preferred formulary step-therapy].

**4. When to HOLD or STOP Therapy:**
*   Hold therapy if Corrected Calcium >10.2 mg/dL.
*   Hold therapy if Phosphorus >6.0 mg/dL (until controlled with binders).
*   Hold therapy if iPTH falls below 150 pg/mL in dialysis patients (to prevent adynamic bone disease). Resume at 50% dose when iPTH recovers.
