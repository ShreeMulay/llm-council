## Thymoglobulin (antithymocyte globulin [rabbit])
**Aliases:** rATG, ATG, rabbit ATG

### Mechanism of Action
Thymoglobulin (antithymocyte globulin [rabbit]) is a purified, pasteurized, gamma immune globulin obtained by immunizing rabbits with human thymocytes. It contains cytotoxic antibodies directed against a wide array of T-cell surface antigens (including CD2, CD3, CD4, CD8, CD11a, CD18, CD25, CD44, CD45, HLA-DR, and others). 
*   **Primary Effect:** Induces profound, rapid, and prolonged T-cell depletion in the peripheral blood and lymphoid tissues via complement-dependent cytotoxicity, cell-mediated antibody-dependent cytotoxicity, and apoptosis.
*   **Secondary Effect:** Modulates T-cell activation, homing, and cytotoxic activities. T-cell counts typically remain depressed for months to over a year post-administration.

### Indications in Nephrology
**FDA-Approved Indications:**
*   **Kidney Transplant Rejection:** Treatment of acute renal transplant rejection in conjunction with concomitant immunosuppression.
*   **Kidney Transplant Induction (Off-label but standard of care):** Prevention of acute rejection in kidney transplant recipients (KDIGO recommends lymphocyte-depleting agents for induction in high-immunologic-risk patients).

**Key Supporting Clinical Trials:**
*   **Brennan et al. (NEJM, 2006):** Compared Thymoglobulin (antithymocyte globulin [rabbit]) vs. Simulect (basiliximab) for induction in high-risk kidney transplant recipients. 
    *   *Outcomes:* Thymoglobulin significantly reduced the incidence of acute rejection at 12 months (15.6% vs. 25.5%, p=0.02) but was associated with a higher incidence of infection (85.8% vs. 75.2%) and cytomegalovirus (CMV) disease.
*   **Gaber et al. (Transplantation, 1998):** Double-blind, randomized trial comparing Thymoglobulin (rabbit) vs. Atgam (equine) for acute rejection.
    *   *Outcomes:* Thymoglobulin showed a higher successful rejection reversal rate (88% vs. 76%) and better 1-year graft survival.

### Dosing
*Note: Dosing is typically based on Actual Body Weight (ABW), but institutional protocols may cap doses or use Ideal Body Weight (IBW) in obese patients. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED] for weight-based dosing strategy.*

**Standard Dosing & Titration:**
*   **Induction (High Immunologic Risk):** 1 to 1.5 mg/kg/day IV for 3 to 5 days (Total cumulative dose: 3 to 7.5 mg/kg). First dose is typically administered intraoperatively prior to allograft reperfusion.
*   **Treatment of Acute Rejection:** 1.5 mg/kg/day IV for 7 to 14 days.
*   **Premedication (Mandatory):** Administer 30-60 minutes prior to each infusion to prevent Cytokine Release Syndrome (CRS):
    *   Corticosteroid: Solu-Medrol (methylprednisolone) 100-500 mg IV (dose tapers over subsequent days)
    *   Antipyretic: Tylenol (acetaminophen) 650-1000 mg PO
    *   Antihistamine: Benadryl (diphenhydramine) 25-50 mg PO or IV

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
Thymoglobulin (antithymocyte globulin [rabbit]) is a large protein cleared by the reticuloendothelial system, not the kidneys. No renal dose adjustments are required.
*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min:** No adjustment
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min:** No adjustment
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min:** No adjustment
*   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** No adjustment

**Hepatic Adjustments:**
*   No specific dose adjustments provided in the manufacturer labeling; use with caution in severe hepatic impairment due to increased infection risk.

### Monitoring
**Baseline Labs Required:**
*   CBC with differential (baseline WBC and platelets are critical)
*   Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP)
*   Viral serologies (CMV, EBV, HIV, HBV, HCV)
*   TB screening (QuantiFERON-TB Gold or T-SPOT)

**Follow-up Lab Schedule:**
*   **Daily during therapy:** CBC with differential (monitor for profound leukopenia and thrombocytopenia).
*   **Post-therapy:** CBC weekly for 1 month, then monthly. Monitor CMV and EBV PCRs per transplant center protocol (typically monthly for 6-12 months).

**Therapeutic Drug Monitoring / Target Labs:**
*   **CD3 Count Monitoring (Optional/Protocol-driven):** Some centers monitor absolute CD3+ T-cell counts to guide dosing. Target CD3 count is typically < 20 to 50 cells/mm³. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED] to determine if CD3 monitoring is utilized locally.
*   **Dose Modification Decision Tree (Toxicity):**
    *   If WBC is 2,000 - 3,000 cells/mm³ OR Platelets are 50,000 - 75,000 cells/mm³: **Reduce dose by 50%**.
    *   If WBC < 2,000 cells/mm³ OR Platelets < 50,000 cells/mm³: **Hold or stop therapy**.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
**Common Side Effects (>5%):**
*   Cytokine Release Syndrome (fever, chills, rigors, headache, nausea, tachycardia) - primarily with the 1st and 2nd doses.
*   Hematologic: Leukopenia (up to 60%), thrombocytopenia (up to 40%), anemia.
*   Infections: Increased susceptibility to bacterial, viral (CMV, EBV, BK virus), and fungal (PJP) infections.

**Serious/Rare Side Effects:**
*   Serum sickness (typically 7-14 days post-infusion)
*   Post-Transplant Lymphoproliferative Disorder (PTLD) and other malignancies
*   Anaphylaxis

**Black Box Warnings:**
*   **Medical Supervision:** Must be administered under the supervision of a physician experienced in immunosuppressive therapy.
*   **Anaphylaxis:** Can cause severe, life-threatening anaphylaxis. Epinephrine and emergency equipment must be immediately available.

**Contraindications:**
*   *Absolute:* History of severe systemic reaction/anaphylaxis to rabbit proteins or any product excipients; active acute systemic infection.
*   *Relative:* Pre-existing severe leukopenia or thrombocytopenia.

**CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:**
*   Patients with ESRD/advanced CKD often have baseline uremic immune dysfunction; the addition of Thymoglobulin (antithymocyte globulin [rabbit]) creates profound immunosuppression, requiring aggressive prophylaxis against PJP (Bactrim), CMV (Valcyte), and fungal infections.

### Drug Interactions
*   **Other Immunosuppressants:** Additive risk of over-immunosuppression, leading to opportunistic infections and malignancies.
*   **Live Vaccines:** STRICTLY CONTRAINDICATED during and for at least 2-6 months after therapy (e.g., MMR, Varicella, Yellow Fever, live attenuated influenza).
*   **CYP450 Interactions:** None (not metabolized by CYP enzymes).

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Historically Category C. May cause fetal harm. Use only if potential benefit justifies the potential risk to the fetus. Women of childbearing potential should use effective contraception during and for a period after therapy.
*   **Elderly Considerations in CKD:** Older adults have a significantly higher risk of severe infections, CMV viremia, and PTLD. Consider lower cumulative doses (e.g., 3-4.5 mg/kg total) or alternative induction (e.g., basiliximab) if immunologic risk allows. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]
*   **Transplant Considerations:** Standard of care for high-immunologic-risk kidney transplant recipients (e.g., high PRA, repeat transplant, African American descent, prolonged cold ischemia time).

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Infusion Reactions:** Explain that the first few doses often cause flu-like symptoms (fever, chills, body aches). Reassure them that premedications will be given to minimize this.
*   **Infection Risk:** Emphasize that their immune system will be profoundly weakened. They must avoid sick contacts, practice strict hand hygiene, and take all prophylactic antimicrobial medications exactly as prescribed.
*   **Warning Signs to Report:** Instruct patients to immediately report fever > 100.4°F (38°C), chills, severe fatigue, unusual bleeding/bruising, or shortness of breath.
*   **Administration:** Inform patients that the infusion takes several hours (typically 4-6 hours for the first dose) and is usually given through a central line to prevent vein irritation.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **Common ICD-10 Codes:**
    *   Z48.22 (Encounter for aftercare following kidney transplant)
    *   T86.10 (Unspecified complication of kidney transplant)
    *   T86.11 (Kidney transplant rejection)
*   **CPT/HCPCS Codes:**
    *   J0895 (Injection, quinupristin/dalfopristin, 10 mg - *Note: historically used, but verify current HCPCS for Thymoglobulin which is often billed under J7511 for lymphocyte immune globulin, antithymocyte globulin, rabbit, 25 mg*). **Correct Code:** J7511 (Antithymocyte globulin, rabbit, 25 mg).

## Class Comparison: Polyclonal Antibodies

While Thymoglobulin (rabbit) is the dominant agent in modern nephrology practice, Atgam (equine) remains available. Understanding the differences is critical for managing allergies or drug shortages.

| Feature | Thymoglobulin (rabbit ATG) | Atgam (equine ATG) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Source** | Rabbit | Horse (Equine) |
| **Standard Induction Dose** | 1 - 1.5 mg/kg/day IV for 3-5 days | 10 - 15 mg/kg/day IV for 14 days |
| **eGFR Adjustments** | None | None |
| **Potency / Depletion** | More profound and prolonged T-cell depletion | Less profound depletion; faster T-cell recovery |
| **Key Trials** | Gaber et al. (1998): Superior rejection reversal vs Atgam | Historically used; largely replaced by Thymoglobulin |
| **Infusion Route** | Central line preferred (can use peripheral with heparin/hydrocortisone) | Central line required |
| **Skin Testing** | Not routinely required | **Required** before first dose (high anaphylaxis risk to equine proteins) |
| **Cost** | High (~$800 - $1,000 per 25 mg vial) | High |

### When to Choose One Agent Over Another
*   **First-Line:** **Thymoglobulin (antithymocyte globulin [rabbit])** is the preferred depleting agent for both induction (in high-risk patients) and treatment of acute cellular rejection due to superior efficacy, better side effect profile, and no requirement for pre-infusion skin testing.
*   **Alternative:** **Atgam (antithymocyte globulin [equine])** is generally reserved for patients who have a known severe allergy to rabbit proteins or who have developed neutralizing antibodies to rabbit ATG from previous exposure.

### Switching Between Agents Within the Class
*   Switching from Thymoglobulin to Atgam (or vice versa) is rare but may be necessary in cases of severe serum sickness or anaphylaxis attributed to the specific animal protein.
*   If switching to Atgam, **equine skin testing is mandatory** prior to administration.
*   Do not use a 1:1 dose conversion. Note the massive dosing difference (Thymoglobulin is ~1.5 mg/kg; Atgam is ~15 mg/kg). Ensure order sets are strictly separated to prevent fatal dosing errors. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED] for cross-checking ATG orders.
