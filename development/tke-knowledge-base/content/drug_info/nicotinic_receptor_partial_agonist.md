## Chantix (varenicline)

### Mechanism of Action
Chantix (varenicline) is a highly selective partial agonist at the $\alpha_4\beta_2$ neuronal nicotinic acetylcholine receptor. It functions through a dual mechanism:
1. **Agonist activity:** Binds to the receptor to stimulate a low-level release of dopamine, which mitigates nicotine withdrawal symptoms and cravings.
2. **Antagonist activity:** Competitively blocks inhaled nicotine from binding to the $\alpha_4\beta_2$ receptors, thereby blunting the rewarding and reinforcing effects of smoking.

### Indications in Nephrology
* **FDA-Approved Indication:** Smoking cessation.
* **Relevance to Nephrology:** The **KDIGO 2024 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of CKD** strongly recommends smoking cessation to slow CKD progression, reduce proteinuria, and mitigate the exceptionally high cardiovascular (CV) risk inherent to the CKD population.
* **Key Supporting Clinical Trials:**
  * **EAGLES Trial (2016):** A randomized, double-blind trial comparing Chantix (varenicline), Zyban (bupropion), nicotine patch, and placebo in 8,144 smokers with and without psychiatric disorders. 
    * *Outcomes:* Chantix (varenicline) demonstrated superior continuous abstinence rates at weeks 9-12 (38.0%) compared to Zyban (bupropion) (26.1%), nicotine patch (26.4%), and placebo (13.7%). It also proved that Chantix (varenicline) does *not* significantly increase the risk of moderate-to-severe neuropsychiatric adverse events compared to placebo.
  * **EVITA Trial (2015):** Evaluated Chantix (varenicline) in patients with acute coronary syndrome. Demonstrated safety and improved cessation rates in high-risk CV patients, a highly relevant population for nephrology.

### Dosing
Chantix (varenicline) requires a 1-week titration to minimize gastrointestinal side effects (nausea). 

**Standard Dosing & Titration Schedule:**
* **Days 1–3:** 0.5 mg PO once daily
* **Days 4–7:** 0.5 mg PO twice daily
* **Day 8 to End of Treatment (Week 12):** 1 mg PO twice daily
* *Duration:* Standard course is 12 weeks. An additional 12-week course is recommended for patients who successfully quit to increase the likelihood of long-term abstinence.

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range (CRITICAL):**
Chantix (varenicline) is primarily eliminated unchanged in the urine (~92%). Dose reduction is mandatory in severe renal impairment to prevent drug accumulation and toxicity.
* **eGFR $\ge$ 60 mL/min/1.73m²:** Standard dosing (titrate to 1 mg PO twice daily).
* **eGFR 30–59 mL/min/1.73m²:** Standard dosing. No adjustment required, but monitor closely for adverse effects (especially nausea/insomnia).
* **eGFR 15–29 mL/min/1.73m²:** **Maximum dose 0.5 mg PO twice daily.** 
  * *Titration:* Start 0.5 mg PO once daily for 3 days, then increase to 0.5 mg PO twice daily.
* **eGFR <15 mL/min/1.73m² / Dialysis (HD/PD):** **Maximum dose 0.5 mg PO once daily.**
  * *Note:* Hemodialysis removes Chantix (varenicline), but the dose remains capped at 0.5 mg once daily. Administer post-dialysis on hemodialysis days.

*Hepatic Adjustment:* No dose adjustment necessary.

### Monitoring
* **Baseline Labs:** 
  * Serum creatinine / eGFR (to determine appropriate dosing tier).
  * Pregnancy test in women of childbearing age.
* **Follow-up Schedule:**
  * **Week 1-2:** Contact patient to assess tolerance (nausea), mood changes, and quit status. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Define whether this is an APP visit, RN phone call, or automated outreach].
  * **Week 4 & Week 12:** Assess smoking status, blood pressure, and heart rate. Target abstinence (0 cigarettes/day).
* **Therapeutic Drug Monitoring:** Not applicable. No trough levels required.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
* **Common Side Effects (>5%):** 
  * Nausea (up to 30% - dose-dependent)
  * Insomnia and abnormal/vivid dreams
  * Headache
* **Serious/Rare Side Effects:**
  * Somnambulism (sleepwalking)
  * Hypersensitivity reactions (angioedema)
  * Severe cutaneous adverse reactions (Stevens-Johnson Syndrome, Erythema Multiforme)
* **Black Box Warnings:** 
  * *Historical Note:* The FDA removed the Black Box Warning for serious neuropsychiatric events in 2016 based on the EAGLES trial. However, providers must still monitor for changes in behavior, hostility, agitation, depressed mood, and suicidal ideation.
* **Contraindications:**
  * *Absolute:* Known history of serious hypersensitivity reactions or skin reactions to Chantix (varenicline).
* **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:**
  * Accumulation in advanced CKD (eGFR <30) dramatically increases the risk of severe nausea and neuropsychiatric side effects if the dose is not appropriately reduced. 

### Drug Interactions
* **CYP450 Interactions:** None clinically significant. Chantix (varenicline) is not metabolized by and does not inhibit/induce CYP450 enzymes.
* **Major Interactions:**
  * **Cimetidine:** Inhibits renal secretion of Chantix (varenicline) via OCT2, increasing varenicline exposure by 29%. Avoid concurrent use in severe CKD.
  * **Nicotine Replacement Therapy (NRT):** Concurrent use with nicotine patches increases the incidence of nausea, headache, and fatigue without significant synergistic efficacy. Generally avoid combination unless directed by a specialized cessation clinic.
* **Food Interactions:** None that alter absorption, but **must be taken with food** to reduce nausea.

### Special Populations
* **Pregnancy:** No adequate data. Weigh risks vs. benefits. Smoking during pregnancy carries severe risks, but non-pharmacologic methods are preferred first-line.
* **Elderly Considerations in CKD:** Older adults frequently have age-related eGFR decline. Strictly calculate eGFR and adjust dose to $\le$ 0.5 mg BID if eGFR <30. Monitor closely for falls secondary to sleep disturbances.
* **Transplant Considerations:** Safe to use. No interactions with calcineurin inhibitors (Prograf (tacrolimus), Gengraf/Neoral (cyclosporine)) or antimetabolites (CellCept (mycophenolate)). 

### Patient Counseling Points
* **Administration:** "Take this medication *after eating a full meal* and with a *full 8 oz glass of water*. This is the best way to prevent nausea."
* **Quit Approaches:** 
  * *Fixed Quit Approach:* Set a quit date for Day 8 (after the 1-week titration).
  * *Flexible Quit Approach:* Begin taking the medication and pick a quit date between Days 8 and 35.
* **Warning Signs to Report:** "Stop the medication and call our clinic immediately if you experience agitation, depressed mood, suicidal thoughts, or changes in behavior that are not typical for you."
* **Expectations:** "You may experience vivid, strange dreams or trouble sleeping. If this happens, taking your second dose earlier in the evening (e.g., at dinner instead of bedtime) can help."

### Prior Authorization & Coding
* **Common ICD-10 Codes:**
  * F17.210 - Nicotine dependence, cigarettes, uncomplicated
  * F17.211 - Nicotine dependence, cigarettes, in remission
  * Z87.891 - Personal history of nicotine dependence
* **CPT Codes for Counseling (Can be billed alongside E&M with modifier 25):**
  * 99406 - Smoking and tobacco use cessation counseling visit; intermediate, greater than 3 minutes up to 10 minutes.
  * 99407 - Smoking and tobacco use cessation counseling visit; intensive, greater than 10 minutes.
* **Tips for PA Success:** Document previous failures with over-the-counter Nicotine Replacement Therapy (NRT). Note that generic varenicline is now widely available and often preferred by Part D and commercial formularies.
* **Cost Considerations:** Generic varenicline significantly reduces cost. Patient assistance programs are available through generic manufacturers for uninsured patients.

## Class Comparison: Smoking Cessation Pharmacotherapy in CKD

*Note: While Chantix (varenicline) is the only nicotinic receptor partial agonist, nephrology providers must choose between it and other cessation classes. This table compares the primary pharmacologic options.*

| Feature | Chantix (varenicline) | Zyban / Wellbutrin (bupropion SR) | Nicotine Replacement Therapy (NRT) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Class** | Nicotinic partial agonist | NDRI (Antidepressant) | Nicotine agonist |
| **Standard Dosing** | 1 mg PO BID | 150 mg PO BID | Varies by form (Patch, gum, lozenge) |
| **eGFR $\ge$ 60** | 1 mg BID | 150 mg BID | Standard dosing |
| **eGFR 30-59** | 1 mg BID | 150 mg once daily | Standard dosing |
| **eGFR <30** | Max 0.5 mg BID | 150 mg every other day | Standard dosing (monitor for CV effects) |
| **Dialysis** | Max 0.5 mg Daily | 150 mg every other day | Standard dosing |
| **Efficacy (Quit Rate)** | Highest (~30-38%) | Moderate (~20-26%) | Moderate (~20-26%) |
| **Key Contraindications** | Severe hypersensitivity | **Seizure disorder**, eating disorders, MAOI use | Immediate post-MI, severe arrhythmias |
| **Cost** | Moderate (Generic available) | Low (Generic available) | Low (OTC available) |

### Decision Tree: Choosing and Managing Therapy

**1. When to Choose Chantix (varenicline):**
* **First-line therapy** for most CKD patients due to superior efficacy (per EAGLES trial).
* Preferred in patients with a history of seizures (where bupropion is contraindicated).
* Preferred in patients who have failed NRT or bupropion in the past.

**2. When to Choose an Alternative (Zyban (bupropion) or NRT):**
* Patient cannot tolerate the nausea associated with Chantix (varenicline).
* Patient has a history of severe hypersensitivity to varenicline.
* Patient has concurrent untreated depression (Zyban (bupropion) may offer dual benefit, though psychiatric consultation is recommended).

**3. When to Escalate or Switch:**
* If a patient on Chantix (varenicline) relapses after a 12-week course, assess adherence and side effects.
* If well-tolerated but unsuccessful, consider a combination of Chantix (varenicline) + NRT (off-label, requires close monitoring for nausea/hypertension) or switch to Zyban (bupropion).
* [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Referral threshold to specialized behavioral health or dedicated smoking cessation clinics for refractory nicotine dependence].

**4. When to Stop Therapy:**
* Immediately discontinue if the patient develops severe neuropsychiatric symptoms, somnambulism, or signs of Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (rash with mucosal involvement).
* Stop at 12 weeks if the patient has made no progress toward quitting; reassess readiness to quit at a future visit.
