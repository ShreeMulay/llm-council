# Clinical Drug Monograph: Phosphate Binders

**Guideline Reference:** KDIGO 2017 Clinical Practice Guideline Update for the Diagnosis, Evaluation, Prevention, and Treatment of Chronic Kidney Disease-Mineral and Bone Disorder (CKD-MBD).
**Target:** Lower elevated phosphate levels toward the normal range (2.5–4.5 mg/dL) while avoiding hypercalcemia. KDIGO 2017 explicitly recommends restricting the dose of calcium-based phosphate binders across all CKD stages to minimize vascular calcification.

---

## Velphoro (sucroferric oxyhydroxide)

### Mechanism of Action
Velphoro (sucroferric oxyhydroxide) is a polynuclear iron(III)-oxyhydroxide, calcium-free, chewable phosphate binder. It binds dietary phosphate in the gastrointestinal tract to form insoluble iron-phosphate complexes, which are excreted in the feces, thereby reducing serum phosphorus levels.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Control of serum phosphorus levels in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) on dialysis.
*   **Off-Label:** Management of hyperphosphatemia in non-dialysis CKD stages 3-5.
*   **Key Trials:** 
    *   *Phase 3 Study (Floege et al., 2014):* Demonstrated non-inferiority to Renvela (sevelamer carbonate) in lowering serum phosphorus in dialysis patients, with a significantly lower pill burden (average 3.3 pills/day vs. 8.1 pills/day).

### Dosing
*   **Standard Starting Dose:** 500 mg (1 chewable tablet) three times daily with meals.
*   **Titration:** Increase by 500 mg/day every 2 to 4 weeks until target serum phosphorus (2.5–4.5 mg/dL) is reached. Maximum dose: 3,000 mg/day (6 tablets).
*   **CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
    *(Note: Velphoro is minimally absorbed systemically; dosing is based on dietary intake and serum PO4, not renal clearance).*
    *   **eGFR ≥60:** 500 mg TID with meals (Off-label)
    *   **eGFR 30-59:** 500 mg TID with meals (Off-label)
    *   **eGFR 15-29:** 500 mg TID with meals (Off-label)
    *   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** 500 mg TID with meals (FDA-approved)
*   **Hepatic Adjustments:** None required.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs:** Serum phosphorus, corrected calcium, intact PTH (iPTH), iron panel (ferritin, TSAT).
*   **Follow-up Schedule:** Serum phosphorus every 2-4 weeks during titration, then every 1-3 months once stable. Monitor iron indices (ferritin, TSAT) every 3 months, as minimal iron absorption can occur.
*   **Lab Targets:** Serum PO4 toward normal (2.5-4.5 mg/dL).

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Discolored (dark/black) feces (up to 20%), diarrhea (24%).
*   **Serious/Rare:** Gastrointestinal bleeding (rare, but dark stools can mask this).
*   **Black Box Warnings:** None.
*   **Contraindications:** Absolute: Hemochromatosis or other iron accumulation disorders.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** While iron absorption is minimal, patients on concurrent IV iron therapy require close monitoring of TSAT and ferritin to avoid iron overload.

### Drug Interactions
*   **Major Interactions:** 
    *   *Doxycycline/Tetracyclines:* Take at least 1 hour before Velphoro.
    *   *Levothyroxine:* Separate by at least 4 hours.
    *   *Alendronate:* Separate by at least 1 hour.
    *   *Oral Vitamin D analogs (e.g., paricalcitol):* Monitor PTH closely; absorption may be affected.
*   **Food Interactions:** Must be taken with meals to be effective.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Category B equivalent (minimal systemic absorption; expected to be safe, but use with caution).
*   **Elderly:** No specific dose adjustments needed.
*   **Transplant:** Safe to use, but monitor for interactions with mycophenolate (separate doses).

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Administration:** Tablets MUST be chewed or crushed; do not swallow whole. Take with meals.
*   **Side Effects:** Your stool will turn dark or black. This is a normal, harmless effect of the iron in the medication.
*   **Warning Signs:** Report severe diarrhea or signs of GI bleeding (vomiting blood, severe abdominal pain).

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **ICD-10 Codes:** E83.39 (Other disorders of phosphorus metabolism), N18.6 (ESRD), N18.4/N18.5 (CKD Stage 4/5).
*   **PA Tips:** Often requires documented failure of or intolerance to generic sevelamer or calcium acetate. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Check specific Medicare Part D formulary step-therapy requirements].
*   **Cost:** High tier. Utilize Vifor Pharma patient assistance programs for eligible patients.

## Phoslyra, PhosLo (calcium acetate)

### Mechanism of Action
Phoslyra/PhosLo (calcium acetate) combines with dietary phosphate in the intestines to form insoluble calcium phosphate, which is excreted in the feces. Calcium acetate binds more phosphorus per gram of calcium absorbed compared to calcium carbonate.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Control of hyperphosphatemia in ESRD.
*   **Off-Label:** Hyperphosphatemia in non-dialysis CKD.
*   **Key Trials:** 
    *   *CARE Study (Qunibi et al., 2004):* Compared calcium acetate to sevelamer; calcium acetate provided equivalent phosphate control but with a higher incidence of hypercalcemia.

### Dosing
*   **Standard Starting Dose:** 1,334 mg (2 capsules/tablets or 20 mL liquid) three times daily with meals.
*   **Titration:** Increase by 667 mg (1 cap/tab or 10 mL) per meal every 2-3 weeks until target PO4 is reached. Maximum recommended dose: 4,669 mg/day (7 capsules/tablets).
*   **CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
    *(Note: KDIGO 2017 recommends restricting calcium-based binders across all CKD stages to prevent vascular calcification).*
    *   **eGFR ≥60:** 1,334 mg TID with meals (Use with caution; restrict total elemental calcium)
    *   **eGFR 30-59:** 1,334 mg TID with meals (Use with caution)
    *   **eGFR 15-29:** 1,334 mg TID with meals (Use with caution)
    *   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** 1,334 mg TID with meals
*   **Hepatic Adjustments:** None required.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs:** Serum phosphorus, corrected calcium, iPTH.
*   **Follow-up Schedule:** Serum calcium and phosphorus twice weekly during titration, then monthly.
*   **Lab Targets:** Serum PO4 toward normal. Corrected calcium < 9.5 mg/dL. Calcium x Phosphorus product < 55 mg²/dL².

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Hypercalcemia (up to 20%), nausea, vomiting, constipation.
*   **Serious/Rare:** Vascular and soft tissue calcification, calciphylaxis.
*   **Black Box Warnings:** None.
*   **Contraindications:** Absolute: Hypercalcemia.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** KDIGO 2017 strongly advises restricting the dose of calcium-based binders to avoid positive calcium balance, which drives arterial calcification and cardiovascular mortality in CKD.

### Drug Interactions
*   **Major Interactions:** 
    *   *Fluoroquinolones (e.g., ciprofloxacin):* Take 2 hours before or 6 hours after PhosLo.
    *   *Tetracyclines:* Separate by at least 2 hours.
    *   *Levothyroxine:* Separate by at least 4 hours.
*   **Food Interactions:** Must be taken with meals.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Safe if maternal calcium levels are monitored closely.
*   **Elderly:** Higher risk for constipation and vascular calcification.
*   **Transplant:** Avoid if post-transplant hypercalcemia is present.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Administration:** Take exactly with meals. If you skip a meal, skip the dose.
*   **Diet:** Do not take over-the-counter calcium supplements or calcium-containing antacids (like Tums) without consulting your nephrologist.
*   **Warning Signs:** Report severe constipation, confusion, or muscle weakness (signs of high calcium).

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **ICD-10 Codes:** E83.39, N18.6.
*   **PA Tips:** Generally Tier 1/Generic. Rarely requires PA. Phoslyra (liquid) may require PA showing inability to swallow pills.
*   **Cost:** Low cost (generic). Phoslyra (liquid) is higher cost.

## Renvela, Renagel (sevelamer)

### Mechanism of Action
Renvela (sevelamer carbonate) and Renagel (sevelamer hydrochloride) are non-absorbed, calcium-free, metal-free cross-linked polymeric amines. They bind phosphate in the GI tract via ion exchange. Renvela (carbonate) is preferred over Renagel (hydrochloride) as it helps maintain acid-base balance and reduces the risk of metabolic acidosis. Sevelamer also binds bile acids, incidentally lowering LDL cholesterol.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Control of serum phosphorus in patients with CKD on dialysis.
*   **Off-Label:** Hyperphosphatemia in non-dialysis CKD.
*   **Key Trials:** 
    *   *DCOR Trial (Suki et al., 2007):* Sevelamer vs. calcium-based binders. Showed a trend toward decreased mortality in patients >65 years and significantly fewer hypercalcemic episodes.
    *   *INDependent Study (Russo et al., 2007):* Sevelamer attenuated the progression of coronary artery calcification compared to calcium-based binders in pre-dialysis CKD.

### Dosing
*   **Standard Starting Dose:** 
    *   Serum PO4 5.5 - 7.5 mg/dL: 800 mg TID with meals.
    *   Serum PO4 > 7.5 mg/dL: 1,600 mg TID with meals.
*   **Titration:** Increase by 800 mg per meal every 2 weeks until target PO4 is reached. Average maintenance dose is 7.2 g/day (3 tablets TID).
*   **CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
    *(Note: Not systemically absorbed; no renal clearance adjustments needed).*
    *   **eGFR ≥60:** 800-1600 mg TID with meals (Off-label)
    *   **eGFR 30-59:** 800-1600 mg TID with meals (Off-label)
    *   **eGFR 15-29:** 800-1600 mg TID with meals (Off-label)
    *   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** 800-1600 mg TID with meals (FDA-approved)
*   **Hepatic Adjustments:** None required.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs:** Serum phosphorus, calcium, chloride, bicarbonate, lipid panel.
*   **Follow-up Schedule:** Serum PO4 every 2-4 weeks during titration. Monitor serum bicarbonate (especially if using Renagel) every 3 months.
*   **Lab Targets:** Serum PO4 toward normal. Bicarbonate > 22 mEq/L.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Nausea, vomiting, dyspepsia, diarrhea, flatulence.
*   **Serious/Rare:** Bowel obstruction, bowel perforation, dysphagia, esophageal stricture.
*   **Black Box Warnings:** None.
*   **Contraindications:** Absolute: Bowel obstruction.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** Renagel (hydrochloride) can exacerbate CKD-associated metabolic acidosis; Renvela (carbonate) is the preferred formulation. Can reduce absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) over time.

### Drug Interactions
*   **Major Interactions:** 
    *   *Mycophenolate mofetil (CellCept):* Sevelamer significantly reduces mycophenolate levels. Separate by at least 2 hours.
    *   *Ciprofloxacin:* Decreases cipro bioavailability by 50%. Take cipro 2 hours before or 6 hours after.
    *   *Levothyroxine:* Separate by at least 4 hours.
*   **Food Interactions:** Must be taken with meals.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Category C equivalent. May decrease maternal absorption of fat-soluble vitamins.
*   **Elderly:** Higher risk for GI motility issues; monitor for severe constipation/obstruction.
*   **Transplant:** Use with extreme caution due to mycophenolate and tacrolimus interaction risks. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Standardize separation times for transplant patients].

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Administration:** Swallow tablets whole. Do not crush, chew, or break (they will expand in water and cause choking). Powder packets are available if you cannot swallow pills.
*   **Diet:** Take right before or during your meal.
*   **Warning Signs:** Stop taking and report immediately if you experience severe stomach pain, severe constipation, or difficulty swallowing.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **ICD-10 Codes:** E83.39, N18.6.
*   **PA Tips:** Generic sevelamer carbonate is widely covered without PA. Brand Renvela or powder packets may require PA showing dysphagia or intolerance to generic tablets.
*   **Cost:** Generic is moderately priced.

## Fosrenol (lanthanum carbonate)

### Mechanism of Action
Fosrenol (lanthanum carbonate) is a non-calcium, non-resin phosphate binder. It dissociates in the acidic environment of the upper GI tract to release lanthanum ions, which bind dietary phosphate to form highly insoluble lanthanum phosphate complexes excreted in feces.

### Indications in Nephrology
*   **FDA-Approved:** Reduction of serum phosphate in patients with ESRD.
*   **Off-Label:** Hyperphosphatemia in non-dialysis CKD.
*   **Key Trials:** 
    *   *Hutchison et al. (2005):* Demonstrated long-term efficacy and tolerability of lanthanum carbonate over 3 years, with effective phosphate control and no evidence of aluminum-like bone toxicity.

### Dosing
*   **Standard Starting Dose:** 1,500 mg/day divided and taken with meals (e.g., 500 mg TID).
*   **Titration:** Increase by 750 mg/day every 2-3 weeks until target PO4 is reached. Usual maintenance dose is 1,500 to 3,000 mg/day. Maximum: 4,500 mg/day.
*   **CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
    *(Note: Minimal systemic absorption; no renal clearance adjustments needed).*
    *   **eGFR ≥60:** 500 mg TID with meals (Off-label)
    *   **eGFR 30-59:** 500 mg TID with meals (Off-label)
    *   **eGFR 15-29:** 500 mg TID with meals (Off-label)
    *   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** 500 mg TID with meals (FDA-approved)
*   **Hepatic Adjustments:** Lanthanum is excreted in bile. Use with caution in severe hepatic impairment.

### Monitoring
*   **Baseline Labs:** Serum phosphorus, calcium.
*   **Follow-up Schedule:** Serum PO4 every 2-3 weeks during titration, then monthly.
*   **Lab Targets:** Serum PO4 toward normal.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
*   **Common (>5%):** Nausea (11%), vomiting (9%), abdominal pain (5%).
*   **Serious/Rare:** Gastrointestinal obstruction, ileus, gastrointestinal perforation (especially if tablets are swallowed whole).
*   **Black Box Warnings:** None.
*   **Contraindications:** Absolute: Bowel obstruction, ileus, fecal impaction.
*   **CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:** Lanthanum is radiopaque and can be seen on abdominal X-rays (may mimic retained contrast or foreign bodies). Theoretical risk of long-term bone deposition, though clinical trials up to 10 years have not shown aluminum-like osteomalacia.

### Drug Interactions
*   **Major Interactions:** 
    *   *Fluoroquinolones & Tetracyclines:* Take at least 2 hours before or 4 hours after Fosrenol.
    *   *Levothyroxine:* Separate by at least 2 hours.
    *   *Antacids (Aluminum/Magnesium/Calcium):* Separate by at least 2 hours.
*   **Food Interactions:** Must be taken with meals.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Category C equivalent. Use only if clearly needed.
*   **Elderly:** Increased risk of GI obstruction. Ensure patient has the dentition to chew tablets completely.
*   **Transplant:** No specific contraindications, but monitor absorption of immunosuppressants.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Administration:** Tablets MUST BE CHEWED COMPLETELY before swallowing. Do not swallow whole (risk of severe bowel blockage). If you have poor teeth/dentures, request the oral powder formulation.
*   **Diet:** Take with or immediately after meals.
*   **Warning Signs:** Report severe stomach pain, inability to pass gas or stool, or severe nausea/vomiting.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **ICD-10 Codes:** E83.39, N18.6.
*   **PA Tips:** Often requires step therapy through sevelamer and/or calcium acetate.
*   **Cost:** High tier. Generic lanthanum carbonate is available but remains relatively expensive.

### Decision Tree: When to Start, Escalate, Switch, or Stop Therapy

**1. When to Start:**
*   Initiate when serum phosphorus remains persistently elevated above the normal range (e.g., >4.5 mg/dL) despite dietary phosphorus restriction (800-1000 mg/day).
*   *First-line choice:* Sevelamer carbonate (Renvela) is generally preferred first-line at TKE to avoid calcium loading, per KDIGO 2017 guidelines. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Confirm preferred first-line agent based on current local payer mix].

**2. When to Escalate:**
*   If PO4 remains >4.5 mg/dL after 2-4 weeks, titrate dose up by 1 pill/meal.
*   Ensure patient compliance and verify they are taking binders *with* meals. (A binder taken without food is useless).

**3. When to Switch:**
*   *Switch from Calcium Acetate to Sevelamer/Velphoro/Fosrenol if:* Corrected calcium > 9.5 mg/dL, PTH is over-suppressed (<150 pg/mL), or evidence of vascular calcification/calciphylaxis.
*   *Switch from Sevelamer to Velphoro/Fosrenol if:* Patient is non-compliant due to high pill burden (e.g., taking 9+ pills/day) or experiences severe GI intolerance/acidosis.
*   *Switch from Velphoro/Fosrenol to Sevelamer if:* Patient cannot tolerate chewable tablets, has poor dentition (for Fosrenol), or refuses due to dark stools (Velphoro).

**4. When to Stop:**
*   Stop or hold if serum phosphorus drops below 2.5 mg/dL.
*   Hold calcium-based binders immediately if hypercalcemia develops.
*   Hold all binders if the patient is NPO (nothing by mouth) for procedures or hospitalization.
