## Repatha (evolocumab)

### Mechanism of Action
Repatha (evolocumab) is a human monoclonal immunoglobulin G2 (IgG2) directed against human proprotein convertase subtilisin kexin type 9 (PCSK9). 
*   **Normal Physiology:** PCSK9 binds to low-density lipoprotein receptors (LDLR) on the surface of hepatocytes, promoting LDLR degradation within the liver. Fewer LDLRs result in higher circulating LDL-C.
*   **Drug Action:** Repatha (evolocumab) binds to PCSK9 and inhibits circulating PCSK9 from binding to the LDLR. This prevents PCSK9-mediated LDLR degradation, allowing LDLRs to recycle back to the hepatocyte surface. The increased number of LDLRs clears LDL-C from the blood, drastically lowering circulating LDL-C levels (often by 50-60%).

### Indications in Nephrology
**FDA-Approved Indications:**
*   Risk reduction of myocardial infarction, stroke, and coronary revascularization in adults with established cardiovascular disease (ASCVD).
*   Primary hyperlipidemia (including heterozygous familial hypercholesterolemia [HeFH]) to reduce LDL-C.
*   Homozygous familial hypercholesterolemia (HoFH).

**Relevance to Nephrology Practice:**
*   **High ASCVD Risk in CKD:** Per KDIGO 2013 Lipid Guidelines and ACC/AHA 2018 Cholesterol Guidelines, CKD patients are considered at high or very high ASCVD risk. Repatha (evolocumab) is utilized when patients cannot achieve target LDL-C on maximally tolerated statin + ezetimibe therapy.
*   **Statin Intolerance:** Highly prevalent in CKD/ESRD patients due to altered pharmacokinetics and polypharmacy.
*   **Kidney Transplant Recipients:** Excellent lipid-lowering option post-transplant because it lacks the cytochrome P450 (CYP3A4) interactions that complicate statin use with calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, cyclosporine).

**Key Supporting Clinical Trials:**
*   **FOURIER Trial (2017):** Demonstrated a 15% relative risk reduction in major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) vs. placebo in patients with established ASCVD. 
*   **FOURIER CKD Sub-analysis (JACC, 2018):** Included 8,077 patients with CKD stage 3 (eGFR 30-59) and 4,500 with eGFR <30. Repatha (evolocumab) reduced LDL-C by 59% across all eGFR groups. The relative risk reduction for MACE was consistent across eGFR groups, but the **absolute risk reduction was greater in patients with more advanced CKD** due to their higher baseline cardiovascular risk. No increase in adverse renal events was observed.

### Dosing
**Standard Dosing:**
*   **Option 1:** 140 mg subcutaneously (SC) every 2 weeks.
*   **Option 2:** 420 mg SC once monthly.
*   *Note:* If switching dosing regimens, administer the first dose of the new regimen on the next scheduled date of the prior regimen.

**CKD Dose Adjustments by eGFR Range:**
Because Repatha (evolocumab) is a monoclonal antibody cleared via the reticuloendothelial system (target-mediated clearance) and not renally excreted, **no dose adjustment is required for any stage of CKD.**
*   **eGFR ≥60:** 140 mg SC Q2W or 420 mg SC Q1M
*   **eGFR 30-59:** 140 mg SC Q2W or 420 mg SC Q1M
*   **eGFR 15-29:** 140 mg SC Q2W or 420 mg SC Q1M
*   **eGFR <15 / Dialysis:** 140 mg SC Q2W or 420 mg SC Q1M *(Note: While pharmacokinetic data in ESRD is limited, clinical use is generally considered safe and effective without adjustment).*

**Hepatic Adjustments:**
*   Mild to Moderate Hepatic Impairment: No adjustment required.
*   Severe Hepatic Impairment: No data available; use with caution.

### Monitoring
**Baseline Labs:**
*   Fasting Lipid Panel (specifically LDL-C)
*   Liver Function Tests (AST, ALT)
*   HbA1c (to establish baseline, though PCSK9i have a neutral effect on glycemia compared to statins)

**Follow-up Lab Schedule:**
*   **4 to 8 weeks post-initiation:** Fasting Lipid Panel to assess therapeutic response.
*   **Annually:** Fasting Lipid Panel once at goal.
*   **Target LDL-C:** 
    *   ACC/AHA 2018 target: **< 70 mg/dL** for very high-risk ASCVD.
    *   Extreme risk (e.g., progressive ASCVD despite LDL <70): Target **< 55 mg/dL**.
    *   *[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]: Confirm preferred TKE practice target for ESRD/Transplant patients with established ASCVD.*

**Therapeutic Drug Monitoring:**
*   Not required. Routine measurement of PCSK9 levels or evolocumab trough levels is not clinically indicated.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
**Common Side Effects (>5%):**
*   Nasopharyngitis (10.5%)
*   Upper respiratory tract infection (9.3%)
*   Injection site reactions (erythema, pain, bruising) (5.7%)
*   Back pain (6.2%)
*   Influenza (7.5%)

**Serious/Rare Side Effects:**
*   Hypersensitivity reactions (rash, eczema, erythema, urticaria).
*   *Note:* Early concerns regarding neurocognitive adverse events (memory loss, confusion) were largely debunked by the EBBINGHAUS trial, which showed no significant difference in cognitive function between Repatha (evolocumab) and placebo.

**Black Box Warnings:**
*   None.

**Contraindications:**
*   **Absolute:** History of serious hypersensitivity reaction to Repatha (evolocumab) or any of its excipients.
*   **Relative:** Needle phobia, severe latex allergy (the needle cover of the prefilled syringe/autoinjector contains dry natural rubber).

**CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:**
*   Highly favorable safety profile in CKD. Does not cause nephrotoxicity. Does not increase the risk of acute kidney injury (AKI). 
*   Unlike statins, there is no increased risk of myopathy or rhabdomyolysis in patients with advanced CKD.

### Drug Interactions
**Major Interactions:**
*   **Statins:** Co-administration with statins increases the clearance of Repatha (evolocumab) by roughly 20% (because statins increase PCSK9 production). However, this interaction is already accounted for in the standard dosing; **no dose adjustment is needed** when used with statins.

**CYP450 Interactions:**
*   **None.** Repatha (evolocumab) is not metabolized by cytochrome P450 enzymes. 
*   *Nephrology Pearl:* This makes it an exceptionally safe choice for kidney transplant patients on Tacrolimus, Cyclosporine, or mTOR inhibitors (Sirolimus/Everolimus), avoiding the severe drug-drug interactions seen with statins.

**Food Interactions:**
*   None.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** No adequate data in pregnant women. Monoclonal antibodies (IgG) cross the placenta, especially in the second and third trimesters. Generally advised to discontinue during pregnancy unless the risk of severe hypercholesterolemia outweighs potential fetal risks.
*   **Lactation:** Unknown if excreted in human milk. Use with caution.
*   **Elderly in CKD:** No dose adjustment needed based on age. Efficacy and safety are comparable to younger populations.
*   **Transplant:** Safe and highly effective. No interaction with calcineurin inhibitors.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **Administration:** Demonstrate proper SC injection technique (abdomen, thigh, or upper arm). Rotate injection sites.
*   **Storage:** Store in the refrigerator (36°F to 46°F). **Do not freeze.** 
*   **Preparation:** Take the medication out of the refrigerator and let it sit at room temperature for at least 30 minutes before injecting to reduce injection site pain. Do not warm it in any other way (no hot water, no microwave).
*   **Room Temp Storage:** Once removed from the refrigerator, it can be kept at room temperature (up to 77°F) for up to 30 days. Protect from direct light.
*   **Warning Signs:** Report any signs of severe allergic reaction (rash, hives, difficulty breathing, swelling of face/throat) immediately.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
PCSK9 inhibitors are heavily restricted by payers and almost always require Prior Authorization (PA).

**Common ICD-10 Codes:**
*   **E78.01** - Familial hypercholesterolemia
*   **E78.00** - Pure hypercholesterolemia, unspecified
*   **I25.10** - Atherosclerotic heart disease of native coronary artery without angina pectoris
*   **I25.2** - Old myocardial infarction

**Tips for Prior Authorization Success:**
1.  **Document Statin Failure:** Most payers require documentation that the patient has tried and failed at least TWO high-intensity statins (e.g., Lipitor (atorvastatin) 40-80mg, Crestor (rosuvastatin) 20-40mg) OR has documented statin intolerance (e.g., severe myalgias, rhabdomyolysis, severe LFT elevation).
2.  **Document Ezetimibe Use:** Many payers require a trial of Zetia (ezetimibe) prior to or concurrent with PCSK9i approval.
3.  **Document Baseline LDL-C:** Must show LDL-C remains > 70 mg/dL despite maximal tolerated oral therapy.
4.  *[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]: Utilize TKE's standard PA template for PCSK9i, ensuring clinical notes explicitly state "statin intolerance due to [reason]" or "max tolerated statin + ezetimibe failure."*

**Cost Considerations:**
*   List price is approximately $5,800/year (significantly reduced from the original $14,000/year launch price).
*   **Patient Assistance:** Amgen offers the "RepathaReady" program, which provides a $5 copay card for commercially insured patients and a patient assistance program for uninsured/underinsured patients who qualify.

## Class Comparison: PCSK9 Targeted Therapies

While Repatha (evolocumab) is the primary agent used, it is important to understand how it compares to the other available PCSK9 monoclonal antibody, Praluent (alirocumab).

### Decision Tree: PCSK9 Inhibitor Therapy in CKD

**1. WHEN TO START:**
*   Patient has established ASCVD (or HeFH/HoFH) **AND**
*   Patient is on maximally tolerated statin + Zetia (ezetimibe) (or has documented statin intolerance) **AND**
*   Fasting LDL-C remains > 70 mg/dL.
*   *Action:* Initiate Repatha (evolocumab) 140 mg SC Q2W.

**2. WHEN TO ESCALATE / ADJUST:**
*   If LDL-C remains > 70 mg/dL after 8 weeks on Repatha (evolocumab) 140 mg Q2W:
    *   Assess medication adherence (most common cause of failure).
    *   *Note:* Increasing to 420 mg Q1M is for *convenience*, not for increased efficacy (total monthly dose is the same). If a patient is truly refractory to PCSK9i + statin + ezetimibe, refer to a lipid specialist or consider adding Nexletol (bempedoic acid).

**3. WHEN TO SWITCH AGENTS:**
*   **Formulary Restrictions:** The choice between Repatha (evolocumab) and Praluent (alirocumab) is almost exclusively driven by patient insurance formulary preference. They are clinically equivalent in efficacy and safety.
*   **Latex Allergy:** If the patient has a severe latex allergy, switch from Repatha (evolocumab) to Praluent (alirocumab).

**4. WHEN TO STOP:**
*   Pregnancy or planned pregnancy.
*   Severe hypersensitivity reaction (anaphylaxis, severe rash).
*   *Note:* Do NOT stop for mild injection site reactions; these usually resolve with proper injection technique (allowing the drug to reach room temperature). Do NOT stop for declining eGFR or initiation of dialysis.
