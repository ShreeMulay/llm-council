# Anti-inflammatory Agents in Nephrology: Colchicine

This monograph provides comprehensive clinical reference guidance for the use of colchicine in nephrology practice at The Kidney Experts (TKE). All recommendations are aligned with the **American College of Rheumatology (ACR) 2020 Guidelines for the Management of Gout** and current FDA prescribing information.

---

## Colcrys, Mitigare (colchicine)

### Mechanism of Action
Colcrys, Mitigare (colchicine) is a unique anti-inflammatory agent that binds to tubulin, causing the depolymerization of microtubules. This disruption impairs leukocyte (specifically neutrophil) chemotaxis, degranulation, and phagocytosis. It also inhibits the activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome, reducing the production of interleukin-1β (IL-1β), a key mediator of crystal-induced inflammation.

### Indications in Nephrology
**FDA-Approved Indications:**
*   **Acute Gout Flares:** Treatment of acute flares (initiate within 12-24 hours of symptom onset).
*   **Gout Prophylaxis:** Prevention of flares during the initiation of urate-lowering therapy (ULT) such as Zyloprim (allopurinol).
*   **Familial Mediterranean Fever (FMF):** Prevention of attacks and secondary AA amyloidosis (a cause of nephrotic syndrome and ESRD).

**Off-Label Uses Common in Nephrology:**
*   **Uremic/Dialysis-Associated Pericarditis:** Used as an adjunct to intensive dialysis and NSAIDs/corticosteroids to reduce recurrence.
*   **Cardiovascular Risk Reduction:** Secondary prevention of CV events in high-risk CKD patients (supported by the **COLCOT [2019]** and **LoDoCo2 [2020]** trials, which demonstrated a ~30% relative risk reduction in major adverse cardiovascular events using 0.5 mg daily). *Note: Lodoco (colchicine) 0.5 mg is now FDA-approved specifically for this indication.*

### Dosing
**CRITICAL WARNING:** Colcrys, Mitigare (colchicine) has a narrow therapeutic index. Toxicity is highly dependent on renal clearance. **Dose reduction is mandatory in CKD.**

#### 1. Acute Gout Flare Treatment
*Initiate at the first sign of a flare. Efficacy drops significantly if started >36 hours after onset.*
*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min:** 1.2 mg at the first sign of flare, followed by 0.6 mg one hour later (Max: 1.8 mg/day). May repeat treatment course after 3 days.
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min:** 1.2 mg followed by 0.6 mg one hour later. May repeat treatment course after 3 days. Monitor closely for toxicity.
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min:** 1.2 mg followed by 0.6 mg one hour later. **DO NOT repeat treatment course for at least 14 days.**
*   **eGFR <15 mL/min / Dialysis:** 0.6 mg as a single dose. **DO NOT repeat treatment course for at least 14 days.** (Not removed by hemodialysis).

#### 2. Gout Prophylaxis (During ULT Initiation)
*Target serum uric acid <6.0 mg/dL (per ACR 2020 Guidelines).*
*   **eGFR ≥60 mL/min:** 0.6 mg QD or BID.
*   **eGFR 30-59 mL/min:** 0.6 mg QD.
*   **eGFR 15-29 mL/min:** 0.3 mg QD *or* 0.6 mg every other day.
*   **eGFR <15 mL/min / Dialysis:** Generally contraindicated for chronic prophylaxis due to high risk of cumulative toxicity (neuromyopathy). If absolutely clinically necessary, [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]: 0.3 mg twice weekly with extreme clinical vigilance.

#### Hepatic Adjustments
*   **Mild to Moderate:** No adjustment needed, but monitor closely.
*   **Severe:** Avoid use.
*   **Combined Renal and Hepatic Impairment:** **ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION.**

### Monitoring
#### Baseline Labs
*   Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP) for baseline eGFR and LFTs.
*   Complete Blood Count (CBC) to rule out baseline cytopenias.
*   Creatine Kinase (CK), especially if the patient is on concomitant statin therapy.

#### Follow-up Schedule
*   **Frequency:** Every 3-6 months for patients on chronic prophylaxis.
*   **Labs:** CBC (monitor for leukopenia/thrombocytopenia), CMP (monitor eGFR decline requiring dose adjustment), CK (monitor for myopathy).
*   **Therapeutic Drug Monitoring:** Not clinically available or useful. Diagnosis of toxicity is clinical.

### Adverse Effects & Safety
**Common Side Effects (>5%):**
*   Gastrointestinal: Diarrhea (up to 23% - often the first sign of toxicity), nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramping.

**Serious/Rare Side Effects:**
*   **Neuromyopathy:** Proximal muscle weakness and elevated CK. Risk increases significantly in CKD and with concomitant statin use.
*   **Myelosuppression:** Agranulocytosis, aplastic anemia, thrombocytopenia.
*   **Multiorgan Failure:** Colchicine toxicity mimics uremia initially (severe N/V/D) but rapidly progresses to shock, ARDS, DIC, and death.

**Contraindications:**
*   Patients with renal OR hepatic impairment who are taking P-glycoprotein (P-gp) or strong CYP3A4 inhibitors.
*   Patients with BOTH severe renal and severe hepatic impairment.

**CKD-Specific Safety Concerns:**
*   **Toxicity Mimicry:** Early toxicity (N/V/D) is frequently mistaken for uremia in advanced CKD patients, leading to delayed recognition.
*   **Non-Dialyzable:** Colchicine is highly protein-bound and has a large volume of distribution; it is **not** removed by hemodialysis. Overdose in ESRD is frequently fatal.

### Drug Interactions
Colchicine is a substrate of both **CYP3A4** and **P-glycoprotein (P-gp)**. 
*   **Fatal Toxicity Risk:** Co-administration with strong CYP3A4 or P-gp inhibitors in patients with CKD has resulted in fatal colchicine toxicity.
*   **Major Nephrology Interactions:**
    *   **Calcineurin Inhibitors (CNIs):** Neoral, Sandimmune (cyclosporine) is a strong P-gp inhibitor. Prograf (tacrolimus) is a moderate inhibitor. *Avoid colchicine in transplant patients on CNIs if possible; if required, reduce colchicine dose by 50-75%.*
    *   **Statins & Fibrates:** Lipitor (atorvastatin), Crestor (rosuvastatin), Tricor (fenofibrate). Increased risk of severe myopathy and rhabdomyolysis.
    *   **Non-Dihydropyridine CCBs:** Cardizem (diltiazem), Calan (verapamil). Moderate CYP3A4 inhibitors. Reduce colchicine dose.
    *   **Macrolide Antibiotics:** Biaxin (clarithromycin) is a strong CYP3A4 inhibitor. **Absolute contraindication** in CKD.
*   **Food Interactions:** Grapefruit juice inhibits CYP3A4 and must be strictly avoided.

### Special Populations
*   **Pregnancy:** Historically Category C. Colchicine crosses the placenta. Use only if the maternal benefit (e.g., preventing FMF flares) outweighs the potential fetal risk.
*   **Elderly:** Age-related decline in renal function increases toxicity risk. Always calculate eGFR and dose conservatively.
*   **Transplant:** High risk of interaction with anti-rejection medications (cyclosporine/tacrolimus). Use alternative flare treatments (e.g., intra-articular steroids) when possible. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED] for gout management in renal transplant recipients.

### Patient Counseling Points
*   **The "Stop" Rule:** Instruct patients to **STOP** taking the medication immediately and call the clinic if they develop severe diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, or muscle weakness.
*   **Flare Instructions:** If on a renally adjusted dose for an acute flare (eGFR <30), explicitly warn the patient: *"Do not take any more of this medication for at least 14 days, even if the pain returns."*
*   **Dietary Warning:** Do not consume grapefruit or grapefruit juice while taking this medication.
*   **Storage:** Store at room temperature, away from light and moisture.

### Prior Authorization & Coding
*   **Common ICD-10 Codes:**
    *   M10.9 (Gout, unspecified)
    *   M10.30 (Gout due to renal impairment, unspecified site)
    *   E85.0 (Non-neuropathic heredofamilial amyloidosis / FMF)
    *   I30.9 (Acute pericarditis, unspecified)
*   **Prior Authorization Tips:** Generic colchicine tablets are usually Tier 1 or 2 and do not require PA. Mitigare (capsules) or Colcrys (brand tablets) may require a PA showing failure or intolerance to generic colchicine tablets.
*   **Cost Considerations:** Generic colchicine is widely available but can still be moderately expensive depending on the pharmacy. GoodRx or similar discount cards often reduce the cost of a flare pack (few tablets) to under $15.

### Decision Tree: Choosing an Anti-inflammatory for Acute Gout in CKD

When a TKE patient presents with an acute gout flare, use the following hierarchy based on eGFR and comorbidities:

1.  **eGFR ≥60 mL/min (No heart failure, no active PUD):**
    *   *First Line:* NSAIDs (e.g., indomethacin, naproxen) OR Colcrys (colchicine) full dose.
2.  **eGFR 30-59 mL/min:**
    *   *First Line:* Colcrys (colchicine) standard dose OR Systemic Corticosteroids (e.g., prednisone 30-40 mg daily tapered over 7-10 days).
    *   *Avoid:* NSAIDs (due to risk of further eGFR decline).
3.  **eGFR <30 mL/min or Dialysis:**
    *   *First Line:* Systemic Corticosteroids (Prednisone) OR Intra-articular corticosteroid injection (if 1-2 large joints involved).
    *   *Second Line:* Colcrys (colchicine) single 0.6 mg dose (DO NOT repeat for 14 days).
    *   *Absolute Contraindication:* NSAIDs.
4.  **Renal Transplant Recipients:**
    *   *First Line:* Intra-articular corticosteroids OR Systemic Corticosteroids.
    *   *Avoid:* NSAIDs (CNI nephrotoxicity risk) and Colchicine (CNI drug-drug interaction risk).

### Switching Between Agents
*   **Colcrys to Mitigare:** 1:1 conversion (0.6 mg tab = 0.6 mg cap). Usually driven by insurance formulary preference.
*   **Colchicine to Corticosteroids:** If a patient fails to respond to a colchicine flare regimen within 48-72 hours, do NOT give more colchicine. Switch to a short course of oral prednisone (e.g., 30 mg daily for 5 days) or administer an intra-articular injection. Ensure no signs of septic arthritis before initiating steroids.
