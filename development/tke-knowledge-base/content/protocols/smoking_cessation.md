# Clinical Reference: Smoking Cessation in Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

**Guidelines Referenced:** KDIGO 2024 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of CKD; USPSTF 2021 Interventions for Tobacco Smoking Cessation; ACC/AHA 2019 Guideline on the Primary Prevention of Cardiovascular Disease.

---

## 1. Clinical Impact of Smoking on CKD
Tobacco use is a potent, modifiable risk factor that synergistically worsens both renal and cardiovascular outcomes in patients with CKD. KDIGO 2024 guidelines strongly recommend advising all patients with CKD to stop using tobacco products to slow progression and mitigate cardiovascular risk.

*   **Accelerated eGFR Decline:** Smoking induces transient reductions in renal plasma flow and increases renovascular resistance, leading to chronic ischemia and accelerated loss of kidney function.
*   **Increased Proteinuria:** Nicotine-induced sympathetic overactivity and endothelial dysfunction increase glomerular capillary pressure. Smoking cessation is critical for achieving the KDIGO target UACR <30 mg/g.
*   **Cardiovascular Risk Multiplier:** Patients with CKD are already at a profoundly elevated risk for atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) and heart failure. Smoking compounds this risk via oxidative stress, systemic inflammation, and pro-thrombotic effects.
*   **Renal Hypoxia:** Carbon monoxide from combustible tobacco binds to hemoglobin, reducing oxygen delivery to the already hypoxia-sensitive renal tubulointerstitium.

## 2. Clinical Approach: The USPSTF "5 A's" Framework
The USPSTF (2021) recommends that clinicians ask all adults about tobacco use, advise them to stop, and provide behavioral interventions and FDA-approved pharmacotherapy. Implement the "5 A's" at every clinic visit:

1.  **Ask:** Identify and document tobacco use status for every patient at every visit.
2.  **Advise:** In a clear, strong, and personalized manner, urge every tobacco user to quit. *("Quitting smoking is the most important thing you can do to protect your remaining kidney function.")*
3.  **Assess:** Determine willingness to make a quit attempt at this time.
4.  **Assist:** For the patient willing to make a quit attempt, offer pharmacotherapy and behavioral counseling. For those unwilling, utilize Motivational Interviewing (MI) to explore barriers.
5.  **Arrange:** Schedule follow-up contact, in person or via telephone, preferably within the first week after the quit date. [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Establish standard follow-up intervals for MAs/RNs to call patients post-quit date].

## 3. Pharmacotherapy & CKD Dosing Adjustments
Pharmacotherapy should be offered to all patients attempting to quit, barring contraindications. Combination therapy is generally more effective than monotherapy. 

### First-Line Agents

| Drug (Brand/Generic) | Standard Dosing & Titration | CKD Dosing Adjustments & eGFR Thresholds |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chantix (varenicline)** | **Days 1-3:** 0.5 mg daily<br>**Days 4-7:** 0.5 mg BID<br>**Weeks 2-12:** 1 mg BID | **eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m²:** Max dose 0.5 mg BID.<br>**Hemodialysis:** Max dose 0.5 mg daily. |
| **Wellbutrin SR / Zyban (bupropion SR)** | **Days 1-3:** 150 mg daily<br>**Weeks 1-12:** 150 mg BID | **eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m²:** Reduce frequency to 150 mg daily or 150 mg every other day. |
| **Nicoderm CQ (nicotine patch)** | **>10 cigs/day:** 21 mg/day x6wks → 14 mg x2wks → 7 mg x2wks<br>**<10 cigs/day:** 14 mg/day x6wks → 7 mg x2wks | **All CKD Stages:** No dose adjustment required. |
| **Nicorette (nicotine gum/lozenge)** | **1st cig ≤30 min of waking:** 4 mg<br>**1st cig >30 min of waking:** 2 mg<br>Use 1 piece q1-2h (max 24/day) | **All CKD Stages:** No dose adjustment required. |

### Efficacy and Combination Strategies
*   **Most Effective Single Agent:** Chantix (varenicline) has an Odds Ratio (OR) of 2.9 vs. placebo for successful cessation.
*   **Combination Therapy:** Combining Chantix (varenicline) + Nicotine Replacement Therapy (NRT), or a long-acting NRT (patch) + short-acting NRT (gum/lozenge for breakthrough cravings) is more effective than monotherapy.

### ⚠️ Safety Concerns & Contraindications
*   **Chantix (varenicline):** Monitor for neuropsychiatric symptoms (agitation, depressed mood, suicidal ideation). *Note: The FDA black box warning was removed, but vigilance is still required.* Nausea is the most common side effect; advise patients to take with food and a full glass of water.
*   **Wellbutrin/Zyban (bupropion SR):** **CONTRAINDICATED** in patients with a seizure disorder, current/prior diagnosis of anorexia nervosa or bulimia, or patients undergoing abrupt discontinuation of alcohol or sedatives. Lowers the seizure threshold, which is particularly relevant in advanced uremia.
*   **E-cigarettes / Vaping:** **NOT RECOMMENDED** as a cessation aid. The long-term effects on renal endothelium and hemodynamics are unknown, and they maintain nicotine dependence.

### Decision Tree: Pharmacotherapy Management
1.  **Start:** Assess patient preference and contraindications. Initiate Chantix (varenicline) as first-line monotherapy OR baseline NRT patch + breakthrough NRT gum/lozenge.
2.  **Escalate:** If cravings persist on monotherapy at 2-4 weeks, add short-acting NRT (gum/lozenge) to Chantix (varenicline) or NRT patch.
3.  **Switch:** If intolerable side effects occur (e.g., severe nausea on varenicline), switch to Wellbutrin/Zyban (bupropion SR) or NRT, ensuring appropriate eGFR dose adjustments.
4.  **Stop:** Discontinue bupropion or varenicline immediately if severe neuropsychiatric symptoms or seizures occur. Standard therapy duration is 12 weeks; an additional 12 weeks may be considered to prevent relapse in successful quitters.

## 4. Behavioral Counseling & Relapse Prevention
Pharmacotherapy combined with behavioral support yields the highest quit rates. Brief clinical interventions (even 3 minutes) significantly increase cessation rates.

*   **Quitlines:** Actively refer patients to the National Network of Tobacco Cessation Quitlines: **1-800-QUIT-NOW** (1-800-784-8669). [TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Insert local Tennessee/county-specific cessation program referral links or internal behavioral health warm-handoff procedures].
*   **Motivational Interviewing (MI):** Use open-ended questions to explore ambivalence. Focus on the "5 R's" for patients unwilling to quit: Relevance (to their kidney disease), Risks, Rewards, Roadblocks, and Repetition.
*   **Relapse Prevention:** 
    *   **Normalize Failure:** Explicitly tell patients that tobacco dependence is a chronic condition. Most successful quitters fail 2 to 3 times before achieving long-term success. 
    *   Frame a relapse not as a failure, but as practice. Analyze what triggered the relapse to build a better plan for the next attempt.

## 5. Documentation & Billing
Accurate documentation and billing for smoking cessation counseling are essential for compliance and revenue capture. Time spent counseling must be explicitly documented in the note.

### ICD-10 Diagnosis Codes
*   **F17.210:** Nicotine dependence, cigarettes, uncomplicated (Use for active smokers).
*   **F17.211:** Nicotine dependence, cigarettes, in remission.
*   **Z87.891:** Personal history of nicotine dependence (Use for ex-smokers).

### CPT & HCPCS Billing Codes
Counseling codes can be billed in addition to standard E/M codes (append modifier -25 to the E/M code).
*   **CPT 99406:** Smoking and tobacco use cessation counseling visit; intermediate, greater than 3 minutes up to 10 minutes.
*   **CPT 99407:** Smoking and tobacco use cessation counseling visit; intensive, greater than 10 minutes.

## 6. Patient Education Talking Points
Use these specific, kidney-focused talking points during patient encounters to drive motivation:

*   **The Kidney Connection:** "Smoking damages the blood vessels in your kidneys just like it damages your heart. Quitting is one of the few things you can control to directly slow down the loss of your kidney function and delay the need for dialysis."
*   **Protein in the Urine:** "Smoking increases the pressure in your kidney filters, causing them to leak protein. Quitting helps lower this pressure and protects the filters."
*   **Timeline of Improvement:**
    *   *20 minutes:* Blood pressure and heart rate begin to drop.
    *   *2 to 12 weeks:* Circulation improves, which helps deliver better blood flow to your kidneys.
    *   *1 year:* Your excess risk of coronary heart disease (a major risk for kidney patients) is cut in half.
*   **Managing Expectations:** "Cravings are normal and temporary. If you slip up and have a cigarette, don't give up. It takes most people 2 or 3 tries to quit for good. We have medications that can make the cravings much easier to handle."
