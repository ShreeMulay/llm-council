# CLINICAL REFERENCE: Kidney Transplant Immunosuppression

## 1. Overview & Guideline Framework
Kidney transplant immunosuppression is divided into **induction** (intense perioperative therapy to prevent acute rejection) and **maintenance** (long-term therapy to preserve graft function while minimizing toxicity). 

This reference is based on the **KDIGO Clinical Practice Guideline for the Care of Kidney Transplant Recipients (2009, with ongoing updates)** and consensus guidelines from the **American Society of Transplantation (AST)**. The core KDIGO principle is to tailor immunosuppression to the patient's immunologic risk while balancing the risks of infection, malignancy, and drug toxicity.

## 2. Induction Therapy
Induction therapy is administered perioperatively. Selection depends on the patient's immunologic risk profile (e.g., Panel Reactive Antibody [PRA], prior transplants, Donor-Specific Antibodies [DSA], cold ischemia time, and recipient age/ethnicity).

### High Immunologic Risk
*Patients with high PRA (>20%), re-transplants, African American recipients, or positive DSA.*
*   **Agent:** Thymoglobulin (rabbit anti-thymocyte globulin / rATG)
*   **Mechanism:** Polyclonal antibody that depletes T-cells.
*   **Dosing:** 1.5 mg/kg IV daily for 3 to 5 doses (total cumulative dose typically 4.5–7.5 mg/kg).
*   **Administration:** Requires premedication with acetaminophen, diphenhydramine, and corticosteroids to prevent cytokine release syndrome. Administer via central line or high-flow peripheral vein with a 0.22-micron filter.
*   **Monitoring:** Daily CBC. Hold or dose-reduce for severe leukopenia (WBC < 2,000/mm³) or thrombocytopenia (Plt < 50,000/mm³).

### Low Immunologic Risk
*First-time transplant, low PRA, living donor, no DSA.*
*   **Agent:** Simulect (basiliximab)
*   **Mechanism:** IL-2 receptor antagonist (non-depleting).
*   **Dosing:** 20 mg IV on Day 0 (pre-op) and Day 4 post-op.
*   **Safety:** Extremely well tolerated; no cytokine release syndrome; does not require premedication.

### Perioperative Corticosteroids
*   **Agent:** Solu-Medrol (methylprednisolone)
*   **Dosing:** 500 mg IV intraoperatively prior to reperfusion.
*   **Taper:** Rapidly tapered over the first week to oral Deltasone (prednisone) maintenance dosing.

## 3. Maintenance Therapy: Standard Triple Regimen
The KDIGO-recommended first-line maintenance regimen is a Calcineurin Inhibitor (CNI) + Antimetabolite + Corticosteroid.

### A. Calcineurin Inhibitors (CNIs)
**Prograf (tacrolimus)** is the first-line CNI over Neoral/Gengraf (cyclosporine) due to superior graft survival and lower rejection rates.
*   **Formulations:** 
    *   Prograf (tacrolimus immediate-release): Dosed BID.
    *   Envarsus XR (tacrolimus extended-release): Dosed once daily. Often preferred for better adherence and flatter pharmacokinetic curve (less peak-dose neurotoxicity). Conversion from IR to Envarsus XR is typically 80% of the total daily IR dose.
*   **Target Trough Levels (Whole Blood):**
    *   Months 1–3: 8–12 ng/mL
    *   Months 3–6: 6–10 ng/mL
    *   After 6 Months: 5–8 ng/mL
*   **Adverse Effects:** Nephrotoxicity (afferent arteriole vasoconstriction), tremor, New-Onset Diabetes After Transplantation (NODAT), hypertension, alopecia.
*   **🚨 CRITICAL DRUG INTERACTIONS (CYP3A4):** 
    *   *Inhibitors (Increase Tac levels):* Azole antifungals (fluconazole), macrolides (clarithromycin), grapefruit juice, Paxlovid (nirmatrelvir/ritonavir - **absolute contraindication or requires extreme dose holding/monitoring**), non-DHP CCBs (diltiazem, verapamil).
    *   *Inducers (Decrease Tac levels):* Rifampin, phenytoin, carbamazepine, St. John's Wort.

### B. Antimetabolites
*   **Agents:** CellCept (mycophenolate mofetil / MMF) or Myfortic (mycophenolic acid / MPA - enteric coated).
*   **Dosing:** 
    *   CellCept (MMF): 1000 mg PO BID.
    *   Myfortic (MPA): 720 mg PO BID.
*   **Adverse Effects:** Gastrointestinal (diarrhea, nausea, abdominal pain), bone marrow suppression (leukopenia, anemia).
*   **Decision Tree for GI Toxicity:**
    1. Rule out infectious diarrhea (CMV, C. diff).
    2. Switch from CellCept to enteric-coated Myfortic.
    3. Divide dose to TID or QID.
    4. Dose reduce by 25-50%.
    5. If refractory, consider switching to an mTOR inhibitor or azathioprine.
*   **🚨 SAFETY WARNING:** Teratogenic. Absolute contraindication in pregnancy. Women of childbearing age must use two forms of contraception (REMS program).

### C. Corticosteroids
*   **Agent:** Deltasone (prednisone).
*   **Dosing:** Typically starts at 20 mg daily post-discharge, tapering down to a maintenance dose of 5 mg daily by month 3 to 6.
*   **Steroid-Free Protocols:** Some centers utilize early steroid withdrawal (within 7 days). This reduces metabolic side effects (NODAT, bone loss, weight gain) but carries a slightly higher risk of mild acute rejection. *[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Confirm primary transplant center's preferred steroid taper schedule].*

## 4. Alternative Maintenance Agents

### A. mTOR Inhibitors
*   **Agents:** Rapamune (sirolimus) or Zortress (everolimus).
*   **Use Cases:** CNI-sparing protocols (due to CNI nephrotoxicity), patients with a history of severe skin cancers or solid organ malignancies (mTORs have antineoplastic properties).
*   **Adverse Effects:** Impaired wound healing, proteinuria, hyperlipidemia, mouth ulcers, interstitial pneumonitis.
*   **Decision Tree (When to Start/Switch):** Do **not** use immediately post-op due to wound dehiscence risk. Wait at least 1-3 months post-transplant. Discontinue if urine protein-to-creatinine ratio (UPCR) > 800-1000 mg/g.

### B. Co-Stimulation Blockers
*   **Agent:** Nulojix (belatacept).
*   **Mechanism:** Blocks CD28-CD80/86 pathway.
*   **Use Case:** CNI-free maintenance option to preserve renal function (proven in the **BENEFIT trial** to yield superior long-term eGFR compared to cyclosporine).
*   **Dosing:** Administered via monthly IV infusion.
*   **🚨 CONTRAINDICATION:** Absolute contraindication in patients who are Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) seronegative due to a high risk of Post-Transplant Lymphoproliferative Disorder (PTLD), particularly in the CNS.

## 5. Infection Prophylaxis
Immunosuppressed patients require prophylaxis against opportunistic infections for at least the first 3-6 months post-transplant, or following treatment for acute rejection.

| Infection | Agent | Dosing & Duration | eGFR Adjustments |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **CMV** | Valcyte (valganciclovir) | 900 mg PO daily x 3-6 months | **eGFR 40-59:** 450 mg daily<br>**eGFR 25-39:** 450 mg every 2 days<br>**eGFR 10-24:** 450 mg twice weekly |
| **PJP / PCP** | Bactrim (TMP-SMX) | 1 SS or DS tab PO daily x 6-12 months | **eGFR 15-30:** 1 SS tab daily<br>**eGFR <15:** Avoid or use alternative (dapsone/atovaquone) |
| **Fungal** | Nystatin suspension | 5 mL swish & swallow QID x 1-3 months | No renal adjustment needed. |

## 6. Monitoring & Surveillance

### Routine Lab Monitoring
*   **Frequency:** Twice weekly for month 1; weekly for months 2-3; every 2 weeks for months 4-6; monthly thereafter.
*   **Parameters:** CMP (monitor SCr, K+, Glu), CBC (monitor WBC for MMF toxicity), Tacrolimus trough levels.
*   **Target UACR:** < 30 mg/g. New onset proteinuria requires workup for recurrent disease, transplant glomerulopathy, or mTOR toxicity.

### Immunologic Monitoring
*   **Donor-Specific Antibodies (DSA):** Monitored per center protocol (e.g., at 1, 3, 6, 12 months, then annually). De novo DSA indicates high risk for antibody-mediated rejection.
*   **Protocol Biopsies:** Center-dependent. Often performed at 3 months, 1 year, and 2 years to detect subclinical rejection or CNI toxicity. *[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED: Insert local transplant center biopsy schedules].*

## 7. Rejection Management Overview
*Any unexplained rise in serum creatinine (>20% from baseline) or new-onset proteinuria requires evaluation for rejection via allograft biopsy.*

*   **Acute Cellular Rejection (ACR):** T-cell mediated. Characterized by tubulitis and interstitial inflammation.
    *   *Treatment:* High-dose IV Solu-Medrol (500 mg x 3 days). If steroid-resistant, use Thymoglobulin.
*   **Antibody-Mediated Rejection (ABMR):** B-cell/plasma cell mediated. Characterized by DSA, peritubular capillaritis, and C4d deposition.
    *   *Treatment:* Plasmapheresis (PLEX) + IVIG. May add Rituxan (rituximab) or Velcade (bortezomib).

## 8. Long-Term Complications & Primary Care
Nephrology and primary care must collaborate on long-term survivorship per KDIGO guidelines:
*   **Malignancy Screening:** Highest risk for squamous cell skin cancer and PTLD. Annual dermatology exams are mandatory. Annual cervical cancer screening for females.
*   **Cardiovascular Risk:** Leading cause of mortality with a functioning graft. Aggressive BP control (Target < 130/80 mmHg). Initiate statin therapy for all adult recipients (monitor for statin-CNI interactions; rosuvastatin or pravastatin preferred).
*   **Bone Health:** High risk for osteoporosis due to steroids and CKD-MBD. Baseline DEXA scan at 3 months post-transplant. Treat with Vitamin D/calcium; consider bisphosphonates if eGFR > 30 mL/min/1.73m².

## 9. Patient Education Talking Points
*   **Adherence is CRITICAL:** "Missing even a few doses of your anti-rejection medication can cause you to lose your kidney. Take them at the exact same time every day."
*   **Sun Protection:** "Your medications increase your risk of skin cancer significantly. Wear SPF 50+ daily, long sleeves, and wide-brimmed hats."
*   **Vaccines:** "You must **AVOID ALL LIVE VACCINES** (e.g., MMR, yellow fever, live attenuated flu/FluMist). You should get the non-live flu shot annually and stay up to date on COVID-19 and RSV vaccines."
*   **OTC Medications:** "Do not take NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, Motrin, Aleve) as they can damage your new kidney. Tylenol is safe. Do not start any herbal supplements without calling us first."
*   **Infection Signs:** "Call the clinic immediately for fever > 100.4°F, burning with urination, or productive cough."
