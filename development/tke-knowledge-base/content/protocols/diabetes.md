# Clinical Reference: Diabetes Management in Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

**Target Audience:** Nephrologists, Advanced Practice Providers (APPs), and Clinical Staff at The Kidney Experts (TKE)
**Primary Guideline Sources:** KDIGO 2022 Clinical Practice Guideline for Diabetes Management in CKD; ADA 2024 Standards of Medical Care in Diabetes

---

## 1. Guideline Foundations & DKD Screening

The management of Diabetic Kidney Disease (DKD) requires a paradigm shift from purely glucose-centric care to a multifactorial approach prioritizing cardiorenal protection. According to the **KDIGO 2022** and **ADA 2024** guidelines, therapy must be individualized based on eGFR, cardiovascular risk, and hypoglycemia risk.

### Screening Protocols
Early detection of DKD is critical for initiating nephroprotective therapies. 
*   **Type 2 Diabetes (T2DM):** Screen at the time of diagnosis, then annually.
*   **Type 1 Diabetes (T1DM):** Screen 5 years after diagnosis, then annually.
*   **Required Annual Tests:** 
    *   Spot Urine Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio (UACR)
    *   Serum Creatinine with eGFR calculation
*   **Diagnostic Criteria for DKD:** Persistent UACR ≥30 mg/g and/or sustained eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m² for >3 months in the setting of diabetes.

## 2. Glycemic Targets & Monitoring

Glycemic targets in CKD must balance the long-term benefits of microvascular protection against the immediate, severe risks of hypoglycemia—which increases exponentially as kidney function declines.

### A1c Targets
*   **Standard Target:** Generally **<7.0%** for most non-pregnant adults.
*   **Individualized Targets:** Range from **6.5% to <8.0%** based on patient factors.
*   **Strict Avoidance:** **AVOID A1c <6.5%** in patients with advanced CKD, high hypoglycemia risk, limited life expectancy, or extensive comorbidities.

### Monitoring Challenges in Advanced CKD
A1c becomes increasingly **unreliable in advanced CKD (Stage G4-G5 and ESKD)**. 
*   **Falsely Low A1c:** Caused by shortened red blood cell (RBC) lifespan, hemolysis, or recent blood transfusions.
*   **Falsely High A1c:** Caused by iron deficiency anemia or metabolic acidosis.
*   **Alternative Monitoring:** 
    *   **Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM):** Strongly recommended by KDIGO 2022 for patients on insulin or sulfonylureas. Focus on Time in Range (TIR) target >70% (70-180 mg/dL) and Time Below Range (TBR) <4% (<70 mg/dL).
    *   **Fructosamine:** Reflects 2-3 week average glucose; unaffected by RBC turnover (target 200-285 µmol/L).

## 3. Pharmacologic Decision Tree for T2DM in CKD

Follow this stepwise approach for managing T2DM in patients with CKD:

1.  **First-Line Foundation:** Lifestyle modification + **Glucophage (metformin)** + **SGLT2 inhibitor**.
    *   *Note:* Initiate SGLT2i if eGFR ≥20 mL/min/1.73 m².
2.  **Second-Line (Add-on for Glycemic Control or High CV Risk):** **GLP-1 Receptor Agonist**.
    *   Preferred agents with proven CV/renal benefits (e.g., Ozempic (semaglutide), Trulicity (dulaglutide)).
3.  **Third-Line (Additional Glycemic Control Needed):** 
    *   **DPP-4 inhibitors** (if not on GLP-1 RA).
    *   **Insulin** (basal first, then prandial).
    *   *Avoid:* Sulfonylureas in advanced CKD.

## 4. Oral Antidiabetic Agents in CKD

### Glucophage (Metformin)
Metformin remains a first-line agent but requires strict dose titration based on renal clearance to prevent metformin-associated lactic acidosis (MALA).

| eGFR (mL/min/1.73 m²) | Dosing & Management | Monitoring |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **≥45** | Full dose (up to 2000 mg/day). | Routine (annually). |
| **30–44** | **Max dose 1000 mg/day.** Do not initiate new starts in this range. | Monitor eGFR every 3–6 months. |
| **<30** | **DISCONTINUE.** Contraindicated. | N/A |

🚨 **SAFETY WARNING - Lactic Acidosis Risk:** Hold Glucophage (metformin) for iodinated contrast studies, major surgery, or acute illness causing dehydration/hypoxia.

### SGLT2 Inhibitors
*Primary kidney-protective agents in DKD.* 
*   **Agents:** Farxiga (dapagliflozin), Jardiance (empagliflozin), Invokana (canagliflozin).
*   **Action:** Initiate if eGFR ≥20. Continue until initiation of dialysis.
*   *(See comprehensive TKE Clinical Reference: SGLT2 Inhibitors for full dosing and monitoring).*

### DPP-4 Inhibitors
Well-tolerated oral agents with low hypoglycemia risk, but require renal dose adjustments (with one notable exception). Do NOT combine with GLP-1 RAs.

| Drug (Brand/Generic) | eGFR ≥45 | eGFR 30–44 | eGFR <30 / ESKD |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Tradjenta (linagliptin)** | 5 mg daily | **5 mg daily (No adjustment)** | **5 mg daily (No adjustment)** |
| **Januvia (sitagliptin)** | 100 mg daily | 50 mg daily | 25 mg daily |
| **Onglyza (saxagliptin)** | 5 mg daily | 2.5 mg daily | 2.5 mg daily |

### Sulfonylureas
🚨 **SAFETY WARNING:** **AVOID** Glucotrol (glipizide), Diabeta/Micronase (glyburide), and Amaryl (glimepiride) in advanced CKD (Stage G4-G5). 
*   Active metabolites accumulate as eGFR declines, causing severe, prolonged, and potentially fatal hypoglycemia. 
*   If an insulin secretagogue is absolutely necessary, short-acting meglitinides (e.g., Prandin (repaglinide)) are preferred, though generally, this class should be avoided in nephrology practice.

## 5. Injectable Therapies in CKD

### GLP-1 Receptor Agonists
*Second-line agents after SGLT2i for kidney/CV protection and weight management.*
*   **Agents:** Ozempic (semaglutide), Trulicity (dulaglutide), Victoza (liraglutide).
*   *(See comprehensive TKE Clinical Reference: GLP-1 Receptor Agonists for full dosing and monitoring).*

### Insulin Management in CKD
Insulin is partially metabolized and cleared by the kidneys. As eGFR declines, insulin half-life prolongs, drastically increasing the risk of hypoglycemia. **Proactive dose reduction is mandatory.**

**Insulin Dose Adjustment by eGFR:**
*   **eGFR >50:** No dose adjustment required.
*   **eGFR 10–50:** Reduce total daily insulin dose by **25%**.
*   **eGFR <10 or Dialysis:** Reduce total daily insulin dose by **50%**.

**Clinical Pearls for Insulin in CKD:**
*   Basal insulins with ultra-long, flat profiles (e.g., Tresiba (insulin degludec), Toujeo (insulin glargine U-300)) are preferred over NPH to minimize nocturnal hypoglycemia.
*   Monitor closely during transitions to dialysis, as uremia correction can acutely change insulin sensitivity.

## 6. Multifactorial Risk Management

DKD management requires aggressive control of all cardiorenal risk factors, not just glucose.

*   **Blood Pressure:** Target **<120 mm Hg systolic** (KDIGO 2021/2024). First-line therapy must include a maximally tolerated ACE inhibitor (e.g., Zestril (lisinopril)) or ARB (e.g., Cozaar (losartan)).
*   **Proteinuria:** Target UACR **<30 mg/g**. Maximize RAS inhibition, ensure SGLT2i is on board, and consider adding a non-steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (nsMRA) like Kerendia (finerenone) if UACR remains ≥30 mg/g despite standard of care (monitor potassium closely).
*   **Lipids:** Initiate moderate-to-high intensity statin (e.g., Lipitor (atorvastatin) 20-40mg or Crestor (rosuvastatin) 10-20mg) for all patients with DKD aged ≥40, regardless of baseline LDL.
*   **Weight Management:** Target >5% weight loss using lifestyle interventions and GLP-1 RAs.

## 7. Patient Education & Safety

Nephrology providers must actively educate patients on the unique risks of diabetes in the setting of CKD.

*   **Hypoglycemia Recognition:** Educate on symptoms (shaking, sweating, confusion, tachycardia). Ensure patients have a fast-acting carbohydrate source (e.g., glucose tablets) and a Baqsimi (glucagon) nasal powder prescription if on insulin.
*   **CGM Benefits:** Explain that fingersticks only show a moment in time, whereas a CGM shows trends and alerts the patient *before* a dangerous hypoglycemic event occurs.
*   **Sick Day Rules (SADMANS):** Instruct patients to hold specific medications during acute illness (vomiting, diarrhea, fever) to prevent acute kidney injury and lactic acidosis.
    *   **S**ulfonylureas
    *   **A**CE inhibitors
    *   **D**iuretics
    *   **M**etformin
    *   **A**RBs
    *   **N**SAIDs
    *   **S**GLT2 inhibitors

## 8. Coding & Billing

Accurate coding ensures appropriate risk stratification and reimbursement for complex DKD management.

### ICD-10 Codes
Always code the systemic disease (Diabetes) with the renal manifestation, followed by the specific CKD stage.
*   **E11.22:** Type 2 diabetes mellitus with diabetic chronic kidney disease
*   **E11.65:** Type 2 diabetes mellitus with hyperglycemia
*   **E10.22:** Type 1 diabetes mellitus with diabetic chronic kidney disease
*   **N18.x:** Chronic kidney disease (Requires secondary code for stage: N18.31 for G3a, N18.4 for G4, etc.)

### CPT Codes (CGM Management)
*   **95249:** Ambulatory CGM of interstitial tissue fluid; patient-provided equipment, sensor placement, hook-up, calibration, patient training, and printout.
*   **95250:** Ambulatory CGM; physician/APP-provided equipment, hook-up, calibration, training, printout.
*   **95251:** Ambulatory CGM; interpretation and report (requires a minimum of 72 hours of data). Can be billed once per month.
