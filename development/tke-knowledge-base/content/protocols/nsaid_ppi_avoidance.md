# CLINICAL REFERENCE: Nephrotoxin Avoidance and Management in CKD

**Target Audience:** Nephrologists, Advanced Practice Providers (APPs), and Clinical Staff at The Kidney Experts (TKE)
**Guideline Basis:** KDIGO 2024 CKD Evaluation and Management Guidelines, ACR/NKF 2020 Consensus on Contrast-Associated AKI, KDIGO 2012 AKI Guidelines.

---

## 1. Nonsteroidal Anti-inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs)

### Mechanism of Kidney Injury
NSAIDs inhibit Cyclooxygenase (COX-1 and COX-2) enzymes, leading to decreased prostaglandin synthesis. Prostaglandins are required to maintain afferent arteriole vasodilation. Loss of this protective vasodilation causes **afferent arteriole vasoconstriction**, resulting in reduced intraglomerular hydrostatic pressure and an acute drop in Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR).

### Risk Factors for NSAID-Induced AKI
*   **Pre-existing CKD** (eGFR < 60 mL/min/1.73 m²)
*   **Volume Depletion / Dehydration**
*   **The "Triple Whammy":** Concurrent use of NSAIDs + RAAS blockade (ACEi/ARB) + Diuretics
*   **Comorbidities:** Heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome
*   **Advanced Age:** > 65 years old

### ⚠️ SAFETY WARNING: ALL NSAIDs are Nephrotoxic
There is **NO safe oral NSAID** in chronic kidney disease. All of the following must be strictly avoided in patients with eGFR < 60 mL/min/1.73 m² unless explicitly approved by nephrology:
*   Advil / Motrin (ibuprofen)
*   Aleve / Naprosyn (naproxen)
*   Voltaren (diclofenac)
*   Celebrex (celecoxib)
*   Mobic (meloxicam)

**Topical NSAIDs:** Voltaren (diclofenac) gel has significantly lower systemic absorption (~6% of oral equivalent). While safer, it can still cause AKI in highly susceptible patients. Use with caution, limit application area, and monitor serum creatinine (SCr) if used chronically.

### Pain Management Decision Tree in CKD

| Step | Therapy Option | Dosing & CKD Adjustments | Clinical Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1** | **Non-Pharmacologic** | Physical therapy, heat/ice, TENS unit | First-line for all CKD patients. |
| **2** | **Topical Agents** | Lidoderm (lidocaine) 5% patch<br>Zostrix (capsaicin) 0.025-0.1% cream | Apply locally to intact skin. No renal dose adjustment required. |
| **3** | **Acetaminophen** | Tylenol (acetaminophen) | **Max dose: 2,000 mg/day (2g/day) in CKD** to prevent toxic metabolite accumulation. |
| **4** | **Neuropathic Agents** | Cymbalta (duloxetine)<br>Neurontin (gabapentin) | **Duloxetine:** AVOID if eGFR < 30.<br>**Gabapentin:** Max 300 mg/day if eGFR 15-29; max 300 mg every other day if eGFR < 15. |
| **5** | **Atypical Opioids** | Ultram (tramadol) | **eGFR < 30:** Max dose 200 mg/day. Increase dosing interval to q12h. Monitor for CNS toxicity/seizures. |

### Acute NSAID Exposure Management
If a CKD patient inadvertently takes NSAIDs and presents with AKI or high risk of AKI:
1.  **STOP** the offending NSAID immediately.
2.  **Hydration:** Administer IV Normal Saline or Lactated Ringer's if volume depleted.
3.  **Hold RAASi:** Temporarily hold ACEi/ARB/ARNI and diuretics until SCr stabilizes.
4.  **Monitor:** Check SCr and serum potassium every 48-72 hours until baseline is re-established.

## 2. Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)

### Chronic PPI Use and Kidney Risk
Observational data strongly links chronic PPI use to two major renal complications:
1.  **Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN):** Often insidious, presenting without the classic triad of rash, fever, and eosinophilia.
2.  **CKD Progression:** Long-term use is associated with incident CKD and faster eGFR decline, likely due to subclinical, recurrent AIN leading to interstitial fibrosis.

**Common PPIs to monitor/deprescribe:**
*   Prilosec (omeprazole)
*   Protonix (pantoprazole)
*   Prevacid (lansoprazole)
*   Nexium (esomeprazole)

### Risk Mitigation & Deprescribing Decision Tree
*   **Assess Indication:** Does the patient have a strict indication for chronic PPI?
    *   *YES:* Barrett's esophagus, severe erosive esophagitis, chronic NSAID use (if unavoidable), or GI bleeding prophylaxis on dual antiplatelet therapy (e.g., Plavix (clopidogrel) + Aspirin).
    *   *NO:* Uncomplicated GERD, dyspepsia, stress ulcer prophylaxis post-discharge.
*   **Action for "NO" (Deprescribe/Switch):**
    1.  Taper PPI over 2-4 weeks to prevent rebound hypersecretion.
    2.  Switch to an H2 Receptor Antagonist (H2RA).
    3.  **H2RA CKD Adjustment:** Pepcid (famotidine) requires dose reduction. If eGFR < 50 mL/min/1.73 m², reduce dose by 50% (e.g., 20 mg daily instead of BID) or extend interval to every 48 hours.

## 3. Iodinated Contrast Media

### Contrast-Associated AKI (CA-AKI)
Per the ACR/NKF 2020 Consensus Guidelines, the risk of true Contrast-*Induced* AKI is historically overstated, but prophylactic measures are required for high-risk patients.
*   **High Risk Thresholds:** eGFR < 30 mL/min/1.73 m² (not on dialysis) or active AKI.
*   **Target:** Prevent SCr rise of > 0.3 mg/dL or > 1.5x baseline within 48-72 hours post-exposure.

### Prevention Protocol
1.  **Volume Expansion:** IV hydration is the *only* proven prophylactic measure.
    *   *Regimen:* Isotonic IV fluids (Normal Saline or Lactated Ringer's) at 1-3 mL/kg/hr for 1 hour before and 3-6 hours after the procedure.
    *   *[TKE PROTOCOL NEEDED]:* Refer to local TKE infusion center guidelines for specific fluid choice and rate adjustments in patients with concurrent heart failure (HFrEF/HFpEF).
2.  **Hold Nephrotoxins:** Hold NSAIDs, diuretics, and RAASi 24 hours prior if possible.

### Metformin Management Around Contrast
Glucophage (metformin) does *not* cause CA-AKI, but CA-AKI can cause metformin accumulation, leading to severe lactic acidosis.
*   **When to hold:** Hold at the time of, or prior to, the imaging procedure IF the patient has eGFR 30-60 mL/min/1.73 m², history of heart failure, hepatic impairment, or is receiving intra-arterial contrast.
*   **When to resume:** Re-evaluate eGFR 48 hours post-procedure. Resume Glucophage (metformin) only if SCr is stable and baseline renal function is confirmed.

## 4. Other Common Nephrotoxins

### Antibiotics
*   **Aminoglycosides:** Garamycin (gentamicin), Bethkis (tobramycin).
    *   *Risk:* Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN).
    *   *Monitoring:* Avoid if possible. If required, use extended-interval dosing and monitor trough levels strictly (target trough < 1-2 mcg/mL depending on agent).
*   **Vancomycin:** Vancocin (vancomycin).
    *   *Risk:* ATN, especially when combined with Zosyn (piperacillin/tazobactam).
    *   *Monitoring:* Use AUC/MIC dosing targeting 400-600 mg*h/L, or trough levels targeting 10-15 mcg/mL for standard infections (15-20 mcg/mL only for severe/CNS infections).

### Psychiatric & Antiviral Medications
*   **Lithium:** Lithobid (lithium).
    *   *Risk:* Nephrogenic Diabetes Insipidus (NDI), chronic tubulointerstitial nephropathy.
    *   *Monitoring:* Maintain lowest effective dose. Target serum trough levels 0.6-0.8 mEq/L. Monitor eGFR and urinalysis every 3-6 months.
*   **Tenofovir:** Viread (tenofovir disoproxil fumarate - TDF).
    *   *Risk:* Proximal tubulopathy (Fanconi syndrome), AKI, CKD progression.
    *   *Action:* Switch to Vemlidy (tenofovir alafenamide - TAF) which has significantly lower renal toxicity, or an integrase inhibitor-based regimen, in consultation with Infectious Disease.

### Herbal Supplements
*   **⚠️ SAFETY WARNING:** Patients with CKD must **AVOID ALL** herbal supplements.
*   *Reasons:*
    1.  Variable and unregulated active ingredient concentrations.
    2.  Hidden high potassium and phosphorus content.
    3.  Direct toxicity (e.g., Aristolochic acid causing Aristolochic Acid Nephropathy / Chinese Herb Nephropathy).
    4.  Heavy metal contamination (lead, mercury, cadmium).

## 5. Patient Education Talking Points

Use these standardized talking points during clinic visits:
*   **"The Kidney Rule:"** *Always check with your nephrologist or TKE staff before starting ANY new over-the-counter medication, supplement, or vitamin.*
*   **Pain Relievers:** *"Avoid NSAIDs like Advil, Motrin, Aleve, and ibuprofen completely. They squeeze the blood vessels in your kidneys and cause permanent damage. Tylenol (acetaminophen) is safe, but do not take more than 2,000 mg (2 grams) in a single day."*
*   **Stomach Pills:** *"Medications like Prilosec or Nexium can cause silent allergic reactions in your kidneys over time. If you don't absolutely need them for a severe stomach condition, we will work with you to switch to a safer alternative like Pepcid."*
*   **CT Scans/Dyes:** *"If a doctor orders a scan with IV dye, let us know. We may need to give you IV fluids beforehand to protect your kidneys, and we will need to pause your metformin if you take it for diabetes."*
