### Background
Chronic kidney disease (CKD) affects approximately 37 million Americans, yet adoption of guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) remains suboptimal, with fewer than 20% of eligible patients receiving quadruple therapy (RAASi, SGLT2i, finerenone, GLP-1 RA) in community settings. We implemented a standardized, 12-domain clinical assessment protocol across a 4-location, APP-led nephrology practice in rural West Tennessee to evaluate whether systematic care standardization could reduce dialysis initiation rates compared to national benchmarks.

### Methods
We conducted a retrospective analysis of [X] patients with CKD stages 3-5 managed at The Kidney Experts between [start date] and [end date]. A 12-domain structured assessment was performed at every encounter, covering: proteinuria, RAAS blockade, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonism, SGLT2i/finerenone/GLP-1 RA therapy, heart failure optimization, anemia, mineral bone disease, electrolytes, smoking status, nephrotoxin avoidance, statin therapy, and diabetes management.

The primary outcome was the rate of dialysis initiation per 100 patient-years, compared to expected rates derived from the Kidney Failure Risk Equation (KFRE) and USRDS national incidence data. Secondary outcomes included quadruple therapy adoption rate, blood pressure control (<120 mmHg systolic), and eGFR trajectory stability.

Care was delivered primarily by advanced practice providers (APPs) using a standardized report card system, with physician oversight for complex cases.

### Conclusions
Implementation of a standardized, 12-domain clinical protocol in a community-based, APP-led nephrology practice was associated with significantly higher GDMT adoption and lower-than-expected dialysis initiation rates. These findings suggest that systematic care standardization — rather than reliance on individual physician judgment — may be a scalable strategy for CKD prevention in community settings.

Further prospective studies are needed to validate these findings and quantify the cost-effectiveness of this model for value-based care contracting.
