# TKE Culture Role-Play Scenarios

> Nephrology-specific scenarios for practicing accountability conversations. Use in team training, huddles, or one-on-one coaching.

---

## How to Use These Scenarios

### Format
1. **Read the scenario** aloud to the group
2. **Pause** — ask "What would you do?"
3. **Identify** the trust-destroying paths vs. trust-building paths
4. **Role-play** the direct conversation using the two rules
5. **Debrief** — what worked? What was hard?

### The Two Rules (Review Before Each Session)
1. **Speak for yourself** — "I think" not "we think"
2. **Statements before questions** — Share your thought, then ask

### Time Per Scenario
- Quick practice: 5-7 minutes
- Deep practice: 15-20 minutes with debrief

## SCENARIO SET 1: PEER-TO-PEER (Clinical)

### Scenario 1A: The Late Documentation

**Setup**: Dr. Patel (APP) has noticed that Dr. Chen (APP) frequently completes patient notes 3-4 days after visits. This delays billing and creates risk if another provider needs to see the patient urgently. Dr. Patel has mentioned it to other staff, who agree it's frustrating.

**The Issue**: Dr. Chen's late documentation
**Trust Destroyer Used**: Gossiping (talking to others instead of Dr. Chen)

**Discussion Questions**:
- What's the trust-destroying path Dr. Patel has taken?
- What should Dr. Patel do now?
- Role-play: Dr. Patel approaches Dr. Chen directly

**Good Opening** (using both rules):
> "Hey, I've noticed documentation has been coming in 3-4 days after visits. I'm concerned because it delays billing and creates risk if someone else needs to see the patient. Can we talk about what's going on?"

**Bad Opening**:
> "Everyone's been talking about how your notes are always late. What's the deal?"

---

### Scenario 1B: The Protocol Skip

**Setup**: Maria (MA) observes that Jennifer (MA) frequently skips the pre-visit medication reconciliation, saying "I'll do it during the visit." Maria knows this creates errors and has heard the providers complain. Maria hasn't said anything to Jennifer because "it's not my place."

**The Issue**: Protocol non-compliance
**Trust Destroyer Used**: Ignoring (hoping it resolves itself)

**Discussion Questions**:
- Why might Maria feel it's "not her place"?
- How does shared fate ("what happens to one, happens to all") apply here?
- Role-play: Maria approaches Jennifer

**Good Opening**:
> "Jennifer, I've noticed you sometimes do med rec during the visit instead of before. I'm worried because I've seen it lead to errors, and when errors happen, it affects all of us. Is there something making the pre-visit process hard?"

---

### Scenario 1C: The Overstepping APP

**Setup**: Dr. Kumar (nephrologist) has noticed that Taylor (APP) sometimes adjusts CKD patient medications beyond their scope—making changes that should require physician review. Dr. Kumar mentioned it to Dr. Mulay instead of talking to Taylor directly.

**The Issue**: Scope of practice concerns
**Trust Destroyer Used**: Escalating (going to the boss)

**Discussion Questions**:
- Why might Dr. Kumar have escalated instead of having a direct conversation?
- What should Dr. Mulay say when Dr. Kumar escalates?
- Role-play: Dr. Kumar approaches Taylor directly

**Good Opening**:
> "Taylor, I want to talk about something I've observed. I've seen a few medication adjustments that I think should have come to me for review first. I'm not questioning your clinical judgment—I want to make sure we're aligned on scope. Can we walk through what happened?"

**What Dr. Mulay should say** (redirecting escalation):
> "Have you talked to Taylor directly about this? What happened in that conversation?"

## SCENARIO SET 2: PEER-TO-PEER (Administrative)

### Scenario 2A: The Phone Avoidance

**Setup**: Lisa (front desk) notices that when difficult patient calls come in, Carmen (front desk) often transfers them to Lisa rather than handling them. Lisa feels she's carrying more than her share of hard conversations. She's vented to the MAs about it.

**The Issue**: Uneven workload distribution
**Trust Destroyer Used**: Gossiping (venting to others)

**Discussion Questions**:
- Why is venting to others tempting in this situation?
- How does this affect trust between Lisa and the MAs?
- Role-play: Lisa approaches Carmen

**Good Opening**:
> "Carmen, I've noticed I've been getting a lot of the difficult calls transferred to me. I'm feeling overwhelmed and like I'm carrying more than my share. I'm not sure if you're aware this is happening, so I wanted to check in."

---

### Scenario 2B: The CCM Enrollment Shortcut

**Setup**: David (care coordinator) notices that Sarah (care coordinator) is enrolling patients in CCM without fully explaining the program—just getting quick signatures. David knows this leads to patient complaints and unenrollments, which hurts the whole team's metrics. He's been silently documenting instances "in case leadership asks."

**The Issue**: Enrollment quality
**Trust Destroyer Used**: Ignoring (documenting but not addressing)

**Discussion Questions**:
- How does "shared metrics create shared fate" apply here?
- What's the risk of David's documentation approach?
- Role-play: David approaches Sarah

**Good Opening**:
> "Sarah, I want to talk about CCM enrollments. I've noticed some patients seem surprised about what they signed up for, and I've seen a few unenroll quickly. I'm wondering if we're rushing the consent process. What's your experience been?"

---

### Scenario 2C: The Scheduling Conflict

**Setup**: Two front desk staff, Angela and Brenda, keep double-booking patients because they don't communicate about schedule changes. When a double-booking happens, each blames the other. Their manager has been mediating repeatedly.

**The Issue**: Communication breakdown
**Trust Destroyer Used**: Multiple escalations (making the manager mediate)

**Discussion Questions**:
- How has repeated escalation affected Angela and Brenda's ability to work together?
- What should the manager say next time?
- Role-play: Angela and Brenda have a direct conversation about their process

**What the manager should say**:
> "Before I get involved, I need to know: have you two talked directly about this? What did you try? I can help you have a more effective conversation, but I'm not going to solve this for you."

## SCENARIO SET 3: TEAM-TO-LEADERSHIP

### Scenario 3A: The Broken Promise

**Setup**: Dr. Mulay committed to getting new BP cuffs for the clinic three months ago. The team has been using faulty equipment and improvising. No one has brought it up directly—they just complain among themselves.

**The Issue**: Unmet leadership commitment
**Trust Destroyer Used**: Gossiping (complaining but not addressing)

**Discussion Questions**:
- Why might staff hesitate to hold leadership accountable?
- How does bidirectional accountability apply?
- Role-play: An MA approaches Dr. Mulay directly

**Good Opening**:
> "Dr. Mulay, three months ago you committed to getting new BP cuffs. We're still using the faulty ones and it's affecting patient care. I wanted to check in on where that stands and when we can expect resolution."

**What Dr. Mulay should say**:
> "You're right, and thank you for bringing this up directly. I own that I dropped this. Here's where it stands... I commit to [specific action] by [specific date]."

---

### Scenario 3B: The Inconsistent Standards

**Setup**: The team has noticed that high-performing staff get away with things (late arrivals, skipped documentation) that others get called out for. Nobody wants to bring it up because "that's just how it is with favorites."

**The Issue**: Inconsistent standard enforcement
**Trust Destroyer Used**: Ignoring (accepting it as normal)

**Discussion Questions**:
- Why does inconsistent enforcement destroy trust?
- How does "Standards are standards" apply?
- Role-play: A team member brings this up in a team meeting

**Good Opening** (for team meeting):
> "I want to raise something difficult. I've observed that some team members seem to have different standards applied to them than others. I think this is affecting team morale and trust. Can we discuss how we ensure consistent expectations?"

---

### Scenario 3C: The Information Shield

**Setup**: Providers keep asking why referral patterns changed, why the call schedule shifted, and why certain decisions were made. Leadership has been vague: "It's complicated." Staff feel out of the loop and decisions feel arbitrary.

**The Issue**: Lack of transparency
**Trust Destroyer Used**: Ignoring (leadership avoiding direct explanation)

**Discussion Questions**:
- How does information withholding affect "shared fate"?
- What's leadership's obligation here?
- Role-play: Provider asks for clarity directly

**Good Opening**:
> "I've noticed several changes lately—referral patterns, call schedule—and the explanations have been vague. I feel like I'm operating without context, which makes it hard to make good decisions. Can you help me understand the reasoning behind these changes?"

## SCENARIO SET 4: PATIENT CARE QUALITY

### Scenario 4A: The Rushed Handoff

**Setup**: During shift change, critical patient information isn't being communicated. One patient with new lab abnormalities was missed because the outgoing nurse said "nothing new" when there was. The incoming nurse found out hours later from the EHR.

**The Issue**: Handoff quality
**Fundamental in Play**: Fix Systems, Own My Part, Grow

**Discussion Questions**:
- What's the system gap?
- What's the individual responsibility?
- How should this be addressed?

**Debrief Framework**:
> System gap: "What in our handoff process allowed this?"
> Personal ownership: "What did I miss? What will I do differently?"
> Growth: "What did I learn from this?"

---

### Scenario 4B: The Dismissive Provider

**Setup**: A patient tells the MA that they felt dismissed during their last visit—the provider seemed rushed and didn't answer their questions. The MA feels torn: report it and risk conflict with the provider, or let it go?

**The Issue**: Patient experience feedback
**Fundamentals in Play**: Direct is Kind, Unreasonable Hospitality

**Discussion Questions**:
- How is staying silent a disservice to the provider?
- How does "Direct is Kind" apply?
- Role-play: MA mentions this to the provider

**Good Opening**:
> "Dr. [Name], Mrs. Johnson mentioned to me that she felt rushed during her visit and didn't get her questions answered. I wanted to let you know because I know that's not the experience you want to give. I figured you'd want to know."

---

### Scenario 4C: The AI Skeptic

**Setup**: One team member refuses to use AI tools, saying "I've always done it this way and my patients are fine." This creates extra work for others who have to pick up tasks that could be automated.

**The Issue**: AI adoption resistance
**Fundamental in Play**: AI in Everything

**Discussion Questions**:
- How does one person's resistance affect the team?
- How should peers address this?
- Role-play: A colleague approaches the AI skeptic

**Good Opening**:
> "I've noticed you're not using [AI tool] like the rest of us. I want to understand your concerns because your approach affects how we divide work. Is there something about the tool that worries you, or is there training that would help?"

## SCENARIO SET 5: GROWTH MINDSET

### Scenario 5A: The Defensive Receiver

**Setup**: Every time someone gives Alex feedback, Alex gets defensive: "I had a good reason" or "That's not what happened." The team has stopped giving Alex feedback because it's not worth the fight.

**The Issue**: Inability to receive feedback
**Trust Destroyer**: Team is now Ignoring (avoiding feedback entirely)

**Discussion Questions**:
- How does Alex's defensiveness create a culture problem?
- What happens when the team stops giving feedback?
- Role-play: A peer gives Alex meta-feedback about receiving feedback

**Good Opening**:
> "Alex, I want to talk about something that's been on my mind. When I've tried to give you feedback in the past, I've noticed you explain or defend immediately. I've started hesitating to bring things up. I don't think you intend this, but I wanted you to know how it lands."

---

### Scenario 5B: The Blame Deflector

**Setup**: When mistakes happen, Jordan always has an explanation that points elsewhere: "The system was down," "I wasn't told," "That's not my job." The pattern is consistent—Jordan never owns their part.

**The Issue**: Lack of personal accountability
**Fundamental in Play**: Fix Systems, Own My Part, Grow

**Discussion Questions**:
- How does constant deflection affect team trust?
- What's the conversation to have?
- Role-play: A peer addresses the pattern with Jordan

**Good Opening**:
> "Jordan, I've noticed a pattern I want to share with you. When things go wrong, I often hear explanations about external factors. I believe you, and sometimes systems DO fail. But I've never heard you say 'here's my part.' That's making it hard for us to trust. Am I missing something?"

---

### Scenario 5C: The Gossip Hub

**Setup**: Everyone knows that if you want news about what's happening, you talk to Pat. Pat seems to know everything—who's in trouble, who's getting promoted, what leadership is planning. Pat has become the informal information center.

**The Issue**: Gossip culture
**Trust Destroyer**: Gossiping (centralized)

**Discussion Questions**:
- Why do people gravitate to Pat?
- How does this undermine direct communication?
- How should a peer address this?

**Good Opening**:
> "Pat, I've noticed that people often come to you for information about what's going on. I'm wondering if you realize how much gets shared through you. I'm concerned because it means people aren't talking directly to each other or to leadership."

## DEBRIEF TEMPLATE

After each role-play, use this debrief structure:

### 1. What Worked?
- Did they use "I" statements?
- Did they share their thought before asking?
- Did they describe without blame?

### 2. What Was Hard?
- What made this conversation difficult?
- What emotions came up?
- Where did they want to take the easy path?

### 3. What Would You Do Differently?
- If you could redo it, what would change?
- What preparation would help?

### 4. Application
- Where might you face a similar situation?
- What will you commit to doing differently?

## FACILITATOR NOTES

### Creating Safe Practice Space
- Remind everyone: "This is practice. Imperfect attempts are valuable."
- Don't correct mid-role-play; save feedback for debrief
- Celebrate courage to try, even if execution is rough

### When to Use These Scenarios
- Team meetings (5-7 min quick practice)
- Training sessions (multiple scenarios, deep practice)
- One-on-one coaching (specific scenario relevant to individual)
- New hire orientation (introduce concepts through scenarios)

### Customization
- Adapt names and roles to match your team
- Create scenarios from real (anonymized) situations
- Let team members suggest new scenarios

### Signs of Progress
- People using "I" statements naturally
- Pre-apologies: "I'm going to share my thought first..."
- Real direct conversations happening outside practice
- People catching themselves: "Wait, I'm gossiping. I should talk to them."
