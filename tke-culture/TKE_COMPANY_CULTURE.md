## TIER 2: THE OPERATING PRINCIPLE

### Shared Fate

> **"What happens to one, happens to all."**

We share a fate. Your success is my success. Your failure is my concern. We hold each other accountable—not by looking to the boss, but by having direct conversations.

This principle, drawn from Eric Coryell's *Revolutionize Teamwork*, is the foundation everything else rests on. Without shared fate, values become posters on walls. With shared fate, values become lived reality.

**Shared metrics create shared fate.** We measure what matters together—patients kept off dialysis, satisfaction scores, promises kept. If you only know how YOU'RE doing, you can't feel what happens to all.

---

### Bidirectional Accountability

At TKE, accountability is not hierarchical—it's mutual. No one is exempt. Title doesn't shield you from direct feedback.

| Direction | What It Means |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Peer ↔ Peer** | I hold my colleagues accountable. They hold me accountable. |
| **Team → Leadership** | The team holds leadership accountable for commitments, decisions, and living the values. |
| **Leadership → Team** | Leadership holds the team accountable for performance, culture, and patient outcomes. |
| **Everyone → Everyone** | An MA can hold the CEO accountable. A physician can receive feedback from front desk staff. |

**What this looks like**:
- An MA can say to the CEO: "You committed to X. It's been 3 weeks. What's happening?"
- An APP can say to a physician: "I don't think that decision aligned with our values. Can we discuss?"
- Leadership publicly tracks their own commitments, not just team commitments
- When leadership falls short, they say "I own that" before being asked

## TIER 3: THE ACCOUNTABILITY SYSTEM

*Based on Eric Coryell's "Revolutionize Teamwork: How to Create and Lead Accountable Teams"*

> **"The mark of a great leader is the ability to describe what happened without assigning blame."**

**Accountability is not punishment. It's trust.**

We hold each other accountable not to shame, but because we care enough about each other and our mission to speak truth. The goal isn't to find fault—it's to find solutions and help each other grow.

When something goes wrong, the skilled teammate describes what happened clearly, without finger-pointing:
- ✅ "The report was late and the client was frustrated. What happened?"
- ❌ "You screwed up and made us look bad."

Same facts, different framing—one builds trust, one destroys it.

This is accountability at TKE: **direct, kind, blameless, and growth-oriented**.

---

### The Contract We Break

On most teams, there's an unspoken psychological contract:

> ~~"I won't talk about your performance if you don't talk about mine."~~

This contract keeps teams from dealing with real issues. It creates a culture where problems fester, resentment builds, and trust erodes.

### The Contract We Make

At TKE, we replace that with:

> **"I care enough about you and our mission to tell you what I see. I expect you to do the same for me."**

---

### Three Paths That Destroy Trust

When issues arise, most people take one of three paths—all of which destroy trust:

| Path | What It Looks Like | Why It Destroys Trust |
|------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **1. Ignoring** | Hoping the problem goes away | Creates resentment, nothing changes, signals you don't care |
| **2. Gossiping** | Talking ABOUT someone instead of TO them | Even if the other person agrees, you both know you can't be trusted |
| **3. Escalating** | Asking the boss to handle it | Signals you won't work things out directly, creates dependency |

All three paths lower short-term anxiety but create long-term damage.

---

### Two Paths That Build Trust

The paths that build trust are harder in the moment but essential:

| Path | What It Looks Like | Why It Builds Trust |
|------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **1. Direct conversation, one-on-one** | "Can we talk? I noticed X and want to understand." | Shows you respect them enough to address it directly |
| **2. Direct conversation, as a team** | "Team, we have an issue. Let's discuss it together." | Shows the team can handle hard things together |

Both require courage. Both create trust.

---

### Two Behavioral Rules

These rules, from Coryell, prevent the communication breakdowns that derail direct conversations:

#### Rule 1: Speak for Yourself

**Say "I think," "I feel," "I believe." Never hide behind "we," "everyone," or "the team."**

**Why it matters**:
- Group pronouns ("we think," "everyone feels") are how we avoid real issues
- They make the other person feel judged and ganged-up on
- They signal you've been talking behind their back
- They destroy trust instantly

**Examples**:
| Instead of... | Say... |
|---------------|--------|
| "We've noticed you've been missing deadlines." | "I noticed you missed the deadline. What happened?" |
| "Everyone thinks that meeting was a disaster." | "I'm concerned about how that meeting went." |
| "The team feels like you're not pulling your weight." | "I've been frustrated because I feel like I'm covering extra work. Can we talk about it?" |

#### Rule 2: Statements Before Questions

**Before asking a question, share the thought that led to it.**

**Why it matters**:
- When you lead with a question, the other person has to guess your intent
- "Why did you do it that way?" could be curious or accusatory—they don't know
- Sharing your thought first prevents miscommunication
- 9 times out of 10, after sharing your thought, you won't even need the question

**Examples**:
| Instead of... | Say... |
|---------------|--------|
| "Why didn't you follow the protocol?" | "I saw you handled that differently than our protocol. I'm trying to understand your reasoning—can you walk me through it?" |
| "Why was the report late?" | "I noticed the report was late. I'm wondering if there's a bottleneck I don't know about." |
| "What were you thinking?" | "I was surprised by that decision. Here's what I was expecting... Can you help me understand your perspective?" |

---

### The Three Types of Real Issues

Every real issue is rooted in one of three things. Knowing which one helps you enter conversations with clarity:

| Type | Description | How to Address |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **Different Expectations** | We assumed different things about what "success" looks like | Clarify: "What did you expect? Here's what I expected." |
| **Different Pictures of Reality** | We see the facts differently | Align: "Here's what I observed. What did you observe?" |
| **Different Views on Impact** | We agree on what happened but disagree on whether it matters | Discuss: "Here's why I think this matters. Help me understand your view." |

---

### When Direct Conversation Isn't Enough

Sometimes direct conversation doesn't resolve the issue. Here's the escalation path that preserves peer accountability:

| Step | Action | Owner |
|------|--------|-------|
| **1** | Direct conversation, one-on-one | You and the other person |
| **2** | Second direct conversation (if first didn't resolve) | You and the other person |
| **3** | Direct conversation with a peer facilitator | You, the other person, and a trusted colleague |
| **4** | Team conversation (if it affects the team) | The whole team, facilitated |
| **5** | Leadership involvement (only after steps 1-4) | You, the other person, and manager |

**Leadership's role at Step 5**:
- First question: "What happened in your direct conversations?"
- Not: "I'll take care of it for you" but: "Let me help you have a more effective conversation"
- Only if pattern persists after multiple attempts: Leadership takes direct action

**The principle**: Leadership is the last resort, not the first call.

---

### Real-Time Behavioral Interventions

Anyone can (and should) intervene when behavioral rules are violated. This is how we build the muscle:

| Violation | Intervention |
|-----------|--------------|
| Someone says "we think" or "everyone feels" | "Can you speak for yourself? What do YOU think?" |
| Someone asks a question without a statement | "Can you share the thought that led to that question?" |
| Someone is talking ABOUT someone not in the room | "Have you talked to them directly? Should we include them?" |
| Someone is escalating before trying direct conversation | "Did you try talking to them first? What happened?" |

**How to intervene**:
- Gently, not punitively: "Hey, can you rephrase that as an 'I' statement?"
- The goal is learning, not shaming
- If you're the one who violated, thank the person who called it out: "You're right. Let me rephrase."

**The norm**: Interventions are expected, welcomed, and thanked.

---

### Courage Over Comfort

Direct conversation is uncomfortable. That's why most people avoid it. At TKE, we choose courage over comfort.

**What we acknowledge**:
- Confronting a peer is scary. Do it anyway.
- Giving feedback to your boss is scary. Do it anyway.
- Receiving critical feedback hurts. Receive it with grace anyway.
- The short-term discomfort of direct conversation is less than the long-term damage of avoidance.

**What we promise**:
- You will not be punished for speaking up respectfully
- Leadership will model receiving feedback publicly
- When someone has the courage to confront directly, we respect it—even if the delivery isn't perfect
- Retaliation for direct, respectful feedback is a terminable offense

**The mantra**: "I care enough about you and our mission to tell you what I see."

## TIER 4: 5 CORE VALUES

Our values are WHO we are. They define our identity and guide major decisions.

### 1. People First, Always

**Meaning**: Patients are people with stories, not cases with numbers. Team members are valued humans, not interchangeable resources.

**What this looks like**:
- We use patient names, not room numbers
- We ask "what matters to you?" not just "what's the matter?"
- We support staff as whole humans, not just workers
- We never sacrifice human dignity for efficiency
- Holding someone accountable IS people first—you care enough to help them grow

**The test**: Would we be comfortable if our family member was treated this way?

---

### 2. Prevention Over Profit

**Meaning**: Our success is measured by patients who never need dialysis. Long-term relationships matter more than short-term revenue.

**What this looks like**:
- Aggressive quadruple therapy for diabetic CKD (protocols over preferences)
- Celebrating health maintained, not just disease treated
- Choosing what's right for patients even when it reduces revenue
- Building our business model around CKD management, not ESRD management

**The paradox we accept**: Every patient we keep off dialysis costs us ~$200-250/month in potential ESRD capitation revenue. This is our mission, not our mistake.

**The test**: Does this decision prevent disease progression, or just manage decline?

---

### 3. Work Smart, Not Long

**Meaning**: We achieve more by working intelligently, not grinding endlessly. Systems and AI preserve human energy for what matters most.

**What this looks like**:
- Building systems that eliminate duplicate work
- Using AI to handle routine tasks so humans can focus on judgment and relationships
- Measuring outcomes, not hours
- Rejecting "work harder" in favor of "build better systems"

**The tension with accountability**: If you can't keep a promise without overworking, the promise was wrong. Renegotiate before the deadline.

**The test**: Can we sustain this pace for 5 years, or are we sprinting a marathon?

---

### 4. Data Over Opinion

**Meaning**: Prove it before scaling it. Evidence drives decisions; experience informs them. "I don't know, let's find out" is stronger than unfounded certainty.

**What this looks like**:
- Testing assumptions with data before committing resources
- Admitting when we're wrong and changing course
- Using KDIGO/KDOQI guidelines as baseline, updating when evidence changes
- Tracking outcomes systematically (dialysis delays, hospitalizations, adherence)

**The test**: What data supports this decision, and what would prove us wrong?

---

### 5. AI Amplifies Human Impact

**Meaning**: We continuously embrace AI to amplify what humans do best. Every manual process is a candidate for automation. Every system is a candidate for improvement.

**What this looks like**:
- Asking "could AI help?" as reflexively as "does this serve the patient?"
- AI handles documentation, flagging, routine tasks
- Humans focus on judgment, empathy, creativity, relationships
- Continuous experimentation with AI tools and workflows

**The test**: Could AI do this, or does it require human judgment/empathy?

## TIER 5: 6 FUNDAMENTALS

Our fundamentals are HOW we operate daily. Each is an individual behavior—something YOU do.

---

### 1. Direct is Kind

> **I speak for myself. I share my thought before questions. I describe without blame. I coach directly.**

The direct path builds trust. Ignoring, gossiping, and escalating destroy it.

When I have an issue with someone, I go to them—within 48 hours. I use "I" statements, not "we think" or "everyone feels." I share the thought that led to my question before asking it. I describe situations without assigning blame.

**What this looks like:**
- Instead of "We've noticed you've been missing deadlines" → "I noticed you missed the deadline. What happened?"
- Instead of "Why didn't you follow the protocol?" → "I saw you handled that differently. I'm curious about your reasoning."
- Instead of complaining to a colleague → Having the direct conversation with the person

**The test**: Did I take the direct path, or did I avoid discomfort?

---

### 2. Listen First, Then Act

> **I understand before solving. I ask before assuming. The best care starts with hearing what matters.**

Whether it's a patient, a colleague, or a problem—I listen before I diagnose. I seek to understand the full picture before jumping to solutions. I ask "what matters to you?" not just "what's the matter?"

**What this looks like:**
- With patients: Letting them finish speaking before offering solutions
- With colleagues: Asking clarifying questions before proposing fixes
- With problems: Understanding root causes before treating symptoms
- In conversations: Reflecting back what I heard to confirm understanding

**The test**: Did I truly listen, or was I just waiting to talk?

---

### 3. Fix Systems, Own My Part, Grow

> **Every failure has system gaps AND personal lessons. I find both. I improve both.**

The Swiss Cheese Model is valid—every failure reveals system gaps. But "fix systems" can't become an excuse. Both are true: the system failed AND I need to do better. And both are opportunities to grow.

**What this looks like:**
- When something goes wrong: "What happened?" not "Who did this?"
- AND: "What's my part in this? What will I do differently?"
- After fixing: "What did I learn? How am I better?"
- Leadership owns their broken promises, not just individual contributors
- "I own that" is said before anyone has to ask

**The balance:**
| Blameless (keep) | Ownership (add) | Growth (add) |
|------------------|-----------------|--------------|
| Fix the system gaps | AND acknowledge my role | AND learn from it |
| No public shaming | AND have direct conversations | AND become better |
| Focus on prevention | AND commit to personal improvement | AND share the lesson |

**The test**: Did I fix the system, own my part, AND grow from it?

---

### 4. Test, Learn, Improve

> **I run small experiments. I learn from everything. Progress beats perfection.**

Small experiments are cheaper than big bets. Learning fast beats planning perfectly. 1% improvement daily = 37x improvement yearly.

**What this looks like:**
- I test new ideas with small pilots before committing resources
- I set clear success criteria BEFORE the test
- I learn from both successes and failures
- Every week, I identify ONE thing that could be improved and either fix it or flag it

**The test**: Am I experimenting and learning, or waiting for perfect conditions?

---

### 5. Unreasonable Hospitality

> **I go beyond expected. I anticipate needs. "Big Expertise. Small-Town Heart." is my promise.**

This differentiates TKE from transactional healthcare. It creates patient loyalty and referrals. It delivers on our promise of clinical excellence with genuine warmth.

**What "unreasonable" means:**
| Reasonable | Unreasonable |
|------------|--------------|
| Answer questions when asked | Anticipate questions before they're asked |
| Be polite | Remember personal details and reference them |
| Solve the problem presented | Solve the problem they didn't know they had |
| Meet expectations | Exceed expectations meaningfully |
| Provide expertise | Provide expertise WITH heart |

**What this looks like:**
- Remembering that Mrs. Johnson's grandson just started college
- Calling to check in after a difficult visit—not because protocol requires it
- Explaining complex kidney function in terms the patient actually understands
- Making the clinical environment feel warm, not sterile

**The test**: Would this feel like family, or like a transaction?

---

### 6. AI in Everything

> **If AI can do it, AI should. I ask "could AI help?" constantly.**

AI handles routine so humans can handle remarkable. Every repetitive task is an automation candidate. I focus my human energy on judgment, empathy, creativity, and relationships.

**The daily question**: "What did I do manually today that AI could help with?"

**By role:**
| Role | AI Should Handle | I Should Handle |
|------|------------------|-----------------|
| Providers | Documentation, coding suggestions, abnormal flags | Medical decisions, patient relationships, complex judgment |
| MAs | Data entry, reminders, refill routing | Patient interaction, clinical prep, in-person support |
| Front desk | Confirmations, insurance verification | Complex scheduling, upset patient de-escalation |
| Billing | Claim scrubbing, denial analysis | Payer negotiations, complex appeals |

**The test**: Could AI do this, or does it require my human judgment/empathy?

## TIER 7: LEADERSHIP EXPECTATIONS

Leaders are held to a higher standard, not a lower one. If leadership doesn't model it, it won't become culture.

### Leadership's Two Jobs

1. **Model peer accountability** — Have direct conversations yourself. Don't gossip, ignore, or ask others to handle your interpersonal issues.

2. **Clear the organizational path** — Remove blockers your team CAN'T solve themselves. Shield them from chaos that flows from above. This is YOUR job, not theirs.

**The distinction:**
- **Peer behavior issue?** Help them have the direct conversation—don't solve it for them.
- **System/resource/organizational blocker?** That's YOUR problem to solve, not theirs.

---

### Leadership Behaviors

| Behavior | What It Looks Like |
|----------|-------------------|
| **Receive feedback publicly** | When someone gives you feedback in a meeting, thank them. Don't get defensive. Say "I'll reflect on that" or "You're right." |
| **Own mistakes first** | Say "I own that" before anyone has to call you out. Don't wait to be confronted. |
| **Track your commitments visibly** | Your promises to the team are tracked the same way you track team performance. |
| **Redirect escalation** | When someone comes to you with a peer issue, first ask: "Did you talk to them?" Help them have the conversation, don't have it for them. |
| **Speak for yourself** | No "leadership thinks" or "we've decided." Say "I think" and "I've decided." Own your decisions. |
| **Create shared metrics** | Ensure every team has visible team-level metrics, not just individual metrics. |
| **Confront directly** | If you have an issue with someone, go to them. Don't send signals through others. |
| **Model courage over comfort** | If it's hard for you, it's hard for everyone. Do it anyway. Show them how. |
| **Clear the path** | Remove blockers your team can't solve themselves. If they need a tool, approval, or resource—get it for them. |
| **Shield your team** | Absorb chaos from above so clarity flows down. Your team shouldn't feel every priority shift from corporate or external pressures. |
| **Standards are standards** | Enforce consistently—no exceptions for favorites, no exceptions for high performers. Inconsistency destroys trust faster than strictness. |
| **48-hour response** | When issues are raised to you (after direct conversation has been tried), respond within 48 hours—even if just to say "I'm working on it." |

**The test for leadership**: Am I modeling what I expect from the team? If I'm not doing it, I can't expect them to.

## TIER 8: SUPPORTING SYSTEMS

### Commitment Tracking

**For everyone**:
- When you commit to something, write it down with a deadline
- If you can't keep it, communicate BEFORE the deadline
- "I'll try" is not a commitment. "I will by [date]" is.

**For leadership**:
- Leadership commitments to the team are tracked publicly (in team meetings, dashboards, or shared docs)
- The team reviews leadership commitments monthly: "What was promised? What was delivered?"
- Leadership reports on their own commitments the same way they expect the team to report

**The principle**: If it's not visible, it's not a real commitment. Visibility creates accountability.

## TIER 9: WHEN VALUES SEEM TO CONFLICT

Sometimes values create tension. Here's how to navigate:

| Tension | Resolution |
|---------|------------|
| "People First" vs. "Accountability" | Holding someone accountable IS people first—you care enough to help them grow. Avoiding the conversation is not kindness. |
| "Prevention Over Profit" vs. "Sustainability" | We can't prevent dialysis if we don't exist. Short-term financial decisions that enable long-term mission are acceptable. |
| "Work Smart, Not Long" vs. "Keep Every Promise" | If you can't keep a promise without overworking, the promise was wrong. Renegotiate before the deadline. |

## APPENDIX C: Glossary

**Shared Fate**: The principle that team members' success and failure are interconnected. "What happens to one, happens to all." Creates motivation for peer accountability.

**Bidirectional Accountability**: Accountability that flows in all directions—peer-to-peer, team-to-leadership, and leadership-to-team. No one is exempt based on title.

**The Psychological Contract**: The unspoken agreement on most teams: "I won't talk about your performance if you don't talk about mine." At TKE, we break this contract.

**Speak for Yourself**: Using "I" statements instead of hiding behind "we," "everyone," or "the team." Owning your perspective.

**Statements Before Questions**: Sharing the thought that led to a question before asking it. Prevents miscommunication and builds trust.

**Unreasonable Hospitality**: Going beyond what's expected; anticipating needs before they're expressed; making people feel genuinely cared for.

**Blameless Problem Solving**: Focusing on fixing systems rather than blaming individuals when things go wrong. Based on the "Swiss Cheese Model" where failures result from multiple system gaps aligning.

**The Swiss Cheese Model**: A framework for understanding failures as the result of multiple system gaps (holes in layers of cheese) aligning, rather than single-point individual failures.

**Carefrontation**: A direct conversation about a difficult issue, delivered with genuine care for the person. Care + Confrontation = Growth.

**Direct is Kind**: The principle that honest, direct communication—delivered with care—is more respectful than avoidance. Combines Coryell's behavioral rules with compassionate framing.

**Listen First, Then Act**: The discipline of understanding before solving. Applies to patient care, peer interactions, and problem-solving.

**Big Expertise. Small-Town Heart.**: TKE's patient-facing promise—clinical excellence delivered with genuine warmth and personal connection, regardless of practice size or location.
