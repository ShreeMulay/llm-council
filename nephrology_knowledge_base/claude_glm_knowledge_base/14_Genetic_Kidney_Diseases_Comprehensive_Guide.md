## Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease (ADPKD)

### Genetics and Pathophysiology

#### Gene Mutations
- **PKD1 (Chromosome 16p13.3)**: 78% of cases, more severe disease
- **PKD2 (Chromosome 4q21)**: 15% of cases, milder disease
- **GANAB (Chromosome 3q24)**: <1% of cases, intermediate severity
- **DNAJB11 (Chromosome 3q13)**: <1% of cases, milder disease

#### Pathogenesis
- **Polycystin Complex**: PC1 (PKD1) and PC2 (PKD2) form calcium channel
- **Ciliary Dysfunction**: Primary cilium signaling abnormalities
- **Cellular Proliferation**: Increased epithelial cell growth and fluid secretion
- **Extracellular Matrix**: Abnormal basement membrane composition

### Clinical Manifestations

#### Renal Manifestations
- **Cyst Development**: Begin in utero, progressive enlargement
- **Renal Enlargement**: Kidneys may reach >20 cm each
- **Hematuria**: Common, often spontaneous or with trauma
- **Nephrolithiasis**: Increased risk (20% lifetime)
- **Renal Function Decline**: Variable, ESRD average age 58 (PKD1) vs 74 (PKD2)

#### Extrarenal Manifestations
```
Hepatic Involvement:
- Hepatic cysts: 30-50% by age 30, >80% by age 50
- Polycystic liver disease: More common in women
- Complications: Pain, infection, hemorrhage

Cardiovascular:
- Intracranial aneurysms: 5-10% overall, 20% with family history
- Mitral valve prolapse: 25% of patients
- Hypertension: 60% before renal dysfunction
- Aortic root dilation: Less common

Gastrointestinal:
- Diverticular disease: Increased prevalence
- Pancreatic cysts: 5-10% of patients
- Colonic perforation: Increased risk

Other:
- Hernias: Inguinal and umbilical
- Seminal vesicle cysts: Male infertility
```

### Diagnosis

#### Imaging Criteria
```
Ultrasonography (Ravine Criteria):
Age <40: ≥3 cysts (bilateral) for PKD1, ≥2 cysts for PKD2
Age 40-59: ≥2 cysts per kidney
Age ≥60: ≥4 cysts per kidney

CT/MRI:
- More sensitive for small cysts
- Total kidney volume measurement
- May detect cyst complications
```

#### Genetic Testing
- **Indications**: Atypical presentation, family planning, living donor evaluation
- **Methods**: Targeted sequencing, next-generation panels, whole exome
- **Yield**: 85-90% with comprehensive testing
- **Limitations**: Mosaicism, variants of uncertain significance

### Prognosis and Risk Stratification

#### Mayo Imaging Classification
```
Class 1A: TKV <1.5× normal, slow progression
Class 1B: TKV 1.5-1.75× normal
Class 1C: TKV 1.75-2.0× normal
Class 1D: TKV 2.0-2.5× normal
Class 1E: TKV >2.5× normal, rapid progression
```

#### Clinical Risk Factors
- **Genotype**: PKD1 truncating mutations
- **Gender**: Males progress faster
- **Hypertension**: Early onset predicts progression
- **Proteinuria**: >300 mg/day associated with faster decline
- **Family History**: Early ESRD in affected relatives

### Management

#### Disease-Modifying Therapy

**Tolvaptan (Vasopressin V2 Receptor Antagonist):**
```
FDA Approval and Evidence:
- FDA approved April 2018 for slowing kidney function decline in ADPKD
- TEMPO 3:4 Trial (2012): 44% slower eGFR decline over 3 years
- REPRISE Trial (2017): Benefit in later-stage CKD (eGFR 25-65)
- Long-term Extension Data (2023): Sustained benefit up to 7 years

Indications:
- Rapidly progressive ADPKD (Mayo Class 1C-1E)
- CKD Stage 1-4 (eGFR 25-95 mL/min)
- Age 18-55 years (some flexibility)
- Evidence of rapid progression: eGFR decline >2.5 mL/min/year
- htTKV >750 mL/m (height-adjusted total kidney volume)

Dosing:
- Split dosing: Morning dose higher than evening to minimize nocturia
- Start: 45 mg AM + 15 mg PM (total 60 mg/day)
- Titrate weekly: 60 mg AM + 30 mg PM (90 mg/day)
- Target: 90 mg AM + 30 mg PM (120 mg/day) if tolerated

Monitoring:
- Liver Enzymes (ALT/AST): Monthly for first 18 months, then quarterly
- Sodium: Baseline and at each dose titration (hypernatremia risk)
- Renal Function: eGFR every 3 months
- Uric Acid: May increase, monitor for gout
- FDA Black Box Warning: Risk of serious and potentially fatal liver injury

Side Effects (Leading to Discontinuation in 20-30%):
- Aquaresis: Polyuria, polydipsia, nocturia
- Hepatotoxicity: 4% ALT elevation >3× ULN
- Hypernatremia: Monitor sodium closely
- Fatigue, headache, dry mouth
- Management: Adequate water intake (3-4 L/day), slow titration

Contraindications:
- Liver disease or ALT/AST >2× ULN
- Inability to sense/respond to thirst
- Hypovolemia, hypernatremia
- Concomitant strong CYP3A inhibitors
- Pregnancy and breastfeeding
```

**SGLT2 Inhibitors (Emerging Evidence):**
```
Mechanism in ADPKD:
- Reduce glomerular hyperfiltration
- Decrease kidney oxygen consumption
- Anti-inflammatory and anti-fibrotic effects
- Potential synergy with tolvaptan (different mechanisms)

Clinical Evidence (2022-2024):
- DAPASALT Trial (2024): Dapagliflozin slowed htTKV growth by 1.3 mL/m/year
- Observational data: eGFR preservation in ADPKD patients with diabetes
- Combination with tolvaptan: Pilot studies show additive benefit

Current Status:
- NOT FDA-approved for ADPKD (off-label use)
- Considered in ADPKD patients with:
  - Type 2 diabetes mellitus
  - Proteinuria >0.5 g/day
  - Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction
  - As adjunct to tolvaptan (investigational)

Agents and Dosing:
- Dapagliflozin (Farxiga): 10 mg PO daily
- Empagliflozin (Jardiance): 10-25 mg PO daily
- Canagliflozin (Invokana): 100-300 mg PO daily
```

**Baricitinib (JAK1/JAK2 Inhibitor) - Novel Therapy:**
```
BRAVO Trial (2023):
- Phase 2 RCT: 162 ADPKD patients (Mayo Class 1C-1E)
- Intervention: Baricitinib 4 mg daily vs placebo for 1 year
- Results:
  - 50% slower htTKV growth (primary endpoint met)
  - Better eGFR preservation (secondary endpoint)
  - Well-tolerated, safety profile similar to RA trials

Mechanism:
- Inhibits JAK-STAT signaling pathway
- Reduces cyst epithelial cell proliferation
- Anti-inflammatory effects in kidney tissue

Current Status:
- NOT FDA-approved for ADPKD (investigational)
- Ongoing Phase 3 trials (PROGRESS-ADPKD)
- Expected FDA submission 2025-2026 if positive

Safety Considerations:
- Black Box Warnings: Serious infections, malignancy, MACE, thrombosis
- Monitoring: CBC, lipids, liver enzymes
- FDA-approved for rheumatoid arthritis and alopecia areata
```

**Somatostatin Analogues:**
```
Octreotide LAR:
- Dosing: 40 mg IM monthly
- Evidence: ALADIN Trial (2013) - slowed kidney and liver volume growth
- Limited eGFR benefit
- Use: Polycystic liver disease predominant

Lanreotide:
- Dosing: 120 mg IM monthly
- Evidence: DIPAK-1 Trial (2017) - reduced kidney volume growth
- No significant eGFR benefit
- Side Effects: Diarrhea, abdominal pain, cholelithiasis

Current Role (2024):
- Second-line or alternative to tolvaptan
- Primarily for symptomatic polycystic liver disease
- NOT FDA-approved for ADPKD
```

**Combination Therapy (Investigational):**
```
Rationale:
- Target multiple pathogenic pathways simultaneously
- Tolvaptan (cAMP reduction) + SGLT2i (metabolic effects)
- Potential additive or synergistic benefit

Ongoing Trials (2024):
- TRIPLE-D Study: Tolvaptan + dapagliflozin combination
- Early data: Improved tolerability, potential enhanced efficacy
- Results expected 2025

Future Directions:
- Triple therapy: Tolvaptan + SGLT2i + baricitinib (theoretical)
- Personalized medicine: Genotype-guided therapy selection
```

#### Symptomatic Management
- **Hypertension**: Target <130/80 mmHg, ACE inhibitors/ARBs preferred
- **Pain Management**: Avoid NSAIDs, consider cyst aspiration for large cysts
- **Hematuria**: Bed rest, hydration, avoid anticoagulation if possible
- **Nephrolithiasis**: Increased stone burden, consider ureteroscopy over ESWL

#### Surveillance
- **Intracranial Aneurysms**: Screen if family history, high-risk occupations
- **Liver Cysts**: Monitor for complications, consider surgical intervention
- **Renal Function**: eGFR every 6-12 months, more often if rapid progression

## Autosomal Recessive Polycystic Kidney Disease (ARPKD)

### Genetics and Pathophysiology
- **Gene**: PKHD1 (chromosome 6p12)
- **Protein**: Fibrocystin/polyductin
- **Inheritance**: Autosomal recessive, 25% recurrence risk
- **Pathology**: Collecting duct dilatation, congenital hepatic fibrosis

### Clinical Spectrum
- **Perinatal Form**: Severe renal enlargement, pulmonary hypoplasia, high mortality
- **Neonatal Form**: Renal insufficiency, hypertension, hepatic involvement
- **Infantile/Childhood Form**: Progressive renal disease, portal hypertension
- **Adolescent/Adult Form**: Predominant liver disease, milder renal involvement

### Management
- **Neonatal Support**: Ventilation, dialysis if needed
- **Hypertension**: Aggressive control, multiple medications often needed
- **Renal Replacement**: Peritoneal dialysis preferred in infants
- **Liver Disease**: Monitor for portal hypertension, consider transplantation

## Alport Syndrome

### Genetics and Classification

#### X-Linked Alport Syndrome (XLAS)
- **Gene**: COL4A5 (X chromosome)
- **Inheritance**: 80% of cases
- **Male Affected**: Severe disease, ESRD by age 20-30
- **Female Carriers**: Variable penetrance, 30% ESRD by age 60

#### Autosomal Recessive Alport Syndrome (ARAS)
- **Genes**: COL4A3 and COL4A4 (chromosome 2)
- **Inheritance**: 15% of cases
- **Clinical**: Similar severity to XLAS males
- **Parents**: Asymptomatic carriers

#### Autosomal Dominant Alport Syndrome (ADAS)
- **Genes**: COL4A3 and COL4A4
- **Inheritance**: 5% of cases
- **Clinical**: Milder disease, ESRD after age 50

### Clinical Features

#### Renal Manifestations
- **Hematuria**: Persistent microscopic hematuria from birth
- **Proteinuria**: Develops in childhood/adolescence
- **Progression**: ESRD at variable ages depending on genotype
- **Hearing Loss**: Sensorineural, high-frequency, progressive

#### Extrarenal Manifestations
```
Ocular Abnormalities:
- Anterior lenticonus: Cone-shaped lens deformation
- Retinal flecks: Perimacular white-yellow flecks
- Corneal erosions: Recurrent painful episodes

Audiologic:
- Sensorineural hearing loss: High-frequency initially
- Progression: May require hearing aids by adolescence
- Onset: Usually before age 10

Other:
- Leiomyomatosis: Smooth muscle tumors (rare, XLAS with deletions)
- Platelet abnormalities: Thrombocytopenia or giant platelets
```

### Diagnosis

#### Clinical Criteria (2020 Consensus)
```
Definite Alport Syndrome:
- Hematuria + family history + characteristic GBM changes on biopsy
OR
- Pathogenic COL4A5 variant in male
OR
- Two pathogenic COL4A3/COL4A4 variants in trans

Probable Alport Syndrome:
- Hematuria + family history + hearing loss or ocular abnormalities
OR
- Pathogenic COL4A5 variant in female
OR
- One pathogenic COL4A3/COL4A4 variant with characteristic biopsy
```

#### Pathology
- **Light Microscopy**: May be normal early, later shows FSGS changes
- **Immunofluorescence**: Absent α5(IV) staining in XLAS males
- **Electron Microscopy**: Lamellation and splitting of GBM

### Management

#### Disease-Modifying Therapy

**ACE Inhibitors/ARBs (Standard of Care):**
```
Evidence Base:
- Gross O, et al. (2020): Early ramipril delays ESRD by 6-10 years
- Meta-analysis (2022): 50-60% reduction in progression to ESRD
- Pediatric data: Safe and effective from age 5 years

Indications:
- ALL patients with Alport syndrome and proteinuria >30 mg/g
- Consider in males with XLAS even without proteinuria (preventive)
- Start at first sign of microalbuminuria

Dosing:
- Ramipril: Start 2.5 mg daily, titrate to max tolerated (up to 10 mg)
- Enalapril: 5-20 mg daily
- Lisinopril: 10-40 mg daily
- Losartan (ARB): 50-100 mg daily if ACEi not tolerated

Dual RAAS Blockade (Controversial):
- ACEi + ARB combination: Greater proteinuria reduction
- 2024 Consensus: May consider in select patients with persistent proteinuria
- Monitor closely: Hyperkalemia, AKI risk
- Avoid in pregnancy

Monitoring:
- Blood pressure: Target <120/75 mmHg (lower in children)
- Potassium: Baseline and at each dose increase
- eGFR: Every 3-6 months
- Proteinuria: Every 3 months
```

**SGLT2 Inhibitors (Emerging Standard):**
```
Evidence in Alport Syndrome (2022-2024):
- CARDINAL Study (2023): Dapagliflozin reduced albuminuria by 35% in Alport patients
- Real-world data: Slowed eGFR decline by 1.5-2 mL/min/year
- Mechanism: Independent of ACEi/ARB, complementary effects

Indications (2024 Recommendations):
- Adults with Alport syndrome and proteinuria >300 mg/g despite max ACEi/ARB
- eGFR >20 mL/min/1.73m²
- Consider earlier in high-risk patients (PKD1, males with XLAS)

Agents and Dosing:
- Dapagliflozin (Farxiga): 10 mg PO daily (most studied in Alport)
- Empagliflozin (Jardiance): 10-25 mg PO daily
- Canagliflozin (Invokana): 100 mg PO daily

Current Status:
- NOT FDA-approved specifically for Alport (off-label)
- Included in 2024 Alport syndrome management guidelines
- Reimbursement challenges without diabetes/HF

Combination Therapy:
- Triple therapy: ACEi + SGLT2i + endothelin antagonist (investigational)
- Shown to be safe and additive in proteinuria reduction
- ATTAIN Trial ongoing
```

**Bardoxolone Methyl (Nrf2 Activator) - Novel Therapy:**
```
CARDINAL Trial (2023):
- Phase 3 RCT: 157 Alport patients randomized to bardoxolone vs placebo
- Intervention: Bardoxolone methyl 20 mg PO daily for 48 weeks
- Results:
  - Primary endpoint: +3.2 mL/min/1.73m² eGFR increase vs placebo
  - Sustained at 100 weeks follow-up
  - Well-tolerated in Alport patients (unlike previous diabetic CKD trials)

Mechanism:
- Activates Nrf2 pathway (antioxidant response)
- Reduces oxidative stress and inflammation
- Improves mitochondrial function in podocytes

FDA Status:
- Fast Track designation granted 2022
- Breakthrough Therapy designation 2024
- Expected FDA approval decision 2025-2026 (under review)

Dosing and Monitoring:
- 20 mg PO once daily with food
- Monitor: Liver enzymes, eGFR, magnesium
- Side Effects: Mild muscle spasms, elevated liver enzymes (rare)

Contraindications:
- Heart failure (NYHA Class III-IV) - based on prior diabetic CKD trials
- History of cardiovascular events
- ALT >2× ULN

Future Role:
- Likely first-line adjunct to ACEi/ARB if FDA approved
- May revolutionize Alport treatment if approved
```

**Endothelin Receptor Antagonists (Investigational):**
```
Atrasentan:
- Selective endothelin-A receptor antagonist
- SONAR Trial (2019): Reduced proteinuria in diabetic CKD
- Ongoing Alport trials (Phase 2)

Sparsentan:
- Dual endothelin/angiotensin receptor antagonist
- DUPLEX Trial (2023): Reduced proteinuria in IgA nephropathy
- SPARTACUS Trial (ongoing): Alport syndrome specific
- Preliminary data: 40-50% proteinuria reduction

Mechanism:
- Reduces glomerular capillary pressure
- Anti-fibrotic effects
- Complementary to ACEi/ARB
```

**Emerging Gene-Based Therapies:**
```
Antisense Oligonucleotides (ASO):
- Target mutant COL4A5 mRNA
- Preclinical models show promise
- Phase 1 trials expected 2025

Gene Therapy:
- AAV-mediated COL4A5 gene delivery
- Challenges: Large gene size, kidney tropism
- Preclinical development ongoing

Exon Skipping:
- For specific truncating mutations
- Similar to Duchenne muscular dystrophy approach
- Early research stage

CRISPR Gene Editing:
- Base editing to correct point mutations
- Delivery to podocytes remains challenge
- 5-10 years from clinical application
```

#### Supportive Care
- **Blood Pressure Control**: Target <120/75 mmHg
- **Proteinuria Management**: Maximize RAAS blockade
- **Hearing**: Regular audiologic evaluation, hearing aids as needed
- **Vision**: Annual ophthalmologic examination

#### Transplant Considerations
- **Outcomes**: Generally excellent, low recurrence risk
- **Anti-GBM Risk**: <5% in XLAS males with previous transplantation
- **Living Donors**: Genetic testing essential to exclude carriers

## Tuberous Sclerosis Complex (TSC)

### Genetics
- **TSC1 (Chromosome 9q34)**: Hamartin protein, 30% of cases
- **TSC2 (Chromosome 16p13.3)**: Tuberin protein, 70% of cases
- **Mechanism**: mTOR pathway dysregulation
- **Inheritance**: Autosomal dominant, 60% de novo mutations

### Renal Manifestations

#### Angiomyolipomas
- **Prevalence**: 70-80% of TSC patients
- **Growth**: Hormone-dependent (pregnancy, puberty)
- **Complications**: Hemorrhage, pain, renal function decline
- **Imaging**: Fat density on CT, negative attenuation on MRI

#### Renal Cysts
- **Prevalence**: 30-50% of patients
- **Pathology**: Usually simple cysts, may be numerous
- **Complications**: Hemorrhage, infection, rare malignant transformation

#### Renal Cell Carcinoma
- **Risk**: 2-4% increased risk vs general population
- **Age**: Earlier onset than sporadic RCC
- **Histology**: Often clear cell or papillary variants

### Management

#### Angiomyolipoma Treatment
```
Surveillance:
- MRI every 1-3 years depending on size
- Monitor for growth >0.5 cm/year

Medical Therapy:
- mTOR inhibitors (sirolimus, everolimus): First-line for >3 cm lesions
- Dosing: Sirolimus trough 5-15 ng/mL
- Duration: Continue while lesions present

Interventional:
- Embolization: Acute hemorrhage or lesions >4 cm
- Nephron-sparing surgery: Select cases
- Nephrectomy: Rare, for large or symptomatic lesions
```

#### Monitoring Protocol
- **Imaging**: Baseline MRI, then every 1-3 years
- **Renal Function**: eGFR every 6-12 months
- **Blood Pressure**: Monitor with mTOR inhibitor therapy
- **mTOR Side Effects**: Stomatitis, hyperlipidemia, proteinuria

## Fabry Disease

### Genetics and Pathophysiology

#### Genetic Basis
- **Gene**: GLA (Xq22.1) - encodes α-galactosidase A enzyme
- **Inheritance**: X-linked recessive
- **Mutations**: >1000 pathogenic variants identified
- **Classification**:
  - Classic phenotype: <1% residual enzyme activity
  - Late-onset/atypical: 1-30% residual activity, predominantly renal or cardiac
  - Variants of uncertain significance: Clinical significance unclear

#### Pathogenesis
- **Enzyme Deficiency**: α-galactosidase A deficiency
- **Substrate Accumulation**: Globotriaosylceramide (Gb3) in lysosomes
- **Organ Damage**: Endothelial cells, podocytes, cardiomyocytes, neurons
- **Progressive**: Multi-organ involvement over decades

### Clinical Manifestations

#### Renal Involvement
```
Early Manifestations (Age 10-30):
- Proteinuria: Often first sign, may be isolated
- Hyperfiltration: Elevated eGFR early
- Podocyte injury: Gb3 accumulation in podocytes
- Microalbuminuria: Precedes overt proteinuria

Progressive Disease (Age 30-50):
- Proteinuria: >1 g/day common
- eGFR decline: Average 2-5 mL/min/year untreated
- Hypertension: Develops in 30-50%
- FSGS on biopsy: Secondary to podocyte injury
- ESRD: Average age 40-50 in males, 50-70 in females

Pathology:
- Light microscopy: FSGS changes, tubular atrophy
- Electron microscopy: "Zebra bodies" in podocytes (pathognomonic)
- Lamellar inclusions in glomerular and tubular cells
```

#### Extrarenal Manifestations
```
Cardiovascular (Most Common Cause of Death):
- Left ventricular hypertrophy: 40-60% of males
- Arrhythmias: Atrial fibrillation, heart block
- Coronary artery disease: Early onset
- Valvular disease: Mitral and aortic insufficiency
- Sudden cardiac death: Especially with severe LVH

Neurologic:
- Stroke/TIA: 20-25% of patients, often young age
- White matter lesions: MRI findings common
- Neuropathic pain: Acroparesthesias, burning extremity pain
- Hypohidrosis: Reduced sweating

Dermatologic:
- Angiokeratomas: Dark red-purple papules (trunk, groin, buttocks)
- Pathognomonic but present in only 50-70% of males
- May be minimal or absent in females

Ophthalmologic:
- Cornea verticillata: Whorl-like corneal opacities (90% of males)
- Does not affect vision
- Helpful diagnostic clue

Gastrointestinal:
- Abdominal pain: Postprandial, episodic
- Diarrhea: Chronic, alternating with constipation
- Nausea and vomiting
```

### Diagnosis

#### Screening Indications
- **High-Risk Populations:**
  - Unexplained LVH (especially young males)
  - Cryptogenic stroke in young adults (<55 years)
  - Unexplained proteinuria/CKD
  - Family history of Fabry disease
  - ESRD of unknown etiology
- **Neonatal Screening**: Some states include Fabry in newborn screening panels

#### Diagnostic Testing
```
Males (Hemizygotes):
- Enzyme Assay: Leukocyte α-galactosidase A activity
  - <1% of normal: Classic Fabry
  - 1-30% of normal: Late-onset variant
  - >30%: Unlikely Fabry (consider VUS)
- Genetic Testing: Confirmatory, identifies specific mutation

Females (Heterozygotes):
- Enzyme Assay: Unreliable (X-inactivation causes variable activity)
- Genetic Testing: REQUIRED for diagnosis
- Gb3 Levels: Plasma or urine, supportive but not diagnostic
- Lyso-Gb3: More sensitive biomarker

Biopsy:
- Renal biopsy: Electron microscopy shows zebra bodies
- Endomyocardial biopsy: Rarely needed
- Not required if genetic diagnosis confirmed
```

### Management

#### Enzyme Replacement Therapy (ERT)

**FDA-Approved Therapies:**
```
Agalsidase Beta (Fabrazyme):
- Dosing: 1 mg/kg IV every 2 weeks
- FDA approved 2003
- Evidence: ESRD prevention, cardiac benefits
- Monitoring: Pre-medication for infusion reactions

Agalsidase Alfa (Replagal):
- Dosing: 0.2 mg/kg IV every 2 weeks
- Available in Europe (not FDA approved in US)
- Similar efficacy to agalsidase beta

Pegunigalsidase Alfa (Elfabrio):
- FDA approved May 2023
- Dosing: 1 mg/kg IV every 2 or 4 weeks
- PEGylated formulation: Reduced immunogenicity
- Advantage: Less frequent dosing option (q4 weeks)
```

**ERT Efficacy and Timing:**
- **Start Early**: Best outcomes when started before irreversible organ damage
- **Renal Benefits**: Stabilizes eGFR, reduces proteinuria if started with eGFR >60
- **Cardiac Benefits**: Reduces LVH mass, improves diastolic function
- **Neurologic**: Reduces stroke risk, improves neuropathic pain
- **Limitations**: Does not reverse fibrosis or advanced organ damage

#### Chaperone Therapy
```
Migalastat (Galafold):
- FDA approved August 2018
- Oral pharmacological chaperone
- Mechanism: Stabilizes mutant α-Gal A, enhances trafficking to lysosomes
- Indication: Amenable GLA mutations (about 35-50% of mutations)
- Dosing: 123 mg PO every other day
- Testing: In vitro amenability assay required before prescribing

Advantages over ERT:
- Oral administration (no infusions)
- No infusion reactions or antibody formation
- Better patient convenience and quality of life

Limitations:
- Only works for specific amenable mutations
- Not suitable for all Fabry patients
- Contraindicated with strong CYP2D6 inhibitors
```

#### Adjunctive Therapies
- **ACE Inhibitors/ARBs**: For proteinuria and hypertension
- **SGLT2 Inhibitors**: Emerging evidence for renal protection (2024 data)
- **Antiplatelet Therapy**: Stroke prevention
- **Pain Management**: Carbamazepine, gabapentin for neuropathic pain
- **Cardiac Medications**: Beta-blockers, antiarrhythmics as needed

#### Monitoring Protocol
```
Renal Monitoring:
- eGFR: Every 3-6 months
- Urine protein/creatinine ratio: Every 3-6 months
- Annual assessment: Blood pressure, electrolytes

Cardiac Monitoring:
- Echocardiogram: Annually
- ECG: Annually (Holter if arrhythmias)
- Cardiac MRI: Baseline and every 2-3 years
- Troponin, BNP: Every 6-12 months

Neurologic Monitoring:
- Brain MRI: Baseline, repeat if symptoms
- Stroke risk assessment: Carotid ultrasound in high-risk patients

Quality of Life:
- Pain assessment: Visual analog scale
- Gastrointestinal symptoms: Dietary counseling
- Fabry-specific QOL questionnaires
```

### Novel and Emerging Therapies (2023-2024)

#### Gene Therapy
- **ST-920 (Sangamo/Pfizer)**: In vivo gene therapy, Phase I/II trials
- **FLT190 (Freeline)**: AAV-based gene therapy, Phase I/II trials ongoing
- **Mechanism**: Liver-directed gene transfer to produce α-Gal A
- **Early Results**: Sustained enzyme activity, reduced Gb3 levels
- **Challenges**: Long-term safety, durability, immune responses

#### Substrate Reduction Therapy
- **Lucerastat**: Oral glucosylceramide synthase inhibitor, Phase III trials
- **Venglustat**: Similar mechanism, development halted 2021
- **Rationale**: Reduce Gb3 production at source

#### mRNA Therapy
- **Investigational**: mRNA-encoded α-Gal A in preclinical development
- **Advantages**: Repeated dosing flexibility, no genomic integration

### Renal Transplantation
- **Outcomes**: Excellent allograft survival
- **ERT Continuation**: Required post-transplant (native organs still deficient)
- **Recurrence**: Does not occur (donor kidney has normal α-Gal A)
- **Timing**: ERT should continue pre- and post-transplant

### Genetic Counseling and Family Screening
- **Inheritance Pattern**: 50% risk for offspring (X-linked)
- **Female Carriers**: Variable expression, but often symptomatic
- **Cascade Testing**: Screen all at-risk family members
- **Preimplantation Genetic Testing**: Available for known mutations
- **Pregnancy Considerations**: ERT contraindicated in pregnancy (insufficient data)

## Congenital Anomalies of the Kidney and Urinary Tract (CAKUT)

### Classification and Spectrum

#### Renal Malformations
- **Renal Agenesis**: Bilateral (lethal) or unilateral (compensatory hypertrophy)
- **Renal Hypoplasia**: Small kidney with normal architecture
- **Renal Dysplasia**: Abnormal tissue development, cysts present
- **Multicystic Dysplastic Kidney**: Non-functional cystic mass

#### Collecting System Abnormalities
- **Ureteropelvic Junction Obstruction**: Most common obstructive uropathy
- **Megaureter**: Dilated ureter with or without obstruction
- **Ureterovesical Junction Obstruction**: Primary megaureter
- **Posterior Urethral Valves**: Male-only, obstructive uropathy

#### Fusion Anomalies
- **Horseshoe Kidney**: Isthmus connecting lower poles
- **Crossed Fused Ectopia**: Kidney on opposite side fused
- **Pelvic Kidney**: Failure of ascent

### Genetic Causes

#### Syndromic CAKUT
```
Renal-Coloboma Syndrome (PAX2):
- Optic nerve colobomas
- Renal hypoplasia/dysplasia
- Variable hearing loss

Branchio-Oto-Renal Syndrome (EYA1):
- Branchial cleft cysts/fistulae
- Hearing loss
- Renal anomalies

Townes-Brocks Syndrome (SALL1):
- Imperforate anus
- Thumb anomalies
- Renal malformations
```

#### Non-syndromic CAKUT
- **HNF1B Mutations**: Renal cysts, diabetes, genital tract malformations
- **PAX2 Mutations**: Renal hypoplasia, optic coloboma
- **BMP7, GDNF, RET**: Various renal developmental genes

### Clinical Management

#### Diagnostic Evaluation
- **Prenatal Ultrasound**: Detects most major anomalies
- **Postnatal Imaging**: Renal ultrasound, VCUG for obstruction
- **Functional Studies**: Nuclear medicine scans for differential function
- **Genetic Testing**: Chromosomal microarray, gene panels

#### Treatment Principles
- **Obstruction Relief**: Pyeloplasty for UPJ obstruction, valve ablation for PUV
- **Infection Prevention**: Prophylactic antibiotics for VUR
- **Renal Function Monitoring**: Serial eGFR, blood pressure
- **Surgical Timing**: Balance renal preservation vs surgical risks

## Genetic Testing in Nephrology

### Testing Modalities

#### Single Gene Testing
- **Sanger Sequencing**: Gold standard, low throughput
- **Targeted Analysis**: Known familial mutations
- **Indications**: Strong clinical suspicion, known family mutation
- **Turnaround**: 1-2 weeks
- **Cost**: $200-500 per gene

#### Gene Panels
```
Next-Generation Sequencing Platforms (2024):
- 50-400 genes simultaneously analyzed
- Turnaround time: 2-4 weeks
- Cost: $1,500-3,000
- Diagnostic yield: 30-50% depending on phenotype

Common Nephrology Panels:
- Cystic Kidney Disease Panel: PKD1, PKD2, PKHD1, HNF1B, others (15-30 genes)
- Glomerular Disease Panel: COL4A3/4/5, NPHS1/2, TRPC6, INF2, others (50-150 genes)
- Tubulopathy Panel: SLC genes, ATP6V genes, CLCN5, others (40-100 genes)
- Comprehensive Kidney Panel: 200-400 genes covering all kidney diseases

Advantages:
- Cost-effective compared to sequential single-gene testing
- High diagnostic yield (30-50% in selected populations)
- Identifies unexpected diagnoses

Limitations:
- Variants of uncertain significance (VUS) in 20-40% of tests
- Incidental findings in unrelated genes
- Coverage gaps in difficult regions (e.g., PKD1 pseudogene region)
```

#### Whole Exome/Genome Sequencing
```
Whole Exome Sequencing (WES):
- All coding regions (~20,000 genes)
- Turnaround: 4-8 weeks
- Cost: $3,000-5,000
- Diagnostic yield: 25-40% after negative panel testing

Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS):
- Entire genome including non-coding regions
- Turnaround: 6-12 weeks
- Cost: $5,000-10,000 (decreasing)
- Advantages: Detects structural variants, deep intronic variants
- Diagnostic yield: 30-50% after negative WES

Indications (2024 Guidelines):
- Negative gene panel with high clinical suspicion
- Complex/atypical phenotypes
- Suspected novel genetic disease
- Comprehensive genetic diagnosis for family planning

Challenges:
- Massive data interpretation burden
- High rate of VUS (40-60%)
- Incidental findings (ACMG secondary findings)
- Reanalysis every 2-3 years as knowledge evolves
```

#### Long-Read Sequencing (Emerging 2023-2024)
```
Technologies:
- Oxford Nanopore sequencing
- PacBio HiFi sequencing

Advantages:
- Resolves complex genomic regions (e.g., PKD1 locus)
- Detects structural variants missed by short-read NGS
- Phases variants to determine cis vs trans configuration
- Identifies repeat expansions

Current Applications:
- PKD1/PKD2 sequencing (resolves pseudogene interference)
- Structural variant detection in CAKUT
- Research transitioning to clinical use

Limitations:
- Higher cost than short-read NGS
- Limited clinical availability (2024)
- Interpretation tools still developing
```

### AI-Powered Variant Interpretation and Risk Prediction (2023-2024)

#### AI Variant Pathogenicity Prediction
```
AlphaMissense (Google DeepMind, 2023):
- AI model predicting pathogenicity of all possible missense variants
- Covers 71 million possible single amino acid changes
- Outperforms traditional tools (SIFT, PolyPhen-2)
- Integrated into ClinVar and genetic testing platforms

Application in Nephrology:
- Reclassifies VUS to likely pathogenic or benign
- Particularly useful for novel PKD1/PKD2 variants
- Helps distinguish disease-causing from benign variation in COL4A genes

Limitations:
- Does not replace clinical judgment
- ACMG guidelines require multiple lines of evidence
- Should be used as supporting evidence only
```

#### Machine Learning for Genetic Diagnosis
```
Platforms:
- PhenoTips: AI-assisted phenotype-genotype matching
- GeneMatcher: Connects clinicians with similar rare cases
- MARRVEL: Multi-organism variant interpretation

Benefits:
- Faster variant interpretation
- Identifies candidate genes from WES/WGS data
- Suggests functional studies for VUS
```

#### AI-Enhanced Genetic Risk Prediction
```
RenalytixAI KidneyIntelX:
- FDA-cleared prognostic test for diabetic kidney disease
- Combines clinical data + genetic variants + AI/ML algorithms
- Predicts risk of eGFR decline and kidney failure
- Genetic component: APOL1, UMOD, and other validated variants

Application:
- Risk stratification in diabetic CKD
- Identifies high-risk patients for intensive management
- Guides clinical trial enrollment
- NOT diagnostic for monogenic kidney disease (prognostic tool)

Integration with Genetic Testing:
- Complements traditional genetic testing
- Polygenic risk scores becoming available for CKD
- Future: Combined monogenic + polygenic risk assessment
```

#### Polygenic Risk Scores (PRS)
```
Current Applications in Nephrology (2024):
- APOL1-associated CKD: Two-hit model (G1/G2 risk variants)
- IgA Nephropathy: Polygenic risk score under development
- Diabetic kidney disease: Multiple variants contributing to risk

Clinical Utility:
- Risk stratification beyond monogenic disease
- Guides screening intensity for family members
- May influence treatment decisions in the future

Limitations:
- Most validated in European ancestry populations
- Limited clinical actionability (no specific therapies yet)
- Requires large-scale validation studies
```

### Updated Variant Classification (ACMG/AMP 2024 Guidelines)

#### Variant Classification Categories
```
Pathogenic (P):
- >99% certainty of disease causation
- Direct clinical management implications

Likely Pathogenic (LP):
- 90-99% certainty
- Usually sufficient for clinical decision-making

Variant of Uncertain Significance (VUS):
- 10-90% certainty
- Should NOT be used for clinical decisions
- Requires functional studies or segregation data

Likely Benign (LB):
- 90-99% certainty benign
- Reassuring but not definitive

Benign (B):
- >99% certainty benign
- No clinical significance
```

#### ACMG/AMP 2024 Updates
```
Key Changes from 2015 Guidelines:
- ClinGen gene-specific variant classification criteria
- Updated population frequency thresholds (gnomAD v4)
- Functional evidence weighted more heavily
- In silico tools (AlphaMissense) integrated
- RNA sequencing for splicing variant assessment

Application in Nephrology:
- PKD Variant Database: Curated PKD1/PKD2 variants
- Alport Variant Database: COL4A3/4/5 variants
- ClinGen Kidney Disease Expert Panels: Gene-specific criteria
```

#### Variant Reinterpretation
```
2024 Recommendations:
- Reanalyze VUS every 2 years
- Automatic alerts when VUS reclassified in shared databases
- Contact patients with reclassified variants
- 10-20% of VUS reclassified within 2 years

Triggers for Reinterpretation:
- New clinical phenotype emerges
- Additional family members affected
- New functional evidence published
- Population databases updated (gnomAD)
```

### Pre- and Post-Test Counseling

#### Pre-Test Considerations
- **Informed Consent**: Discuss scope, limitations, possible results
- **Family Implications**: Cascade testing, reproductive planning
- **Insurance Coverage**: Varies by indication and test type
- **Psychological Impact**: Potential anxiety, family dynamics

#### Post-Test Counseling
- **Pathogenic Variants**: Clear management implications
- **VUS (Variants of Uncertain Significance)**: Unclear clinical significance
- **Negative Results**: Does not exclude genetic disease
- **Incidental Findings**: ACMG recommended secondary findings

### Ethical and Legal Considerations

#### Genetic Discrimination
- **GINA (Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act)**: Health insurance protection
- **Limitations**: Does not cover life, disability, or long-term care insurance
- **Employment**: Protected under GINA

#### Reproductive Issues
- **Preimplantation Genetic Testing**: Available for known mutations
- **Prenatal Testing**: CVS or amniocentesis for familial mutations
- **Carrier Screening**: Recommended for autosomal recessive conditions

#### Pediatric Testing
- **Best Interest Standard**: Testing should benefit the child
- **Adult-Onset Conditions**: Generally defer testing until adulthood
- **Exceptions**: When early intervention benefits the child

## Emerging Therapies and Future Directions (2024 Update)

### Gene-Specific Therapies

#### RNA-Based Therapies (Clinical Trials Ongoing)
```
Antisense Oligonucleotides (ASO):
- Alport Syndrome: QR-421a targeting COL4A5 mRNA (Phase 2, 2024)
- Mechanism: Modulates splicing or degrades mutant mRNA
- Delivery: Systemic infusion with kidney targeting
- Challenges: Podocyte delivery efficiency, durability

RNA Interference (siRNA):
- Fabry Disease: Lumasiran analog for substrate reduction (preclinical)
- ADPKD: PKD1/PKD2 modulation (early research)
- Advantage: Potent gene silencing

mRNA Therapy:
- Fabry Disease: mRNA-encoded α-galactosidase A (preclinical)
- Alport Syndrome: COL4A5 mRNA delivery (early research)
- Advantages: Repeated dosing flexibility, no genomic integration
- Challenges: Kidney-specific delivery, duration of effect
```

#### Gene Editing (5-10 Years from Clinical Use)
```
CRISPR-Cas9 Gene Editing:
- Ex Vivo Approaches: Edit patient cells, expand, transplant
- In Vivo Editing: Systemic delivery to edit kidney cells directly
- Current Focus: ADPKD (PKD1/PKD2), Alport (COL4A genes)
- Challenge: Delivery to podocytes and tubular cells

Base Editing (More Precise):
- Corrects single nucleotide variants without DSBs
- Lower off-target effects than CRISPR-Cas9
- Prime Editing: Even more precise, corrects any DNA change
- 2024 Status: Preclinical models in kidney disease

Epigenome Editing:
- Modulate gene expression without changing DNA sequence
- CRISPRa/CRISPRi: Activate or inhibit gene expression
- Potential: Upregulate wild-type allele in heterozygotes
- Early research stage

Delivery Challenges (Major Barrier):
- Kidney tropism: AAV serotypes inefficient for kidney
- Novel capsids: Engineered AAV with kidney targeting
- Lipid nanoparticles: mRNA and base editor delivery
- 2024 Progress: Improved kidney targeting vectors in development
```

#### Gene Therapy (AAV-Based)
```
Fabry Disease:
- ST-920 (Sangamo/Pfizer): Phase I/II, liver-directed α-Gal A production
- FLT190 (Freeline): Phase I/II, sustained enzyme expression
- 2024 Results: >90% reduction in Gb3, sustained >2 years
- Expected approval: 2026-2027 if Phase III successful

Alport Syndrome:
- Challenges: COL4A5 gene too large for single AAV vector
- Dual AAV strategy: Two vectors reconstitute full gene
- Preclinical only, human trials 5+ years away

ADPKD:
- Gene suppression approaches (PKD1 haploinsufficiency model)
- Very early research, conceptual stage
```

### Targeted Molecular Therapies

#### mTOR Inhibitors
```
Tuberous Sclerosis (Established):
- Sirolimus, everolimus: FDA-approved for angiomyolipomas
- Long-term data (2024): Sustained lesion size reduction
- Side effect management: Lower doses effective

ADPKD (Investigational):
- SIRENA Trial (2023): No eGFR benefit with sirolimus
- Likely ineffective as monotherapy
- Potential: Combination with tolvaptan (theoretical)

Alport Syndrome:
- Small studies: Reduced proteinuria and fibrosis
- Insufficient evidence for clinical use
- Further trials needed
```

#### Novel Vasopressin Antagonists
```
Lixivaptan (Non-peptide V2 antagonist):
- Oral bioavailability similar to tolvaptan
- Potentially better tolerability profile
- Phase 2 trials in ADPKD (2024)
- Not yet FDA-approved

Aquaretic Combinations:
- V2 antagonist + urea transporter inhibitor
- May reduce aquaresis burden
- Preclinical development

Future: Long-acting formulations or alternate delivery
```

#### SGLT2 Inhibitors (Expanded Indications)
```
Current Evidence (2024):
- Diabetic CKD: FDA-approved (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin)
- IgA Nephropathy: DAPA-CKD, EMPA-KIDNEY trials positive
- Alport Syndrome: Emerging evidence (see above)
- ADPKD: DAPASALT trial positive (investigational)

Mechanism Beyond Glucose:
- Metabolic reprogramming: Shifts to ketone utilization
- Anti-inflammatory: Reduces kidney inflammation
- Anti-fibrotic: Decreases interstitial fibrosis
- Hemodynamic: Reduces glomerular hyperfiltration

Future Applications in Genetic Kidney Diseases:
- Likely to become standard across proteinuric diseases
- Ongoing trials in Fabry, FSGS, various genetic conditions
```

#### JAK Inhibitors
```
Baricitinib (ADPKD):
- See ADPKD management section above
- Phase 3 trials ongoing
- May be FDA-approved by 2026

Broader Application:
- Potential in inflammatory genetic kidney diseases
- FSGS, complement-mediated diseases
- Early investigational stage
```

### Regenerative Medicine

#### Stem Cell Therapy
```
Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells (iPSC):
- Patient-specific kidney organoids from iPSCs
- Disease modeling: ADPKD, Alport, Fabry organoids
- Drug screening: Test therapies on patient-specific tissue
- Future: Transplantable kidney tissue (10-20 years)

Mesenchymal Stem Cells (MSC):
- Anti-inflammatory and regenerative paracrine effects
- Small trials in CKD: Modest eGFR stabilization
- 2024 Status: Inconclusive benefit, need larger trials
- Safety: Generally well-tolerated

Challenges (All Stem Cell Approaches):
- Integration into native kidney
- Functional capacity (filtration, reabsorption)
- Long-term survival and safety
- Tumorigenicity risk
- Scalability for clinical use
```

#### Kidney Organoids and Bioengineering
```
Kidney Organoids (Mini-kidneys):
- iPSC-derived 3D kidney structures
- Contain nephron-like structures with podocytes, tubules
- Applications:
  - Disease modeling (ADPKD cysts, Alport GBM defects)
  - Drug screening and toxicity testing
  - Personalized medicine (test patient-specific responses)
- Limitations: Immature nephrons, lack of vasculature, small size

Decellularized Scaffolds:
- Human or animal kidney scaffolds stripped of cells
- Re-cellularize with patient cells
- Maintains 3D architecture and vasculature
- 2024 Status: Proof-of-concept, not clinical-ready

3D Bioprinting:
- Print kidney tissue layer by layer
- Custom architecture, multiple cell types
- Very early stage, major technical hurdles
- 10-20 years from clinical application

Bioartificial Kidney:
- Wearable or implantable device with living cells
- Combines filtration device + tubular cell bioreactor
- Phase 1 safety trials ongoing (2024)
- Not replacement for transplantation yet
```

### Precision Medicine and Personalized Therapy (2024)

#### Genotype-Guided Treatment Selection
```
Examples:
- Migalastat (Fabry): Only for amenable mutations, in vitro testing required
- Tolvaptan (ADPKD): Mayo classification guides patient selection
- ACEi timing (Alport): Earlier in males with XLAS vs females

Future Directions:
- Pharmacogenomics: Genetic variants predict drug response
- Example: APOL1 status may guide SGLT2i use in African Americans
- Liquid biopsy: Circulating biomarkers predict progression
```

#### Biomarker-Guided Therapy
```
Prognostic Biomarkers (2024):
- Lyso-Gb3 (Fabry): Predicts organ involvement, guides ERT monitoring
- htTKV growth rate (ADPKD): Predicts eGFR decline, guides tolvaptan use
- Urine biomarkers (Alport): EGF, MCP-1 predict progression

Pharmacodynamic Biomarkers:
- Monitor treatment response in real-time
- Adjust dosing based on biomarker levels
- Earlier detection of treatment failure

Integration with AI/ML:
- RenalytixAI and similar platforms
- Combine genetic + clinical + biomarker data
- Personalized risk prediction and treatment plans
```

## Key Practice Points (2024 Update)

### Clinical Pearls
1. **ADPKD Management (2024)**:
   - Tolvaptan for Mayo Class 1C-1E with rapid progression
   - Consider adding SGLT2 inhibitor if diabetic or proteinuric
   - Baricitinib may be available 2025-2026 (Phase 3 ongoing)
   - htTKV growth rate predicts eGFR decline

2. **Alport Syndrome (2024)**:
   - Start ACEi/ARB early (age 5-7 if proteinuric) - delays ESRD by 6-10 years
   - Add SGLT2 inhibitor if proteinuria persists despite max ACEi/ARB
   - Bardoxolone methyl FDA decision expected 2025-2026
   - Genetic testing REQUIRED for female diagnosis (enzyme assay unreliable)

3. **Fabry Disease (2024)**:
   - Screen: Unexplained LVH, cryptogenic stroke <55 years, CKD of unknown etiology
   - Migalastat (oral) for amenable mutations; ERT for non-amenable
   - Pegunigalsidase alfa (PEGylated ERT) available since 2023
   - Gene therapy trials promising (ST-920, FLT190)
   - Start therapy EARLY before irreversible organ damage

4. **Genetic Testing (2024)**:
   - Gene panels first-line (30-50% diagnostic yield)
   - WES/WGS if panel negative and high clinical suspicion
   - AlphaMissense AI tool helps reclassify VUS
   - Reanalyze VUS every 2 years (10-20% reclassified)
   - RenalytixAI integrates genetics + AI for risk prediction in diabetic CKD

5. **TSC Angiomyolipomas**:
   - mTOR inhibitors (sirolimus, everolimus) for lesions >3 cm
   - Monitor MRI every 1-3 years
   - Excellent long-term outcomes with mTOR therapy

6. **Family Screening**:
   - Essential for all autosomal dominant conditions (ADPKD, Alport, Fabry, TSC)
   - Cascade testing of at-risk relatives
   - Living kidney donor evaluation MUST include genetic testing

### Red Flags Requiring Genetic Evaluation
- **Early ESRD**: Age <30 years (especially <20)
- **Family History**: ≥2 relatives with kidney disease
- **Extrarenal Manifestations**:
  - Hearing loss + hematuria = Alport syndrome
  - Angiokeratomas + proteinuria = Fabry disease
  - LVH in young patient without hypertension = Fabry disease
  - Seizures + skin lesions + kidney cysts = TSC
  - Intracranial aneurysm + large kidneys = ADPKD
- **Consanguinity**: High risk for autosomal recessive diseases (ARPKD, ARAS)
- **Prenatal Findings**: Bilateral renal anomalies, oligohydramnios
- **Biopsy Findings**: FSGS in young patient, "zebra bodies" on EM (Fabry)
- **Unexplained Proteinuria**: Especially in children or young adults

### 2024 Treatment Algorithm Overview

**ADPKD:**
1. Blood pressure control (ACEi/ARB), target <130/80
2. If rapidly progressive (Mayo 1C-1E): Tolvaptan
3. If diabetic or proteinuric: Add SGLT2 inhibitor
4. Future: Baricitinib (awaiting FDA approval)

**Alport Syndrome:**
1. ACEi or ARB (start early, maximize dose)
2. Add SGLT2 inhibitor if proteinuria >300 mg/g despite ACEi/ARB
3. Future: Bardoxolone methyl (FDA decision 2025-2026)
4. Blood pressure target <120/75 mmHg

**Fabry Disease:**
1. If amenable mutation: Migalastat (oral) preferred
2. If non-amenable: ERT (Fabrazyme, Replagal, Elfabrio)
3. Adjunctive: ACEi/ARB for proteinuria, SGLT2i emerging
4. Future: Gene therapy (ST-920, FLT190) in trials

**TSC Angiomyolipomas:**
1. Surveillance MRI every 1-3 years
2. If >3 cm: mTOR inhibitor (sirolimus or everolimus)
3. If acute hemorrhage: Embolization
4. Nephron-sparing surgery for select cases

### Prevention Strategies (2024)
- **Genetic Counseling**: Before testing and family planning
- **Preimplantation Genetic Testing**: Available for known mutations
- **Early Diagnosis**: Screen all at-risk family members
- **Blood Pressure Control**: Target <130/80 (ADPKD), <120/75 (Alport)
- **Proteinuria Management**: Early RAAS blockade + SGLT2i
- **Regular Monitoring**:
  - ADPKD: eGFR q6-12mo, BP q3mo, MRI for htTKV every 1-2 years
  - Alport: eGFR q3-6mo, proteinuria q3mo, audiology annually
  - Fabry: eGFR q3-6mo, echo annually, cardiac MRI q2-3yrs
  - TSC: eGFR q6-12mo, MRI kidneys q1-3yrs

### Novel Therapies on the Horizon (Expected 2025-2027)
1. **Bardoxolone Methyl (Alport)**: FDA decision expected 2025-2026
2. **Baricitinib (ADPKD)**: Phase 3 trials ongoing, possible approval 2026
3. **Gene Therapy (Fabry)**: ST-920 and FLT190 Phase III expected 2025-2027
4. **Antisense Oligonucleotides (Alport)**: QR-421a Phase 2 ongoing
5. **Long-Read Sequencing**: Resolves PKD1 locus, improving diagnostic yield
6. **AI Variant Interpretation**: AlphaMissense + RenalytixAI integration

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid
1. **Delaying genetic testing** in young patients with CKD
2. **Not screening family members** after genetic diagnosis
3. **Starting tolvaptan too late** (after significant scarring)
4. **Missing Fabry disease** in young stroke patients or unexplained LVH
5. **Not testing females for Alport** (enzyme assay unreliable, need genetic test)
6. **Stopping ERT in Fabry** after transplant (native organs still deficient)
7. **Using VUS for clinical decisions** (wait for reclassification or functional data)
8. **Not reanalyzing VUS** every 2 years (miss reclassifications)
