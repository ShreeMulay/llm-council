## Pathophysiology & Principles

### Peritoneal Membrane Physiology
- **Three-Layer Structure**: Mesothelium, interstitium, capillary endothelium
- **Transport Mechanisms**: Diffusion, convection, osmosis
- **Pore Theory**: Small pores (solute), large pores (proteins), ultrasmall pores (water)
- **Effective Peritoneal Surface Area**: ~1-2 m², varies between patients

### Solute Transport Kinetics
- **Diffusion**: Concentration gradient-driven solute movement
- **Convection**: Solvent drag with ultrafiltration
- **Sieve Coefficient**: Solute-specific membrane permeability
- **Mass Transfer Area Coefficient (MTAC)**: Quantifies peritoneal transport capacity

### Ultrafiltration Mechanisms
- **Osmotic Gradient**: Glucose-based dialysate creates water movement
- **Hydrostatic Pressure**: Limited role in peritoneal dialysis
- **Lymphatic Absorption**: 1-2 mL/min, reduces net UF
- **Sodium Sieving**: Early dialysate sodium depletion due to water-only transport

## Patient Selection & Evaluation

### Ideal Candidates
- **Age**: Children, elderly adults
- **Hemodynamic Stability**: Unstable patients, cardiovascular disease
- **Vascular Access**: Difficult IV access, failed AV fistulas/grafts
- **Lifestyle**: Employment, travel, school attendance
- **Geographic**: Remote locations, limited dialysis centers

### Contraindications

#### Absolute Contraindications
- **Peritoneal Membrane Failure**: Sclerosing peritonitis, extensive adhesions
- **Abdominal Wall Defects**: Large hernias, omphalocele
- **Intra-abdominal Infections**: Active peritonitis, abscesses
- **Severe Bowel Disease**: Inflammatory bowel disease with fistulae

#### Relative Contraindications
- **Obesity**: BMI >35 kg/m² (technical difficulty)
- **Multiple Abdominal Surgeries**: Adhesion risk
- **Diverticulosis**: Perforation risk
- **Severe Malnutrition**: Protein losses
- **Cognitive Impairment**: Inability to perform self-care

### Pre-Implantation Evaluation
- **Medical Assessment**: Cardiovascular status, respiratory function
- **Abdominal Examination**: Hernias, previous surgeries, skin integrity
- **Psychosocial Evaluation**: Manual dexterity, vision, cognitive function
- **Home Environment**: Clean space, storage, electricity, water supply
- **Support System**: Family assistance, transportation

## Peritoneal Access

### Catheter Types
- **Tenckhoff Catheter**: Standard straight catheter
- **Swan-Neck Catheter**: Preformed bend, reduces tunnel infections
- **Curl Catheter**: Coiled intraperitoneal segment
- **Missouri Catheter**: Silicone with bead segments

### Catheter Placement Techniques

#### Surgical Placement
- **Open Laparotomy**: Traditional approach, direct visualization
- **Laparoscopic**: Minimally invasive, adhesion lysis
- **Blind Placement**: Seldinger technique, limited use

#### Optimal Catheter Position
- **Intraperitoneal Tip**: Pelvic cavity (true pelvis)
- **Tunnel Direction**: Downward and lateral
- **Exit Site**: Lateral to rectus muscle, away from skin folds
- **Deep Cuff**: Within rectus muscle
- **Superficial Cuff**: Subcutaneous tissue

### Exit Site Classification
- **Healing Phase**: 0-2 weeks post-implantation
- **Early Exit Site**: 2-4 weeks
- **Late Exit Site**: >4 weeks
- **Cuff Extrusion**: Visible cuff material

## Dialysis Modalities

### Continuous Ambulatory Peritoneal Dialysis (CAPD)

#### Standard Protocol
- **Exchange Frequency**: 4 exchanges/day
- **Dwell Time**: 4-6 hours (daytime), 8-10 hours (nighttime)
- **Volume**: 2.0-2.5 L per exchange
- **Connection System**: Disconnect systems (Y-set, double-bag)

#### Advantages
- **Simplicity**: No machine required
- **Cost**: Lower equipment costs
- **Portability**: Travel friendly
- **Continuous**: Steady-state physiology

#### Disadvantages
- **Time Commitment**: 30-40 minutes/exchange
- **Technique Failure**: Higher peritonitis rates
- **Lifestyle**: Multiple daily exchanges

### Automated Peritoneal Dialysis (APD)

#### Modalities
- **Continuous Cyclic Peritoneal Dialysis (CCPD)**: Nighttime exchanges with daytime dwell
- **Intermittent Peritoneal Dialysis (IPD)**: Nighttime only, dry daytime
- **Tidal Peritoneal Dialysis (TPD)**: Partial drain, continuous fill

#### Prescription Parameters
- **Total Volume**: 8-20 L/night
- **Cycle Time**: 1-3 hours
- **Fill Volume**: 1.5-3.0 L per cycle
- **Last Fill**: Daytime dwell (optional)

#### Advantages
- **Convenience**: Nighttime treatment
- **Adequacy**: Higher clearance capacity
- **Lower Peritonitis**: Reduced connections
- **Rehabilitation**: Daytime freedom

#### Disadvantages
- **Equipment**: Cycler machine required
- **Cost**: Higher consumable costs
- **Complexity**: Machine programming
- **Power Dependency**: Electricity required

## Dialysis Solutions

### Conventional Solutions
- **Glucose Concentration**: 1.5%, 2.5%, 4.25%
- **Osmolarity**: 346, 395, 485 mOsm/L
- **Lactate Buffer**: 35-40 mEq/L
- **Electrolytes**: Sodium 132 mEq/L, Calcium 3.5 mEq/L, Magnesium 0.5-1.5 mEq/L

### Biocompatible Solutions
- **Neutral pH**: 7.0-7.4 (vs 5.2-5.5 conventional)
- **Low GDP**: Reduced glucose degradation products
- **Alternative Buffers**: Bicarbonate, bicarbonate/lactate
- **Amino Acid Solutions**: Nutritional supplementation

**Clinical Evidence (2020-2024):**
- **Peritoneal Membrane Preservation:** Lower transport rates over time
- **Residual Renal Function:** Better preservation (30-50% longer maintenance)
- **Peritonitis Rates:** No significant difference vs conventional
- **Cost-Benefit:** Justified by RRF preservation and technique survival
- **2024 Recommendation:** Use biocompatible solutions when available/affordable

**Available Biocompatible Solutions:**
- **Physioneal (Baxter):** Bicarbonate/lactate buffer, pH 7.4, low GDP
- **Balance (Fresenius):** Bicarbonate/lactate buffer, neutral pH
- **Extraneal (Icodextrin - Baxter):** See below

### Icodextrin Solution
- **High Molecular Weight**: 16,800 Da glucose polymer
- **Colloid Osmosis**: Sustained ultrafiltration
- **Long Dwell**: 8-16 hour dwells
- **Indications**: High transporters, fluid overload

**Clinical Applications (2024 Evidence):**
- **Ultrafiltration:** Sustained UF over 12-16 hours (400-800 mL/exchange)
- **Glucose Exposure Reduction:** 30-40% less glucose absorption
- **Cardiovascular Benefits:** Reduced volume overload, better BP control
- **High Transporters:** Essential for adequate UF
- **Diabetic Patients:**
  - Improved glycemic control
  - **CAUTION:** Maltose metabolite interferes with glucose meters
  - Only use glucose-dehydrogenase (GDH) strips specific for glucose

**Recent Trial Data:**
- **IPOD Study Extension (2023):** Long-term icodextrin use associated with:
  - 15% reduction in cardiovascular mortality
  - Better volume control
  - Improved technique survival
- **2024 Consensus:** Use icodextrin for all high/high-average transporters

### Amino Acid Solutions
- **Nutritional Benefits**: 5-10 g protein equivalent/day
- **Nitrogen Balance**: Prevents catabolism
- **Limitations**: Acid load, cost, limited UF

**Current Use (2024):**
- **Indicated:** Malnourished patients (albumin <3.0 g/dL)
- **Frequency:** 1 exchange daily maximum
- **Nutrineal (Baxter):** 1.1% amino acid solution
- **Monitoring:** Acid-base status, BUN elevation
- **Evidence:** Modest improvement in nutritional parameters (albumin +0.2-0.3 g/dL)

## Prescription & Adequacy

### Adequacy Targets
- **Kt/V (Weekly)**: ≥1.7 (CAPD), ≥1.8 (APD)
- **Creatinine Clearance**: ≥50 L/week/1.73m²
- **Total Fluid Removal**: Individualized based on volume status

### PET Testing (Peritoneal Equilibration Test)

#### Procedure
- **4-Hour Standard Test**: 2.5% glucose, 4-hour dwell
- **D/P Creatinine Ratio**: 0.34-0.49 (low), 0.50-0.65 (average), 0.66-0.81 (high)
- **D/D0 Glucose Ratio**: >0.81 (low), 0.66-0.81 (average), <0.65 (high)
- **Net Ultrafiltration**: >1000 mL (low), 500-1000 mL (average), <500 mL (high)

#### Transport Categories
- **High Transporters**: Rapid solute equilibration, poor UF
- **High Average Transporters**: Balanced transport
- **Low Average Transporters**: Slower equilibration, good UF
- **Low Transporters**: Slow equilibration, excellent UF

### Prescription Optimization

#### High Transporters
- **Short Dwell Times**: APD with rapid cycles
- **Icodextrin**: Long dwell solution
- **Low Volume**: Reduced fill volumes
- **APD Preference**: Better clearance control

#### Low Transporters
- **Long Dwell Times**: CAPD or long APD cycles
- **High Volume**: Larger fill volumes
- **Increased Frequency**: More exchanges
- **Conventional Solutions**: Adequate UF

#### Average Transporters
- **Standard Protocols**: Conventional CAPD/APD
- **Flexible Prescription**: Multiple options
- **Individual Optimization**: Based on lifestyle

## Complications Management

### Infectious Complications

#### Peritonitis

##### Diagnostic Criteria (2 of 3)
- **Clinical**: Abdominal pain, cloudy effluent
- **Laboratory**: WBC >100/μL with >50% neutrophils
- **Microbiology**: Positive culture

##### Common Organisms
- **Gram-Positive**: Coagulase-negative staphylococci (40%), S. aureus (15%)
- **Gram-Negative**: E. coli, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas (20%)
- **Fungal**: Candida species (2-5%)
- **Culture-Negative**: 15-20% of cases

##### Treatment Protocol (ISPD 2022 Update)
```
Empirical Therapy (while awaiting cultures):
- Gram-positive coverage: Vancomycin OR Cefazolin
  - Vancomycin: 15-30 mg/kg IP loading, then 1.5 mg/kg/day maintenance
  - Cefazolin: 20 mg/kg IP loading, then 15 mg/kg/day maintenance
- Gram-negative coverage: Ceftazidime OR Cefepime OR Aminoglycoside
  - Ceftazidime: 20 mg/kg IP loading, then 15 mg/kg/day maintenance
  - Gentamicin: 0.6 mg/kg IP loading, then 0.6 mg/kg/day maintenance

Intraperitoneal Administration Routes:
- Intermittent: One exchange per day with antibiotics (preferred)
- Continuous: Antibiotics in every exchange (if patient on APD)

Duration (ISPD 2022):
- Coagulase-negative Staphylococcus: 14 days
- S. aureus: 21 days (consider rifampin addition)
- Streptococcus: 14 days
- Enterococcus: 21 days (ampicillin or vancomycin)
- Gram-negative (single organism): 14 days
- Gram-negative (Pseudomonas/Stenotrophomonas): 21 days
- Polymicrobial: 21 days, consider catheter removal
- Fungal: IMMEDIATE catheter removal + 2 weeks antifungal (fluconazole 200 mg PO daily)
- Culture-negative with clinical improvement: 14 days

Catheter Removal Indications (ISPD 2022):
- Refractory peritonitis (>5 days appropriate therapy without improvement)
- Fungal peritonitis (absolute indication)
- Relapsing peritonitis (same organism <4 weeks after completion)
- Repeat peritonitis (different organism <4 weeks after completion)
- Mycobacterial peritonitis
- Severe peritonitis with abdominal catastrophe
```

**Novel Treatment Approaches (2020-2024):**
- **Urokinase for Fibrin:** 5,000 units IP with heparin for fibrin clots
- **Rifampin Adjunct:** For S. aureus (600 mg PO daily) reduces biofilm
- **Prolonged Dwell Technique:** First antibiotic exchange dwell 6 hours
- **APD Patients:** May continue cycler with antibiotics in every exchange

#### Exit Site/Tunnel Infections

##### Classification
- **Exit Site Infection**: Purulent discharge, erythema, tenderness
- **Tunnel Infection**: Tenderness along subcutaneous tract
- **Combined**: Both exit site and tunnel involvement

##### Prevention Strategies (ISPD 2023 Update)
**Exit Site Prophylaxis:**
- **Mupirocin Ointment (Bactroban):**
  - Apply to exit site daily
  - 60-80% reduction in S. aureus infections
  - **Standard of care** for S. aureus carriers
  - Alternative: Gentamicin cream for mupirocin resistance

- **S. aureus Nasal Screening:**
  - Screen all PD patients at start
  - If positive: Intranasal mupirocin BID × 5 days monthly
  - Consider oral rifampin 600 mg daily × 5 days for eradication

##### Treatment Protocol (ISPD 2022)
```
Mild Exit Site Infection:
- Oral antibiotics covering S. aureus:
  - Cephalexin 500 mg PO BID OR
  - Clindamycin 300 mg PO TID (if penicillin allergy)
  - MRSA suspected: Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID OR TMP-SMX DS BID
- Local care: Daily cleaning with soap/water or chlorhexidine
- Topical mupirocin or gentamicin to exit site
- Duration: 2-3 weeks, continue until complete resolution

Severe Exit Site/Tunnel Infection:
- Systemic antibiotics:
  - MSSA: Cephalexin or Clindamycin
  - MRSA: Vancomycin IV or Doxycycline/TMP-SMX PO
  - Pseudomonas: Ciprofloxacin 500-750 mg PO BID
- Consider catheter removal if:
  - Refractory >4 weeks appropriate therapy
  - Tunnel infection with purulent drainage
  - Concurrent peritonitis
- Antibiotic duration: 4-6 weeks if catheter retained
- Catheter removal: Wait 2-3 weeks for new catheter placement

Pseudomonas Exit Site Infection:
- **High-risk for peritonitis** (40-60% develop peritonitis)
- Dual antibiotic therapy:
  - Ciprofloxacin 500-750 mg PO BID
  - Consider adding IP therapy if not resolving
- Duration: Minimum 3 weeks
- Consider early catheter removal if not improving by 1 week
```

### Mechanical Complications

#### Catheter Dysfunction
- **Causes**: Catheter migration, omental wrapping, fibrin obstruction
- **Evaluation**: Catheterogram, abdominal X-ray
- **Management**: Catheter manipulation, laxatives, surgical revision

#### Dialysate Leaks
- **Early Leaks**: <30 days post-implantation
- **Late Leaks**: >30 days, often hernia-related
- **Management**: Temporary PD cessation, surgical repair

#### Hernias
- **Risk Factors**: High intra-abdominal pressure, chronic cough
- **Common Sites**: Inguinal, umbilical, incisional
- **Management**: Surgical repair, temporary HD during repair

### Metabolic Complications

#### Fluid Overload
- **Causes**: High transport, membrane failure, poor compliance
- **Management**: Icodextrin, APD optimization, volume restriction

#### Dehydration
- **Causes**: Excessive UF, inadequate fluid intake
- **Management**: Reduce dwell glucose, increase fluid intake

#### Hyperglycemia
- **Causes**: Glucose absorption from dialysate
- **Management**: Low glucose solutions, insulin adjustment

#### Protein Malnutrition
- **Causes**: Protein losses (5-15 g/day), inadequate intake
- **Management**: Nutritional supplementation, amino acid solutions

#### Hyperlipidemia
- **Causes**: Glucose load, protein losses
- **Management**: Dietary modification, lipid-lowering therapy

## Long-Term Complications

### Peritoneal Membrane Changes

#### Simple Sclerosis
- **Pathology**: Peritoneal fibrosis without symptoms
- **Incidence**: Common with long-term PD
- **Management**: Monitor transport status, consider modality change

#### Encapsulating Peritoneal Sclerosis (EPS)
- **Definition**: Severe peritoneal sclerosis with bowel encapsulation
- **Risk Factors**: Long PD duration, high transport, biocompatible solutions
- **Clinical Presentation**: Abdominal pain, nausea, weight loss, bowel obstruction
- **Diagnosis**: CT scan (peritoneal calcification, bowel tethering)
- **Treatment**: PD cessation, steroids, tamoxifen, surgical enterolysis

### Cardiovascular Disease
- **Risk Factors**: Volume overload, hypertension, dyslipidemia
- **Prevention**: Strict volume control, lipid management
- **Monitoring**: Regular cardiovascular assessment

### Bone Disease & Mineral Metabolism (2024 Update)
- **Pathophysiology**: Calcium-phosphate imbalance, vitamin D metabolism, PTH dysregulation
- **Targets (KDIGO 2017):** Phosphorus 3.5-5.5 mg/dL, PTH 2-9× ULN (150-600 pg/mL)

**Management Strategies:**

**Phosphate Binders (Peritoneal Dialysis-Specific Management):**

**Calcium-Based Binders:**
- **Calcium carbonate (Tums, generic)**
  - Dosing: 500-1000 mg elemental calcium with meals TID
  - **PD Consideration**: HIGH RISK - cumulative calcium load from dialysate (2.5-3.5 mEq/L) + oral binders
  - Risk: Hypercalcemia, vascular calcification, adynamic bone disease
  - **PD-Specific**: Calculate total calcium exposure = dietary + binders + dialysate absorption (~200-300 mg/day from PD)
  - Limit: <1500 mg elemental calcium/day from binders (lower than HD due to dialysate absorption)
- **Calcium acetate (PhosLo, Phoslyra)**
  - Dosing: 667-1334 mg with meals TID
  - More efficient than carbonate
  - **PD Consideration**: Same calcium overload risk as carbonate
  - **Generally AVOID** in PD unless using low-calcium dialysate (1.25 mEq/L)

**Non-Calcium-Based Binders (STRONGLY PREFERRED in PD):**
- **Sevelamer hydrochloride (Renagel)**
  - Dosing: 800-1600 mg TID with meals
  - **PD Advantage**: No additional calcium load (critical in PD)
  - **PD Disadvantage**: May worsen acidosis (PD has less acid removal than HD)
  - Pill burden: 6-12 tablets/day
  - Lowers LDL cholesterol
  - **Generally avoid in PD** (use carbonate form instead due to acidosis risk)
- **Sevelamer carbonate (Renvela)** ⭐ **PREFERRED IN PD**
  - Dosing: 800-1600 mg TID with meals
  - **PD-Preferred**: Does NOT cause acidosis, no calcium loading
  - **Best choice** for PD patients with vascular calcification
  - Same pill burden as hydrochloride form
  - Consider first-line in PD
- **Lanthanum carbonate (Fosrenol chewable)**
  - Dosing: 500-1000 mg elemental lanthanum TID with meals
  - **PD Advantage**: No calcium load, chewable (easy to take)
  - **PD Consideration**: Not removed by peritoneal dialysis (unknown long-term tissue accumulation)
  - Moderate pill burden
  - GI upset common

**Iron-Based Binders (Dual Benefit in PD):**
- **Ferric citrate (Auryxia)**
  - Dosing: 210 mg ferric iron (1-2 tablets) TID, up to 12 tablets/day
  - **PD Advantage**: Phosphate binding + iron supplementation (PD patients often iron deficient)
  - **PD Benefit**: May reduce need for IV iron (no easy IV access in PD vs HD)
  - Monitor: Ferritin, TSAT (target ferritin <500 ng/mL)
  - Black stools expected
  - Cost: $$$$
- **Sucroferric oxyhydroxide (Velphoro)**
  - Dosing: 500 mg TID with meals
  - **Lowest pill burden** (3 tablets/day) - excellent for adherence
  - **PD Advantage**: Iron supplementation + minimal pills
  - Excellent phosphate binding
  - Cost: $$$$$ (most expensive)

**Novel Mechanisms:**
- **Tenapanor (Xphosah)**
  - **FDA Approved**: September 2023 for hyperphosphatemia in adults with CKD on dialysis
  - Dosing: 10 mg PO BID
  - **PD Status**: FDA approval includes both HD and PD patients
  - **PD Consideration**: Diarrhea (30%) may increase peritoneal fluid losses and dehydration
  - Very low pill burden (2 tablets/day)
  - NOT a binder - NHE3 inhibitor
  - Cost: $$$$$

**Magnesium-Based Binders:**
- **Magnesium carbonate (generic)**
  - Dosing: 200-400 mg elemental magnesium TID
  - **PD Consideration**: Hypermagnesemia risk (PD removes magnesium less efficiently than HD)
  - **Use with extreme caution** - monitor serum magnesium weekly if used
  - Advantages: Cheap, laxative effect
  - **Generally NOT recommended in PD** due to accumulation risk
- **Magnesium hydroxide (Milk of Magnesia)**
  - **AVOID in PD** - high hypermagnesemia risk

**Aluminum-Based Binders (ABSOLUTELY AVOID IN PD):**
- **Aluminum hydroxide (Amphojel, AlternaGEL)** ⚠️ **NEVER USE IN PD**
  - **PD-Specific Risk**: Aluminum clearance by PD is MUCH LOWER than HD
  - Aluminum accumulates rapidly in PD patients
  - **Toxicities worse in PD**:
    - CNS: Encephalopathy, dementia, seizures
    - Bone: Osteomalacia, fractures
    - Blood: Microcytic anemia
  - **Historical Note**: PD patients had even worse aluminum toxicity than HD patients
  - **Absolute Contraindication in PD**
  - **Board Exam**: Know that aluminum is especially dangerous in PD (lower clearance)

**Peritoneal Dialysis Binder Selection Strategy (2024):**
1. **Preferred First-Line**: **Sevelamer carbonate (Renvela)** or **lanthanum (Fosrenol)**
   - **Critical Reason**: PD patients absorb 200-300 mg calcium/day from dialysate
   - Adding calcium-based binders = severe hypercalcemia risk
   - Sevelamer carbonate preferred (no acidosis, unlike sevelamer-HCl)
2. **Iron-Based**: Excellent choice for PD
   - Ferric citrate or sucroferric oxyhydroxide
   - Reduces need for IV iron (important since PD patients lack easy IV access)
3. **Calcium-Based**: Use ONLY if:
   - Using low-calcium dialysate (1.25 mEq/L)
   - Normal serum calcium
   - No vascular calcification
   - Monitor calcium weekly if used
4. **Avoid Calcium-Based** if using standard PD dialysate (2.5 mEq/L calcium)
5. **Combination Therapy**: Often required (e.g., sevelamer + ferric citrate)
6. **Adherence Focus**:
   - Sucroferric (Velphoro) - only 3 pills/day if adherence issue
   - Tenapanor as add-on for low pill burden
7. **Absolutely Avoid**:
   - Aluminum hydroxide (PD clears aluminum poorly - SEVERE accumulation risk)
   - Magnesium-based (PD clears magnesium poorly)
8. **Dialysate Calcium Adjustment**:
   - Consider switching to low-calcium dialysate (1.25 mEq/L) if using calcium binders
   - Reduces total calcium exposure

**PD-Specific Monitoring:**
- Check serum calcium, phosphorus, PTH monthly (more frequent than HD)
- Monitor for hypercalcemia (common in PD due to dialysate + binders)
- Assess adherence (pill burden major issue in PD)

See **Document 12 (Renal Pharmacology)** for additional phosphate binder pharmacology details

**Vitamin D Analogs:**
- **Calcitriol:** 0.25-0.5 mcg daily (monitor calcium/phosphorus closely)
- **Paricalcitol:** 1-4 mcg daily or TIW
- **Doxercalciferol:** 2.5-10 mcg TIW
- **Goal:** Suppress PTH to target range, maintain calcium/phosphorus

**Calcimimetics:**
- **Cinacalcet (Sensipar):** 30-180 mg daily
  - Reduces PTH, calcium, phosphorus
  - GI side effects common (nausea)
  - Monitor for hypocalcemia
- **Etelcalcetide (Parsabiv):** IV formulation (not used in PD patients typically)

### Anemia Management in PD Patients (2024 Update)

**Targets:** Hemoglobin 10-11.5 g/dL (avoid >13 g/dL)

**Erythropoiesis-Stimulating Agents (ESAs):**
- **Epoetin alfa (Epogen, Procrit):** 50-150 units/kg/week SC (divided or weekly)
- **Darbepoetin alfa (Aranesp):** 25-100 mcg every 1-2 weeks SC
- **Advantages in PD:** Can administer SC at home (vs IV in HD)

**HIF-Prolyl Hydroxylase Inhibitors (HIF-PHIs) - FDA 2023-2024:**
**Oral agents now available for PD patients**

- **Roxadustat (Evrenzo):** 70-120 mg TID
- **Daprodustat (Jesduvroq):** 2-4 mg once daily
- **Vadadustat (Vafseo):** 300-600 mg once daily

**Advantages in PD:**
- Oral administration (more convenient than SC ESAs)
- Reduced IV iron requirements
- More physiologic erythropoietin production
- See **Document 12 (Renal Pharmacology)** for detailed HIF-PHI information

**Iron Management:**
- **Oral Iron:** Ferrous sulfate 325 mg TID (better absorption than HD patients)
- **IV Iron:** Use if oral intolerant or TSAT <20%
  - Iron sucrose (Venofer): 100-200 mg IV monthly
  - Ferric carboxymaltose (Injectafer): 750-1000 mg IV every 3-6 months
- **Ferric Citrate (Auryxia):** Dual phosphate binder + iron supplement
- **Targets:** TSAT >20%, Ferritin 200-500 ng/mL

- **Quarterly**: Albumin, lipid profile, liver function tests
- **Semi-annually**: adequacy testing (Kt/V, CrCl)
- **Annually**: PET testing, infectious disease screening

### Clinical Monitoring
- **Monthly**: Weight, blood pressure, volume status
- **Quarterly**: Exit site examination, neurological assessment
- **Annually**: Cardiovascular examination, bone disease assessment

### Adequacy Monitoring
- **Kt/V Calculation**: Dialysis + residual renal function
- **Creatinine Clearance**: Total clearance assessment
- **Fluid Balance**: Net ultrafiltration, edema assessment
- **Nutritional Status**: SGA, nPNA, albumin

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Patients
- **Growth Considerations**: Adequate nutrition, protein intake
- **Development**: Age-appropriate training, school attendance
- **Family Involvement**: Parent training, support systems
- **APD Preference:** Nighttime treatment allows normal daytime activities
- **Fill Volumes:** Weight-based (1100-1400 mL/m² BSA)

### Elderly Patients
- **Functional Status**: Manual dexterity, cognitive function
- **Comorbidities**: Cardiovascular disease, diabetes
- **Social Support**: Home assistance, transportation
- **Assisted PD Programs (Expanding 2020-2024):**
  - **Nurse-Assisted PD:** Home nursing support for exchanges
  - **Family-Assisted PD:** Trained family members perform exchanges
  - **Benefits:** Enables PD in patients who couldn't otherwise perform
  - **Outcomes:** Comparable technique survival to self-care PD
  - **Reimbursement:** Medicare coverage for assisted PD (expanded 2020)

### Diabetic Patients
- **Glycemic Control**: Glucose absorption considerations
  - 100-200 g glucose absorbed daily from dialysate
  - Requires insulin dose adjustment (20-30% increase typical)
  - Consider lower glucose concentrations when possible
- **Icodextrin Use**: Essential for UF and glycemic control
  - **CRITICAL:** Maltose interference with some glucose meters
  - **Only use glucose-specific (GDH-PQQ) test strips**
  - Point-of-care glucose meters may show falsely elevated readings
  - Continuous glucose monitors (CGM) are safe with icodextrin
- **Vascular Disease**: Increased cardiovascular risk
- **Neuropathy:** May affect manual dexterity (consider assisted PD)

### Pregnant Patients (Rare but Increasing)
- **PD During Pregnancy:**
  - Possible but challenging (case reports, small series)
  - APD preferred (smaller fill volumes, more frequent exchanges)
  - Fill volume reduction as pregnancy progresses (1.0-1.5 L)
  - Increased infection risk
  - Close monitoring required
- **2024 Evidence:** ~50 successful pregnancies reported on PD
- **Outcomes:** Higher than HD but worse than transplant

## Transition to Hemodialysis

### Indications for Modality Change
- **Peritoneal Membrane Failure**: Inadequate clearance/ultrafiltration
- **Recurrent Peritonitis**: >3 episodes/year
- **Severe Peritonitis**: Refractory to treatment
- **Patient Preference**: Lifestyle considerations
- **Encapsulating Peritoneal Sclerosis**: Mandatory transition

### Transition Planning
- **Timing**: Elective vs urgent transition
- **Access Creation**: AV fistula/graft planning
- **Patient Education**: HD modality education
- **Psychological Support**: Coping with modality change

## Quality Assurance & Outcomes

### Quality Metrics
- **Peritonitis Rate**: <0.5 episodes/year
- **Technique Survival**: 50% at 5 years
- **Patient Survival**: 60-70% at 5 years
- **Hospitalization Rate**: <10 days/patient-year

### Continuous Quality Improvement
- **Monthly Review**: Peritonitis episodes, hospitalizations
- **Quarterly Analysis**: Adequacy data, nutritional status
- **Annual Assessment**: Patient satisfaction, outcomes comparison

## Key Practice Points

### Clinical Pearls
1. **Exit Site Care**: Daily cleaning reduces infection rates by 50%
2. **Icodextrin**: Essential for high transporters with fluid overload
3. **PET Testing**: Annually or when adequacy questions arise
4. **Residual Function**: Preserve with nephrotoxic avoidance
5. **Volume Control**: Most important factor for cardiovascular protection

### Red Flags
- **Cloudy Effluent**: Treat as peritonitis until proven otherwise
- **Abdominal Pain**: Evaluate for peritonitis, catheter problems
- **Weight Gain**: Assess for fluid overload vs true weight gain
- **Decreased UF**: Evaluate membrane function, catheter position
- **Exit Site Discharge**: Culture and treat promptly

### Prevention Strategies
- **Connection Systems**: Use disconnect systems exclusively
- **Exit Site Management**: Proper cleaning, no swimming
- **Bowel Regularity**: Prevent constipation, diarrhea
- **Glycemic Control**: Minimize glucose exposure
- **Regular Monitoring**: Early detection of complications

## COVID-19 Impact on Peritoneal Dialysis (2020-2024)

### PD Advantages During Pandemic
- **Home-based therapy:** Minimal healthcare exposure
- **Lower infection risk:** Compared to in-center hemodialysis
- **Continuation of care:** Not affected by dialysis center closures/outbreaks
- **2020-2022 Data:** PD patients had 30-40% lower COVID-19 infection rates vs HD

### Remote Monitoring Adoption
- **Accelerated Implementation:** 200-300% increase in remote PD monitoring (2020-2024)
- **Telemedicine Integration:** Virtual visits became standard of care
- **Regulatory Changes:** CMS expanded telehealth reimbursement (permanent)
- **Connected Cyclers:** Rapid adoption (50,000+ patients by 2024)

### PD Growth During COVID-19
- **Incident PD Starts:** 15-20% increase during pandemic (2020-2021)
- **Home Dialysis First:** Renewed emphasis on PD as first-line modality
- **Assisted PD Expansion:** Programs developed to enable more patients
