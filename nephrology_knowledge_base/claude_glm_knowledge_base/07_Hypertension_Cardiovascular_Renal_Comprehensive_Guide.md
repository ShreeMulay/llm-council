**Difficulty Level:** Intermediate-Advanced  
**Frequency of Use:** Daily  
**Clinical Impact:** High  
**Regulatory Requirements:** None specific  
**Prerequisites:** Cardiovascular physiology, renal hemodynamics  
**Competency Development Time:** 12-18 months  
**Cross-references:** Vascular Disorders (Domain I.5), CKD (Domain I.2), Pharmacology (Domain V.12)

## 1. Hypertension Classification & Pathophysiology

### Blood Pressure Classification

#### ACC/AHA 2017 Guidelines
**Normal:** SBP <120 mmHg AND DBP <80 mmHg
**Elevated:** SBP 120-129 mmHg AND DBP <80 mmHg
**Stage 1:** SBP 130-139 mmHg OR DBP 80-89 mmHg
**Stage 2:** SBP ≥140 mmHg OR DBP ≥90 mmHg

#### Hypertensive Urgency vs Emergency
**Hypertensive Urgency:**
- Severely elevated BP without acute target organ damage
- SBP >180 mmHg OR DBP >120 mmHg
- No immediate organ dysfunction
- Outpatient management usually appropriate

**Hypertensive Emergency:**
- Severely elevated BP WITH acute target organ damage
- Requires immediate BP reduction (not to normal)
- Inpatient management mandatory
- Organ-specific complications

### Pathophysiological Mechanisms

#### Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS)
**Normal Physiology:**
- Renin release from juxtaglomerular cells
- Angiotensin I conversion to Angiotensin II
- Aldosterone secretion from adrenal cortex
- Sodium and water retention

**Pathologic Activation:**
- Volume depletion
- Renal artery stenosis
- Diuretic therapy
- Genetic factors

#### Sympathetic Nervous System
**Mechanisms:**
- Increased norepinephrine release
- Alpha-adrenergic vasoconstriction
- Beta-adrenergic cardiac stimulation
- Renin stimulation

#### Sodium Handling
**Pressure Natriuresis:**
- Normal: increased BP → increased sodium excretion
- Hypertension: right-shifted curve
- Requires higher BP for sodium excretion

#### Vascular Remodeling
**Structural Changes:**
- Media hypertrophy
- Lumen reduction
- Increased stiffness
- Rarefaction of microvessels

## 2. Primary (Essential) Hypertension

### Epidemiology & Risk Factors

#### Prevalence
- Affects 30-45% of adults worldwide
- Increases with age
- Higher in African Americans
- Associated with obesity epidemic

#### Modifiable Risk Factors
**Lifestyle Factors:**
- Obesity (BMI >30)
- Physical inactivity
- High sodium intake (>2,300 mg/day)
- Excessive alcohol consumption
- Smoking

**Metabolic Factors:**
- Diabetes mellitus
- Dyslipidemia
- Metabolic syndrome
- Sleep apnea

#### Non-Modifiable Risk Factors
- Age >65 years
- African American ethnicity
- Family history of hypertension
- Male gender (younger age)
- Low birth weight

### Pathogenesis

#### Genetic Factors
**Polygenic Inheritance:**
- Multiple gene variants
- Each contributes small effect
- Gene-environment interactions
- Epigenetic modifications

**Specific Genes:**
- ACE gene polymorphism
- Angiotensinogen variants
- Adducin gene mutations
- Alpha-adducin polymorphisms

#### Environmental Factors
**Dietary Influences:**
- Sodium sensitivity (20-30% of population)
- Potassium deficiency
- Calcium and magnesium intake
- High-fructose corn syrup

**Stress Factors:**
- Chronic psychosocial stress
- Low socioeconomic status
- Sleep deprivation
- Shift work

### Clinical Presentation

#### Asymptomatic Phase
- Often discovered incidentally
- May have headaches, fatigue
- Usually no specific symptoms
- Silent organ damage occurring

#### Target Organ Damage
**Cardiac:**
- Left ventricular hypertrophy
- Diastolic dysfunction
- Coronary artery disease
- Heart failure

**Cerebrovascular:**
- Lacunar infarcts
- White matter changes
- Microhemorrhages
- Cognitive impairment

**Renal:**
- Microalbuminuria
- Reduced GFR
- Nephrosclerosis
- Progression to ESRD

**Vascular:**
- Atherosclerosis acceleration
- Aortic root dilation
- Peripheral arterial disease
- Retinopathy

### Diagnostic Evaluation

#### Initial Assessment
**Blood Pressure Measurement:**
- Proper technique: seated, 5 minutes rest
- Multiple readings on separate occasions
- Home BP monitoring confirmation
- Ambulatory BP monitoring if needed

**History Taking:**
- Duration of hypertension
- Previous treatment responses
- Lifestyle factors
- Family history
- Symptoms of target organ damage

**Physical Examination:**
- Fundoscopic examination
- Cardiac examination (LVH signs)
- Abdominal bruits (renal artery stenosis)
- Peripheral pulses
- Neurologic assessment

#### Laboratory Evaluation
**Basic Metabolic Panel:**
- Serum creatinine, eGFR
- Electrolytes (Na+, K+)
- Bicarbonate
- Glucose

**Urinalysis:**
- Proteinuria/albuminuria
- Microscopic hematuria
- Casts

**Additional Tests:**
- Lipid panel
- TSH
- ECG (LVH, ischemia)
- Echocardiogram if indicated

#### Secondary Hypertension Workup
**Indications for Evaluation:**
- Age <30 years
- Sudden severe hypertension
- Resistant hypertension
- Hypokalemia
- Abdominal bruit
- Asymmetric kidney size

**Targeted Testing:**
- Plasma renin activity
- Aldosterone/renin ratio
- Urine metanephrines
- Sleep study if OSA suspected
- Renal imaging if indicated

## 3. Secondary Hypertension

### Renal Artery Stenosis

#### Clinical Features
**Clues to Diagnosis:**
- Sudden worsening of hypertension
- Resistant to multiple medications
- Abdominal or flank bruit
- Asymmetric kidney size
- Flash pulmonary edema

**Risk Factors:**
- Age >55 years (atherosclerotic)
- Age <30 years (fibromuscular dysplasia)
- Smoking history
- Atherosclerotic disease elsewhere

#### Diagnostic Approach
**Screening Tests:**
- Duplex Doppler ultrasound
- CTA or MRA
- Renal scintigraphy with ACE inhibitor

**Confirmatory Testing:**
- Digital subtraction angiography
- Pressure gradient measurement
- Renal vein renin sampling

#### Treatment
**Medical Management:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs (monitor renal function)
- Multiple antihypertensives
- Statin therapy
- Antiplatelet therapy

**Revascularization:**
- Percutaneous angioplasty with stenting
- Surgical revascularization
- Indications: refractory hypertension, declining renal function

### Primary Aldosteronism

#### Epidemiology
- Prevalence: 5-10% of hypertensives
- Higher in resistant hypertension (15-20%)
- Most common secondary cause

#### Clinical Features
**Typical Presentation:**
- Hypertension (often severe)
- Hypokalemia (spontaneous or diuretic-induced)
- Metabolic alkalosis
- Suppressed renin activity

**Screening Indications:**
- Resistant hypertension
- Hypokalemia
- Adrenal incidentaloma
- Family history of early-onset hypertension

#### Diagnostic Algorithm
**Screening:**
- Aldosterone/renin ratio >20-30
- Morning measurement
- Correct hypokalemia first
- Hold interfering medications

**Confirmation:**
- Saline infusion test
- Captopril challenge test
- Oral sodium loading test
- Fludrocortisone suppression test

**Subtype Localization:**
- CT adrenal imaging
- Adrenal vein sampling
- MRI if CT contraindicated

#### Treatment
**Unilateral Disease:**
- Laparoscopic adrenalectomy
- Cure rates: 60-70%
- Preoperative medical preparation

**Bilateral Disease:**
- Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists
- Spironolactone first-line
- Eplerenone if side effects
- Amiloride alternative

### Pheochromocytoma/Paraganglioma

#### Clinical Features
**Classic Triad (50% have):**
- Paroxysmal headaches
- Diaphoresis
- Palpitations

**Other Symptoms:**
- Paroxysmal hypertension
- Orthostatic hypotension
- Anxiety, panic attacks
- Weight loss

**Triggering Factors:**
- Stress, exercise
- Certain medications
- Foods (tyramine)
- Procedures

#### Diagnostic Evaluation
**Biochemical Testing:**
- Plasma free metanephrines
- 24-hour urinary metanephrines
- Fractionated metanephrines
- VMA (less sensitive)

**Localization:**
- CT/MRI of abdomen/pelvis
- ^123I-MIBG scintigraphy
- ^68Ga-DOTATATE PET/CT
- Functional imaging for metastases

#### Treatment
**Preoperative Preparation:**
- Alpha blockade (phenoxybenzamine)
- Beta blockade after alpha
- Volume expansion
- Blood pressure control

**Surgical Management:**
- Laparoscopic adrenalectomy
- Cortical-sparing if bilateral
- Genetic testing if indicated

**Medical Therapy:**
- Phenoxybenzamine
- Doxazosin
- Metyrosine (tyrosine hydroxylase inhibitor)

### Cushing's Syndrome

#### Hypertension Mechanisms
- Mineralocorticoid effects of cortisol
- Increased sensitivity to catecholamines
- Activation of RAAS
- Sodium retention

#### Clinical Features
**Typical Signs:**
- Central obesity
- Moon facies
- Buffalo hump
- Purple striae

**Metabolic Effects:**
- Glucose intolerance
- Dyslipidemia
- Osteoporosis
- Immune suppression

#### Diagnostic Approach
**Screening Tests:**
- 24-hour urinary free cortisol
- Late-night salivary cortisol
- 1 mg dexamethasone suppression test

**Localization:**
- ACTH level
- MRI pituitary
- CT adrenal
- Inferior petrosal sinus sampling

#### Treatment
- Surgical removal of tumor
- Medical therapy if not surgical
- Hypertension management during treatment

## 4. Resistant Hypertension

### Definition & Prevalence

#### Definition
- Blood pressure remains above goal despite:
  - 3 antihypertensive agents including diuretic
  - Optimal doses
  - Good adherence
  - Excludes secondary causes

#### Prevalence
- General population: 10-15%
- Treated hypertensives: 20-30%
- CKD patients: 40-50%
- Higher in elderly and African Americans

### Etiologic Factors

#### Pseudoresistance
**Measurement Issues:**
- Improper technique
- White coat effect
- Cuff size problems
- Observer bias

**Medication Issues:**
- Poor adherence
- Subtherapeutic doses
- Drug interactions
- Inappropriate combinations

#### True Resistance
**Volume Overload:**
- Inadequate diuretic therapy
- CKD with sodium retention
- Hyperaldosteronism
- High sodium intake

**Secondary Causes:**
- Primary aldosteronism
- Renal artery stenosis
- Obstructive sleep apnea
- Pheochromocytoma

**Vascular Factors:**
- Arterial stiffness
- Increased vascular resistance
- Sympathetic overactivity
- Endothelial dysfunction

### Evaluation Approach

#### Confirm True Resistance
**Proper BP Measurement:**
- Automated validated devices
- Multiple readings
- Home BP monitoring
- Ambulatory BP monitoring

**Medication Review:**
- Verify adherence
- Check doses
- Review interactions
- Assess diuretic adequacy

#### Secondary Cause Workup
**Targeted Testing:**
- Aldosterone/renin ratio
- Basic metabolic panel
- Sleep study if symptoms
- Renal imaging if indicated

**Advanced Testing:**
- Ambulatory BP monitoring
- Plasma metanephrines
- Renal artery Doppler
- Hormonal evaluation

### Treatment Strategies

#### Pharmacologic Optimization
**Diuretic Strategy:**
- Ensure adequate diuretic dose
- Consider chlorthalidone over HCTZ
- Add loop diuretic if CKD
- Sequential nephron blockade

**Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists:**
- Spironolactone 12.5-50 mg daily
- Eplerenone if side effects
- Monitor potassium and renal function
- Particularly effective in Black patients

**Other Additions:**
- Beta-blockers
- Alpha-blockers
- Central agonists
- Vasodilators

#### Non-Pharmacologic Approaches
**Lifestyle Modifications:**
- Sodium restriction <2 g/day
- Weight loss
- Regular exercise
- Limit alcohol

**Device Therapy:**
**Renal Denervation:**
- Catheter-based renal sympathetic denervation
- SYMPLICITY HTN-3 trial results mixed
- New generation devices promising
- Consider in carefully selected patients

**Baroreflex Activation Therapy:**
- Device-based baroreceptor stimulation
- Limited availability
- Adjunctive therapy

#### Special Populations
**CKD Patients:**
- Volume management crucial
- Loop diuretics often needed
- Monitor for hyperkalemia
- Consider earlier nephrology referral

**Diabetic Patients:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs essential
- SGLT2 inhibitors beneficial
- Multiple agents often needed
- Aggressive targets

## 5. Hypertensive Emergencies

### Definition & Classification

#### Definition
- Severe elevation in BP (>180/120 mmHg)
- WITH acute target organ damage
- Requires immediate controlled BP reduction
- Medical emergency

#### Target Organ Involvement
**Cerebral:**
- Hypertensive encephalopathy
- Intracerebral hemorrhage
- Ischemic stroke
- Subarachnoid hemorrhage

**Cardiac:**
- Acute decompensated heart failure
- Acute coronary syndromes
- Aortic dissection
- Myocardial infarction

**Renal:**
- Acute kidney injury
- Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia
- Progressive renal failure

**Other:**
- Retinopathy with papilledema
- Pre-eclampsia/eclampsia
- Sympathomimetic toxicity

### Clinical Presentations

#### Hypertensive Encephalopathy
**Symptoms:**
- Severe headache
- Confusion, agitation
- Visual disturbances
- Seizures, coma

**Pathophysiology:**
- Cerebral autoregulation failure
- Cerebral edema
- Hyperperfusion injury
- Reversible with treatment

#### Acute Heart Failure
**Presentation:**
- Dyspnea, orthopnea
- Pulmonary edema
- S3 gallop
- Elevated JVP

**Mechanism:**
- Afterload elevation
- Diastolic dysfunction
- Volume overload
- Myocardial ischemia

#### Aortic Dissection
**Symptoms:**
- Severe tearing chest pain
- Radiation to back
- Pulse deficits
- Hypotension (Type A)

**Diagnosis:**
- CT angiography
- Transesophageal echo
- MRI angiography
- Chest X-ray (widened mediastinum)

### Treatment Principles

#### General Approach
**Initial Goals:**
- Admit to ICU
- Continuous BP monitoring
- Arterial line if severe
- Organ-specific assessment

**BP Reduction Strategy:**
- Reduce MAP by 10-20% in first hour
- Additional reduction to 160/100-110 in next 2-6 hours
- Avoid rapid drops in chronic hypertension
- Individualize based on presentation

#### Specific Therapies

#### Hypertensive Encephalopathy
**First-line:**
- Labetalol 20-80 mg IV every 10 minutes
- Nicardipine 5 mg/hour infusion, titrate
- Avoid rapid BP reduction

**Alternative:**
- Sodium nitroprusside 0.25-10 mcg/kg/min
- Esmolol infusion
- Fenoldopam (renal protective)

#### Acute Heart Failure
**Vasodilators:**
- Nitroglycerin infusion
- Nitroprusside (if severe)
- Nesiritide (controversial)

**Diuretics:**
- Furosemide IV bolus
- Continuous infusion if needed
- Monitor electrolytes

#### Aortic Dissection
**Goal:**
- SBP 100-120 mmHg
- Heart rate <60 bpm
- Reduce shear stress

**Therapy:**
- Esmolol infusion first
- Add vasodilator after beta-blockade
- Avoid tachycardia
- Surgical consultation

#### Acute Coronary Syndromes
**Beta-blockers:**
- Metoprolol 5 mg IV every 5 minutes
- Labetalol if needed
- Avoid in acute heart failure

**Nitrates:**
- Nitroglycerin infusion
- Reduce preload and afterload
- Monitor for hypotension

#### Medication Profiles
**Labetalol:**
- Combined alpha and beta blockade
- 20-80 mg IV every 10 minutes
- 2 mg/min infusion
- Contraindicated in asthma, heart block

**Nicardipine:**
- Dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker
- 5 mg/hour starting dose
- Titrate by 2.5 mg/hour every 5 minutes
- No effect on heart rate

**Nitroprusside:**
- Potent arterial and venous dilator
- 0.25-10 mcg/kg/min infusion
- Cyanide toxicity risk (>72 hours)
- Requires arterial line monitoring

**Esmolol:**
- Ultra-short acting beta-blocker
- 50-200 mcg/kg/min infusion
- Titrate rapidly
- Useful in aortic dissection

**Fenoldopam:**
- Dopamine-1 agonist
- Renal vasodilation
- Natriuresis
- 0.1-0.3 mcg/kg/min

### Monitoring & Follow-up

#### Acute Monitoring
- Arterial blood pressure every 5-15 minutes
- Neurologic status hourly
- Cardiac monitoring continuously
- Urine output monitoring
- Laboratory tests every 6-12 hours

#### Transition to Oral Therapy
- When stable for 12-24 hours
- Overlap IV and oral medications
- Choose appropriate oral regimen
- Monitor for rebound hypertension

#### Long-term Management
- Identify and treat underlying cause
- Optimize outpatient regimen
- Patient education
- Close follow-up within 1 week

## 6. Cardiorenal Syndromes

### Classification & Pathophysiology

#### Type 1: Acute Cardiorenal Syndrome
**Definition:**
- Acute cardiac dysfunction leading to AKI
- Most common type
- Usually reversible

**Pathophysiology:**
- Reduced cardiac output
- Renal hypoperfusion
- Venous congestion
- Neurohormonal activation

**Clinical Features:**
- Acute decompensated heart failure
- Rising creatinine
- Volume overload
- Low output state

#### Type 2: Chronic Cardiorenal Syndrome
**Definition:**
- Chronic cardiac dysfunction causing progressive CKD
- Slow progression
- Often irreversible

**Mechanisms:**
- Chronic low perfusion
- Persistent congestion
- RAAS activation
- Inflammation and fibrosis

#### Type 3: Acute Renocardiac Syndrome
**Definition:**
- AKI causing acute cardiac dysfunction
- Less common than Type 1
- High mortality

**Mechanisms:**
- Volume overload
- Uremic toxins
- Electrolyte abnormalities
- Inflammation

#### Type 4: Chronic Renocardiac Syndrome
**Definition:**
- CKD causing cardiovascular disease
- Most common cause of death in CKD
- Accelerated atherosclerosis

**Pathophysiology:**
- Volume overload
- Hypertension
- Anemia
- Mineral bone disease

#### Type 5: Secondary Cardiorenal Syndrome
**Definition:**
- Systemic disease affecting both heart and kidneys
- Examples: sepsis, diabetes, amyloidosis
- Multi-organ dysfunction

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Biomarkers
**Cardiac Biomarkers:**
- Troponin (elevated in CKD)
- BNP/NT-proBNP (diagnostic challenges)
- ST2, galectin-3 (prognostic)

**Renal Biomarkers:**
- NGAL, KIM-1
- Cystatin C
- Novel biomarkers under investigation

#### Imaging Studies
**Cardiac Evaluation:**
- Echocardiography
- Cardiac MRI
- Nuclear imaging

**Renal Assessment:**
- Renal ultrasound
- Doppler studies
- Functional imaging

#### Hemodynamic Monitoring
- Central venous pressure
- Pulmonary artery catheter
- Echocardiographic assessment
- Bioimpedance

### Management Strategies

#### General Principles
- Optimize volume status
- Improve cardiac output
- Reduce renal congestion
- Minimize nephrotoxins

#### Pharmacologic Therapy
**Diuretics:**
- Loop diuretics high dose
- Combination therapy
- Continuous infusion
- Monitor for resistance

**Vasodilators:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs
- Hydralazine/nitrates
- Nesiritide (limited role)
- SGLT2 inhibitors

**Neurohormonal Modulation:**
- Beta-blockers (careful titration)
- Mineralocorticoid antagonists
- ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan)
- SGLT2 inhibitors

#### Device Therapy
**Ultrafiltration:**
- Mechanical fluid removal
- Useful in diuretic resistance
- Risks: hypotension, renal injury
- Careful patient selection

**Renal Replacement Therapy:**
- Peritoneal dialysis (continuous)
- Hemodialysis (intermittent)
- SLED (sustained low efficiency)
- CRRT (critically ill)

#### Special Considerations
**Anemia Management:**
- ESA therapy
- Iron supplementation
- Target hemoglobin 10-11 g/dL

**Mineral Bone Disease:**
- Phosphate binders
- Vitamin D analogs
- Calcimimetics
- Avoid vascular calcification

## 7. Hypertension in Special Populations

### Chronic Kidney Disease

#### Pathophysiology
- Volume overload
- RAAS activation
- Sympathetic overactivity
- Sodium sensitivity

#### Blood Pressure Targets (2024 KDIGO Update) ⭐

**General CKD (2024 KDIGO Guideline - March 2024):**
- **Preferred Target**: <120/80 mmHg (systolic <120) if tolerated
  - **Evidence**: SPRINT trial showed 19% reduction in CV events, 15% reduction in death
  - Applies to most adults with CKD (diabetic and non-diabetic)
- **Alternative Target**: <130/80 mmHg if <120/80 not tolerated
  - For patients with:
    - Frailty or limited life expectancy
    - High risk of orthostatic hypotension
    - Severe comorbidities
- **Individualization Required**:
  - Age ≥75 years: assess frailty, fall risk
  - Standing BP measurement to detect orthostatic hypotension
  - Symptoms of hypotension should prompt dose reduction

**Proteinuric CKD (Albuminuria >30 mg/g):**
- **Target**: <120/80 mmHg (systolic <120 strongly preferred)
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs essential (dual blockade NOT recommended)
- SGLT2 inhibitors provide additional benefit
- Monitor for hyperkalemia (especially with ACEi/ARB + aldosterone antagonists)
- Greater BP reduction = greater proteinuria reduction

**Key Changes in 2024 KDIGO vs 2021:**
1. **Lower Target**: Emphasizes <120/80 (systolic <120) vs previous <130/80
2. **Home BP Monitoring**: Strongly recommended for all CKD patients
   - Target: <120/80 mmHg (home BP ~5 mmHg lower than office)
3. **Resistant Hypertension**: New algorithms added
4. **SGLT2 Inhibitors**: Elevated to first-line alongside ACEi/ARB
5. **Medication Simplification**: Focus on once-daily regimens for adherence

#### Treatment Strategies
**First-line Agents:**
- ACE inhibitors or ARBs
- SGLT2 inhibitors (down to eGFR 20)
- Calcium channel blockers
- Diuretics (loop if eGFR <30)

**Avoid/Use Caution:**
- Direct renin inhibitors
- Aldosterone antagonists (monitor K+)
- Beta-blockers (if bradycardia)

### Diabetes Mellitus

#### Pathophysiology
- Hyperinsulinemia
- Sodium retention
- Vascular stiffness
- Autonomic dysfunction

#### Blood Pressure Targets
- <130/80 mmHg (ADA 2024)
- <120/70 mmHg if high CV risk
- Individualize elderly patients

#### Preferred Agents
**ACE inhibitors/ARBs:**
- Renal protection
- Reduce proteinuria
- First-line choice

**SGLT2 Inhibitors:**
- Cardiovascular protection
- Renal protection
- Blood pressure reduction

**Thiazide-like Diuretics:**
- Chlorthalidone preferred
- Cardiovascular outcomes
- Monitor electrolytes

### Pregnancy

#### Hypertensive Disorders
**Chronic Hypertension:**
- Pre-existing hypertension
- BP >140/90 before pregnancy
- Continue antihypertensives

**Gestational Hypertension:**
- New onset >20 weeks
- No proteinuria
- Monitor for pre-eclampsia

**Pre-eclampsia:**
- Hypertension + proteinuria
- End-organ dysfunction
- Delivery is definitive treatment

#### Safe Medications
**First-line:**
- Labetalol (oral/IV)
- Nifedipine (extended release)
- Methyldopa

**Avoid:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs (teratogenic)
- Direct renin inhibitors
- Atenolol (fetal growth restriction)

### Elderly Patients

#### Special Considerations
- Orthostatic hypotension risk
- Cognitive impairment
- Polypharmacy
- Frailty

#### Blood Pressure Targets
- <130/80 mmHg if fit
- Individualize if frail
- Avoid symptomatic hypotension
- Monitor falls

#### Treatment Approach
- Start low, go slow
- Single agent first
- Add medications gradually
- Monitor orthostatics
- Review medications regularly

## 8. Clinical Algorithms & Decision Trees

### Resistant Hypertension Evaluation

```
BP >140/90 on 3 medications including diuretic
        ↓
Confirm True Resistance:
- Verify adherence
- Proper BP measurement
- Home/Ambulatory BP monitoring
        ↓
Medication Optimization:
- Maximize diuretic dose
- Add spironolactone
- Consider chlorthalidone
        ↓
Secondary Cause Workup:
- Aldosterone/renin ratio
- Sleep study if OSA suspected
- Renal imaging if indicated
- Basic metabolic panel
        ↓
Treat Identified Cause
OR
Consider Device Therapy
```

### Hypertensive Emergency Management

```
BP >180/120 + Target Organ Damage
        ↓
ICU Admission + Arterial Line
        ↓
Identify Organ Involvement:
- Brain → Labetalol/Nicardipine
- Heart → Nitroglycerin/Diuretics
- Aorta → Esmolol + Vasodilator
- Kidney → Nicardipine/Fenoldopam
        ↓
Reduce MAP 10-20% in first hour
        ↓
Additional reduction to 160/100-110 in 2-6 hours
        ↓
Transition to oral therapy when stable
```

## 9. Key Take-Home Points

1. **BP Classification**: Use ACC/AHA 2017 guidelines; treat based on cardiovascular risk
2. **Secondary Hypertension**: Screen in young patients, resistant hypertension, or hypokalemia
3. **Resistant Hypertension**: Confirm true resistance; optimize diuretic therapy; consider spironolactone
4. **Hypertensive Emergencies**: Admit to ICU; reduce BP gradually; treat based on organ involvement
5. **Cardiorenal Syndromes**: Understand the 5 types; manage volume status; use SGLT2 inhibitors
6. **CKD Hypertension**: Target <130/80; use ACEi/ARB + SGLT2i; monitor potassium
7. **Diabetes**: Aggressive control; SGLT2 inhibitors provide cardio-renal protection
8. **Pregnancy**: Avoid ACEi/ARBs; use labetalol, nifedipine, methyldopa
9. **Elderly**: Individualize targets; start low, go slow; monitor for falls
10. **Lifestyle**: Sodium restriction, weight loss, exercise are essential components
