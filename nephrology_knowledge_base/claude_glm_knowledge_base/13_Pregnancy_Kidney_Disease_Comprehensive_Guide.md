## Physiologic Renal Changes in Pregnancy

### Hemodynamic Changes

#### Renal Blood Flow and GFR
- **Renal Plasma Flow**: Increases 50-70% by end of first trimester
- **GFR**: Increases 40-50% (peaks 9-11 weeks, maintained throughout)
- **Serum Creatinine**: Decreases to 0.4-0.6 mg/dL (may be lower in twins)
- **BUN**: Decreases to 8-10 mg/dL
- **Clinical Implication**: Normal pregnancy values may mask early CKD

#### Renal Structural Changes
- **Kidney Size**: Increases 1-1.5 cm in length
- **Collecting System**: Dilatation of ureters and renal pelvis (physiologic hydronephrosis)
- **Bladder Changes**: Increased capacity, decreased tone

### Electrolyte and Acid-Base Changes

#### Sodium and Water Balance
- **Plasma Volume**: Increases 40-50% (peaks 28-32 weeks)
- **Total Body Water**: Increases 6-8 liters
- **Sodium Retention**: 500-900 mEq total retention
- **Osmolality**: Decreases 5-10 mOsm/kg (reset osmostat)

#### Acid-Base Regulation
- **Respiratory Alkalosis**: Chronic hyperventilation → PaCO2 30 mmHg
- **Compensated**: Bicarbonate decreases to 18-22 mEq/L
- **pH**: Slightly alkaline (7.40-7.45)

#### Other Electrolytes
- **Potassium**: Slight decrease, normal levels maintained
- **Calcium**: Decreased total calcium, normal ionized calcium
- **Magnesium**: Decreased levels, especially in third trimester

### Hormonal Changes

#### RAAS Activation
- **Renin**: Increases 3-4 fold
- **Angiotensin II**: Increases 2-3 fold
- **Aldosterone**: Increases 5-10 fold
- **Purpose**: Maintain sodium retention and volume expansion

#### Other Hormonal Effects
- **Progesterone**: Natriuretic and smooth muscle relaxation effects
- **Estrogen**: Increases hepatic production of binding proteins
- **hPL**: Anti-insulin effects, lipolysis

## Pre-existing Kidney Disease in Pregnancy

### Chronic Kidney Disease

#### Pregnancy Considerations by CKD Stage
```
Stage 1-2 CKD (eGFR >60):
- Generally well-tolerated
- 20% risk of accelerated decline
- Monitor blood pressure, proteinuria every 4-6 weeks

Stage 3 CKD (eGFR 30-59):
- Moderate risk (40-50% complications)
- 30% risk of accelerated decline
- Monitor every 2-4 weeks
- Consider preconception counseling

Stage 4-5 CKD (eGFR <30):
- High-risk pregnancy
- 60% risk of ESRD progression
- Monitor every 1-2 weeks
- Discuss dialysis vs transplantation timing
```

#### Contraindications to Pregnancy
- **eGFR <20 mL/min**: Unless on dialysis or post-transplant
- **Uncontrolled Hypertension**: BP >160/110 despite therapy
- **Severe Proteinuria**: >3 g/day with nephrotic syndrome
- **Rapidly Progressive GN**: Active disease or recent flare

#### Management Principles
- **Preconception Counseling**: Optimize disease control before conception
- **Medication Adjustments**: Switch from teratogenic drugs
- **Blood Pressure Control**: Target <130/80 mmHg
- **Proteinuria Monitoring**: Quantify every trimester
- **GFR Monitoring**: Every trimester, more often if unstable

### Specific Kidney Diseases

#### Diabetic Nephropathy
- **Risks**: Preterm delivery (50%), preeclampsia (40%), fetal growth restriction
- **Management**: 
  - Tight glycemic control (HbA1c <6.5% preconception)
  - ACE inhibitors/ARBs: Stop before conception
  - Blood pressure: <130/80 mmHg
  - Monitor for worsening proteinuria

#### Lupus Nephritis
- **Timing**: Wait 6 months after remission before conception
- **Risks**: Flare (20-30%), preeclampsia (30%), fetal loss (15-20%)
- **Medications**:
  - Continue hydroxychloroquine
  - Low-dose steroids acceptable
  - Azathioprine preferred over mycophenolate
  - Avoid cyclophosphamide, mycophenolate, methotrexate

#### IgA Nephropathy
- **Prognosis**: Generally favorable if well-controlled
- **Risks**: Worsening proteinuria, hypertension
- **Management**: 
  - ACE inhibitors/ARBs: Stop before conception, restart in 2nd trimester if needed
  - Blood pressure control essential
  - Monitor proteinuria closely

#### Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)
- **High Risk**: Significant proteinuria, potential for rapid progression
- **Management**: 
  - Aggressive proteinuria control
  - Consider preconception remission induction
  - High risk of preeclampsia and preterm delivery

#### Polycystic Kidney Disease (ADPKD)
- **Generally Well-Tolerated**: If normal renal function
- **Risks**: Hypertension exacerbation, renal stones, hemorrhage
- **Management**: 
  - Blood pressure control
  - Monitor for cyst complications
  - Screen for intracranial aneurysms if family history

## Pregnancy-Specific Renal Disorders

### Preeclampsia

#### Pathophysiology
- **Placental Ischemia**: Abnormal trophoblast invasion
- **Endothelial Dysfunction**: Widespread vascular injury
- **Anti-angiogenic Factors**: Increased sFlt-1 (soluble fms-like tyrosine kinase-1), decreased PlGF (placental growth factor)

#### Novel Biomarkers for Prediction (2024 Update)

**sFlt-1/PlGF Ratio (FDA-Cleared Tests):**
- **Elecsys Immunoassay (Roche):** FDA-cleared 2018
- **PerkinElmer Assay:** Alternative platform
- **Clinical Use:**
  - Helps rule out preeclampsia in women with suspected disease
  - Assists in short-term prediction of preeclampsia onset

**Interpretation (Elecsys sFlt-1/PlGF Ratio):**
```
sFlt-1/PlGF Ratio <38:
- Preeclampsia unlikely in next 1 week
- Negative predictive value: 99.3%
- Safe to monitor outpatient

sFlt-1/PlGF Ratio >38:
- Increased preeclampsia risk
- <34 weeks: Ratio >85 highly predictive (onset within 2 weeks)
- ≥34 weeks: Ratio >85 predictive
- Close monitoring required

sFlt-1/PlGF Ratio >655:
- Very high risk, severe disease
- Consider delivery planning
```

**Clinical Applications (2024):**
- **Triage Tool:** Helps distinguish preeclampsia from other hypertensive disorders
- **Timing:** Serial measurements not routinely recommended (evolving evidence)
- **Limitations:**
  - Not diagnostic alone (clinical criteria still required)
  - Cannot distinguish preeclampsia from HELLP or other TMAs
  - Not validated for women on dialysis or post-transplant
- **PROGNOSIS Trial (2016):** sFlt-1/PlGF ratio guided care reduced maternal adverse outcomes

**Prevention Strategies (2024 Update):**

**Low-Dose Aspirin:**
- **USPSTF Recommendation (2021, Reaffirmed 2024):**
  - Start at 12-16 weeks gestation (ideally before 16 weeks)
  - Dose: 81-162 mg daily (81 mg most common in US)
  - Continue until delivery
- **Indications (High-Risk Criteria - need ≥1):**
  - Pre-existing hypertension or renal disease
  - Previous preeclampsia (especially if <34 weeks)
  - Type 1 or 2 diabetes
  - Multi-fetal gestation
  - Antiphospholipid syndrome
  - Chronic kidney disease
  - Autoimmune disease (SLE, RA)
- **Efficacy:** 15-20% relative risk reduction in preeclampsia
- **NNT:** ~50-70 for preventing one case of preterm preeclampsia
- **Safety:** Well-tolerated, minimal bleeding risk at low dose

**Calcium Supplementation:**
- **WHO Recommendation:** 1.5-2 g elemental calcium daily
- **Benefit:** Primarily in populations with low dietary calcium intake
- **Limited benefit in US/developed countries with adequate calcium intake

#### Diagnostic Criteria (ACOG 2020)
```
Blood Pressure:
- ≥140/90 mmHg on two occasions 4 hours apart
- After 20 weeks gestation in previously normotensive woman

AND one of the following:
- Proteinuria: ≥300 mg/24 hours or protein/creatinine ratio ≥0.3
- Thrombocytopenia: Platelets <100,000/μL
- Renal insufficiency: Creatinine >1.1 mg/dL or doubling
- Pulmonary edema
- New-onset cerebral/visual disturbances
```

#### Severity Features
- **Severe Range BP**: ≥160/110 mmHg
- **Thrombocytopenia**: <50,000/μL
- **Elevated Liver Enzymes**: AST/ALT >2× normal
- **Progressive Renal Insufficiency**: Creatinine doubling
- **Pulmonary Edema**: New onset
- **Cerebral/Visual Symptoms**: Severe headache, visual changes

#### Management
```
Mild Preeclampsia (34-37 weeks):
- Outpatient monitoring twice weekly
- BP control with labetalol, nifedipine, methyldopa
- Delivery at 37 weeks

Severe Preeclampsia:
- Hospital admission
- Magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis
- BP control with IV labetalol or hydralazine
- Delivery after 34 weeks (or earlier if maternal/fetal compromise)
```

### HELLP Syndrome
- **Hemolysis**: LDH >600 IU/L, peripheral smear
- **Elevated Liver Enzymes**: AST/ALT >70 IU/L
- **Low Platelets**: <100,000/μL
- **Management**: Immediate delivery, supportive care, consider plasma exchange

### Acute Kidney Injury in Pregnancy

#### Causes by Trimester
```
First Trimester:
- Hyperemesis gravidarum (pre-renal AKI)
- Sepsis from abortion
- Acute fatty liver of pregnancy (rare)

Second Trimester:
- Preeclampsia
- Urinary tract obstruction (physiologic hydronephrosis)
- Sepsis

Third Trimester/Postpartum:
- Severe preeclampsia/HELLP
- Placental abruption
- Postpartum hemorrhage
- Amniotic fluid embolism
- Postpartum atypical HUS (aHUS)
- Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP)
```

#### Pregnancy-Associated Thrombotic Microangiopathies (2024 Update)

**Distinguishing Preeclampsia/HELLP from aHUS/TTP:**

```
Preeclampsia/HELLP:
- Onset: >20 weeks gestation, resolves postpartum (usually <72 hours)
- Delivery: Curative
- Persistent thrombocytopenia >72 hours postpartum: Consider alternate diagnosis

Postpartum aHUS:
- Onset: Postpartum period (hours to weeks after delivery)
- Trigger: Pregnancy/delivery activates complement dysregulation
- Persistent/worsening AKI and thrombocytopenia DESPITE delivery
- Does NOT resolve spontaneously
- **Life-threatening if untreated**

TTP:
- ADAMTS13 activity <10%
- Neurologic symptoms more prominent
- May occur any trimester or postpartum
- Requires plasma exchange
```

**Postpartum Atypical HUS Management:**
- **High Suspicion:** AKI + MAHA + thrombocytopenia persisting >72 hours postpartum
- **Diagnostic Workup:**
  - ADAMTS13 activity (rule out TTP)
  - Complement testing (C3, C4, CH50)
  - Genetic testing for complement mutations (after acute period)
  - Haptoglobin, LDH, peripheral smear
- **Treatment: URGENT Complement Inhibition**
  - **Eculizumab (Soliris):** Standard of care, FDA-approved for aHUS
  - **Meningococcal Vaccination:** Required (or prophylactic antibiotics if urgent)
  - **Dosing:** Same as non-pregnant patients (see Document 03, Document 05)
  - **Ravulizumab (Ultomiris):** Long-acting alternative
  - **Plasma Exchange:** Not effective for aHUS (may temporize while awaiting eculizumab)
- **Prognosis:**
  - Excellent response to complement inhibition (80-90% recovery)
  - Delay in treatment worsens outcomes
  - May require long-term eculizumab (variable duration)
- **Future Pregnancies:**
  - High recurrence risk (up to 70-80%)
  - Prophylactic eculizumab recommended for subsequent pregnancies
  - See **Document 03 (Glomerular Diseases) and Document 05 (Vascular Disorders)** for comprehensive aHUS information

#### Management Principles
- **Identify and Treat Underlying Cause**
- **Hemodynamic Support**: Maintain uterine perfusion
- **Avoid Nephrotoxic Agents**: NSAIDs, iodinated contrast
- **Consider Early Delivery**: If maternal condition deteriorates
- **High Index of Suspicion for TMA:** Persistent thrombocytopenia/AKI postpartum

## Dialysis in Pregnancy

### Pregnancy on Dialysis

#### Outcomes with Modern Intensified Dialysis
- **Fetal Survival**: 80-90% with >20 hours/week dialysis
- **Maternal Complications**: Hypertension (60%), anemia (50%), polyhydramnios (30%)
- **Preterm Delivery**: 80% (mean gestational age 34 weeks)

#### Dialysis Prescription
```
Hemodialysis:
- Frequency: 5-6 sessions per week
- Duration: 4-6 hours per session
- Target: BUN <50 mg/dL pre-dialysis
- Ultrafiltration: Gradual, avoid hypotension

Peritoneal Dialysis:
- Increased exchange frequency
- Larger fill volumes if tolerated
- Consider switching to HD if inadequate clearance
```

#### Special Considerations
- **Nutritional Needs**: 1.2-1.5 g/kg/day protein, 300-500 kcal/day extra
- **Anemia Management**: Higher EPO requirements, iron supplementation
- **Mineral Metabolism**: Aggressive phosphate control, vitamin D
- **Volume Management**: Avoid rapid fluid shifts

### Pregnancy After Transplantation

#### Timing Recommendations
- **Wait Period**: Minimum 1 year post-transplant
- **Graft Function**: Stable eGFR >40 mL/min
- **Immunosuppression**: Stable maintenance doses
- **Rejection History**: No rejection in past year

#### Medication Adjustments
```
Continue:
- Prednisone (lowest effective dose)
- Azathioprine (safe in pregnancy)
- Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus preferred)

Stop/Replace:
- Mycophenolate mofetil: Stop 6 weeks before conception
- Sirolimus/Everolimus: Stop before conception
- Switch MMF to azathioprine if needed
```

#### Outcomes
- **Graft Survival**: 90% at 5 years post-pregnancy
- **Live Birth Rate**: 70-80%
- **Complications**: Preterm birth (50%), preeclampsia (30%), low birth weight (30%)

## Medication Management in Pregnancy

### Antihypertensive Agents

#### Safe in Pregnancy
- **Labetalol**: First-line, α and β blocker
- **Nifedipine**: Long-acting calcium channel blocker
- **Methyldopa**: Historically first-line, limited use now
- **Hydralazine**: IV for severe hypertension

#### Contraindicated/Avoid
- **ACE Inhibitors**: Teratogenic (fetal renal dysgenesis, oligohydramnios, IUGR)
  - Stop BEFORE conception (ideally 3 months prior)
  - If discovered during pregnancy: Stop immediately
- **ARBs**: Similar risks to ACE inhibitors
  - Stop BEFORE conception
- **Direct Renin Inhibitors (Aliskiren)**: Limited data, contraindicated
- **SGLT2 Inhibitors (Empagliflozin, Dapagliflozin, Canagliflozin):**
  - **Contraindicated in pregnancy**
  - Stop BEFORE conception
  - Limited data but theoretical risks (glycosuria, volume depletion)
  - Can resume postpartum if not breastfeeding

#### Use with Caution
- **β-blockers**: Atenolol associated with fetal growth restriction
  - Labetalol preferred
- **Diuretics**: May reduce placental perfusion
  - Reserve for volume overload, heart failure

### Immunosuppressive Medications

#### Compatible with Pregnancy
- **Prednisone**: Crosses placenta, minimal fetal effects
- **Azathioprine**: Safe, metabolized by fetus
- **Calcineurin Inhibitors**: Tacrolimus and cyclosporine relatively safe
- **Hydroxychloroquine**: Safe, recommended in SLE

#### Contraindicated
- **Mycophenolate Mofetil**: Teratogenic (ear, facial defects)
- **Methotrexate**: Teratogenic, abortifacient
- **Cyclophosphamide**: Teratogenic, especially first trimester

### Antibiotics in Pregnancy

#### Generally Safe
- **Penicillins**: Category B
- **Cephalosporins**: Category B
- **Macrolides**: Azithromycin safe, erythromycin may cause infantile hypertrophic pyloric stenosis

#### Use with Caution
- **Fluoroquinolones**: Cartilage damage in animal studies
- **Aminoglycosides**: Fetal ototoxicity and nephrotoxicity
- **Sulfonamides**: Avoid in third trimester (kernicterus risk)

## Postpartum Management

### Immediate Postpartum Period (0-6 weeks)

#### Renal Function Changes
- **GFR**: Returns to pre-pregnancy baseline by 6-8 weeks
- **Proteinuria**: May persist for 3-6 months
- **Blood Pressure**: Monitor for postpartum preeclampsia

---

### Postpartum Preeclampsia (Comprehensive Management) ⭐

#### Definition and Epidemiology

**Definition:**
- New-onset or persistent hypertension (≥140/90 mmHg) + proteinuria OR severe features
- Occurring from delivery up to 6 weeks postpartum

**Classification:**
```
De Novo Postpartum Preeclampsia:
- NO preeclampsia during pregnancy
- NEW onset of preeclampsia AFTER delivery
- Accounts for 25-30% of all postpartum preeclampsia cases

Persistent/Worsening Postpartum Preeclampsia:
- Had preeclampsia during pregnancy
- Continues or worsens after delivery
- Accounts for 70-75% of postpartum preeclampsia cases
```

**Epidemiology:**
- Incidence: 3-5% of all deliveries
- Timing: 50% occur within first 48 hours postpartum
  - 27% occur day 0-1 (immediate postpartum)
  - 23% occur day 2-6 (early postpartum)
  - 50% occur day 7-42 (late postpartum, up to 6 weeks)
- Peak risk: Days 3-7 postpartum ⭐
- Late-onset (>48 hours): Higher risk of complications (seizure, stroke)

---

#### Risk Factors ⭐

**High-Risk Factors:**
```
Strongest Predictors:
- Preeclampsia during pregnancy (OR 15-20×)
- Gestational hypertension (OR 5-8×)
- Chronic hypertension (OR 3-5×)
- Pre-existing kidney disease (OR 4-6×)
- Obesity (BMI ≥30: OR 3-4×)
- Diabetes mellitus (OR 2-3×)
- Autoimmune disease (SLE, APS) (OR 3-5×)
- First pregnancy (nulliparity) (OR 2-3×)
- Multi-fetal gestation (OR 3-4×)
- Age >40 years (OR 2×)

Postpartum-Specific Risk Factors:
- Fluid mobilization (rapid third-spacing resolution → hypervolemia)
- NSAIDs (fluid retention, vasoconstriction)
- Delayed presentation to care (lack of postpartum monitoring)
- Preterm delivery <37 weeks (OR 2-3×)
- Cesarean section (OR 1.5-2×)
```

---

#### Clinical Presentation ⭐⭐⭐

**Classic Triad:**
1. Hypertension (≥140/90 mmHg)
2. Proteinuria (≥300 mg/24 hr or PCR ≥0.3)
3. Severe features (headache, visual changes, RUQ pain)

**Symptoms (May Be Subtle or Absent):**
```
Severe Headache:
- Frontal, persistent, not relieved by acetaminophen
- Most common presenting symptom (60-70%)
- May indicate cerebral edema, increased ICP

Visual Disturbances:
- Blurred vision, scotomata (blind spots), photophobia
- Indicates cortical blindness risk (PRES syndrome)
- Requires urgent evaluation

Epigastric/RUQ Pain:
- Indicates hepatic involvement (capsular distension)
- May herald HELLP syndrome or hepatic rupture (rare)

Dyspnea:
- Pulmonary edema (fluid mobilization + HTN)
- Occurs in 3-5% of severe postpartum preeclampsia

Other Symptoms:
- Nausea/vomiting
- Altered mental status (confusion, drowsiness)
- Seizure (eclampsia) - rare but most feared complication
```

**Physical Examination:**
```
Vital Signs:
- Blood pressure: ≥140/90 mmHg (often ≥160/110 in severe cases)
- Heart rate: May be normal or tachycardic
- Respiratory rate: Increased if pulmonary edema

General:
- Edema: Facial, periorbital, peripheral (non-specific, common postpartum)
- Weight gain: Rapid weight gain (fluid retention)

Neurologic:
- Hyperreflexia (3+ or 4+ reflexes)
- Clonus (sustained ankle clonus concerning)
- Visual field deficits (cortical blindness - PRES)
- Altered mental status (confusion, somnolence)

Abdominal:
- RUQ tenderness (hepatic involvement)
- Peritoneal signs (rare - consider hepatic rupture)

Cardiopulmonary:
- Crackles (pulmonary edema)
- S3 gallop (volume overload)
```

---

#### Diagnostic Criteria (ACOG 2020) ⭐

**Blood Pressure Criteria:**
```
Hypertension:
- SBP ≥140 mmHg OR DBP ≥90 mmHg
- Measured on TWO occasions at least 4 hours apart
- OR SBP ≥160 mmHg OR DBP ≥110 mmHg (severe-range) - can diagnose on SINGLE measurement

Important Notes:
- First BP measurement may be normal (BP rises postpartum)
- Home BP monitoring often reveals HTN missed at clinic visits
- Severe-range HTN (≥160/110) requires IMMEDIATE treatment
```

**AND one of the following:**

```
1. Proteinuria:
   - ≥300 mg in 24-hour urine collection
   - OR Protein/creatinine ratio ≥0.3 mg/mg (or ≥30 mg/mmol)
   - OR Dipstick ≥2+ (if quantitative not available)

2. OR Severe Features (in absence of proteinuria):
   - Thrombocytopenia: Platelets <100,000/μL
   - Renal insufficiency: Creatinine >1.1 mg/dL (or doubling from baseline)
   - Elevated liver enzymes: AST or ALT >2× upper limit normal
   - Pulmonary edema
   - New-onset cerebral symptoms: Severe headache unresponsive to medication, visual disturbances
   - RUQ or epigastric pain
```

**NOTE:** Proteinuria is NO LONGER REQUIRED for diagnosis if severe features present ⭐

---

#### Laboratory Evaluation ⭐

**Initial Workup (All Patients with Suspected Postpartum Preeclampsia):**
```
Blood Tests:
- Complete blood count: Platelets (r/o HELLP, TTP), hematocrit (hemoconcentration)
- Comprehensive metabolic panel:
  - Creatinine (renal function)
  - AST, ALT (hepatic involvement)
  - Albumin, total protein
- LDH: Hemolysis screening (HELLP syndrome)
- Peripheral blood smear: If thrombocytopenia or anemia (schistocytes in TMA)
- Coagulation panel: PT/INR, aPTT, fibrinogen (if bleeding or DIC suspected)

Urine Tests:
- Urinalysis: Protein, RBC, WBC
- Urine protein/creatinine ratio (spot) OR 24-hour urine protein
  - PCR ≥0.3 mg/mg diagnostic
  - 24-hour >300 mg diagnostic

Optional/If Severe Symptoms:
- sFlt-1/PlGF ratio: May help differentiate from other diagnoses (limited postpartum data)
- ADAMTS13 activity: If suspected TTP (platelets <50K, neurologic symptoms)
- Complement levels (C3, C4, CH50): If suspected aHUS (persistent AKI + thrombocytopenia >72 hr)
- Brain MRI: If neurologic symptoms persistent (PRES syndrome)
- Chest X-ray: If dyspnea (pulmonary edema)
```

**Laboratory Findings by Severity:**
```
Mild Postpartum Preeclampsia:
- BP 140-159/90-109 mmHg
- Proteinuria present or absent
- Normal platelets, renal/hepatic function
- No severe symptoms

Severe Postpartum Preeclampsia:
- BP ≥160/110 mmHg
- Platelets <100K/μL
- Creatinine >1.1 mg/dL (or doubling)
- AST/ALT >2× normal
- LDH elevated (hemolysis)
- Severe symptoms (headache, visual changes, RUQ pain)
```

---

#### Differential Diagnosis ⭐

**Must Distinguish from:**

```
1. Chronic Hypertension:
   - History of HTN before pregnancy or <20 weeks
   - No proteinuria or new-onset severe features
   - Stable BP pattern (not rapidly rising)

2. Postpartum Atypical HUS (aHUS): ⭐⭐⭐
   - AKI + thrombocytopenia + hemolytic anemia PERSISTING >72 hours
   - Does NOT improve with delivery (unlike preeclampsia)
   - Schistocytes on smear, elevated LDH, low haptoglobin
   - Requires complement inhibitor (eculizumab)
   - See Document 03 and Document 05 for full aHUS management

3. TTP (Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura):
   - Severe thrombocytopenia (<30-50K/μL)
   - PROMINENT neurologic symptoms (confusion, focal deficits)
   - ADAMTS13 activity <10%
   - Requires urgent plasma exchange

4. HELLP Syndrome:
   - Hemolysis (LDH >600, schistocytes)
   - Elevated Liver enzymes (AST/ALT >70)
   - Low Platelets (<100K)
   - May overlap with severe preeclampsia

5. Postpartum Cardiomyopathy:
   - Dyspnea, orthopnea, lower extremity edema
   - Echocardiography: Reduced EF <45%
   - BNP elevated
   - Onset: Last month of pregnancy to 5 months postpartum

6. Gestational Hypertension:
   - Hypertension without proteinuria or severe features
   - Resolves by 12 weeks postpartum
   - Lower risk than preeclampsia

7. Secondary Hypertension:
   - Renal artery stenosis (bruit, asymmetric kidneys)
   - Primary aldosteronism (hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis)
   - Pheochromocytoma (episodic HTN, palpitations, sweating)
```

**Key Distinguishing Feature: Timing**
- **Preeclampsia/HELLP**: Usually RESOLVES within 48-72 hours postpartum
- **aHUS/TTP**: PERSISTS or WORSENS beyond 72 hours postpartum
- **If thrombocytopenia/AKI not improving by day 3-4 postpartum → Consider aHUS/TTP**

---

#### Management ⭐⭐⭐

**General Principles:**
1. **Admit if severe features** (BP ≥160/110, symptoms, labs abnormal)
2. **Treat severe-range hypertension immediately** (target <160/110 within 30-60 min)
3. **Magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis** if severe features
4. **Monitor closely** (BP q4-6h, daily labs, symptoms)
5. **Breastfeeding considerations** (most medications compatible)

---

##### Antihypertensive Therapy ⭐⭐⭐

**ACUTE Severe Hypertension (≥160/110 mmHg):** ⭐

**Goal:** Reduce BP to <160/110 mmHg within 30-60 minutes (NOT normalization)

```
First-Line Agents (ALL compatible with breastfeeding):

1. Labetalol (IV):
   - Dose: 20 mg IV push, then 40 mg, then 80 mg q10min (max total 300 mg)
   - Onset: 5 minutes, peak 10-20 minutes
   - Contraindication: Asthma, heart block, heart failure
   - Most commonly used in US

2. Hydralazine (IV):
   - Dose: 5-10 mg IV push q20min (max 30 mg)
   - Onset: 10-20 minutes
   - Side effects: Reflex tachycardia, hypotension
   - May cause fetal distress during pregnancy (not an issue postpartum)

3. Nifedipine (PO immediate-release):
   - Dose: 10-20 mg PO, repeat q20min if needed (max 50 mg in 1 hour)
   - Onset: 10-20 minutes
   - Advantage: Oral route, easy administration
   - Increasingly used as first-line alternative to IV labetalol
```

**CHRONIC Hypertension Management (Outpatient):** ⭐

**Goal:** BP <140/90 mmHg (or <130/80 if end-organ damage)

```
Preferred Agents (ALL compatible with breastfeeding):

1. Labetalol (PO):
   - Dose: Start 100-200 mg PO BID, titrate up to 800 mg BID
   - First-line for postpartum HTN
   - Safe with breastfeeding

2. Nifedipine extended-release (PO):
   - Dose: Start 30 mg PO daily, titrate up to 90-120 mg daily
   - Good alternative if β-blocker contraindicated
   - Safe with breastfeeding

3. Methyldopa (PO):
   - Dose: Start 250 mg PO BID-TID, max 3 g/day
   - Extensively studied in pregnancy/postpartum
   - May cause fatigue, depression (limit use)
   - Safe with breastfeeding

4. Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ):
   - Dose: 12.5-25 mg PO daily
   - Useful if volume overload
   - May reduce milk supply at higher doses (use lowest effective dose)
   - Safe with breastfeeding at low doses

5. ACE Inhibitors (enalapril, captopril): ⭐
   - Dose: Enalapril 5-20 mg PO daily OR Captopril 25-50 mg PO TID
   - Can use if NOT breastfeeding OR if benefits outweigh risks
   - COMPATIBLE with breastfeeding per AAP (very low milk levels)
   - Captopril and enalapril preferred ACE-Is for breastfeeding
   - Monitor infant for hypotension (rare)

6. ARBs (losartan, valsartan):
   - Limited data in breastfeeding
   - AVOID if possible, use ACE-I instead
   - If must use: Losartan has lowest milk levels
```

**AVOID Postpartum:**
```
Atenolol:
- Concentrates in breast milk
- May cause neonatal bradycardia, hypoglycemia
- Use labetalol instead

Reserpine:
- May cause nasal congestion, sedation in infant

Nitroprusside:
- Cyanide toxicity risk (prolonged use)
```

##### Magnesium Sulfate (Seizure Prophylaxis) ⭐

**Indications:**
```
Severe Postpartum Preeclampsia with ANY of:
- Severe-range BP (≥160/110) with symptoms
- Severe persistent headache
- Visual disturbances
- RUQ/epigastric pain
- Hyperreflexia with sustained clonus
- Platelets <100K
- Creatinine >1.1 mg/dL or rising
- Pulmonary edema

Postpartum Eclampsia (seizure):
- Continue magnesium for 24 hours post-seizure
```

**Dosing:**
```
Loading Dose:
- 4-6 g IV over 15-20 minutes

Maintenance:
- 1-2 g/hour IV continuous infusion
- Continue for 24 hours after:
  * Resolution of severe symptoms
  * OR last seizure (if eclampsia occurred)
  * OR delivery if started antepartum
```

**Monitoring:**
```
Clinical:
- Deep tendon reflexes (DTRs) q1-2h
  - Loss of patellar reflex = early toxicity sign
- Respiratory rate q1h (should be >12/min)
- Urine output q1h (should be >25-30 mL/hr)
- Continuous pulse oximetry

Laboratory:
- Magnesium level q6h (target 4-7 mEq/L or 4.8-8.4 mg/dL)
- Baseline creatinine, calcium

Signs of Magnesium Toxicity:
- Loss of DTRs: 7-10 mEq/L
- Respiratory depression: 10-13 mEq/L
- Cardiac arrest: >15 mEq/L
```

**Antidote for Magnesium Toxicity:**
```
Calcium Gluconate:
- 1 g (10 mL of 10% solution) IV push over 3 minutes
- Give if:
  * Loss of DTRs
  * Respiratory rate <12/min
  * Oxygen saturation <95%
  * Magnesium level >8-9 mEq/L with symptoms
```

---

##### Supportive Care

```
Fluid Management:
- Restrict to 80-100 mL/hour (avoid fluid overload → pulmonary edema)
- Total: <2.5 L in 24 hours
- Monitor strict I/O (Foley catheter if severe)
- Goal urine output: >25-30 mL/hour (oliguria common in severe preeclampsia)

Avoid NSAIDs:
- NSAIDs worsen HTN, cause fluid retention, impair renal function
- Use acetaminophen for pain control instead
- If NSAIDs necessary (postpartum pain), use lowest dose for shortest duration

Monitoring:
- Vital signs: BP q4-6h (q1-2h if severe)
- Daily weights
- Symptoms assessment: Headache, visual changes, RUQ pain
- Laboratory: CBC, CMP, LFTs q24-48h until improving
```

---

#### Duration of Treatment and Follow-Up ⭐

**Inpatient Monitoring:**
```
Mild Postpartum Preeclampsia:
- Monitor 24-48 hours postpartum
- Discharge if:
  * BP <150/100 on ≤1 medication
  * No severe symptoms
  * Labs stable/improving
  * Patient reliable for outpatient follow-up

Severe Postpartum Preeclampsia:
- Admit to L&D or postpartum unit
- Magnesium sulfate × 24 hours if severe features
- Monitor until BP stable <160/110 for 24 hours
- Labs normalizing (platelets rising, Cr stable/improving)
- Discharge on antihypertensives
```

**Outpatient Follow-Up:** ⭐
```
First Visit (3-7 days post-discharge):
- BP check, symptom assessment
- Labs: CBC, CMP if abnormal at discharge
- Adjust antihypertensives as needed
- Reinforce warning signs

Weekly Visits:
- Continue until BP normalized (<140/90) or stable on medications
- May take 6-12 weeks for BP to normalize

6-Week Postpartum Visit:
- BP check, consider HBPM (home BP monitoring)
- Assess need for continued antihypertensives
- Counsel on long-term cardiovascular risk
- Discuss future pregnancy planning

Long-Term Follow-Up:
- 50% of women require antihypertensives for >6 weeks
- 25% develop chronic hypertension
- Annual BP screening lifelong
- Cardiovascular risk assessment (lipids, glucose, BMI)
```

**Duration of Antihypertensive Therapy:**
```
Typical Course:
- Most women: 6-12 weeks of antihypertensives
- Some women: Lifelong (transition to chronic HTN)

Weaning Antihypertensives:
- Attempt taper at 6-8 weeks postpartum IF:
  * BP <140/90 on single agent
  * Home BP monitoring shows normal readings
  * No symptoms
- If BP rises during taper: Resume medication, reassess in 4-6 weeks
- If BP remains elevated off medications: Diagnose as chronic HTN
```

#### Complications and Red Flags ⭐⭐⭐

**Life-Threatening Complications:**

```
1. Eclampsia (Seizure): ⭐
   - Occurs in 0.1-0.3% of postpartum preeclampsia
   - 25-30% of eclamptic seizures occur POSTPARTUM
   - Risk highest in first 48 hours, but can occur up to 6 weeks
   - Management:
     * Protect airway, turn on left side
     * Magnesium sulfate 6 g IV load, then 2 g/hr
     * Call for help (anesthesia, ICU)
     * Consider benzodiazepines if seizure >5 min

2. Posterior Reversible Encephalopathy Syndrome (PRES):
   - Severe headache, visual changes, altered mental status, seizures
   - Brain MRI: Vasogenic edema (parieto-occipital regions)
   - Management: BP control, supportive care (usually reversible)

3. Stroke (Hemorrhagic or Ischemic):
   - Severe headache, focal neurologic deficits
   - Risk with severe uncontrolled HTN (≥160/110)
   - Leading cause of maternal mortality in preeclampsia
   - Management: Urgent BP control, neurology consult, brain imaging

4. Pulmonary Edema:
   - Dyspnea, hypoxia, crackles on exam
   - Caused by: Fluid overload + HTN + low oncotic pressure
   - Management: Diuresis, BP control, oxygen, ICU monitoring

5. Hepatic Rupture (Rare):
   - Sudden severe RUQ/epigastric pain, shoulder pain, shock
   - Associated with HELLP syndrome
   - Surgical emergency (mortality 50-75%)

6. Acute Kidney Injury:
   - Creatinine >1.1 mg/dL (or doubling from baseline)
   - Oliguria (<500 mL/24 hr)
   - Usually recovers with delivery + BP control
   - If persistent >72 hr: Consider aHUS (see differential diagnosis)

7. DIC (Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation):
   - Low platelets, low fibrinogen, elevated PT/aPTT
   - Associated with placental abruption, HELLP
   - Management: Supportive, treat underlying cause
```

**IMMEDIATE Escalation Criteria (Call Rapid Response/MFM/ICU):**
```
- BP ≥180/120 mmHg despite treatment
- Seizure (eclampsia)
- Altered mental status (confusion, somnolence)
- Severe persistent headache unresponsive to medication
- Visual loss or persistent visual disturbances
- Pulmonary edema (oxygen saturation <94% on room air)
- Oliguria (<25 mL/hr for 2 hours despite fluid bolus)
- Platelets <50,000/μL (risk of bleeding)
- AST/ALT >500 IU/L (hepatic necrosis risk)
- Epigastric/RUQ pain with elevated liver enzymes (hepatic rupture risk)
```

#### Patient Education and Warning Signs ⭐

**Discharge Instructions (CRITICAL for All Postpartum Women):**

```
"Call your doctor IMMEDIATELY or go to ER if you have ANY of these symptoms:"

1. Severe Headache:
   - Worst headache of your life
   - Not relieved by acetaminophen
   - Located in front of head or all over
   - May be sign of stroke or seizure risk

2. Vision Changes:
   - Blurred vision
   - Seeing spots or flashing lights
   - Temporary vision loss
   - Double vision

3. Severe Belly Pain:
   - Right upper abdomen (below ribs)
   - Stomach or chest pain
   - Shoulder pain (may indicate liver involvement)

4. Trouble Breathing:
   - Shortness of breath
   - Cannot catch your breath
   - Chest pain or pressure

5. Swelling:
   - Sudden swelling of face, hands, or legs
   - Especially if with headache or vision changes

6. Nausea/Vomiting:
   - Persistent nausea
   - Inability to keep food/fluids down

7. Seizures:
   - Any convulsion or loss of consciousness
   - CALL 911 immediately
```

**Home Blood Pressure Monitoring:**
```
When to Check:
- At least once daily for first 7-10 days postpartum
- If on antihypertensives: Twice daily until stable

Technique:
- Sit quietly for 5 minutes before measurement
- Feet flat on floor, arm at heart level
- Use validated automatic BP cuff (upper arm)
- Record readings in log to share with provider

When to Call:
- BP ≥140/90 mmHg on 2 occasions 4 hours apart
- BP ≥160/110 mmHg at ANY time (call immediately)
- Any severe symptoms (above warning signs)
```

#### Long-Term Cardiovascular Risk ⭐

**Postpartum preeclampsia is a MARKER of future cardiovascular disease**

```
Increased Lifetime Risks:
- Chronic hypertension: 3-4× increased risk
- Ischemic heart disease: 2× increased risk
- Stroke: 1.8× increased risk
- Heart failure: 2× increased risk
- End-stage renal disease: 4-5× increased risk
- Overall cardiovascular mortality: 2× increased risk

Risk Modification Strategies:
- Annual BP screening lifelong
- Lipid panel every 3-5 years
- Fasting glucose/HbA1c screening
- Maintain healthy weight (BMI <25)
- Regular exercise (150 min/week moderate activity)
- DASH diet, limit sodium <2.3 g/day
- Smoking cessation
- Consider aspirin 81 mg daily for CV prevention (discuss with PCP)
```

**Future Pregnancy Counseling:**
```
Recurrence Risk:
- De novo postpartum preeclampsia: 20-30% recurrence
- Severe preeclampsia/eclampsia in pregnancy: 30-50% recurrence

Recommendations for Future Pregnancy:
- Preconception counseling with MFM
- Optimize BP control before conception (target <140/90)
- LOW-DOSE ASPIRIN 81-162 mg daily starting at 12-16 weeks
- Early prenatal care (first trimester)
- Increased antenatal monitoring (BP, labs, growth scans)
- Plan delivery at tertiary care center with NICU
```

#### BOARD EXAM HIGH-YIELD CONCEPTS ⭐⭐⭐

```
1. Timing of Postpartum Preeclampsia:
   - Peak incidence: Days 3-7 postpartum
   - 50% occur within first 48 hours
   - 50% occur 2-42 days postpartum
   - Can occur up to 6 weeks postpartum

2. Diagnostic Criteria:
   - BP ≥140/90 mmHg (≥160/110 = severe-range)
   - Proteinuria NO LONGER REQUIRED if severe features present
   - Severe features alone sufficient for diagnosis

3. Acute BP Management:
   - Goal: Reduce BP to <160/110 within 30-60 minutes
   - First-line: IV labetalol, IV hydralazine, OR PO nifedipine immediate-release
   - Do NOT normalize BP acutely (risk of hypoperfusion)

4. Chronic BP Management (Breastfeeding-Compatible):
   - First-line: Labetalol, nifedipine ER, methyldopa
   - ACE-I (enalapril, captopril): COMPATIBLE with breastfeeding per AAP
   - AVOID: Atenolol (concentrates in milk), ARBs (limited data)

5. Magnesium Sulfate:
   - Indicated for severe postpartum preeclampsia with symptoms
   - Loading: 4-6 g IV over 15-20 min
   - Maintenance: 1-2 g/hour × 24 hours
   - Toxicity signs: Loss of DTRs (7-10 mEq/L), respiratory depression (>10 mEq/L)
   - Antidote: Calcium gluconate 1 g IV push

6. Differential Diagnosis:
   - Postpartum aHUS: AKI + thrombocytopenia PERSISTING >72 hr (does NOT resolve)
   - TTP: ADAMTS13 <10%, prominent neurologic symptoms
   - Key distinction: Preeclampsia/HELLP RESOLVES within 48-72 hr

7. Complications:
   - Eclampsia: 25-30% of eclamptic seizures occur postpartum
   - PRES: Posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (reversible with BP control)
   - Stroke: Leading cause of maternal mortality (severe uncontrolled HTN)

8. NSAIDs:
   - AVOID or minimize use (worsen HTN, fluid retention, impair renal function)
   - Use acetaminophen for pain instead

9. Duration of Treatment:
   - Most women: 6-12 weeks of antihypertensives
   - 25% develop chronic HTN (lifelong treatment)

10. Long-Term Risk:
    - 3-4× increased risk of chronic HTN
    - 2× increased risk of CVD and stroke
    - Annual BP screening LIFELONG
    - Recurrence risk: 20-50% in future pregnancies
```

#### Medication Adjustments
- **ACE Inhibitors/ARBs**: Can restart if breastfeeding not planned
- **Diuretics**: May be needed for volume control
- **Immunosuppression**: Return to pre-pregnancy regimen

#### Breastfeeding Considerations
- **ACE Inhibitors**: Generally compatible (monitor infant)
- **ARBs**: Limited data, avoid if possible
- **Calcineurin Inhibitors**: Compatible with breastfeeding
- **Azathioprine**: Compatible with breastfeeding

### Long-Term Follow-Up

#### Cardiovascular Risk
- **Increased Lifetime Risk**: Especially after preeclampsia
- **Screening**: BP, lipids, glucose annually
- **Risk Factor Modification**: Lifestyle, weight control

#### Renal Prognosis
- **Recovery**: Most recover normal function
- **Persistent Proteinuria**: May indicate underlying CKD
- **Future Pregnancy Risk**: Counsel based on outcomes

#### Contraception Counseling
- **Combined Oral Contraceptives**: Avoid with hypertension, proteinuria
- **Progestin-Only**: Generally safe
- **IUD**: Excellent option for most
- **Barrier Methods**: Less effective but safe

## Fetal and Neonatal Considerations

### Fetal Monitoring
- **Growth Scans**: Every 4 weeks starting at 20 weeks
- **Doppler Studies**: If growth restriction or hypertension
- **Amniotic Fluid**: Monitor for polyhydramnios (maternal renal failure)

### Neonatal Complications
- **Preterm Birth**: Most common complication
- **Growth Restriction**: Related to maternal hypertension, proteinuria
- **Neonatal AKI**: Rare, related to maternal medications

### Long-Term Child Health
- **Cardiovascular Risk**: Increased after maternal preeclampsia
- **Renal Health**: Monitor blood pressure and renal function
- **Developmental Outcomes**: Generally normal with appropriate care

## Key Practice Points

### Clinical Pearls
1. **Serum Creatinine**: 0.4-0.6 mg/dL is normal in pregnancy
2. **Proteinuria**: >300 mg/24 hours after 20 weeks warrants evaluation
3. **Blood Pressure**: >140/90 mmHg after 20 weeks is abnormal
4. **Medication Review**: Stop ACE inhibitors/ARBs before conception
5. **Dialysis Intensity**: >20 hours/week improves fetal outcomes

### Red Flags
- **Rising Creatinine**: >0.8 mg/dL in pregnancy needs evaluation
- **Severe Hypertension**: ≥160/110 mmHg requires immediate treatment
- **Proteinuria**: >5 g/day associated with poor outcomes
- **Decreased Fetal Movements**: May indicate fetal compromise
- **Severe Headache/Visual Changes**: Evaluate for preeclampsia

### Prevention Strategies
- **Preconception Counseling**: Optimize disease control before pregnancy
- **Early Prenatal Care**: First trimester evaluation and planning
- **Regular Monitoring**: Blood pressure, proteinuria, renal function
- **Medication Safety**: Use pregnancy-compatible medications
- **Multidisciplinary Care**: Nephrology, maternal-fetal medicine, neonatology
