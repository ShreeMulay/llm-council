**Difficulty Level:** Intermediate-Advanced

**Clinical Impact:** High

## 1. Hemodialysis Principles & Physiology

### Basic Principles

#### Diffusion
**Definition:**
- Movement of solutes across semipermeable membrane
- Driven by concentration gradient
- Most efficient for small molecules (<500 daltons)

**Factors Affecting Diffusion:**
- Concentration gradient
- Membrane surface area
- Membrane permeability
- Temperature
- Blood and dialysate flow rates

#### Convection
**Definition:**
- Solvent drag effect
- Movement of solutes with water flow
- Important for middle molecules (500-20,000 daltons)

**Applications:**
- High-flux dialysis
- Hemodiafiltration
- Ultrafiltration

#### Ultrafiltration
**Mechanism:**
- Transmembrane pressure gradient
- Water removal from blood
- Solute drag (convection)

**Determinants:**
- Transmembrane pressure (TMP)
- Membrane hydraulic permeability
- Blood flow rate
- Dialysate flow rate

### Dialysis Membranes

#### Material Types
**Cellulosic Membranes:**
- Cuprophan, cellulose acetate
- Low biocompatibility
- Complement activation
- Rarely used today

**Synthetic Membranes:**
- Polysulfone, polyamide, PMMA
- High biocompatibility
- High flux capability
- Standard of care

**Novel Membrane Technologies (2020-2024):**
**Medium Cut-Off (MCO) Membranes:**
- **Theranova dialyzers** (Baxter)
- Enhanced middle molecule clearance (5-60 kDa range)
- Preserves albumin (minimal albumin loss)
- **Indications:**
  - Improved β2-microglobulin removal (40-60% vs high-flux)
  - Enhanced clearance of inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α)
  - Potential reduction in cardiovascular mortality
- **CONVINCE Trial (2023):**
  - MCO vs high-flux HD in 1360 patients
  - Primary outcome: All-cause mortality or cardiovascular events
  - Results: Trend toward benefit (ongoing analysis)

**Expanded Hemodialysis (HDx):**
- Uses MCO membranes with standard HD equipment
- No substitution fluid required (vs HDF)
- Simplified technique
- Cost-effective middle molecule clearance

#### Membrane Characteristics
**Surface Area:**
- Range: 0.8-2.5 m²
- Larger area = higher clearance
- Selected based on patient size
- Urea reduction ratio target

**Flux Characteristics:**
**Low-Flux:**
- Kuf <10 mL/hour/mmHg
- Limited middle molecule clearance
- Shorter treatment times
- Less expensive

**High-Flux:**
- Kuf >20 mL/hour/mmHg
- Enhanced middle molecule clearance
- Longer treatment times
- Better outcomes

#### Biocompatibility
**Complement Activation:**
- C3a, C5a generation
- Leukocyte activation
- Cytokine release
- Clinical symptoms

**Modern Membranes:**
- Minimal complement activation
- Reduced inflammatory response
- Better tolerance
- Improved outcomes

### Hemodiafiltration (HDF) - Advanced Modality

#### Online Hemodiafiltration
**Mechanism:**
- Combines diffusion (dialysis) and convection (filtration)
- Large volumes of ultrapure substitution fluid
- Enhanced middle molecule clearance
- Superior to conventional HD

**Post-Dilution HDF (Standard):**
- Substitution fluid added after dialyzer
- Maximum middle molecule clearance
- Convection volume: 20-25 L per session
- Most common HDF technique

**Pre-Dilution HDF:**
- Substitution fluid added before dialyzer
- Better for high hematocrit patients
- Reduced filtration fraction
- Lower clearance efficiency

#### Clinical Benefits (2024 Evidence)
**CONVINCE Trial (2023):**
- 1360 incident HD patients randomized to HDF vs high-flux HD
- **Primary outcome:** All-cause mortality or cardiovascular events
- **Results:** 23% reduction in primary outcome (HR 0.77, p=0.005)
- **Secondary outcomes:**
  - 28% reduction in all-cause mortality
  - Improved intradialytic tolerability
  - Better phosphate control

**Meta-Analysis Data (2020-2024):**
- Mortality benefit with convection volume >23 L/session
- 20-30% reduction in cardiovascular mortality
- Improved quality of life
- Reduced β2-microglobulin-related amyloidosis

**Current Recommendations (2024):**
- Consider HDF for patients with:
  - Intradialytic hypotension
  - High phosphate burden
  - Long-term dialysis vintage (>5 years)
  - High cardiovascular risk
- Requires high-flux dialyzers and ultrapure dialysate

## 2. In-Center Hemodialysis Protocols

### Standard Prescription Parameters

#### Treatment Frequency
**Thrice Weekly (Standard):**
- Monday-Wednesday-Friday or Tuesday-Thursday-Saturday
- 3.5-4.5 hours per treatment
- Most common schedule
- Medicare coverage standard

**Twice Weekly:**
- Limited to residual renal function
- Kt/V >2.0 weekly
- Close monitoring required
- Transitional option

**More Frequent:**
- 4-6 times weekly
- Better outcomes in selected patients
- Higher cost
- Patient burden

#### Treatment Time
**Standard Duration:**
- 3.5-4.5 hours
- Based on Kt/V targets
- Patient tolerance
- Vascular access flow

**Extended Time:**
- 5-8 hours
- Better phosphate control
- Improved middle molecule clearance
- Nocturnal dialysis

#### Blood Flow Rate
**Standard Rates:**
- 250-500 mL/minute
- Based on vascular access
- Patient tolerance
- Dialysis machine capabilities

**High Flow:**
- >500 mL/minute
- Requires excellent access
- Enhanced clearance
- Higher recirculation risk

#### Dialysate Flow Rate
**Standard Rates:**
- 500-800 mL/minute
- 1:1 to 1:2 ratio with blood flow
- Affects clearance efficiency
- Machine dependent

**High Flow:**
- 800-1200 mL/minute
- Diminishing returns >800
- Increased dialysate consumption
- Limited benefit

### Dialysate Composition

#### Standard Composition
**Sodium:**
- 138-142 mEq/L (individualized)
- Affects intradialytic hemodynamics
- Sodium modeling protocols
- Patient symptoms

**Potassium:**
- 2.0-4.0 mEq/L (individualized)
- Based on pre-dialysis K+
- Cardiac considerations
- Medication effects

**Calcium:**
- 2.5-3.5 mEq/L (1.25-1.75 mmol/L)
- Affects bone metabolism
- Vascular calcification risk
- PTH management

**Magnesium:**
- 0.5-1.0 mEq/L
- Neuromuscular effects
- Cardiac stability
- Often individualized

**Bicarbonate:**
- 35-38 mEq/L
- Acid-base correction
- Individualized based on labs
- Monitoring required

#### Individualized Prescriptions
**Sodium Modeling:**
- Stepwise or linear profiles
- Reduce intradialytic symptoms
- Prevent hypotension
- Improve tolerance

**Potassium Adjustment:**
- Based on pre-dialysis levels
- Cardiac arrhythmia risk
- Medication interactions
- Dietary considerations

**Calcium Modification:**
- Bone disease management
- Vascular calcification prevention
- PTH target ranges
- Patient symptoms

### Adequacy Monitoring

#### Kt/V Calculation
**Single Pool Kt/V:**
- Standard adequacy measure
- Target >1.2 per treatment
- Urea kinetic modeling
- Monthly monitoring

**Equilibrated Kt/V:**
- More accurate measure
- Accounts for rebound
- Target >1.0
- Research applications

**Urea Reduction Ratio (URR):**
- Simpler calculation
- Target >65%
- Less accurate than Kt/V
- Historical measure

#### Monitoring Schedule
**Monthly:**
- Pre- and post-dialysis BUN
- Kt/V calculation
- URR calculation
- Treatment time compliance

**Quarterly:**
- Dialysis delivery review
- Access flow measurements
- Recirculation studies
- Prescription optimization

#### Factors Affecting Adequacy
**Patient Factors:**
- Vascular access flow
- Blood pressure stability
- Treatment adherence
- Protein catabolic rate

**Technical Factors:**
- Blood flow rate
- Dialysate flow rate
- Membrane surface area
- Treatment time

**Operational Factors:**
- Needle placement
- Access recirculation
- Machine calibration
- Staff technique

## 5. Dialysis Prescription & Adequacy

### Prescription Optimization

#### Individualized Prescriptions
**Body Size Considerations:**
- V-based dosing (Kt/V)
- Surface area adjustments
- Gender differences
- Nutritional status

**Residual Renal Function:**
- Incorporate into Kt/V calculations
- Adjust prescription accordingly
- Monitor decline over time
- Preserve function when possible

#### Volume Management
**Ultrafiltration Goals:**
- Individualized target weights
- Gradual weight changes
- Interdialytic weight gain monitoring
- Clinical assessment

**UF Rate Limitations:**
- <10-13 mL/kg/hour recommended
- Higher rates increase mortality
- Patient symptoms guide limits
- Consider longer treatment time

**Novel Volume Assessment Technologies (2024):**

**Blood Volume Analysis (BVA - Daxor System):**
- **FDA-cleared** technology for precise blood volume measurement
- **Mechanism:** I-131 albumin tracer dilution method
- **Clinical Applications in Hemodialysis:**
  - Objective dry weight determination
  - Distinguish true volume overload from poor cardiac function
  - Guide ultrafiltration prescription
  - Reduce intradialytic hypotension episodes
- **Procedure:**
  - 5-minute test performed pre-dialysis
  - Measures total blood volume, plasma volume, RBC mass
  - Compares to ideal blood volume (based on height, weight, gender)
  - Results guide individualized UF targets
- **Clinical Impact:**
  - Reduces trial-and-error dry weight adjustments
  - Prevents both under- and over-ultrafiltration
  - Particularly valuable in:
    - Frequent intradialytic hypotension
    - Residual volume overload despite UF
    - Cardiovascular instability
    - New dialysis initiations

**Bioimpedance Spectroscopy (BCM - Body Composition Monitor):**
- **Fresenius BCM (Body Composition Monitor)**
- Non-invasive measurement of:
  - Extracellular water (ECW)
  - Intracellular water (ICW)
  - Total body water (TBW)
  - Overhydration status
- **Clinical Use:**
  - Objective fluid status assessment
  - Target weight optimization
  - Longitudinal tracking
- **BISTRO Trial (2023):**
  - Bioimpedance-guided fluid management
  - Reduced intradialytic hypotension by 40%
  - Improved blood pressure control
  - Lower cardiovascular events

**Relative Blood Volume Monitoring:**
- Real-time hematocrit measurement during dialysis
- Crit-Line monitor or integrated machine sensors
- Predicts hypotension risk
- Enables biofeedback UF control

### Adequacy Targets

#### Minimum Standards
**Kt/V Targets:**
- Single pool Kt/V ≥1.2 per treatment
- Weekly Kt/V ≥3.6 for thrice weekly
- Higher targets for home/nocturnal
- Individualize based on clinical status

**URR Targets:**
- ≥65% per treatment
- ≥70% for high-flux dialysis
- Monitor trends over time
- Correlate with clinical outcomes

#### Optimal Targets
**High-Flux Dialysis:**
- Kt/V ≥1.4
- β2-microglobulin clearance >20 mL/minute
- Middle molecule removal
- Improved outcomes

**Nocturnal/Home Dialysis:**
- Weekly Kt/V ≥5.0
- Standardized Kt/V ≥2.3
- Superior outcomes
- Near-normal physiology

## 6. Vascular Access Management

#### Access Types
**Arteriovenous Fistula (AVF):**
- Preferred access (Fistula First)
- Highest patency rates
- Lowest infection rates
- 6-8 weeks maturation time

**Arteriovenous Graft (AVG):**
- Synthetic material (PTFE, ePTFE)
- Faster maturation (2-3 weeks)
- Higher complication rates
- Secondary choice

**Central Venous Catheter (CVC):**
- Tunneled vs non-tunneled
- Highest infection rates
- Highest thrombosis rates
- Catheter Last initiative

### Novel Vascular Access Technologies (2020-2024)

#### Endovascular AVF Creation
**WavelinQ EndoAVF System (Becton Dickinson):**
- **FDA-approved** 2018, expanded use 2020-2024
- Percutaneous AVF creation (radial or ulnar artery to vein)
- **Procedure:**
  - Ultrasound-guided arterial and venous access
  - Thermal resistance wire creates anastomosis
  - Outpatient procedure (1-2 hours)
  - Local anesthesia only
- **Advantages:**
  - No surgical incision
  - Reduced periprocedural pain
  - Same-day discharge
  - Preservation of surgical sites
- **Maturation:**
  - 4-8 weeks typical maturation time
  - Maturation rates: 60-75%
  - May require angioplasty for maturation
- **Outcomes (2020-2024 Data):**
  - 6-month patency: 70-80%
  - 12-month patency: 60-70%
  - Lower maturation rates than surgical AVF but improving
- **Indications:**
  - Patients with suitable anatomy
  - Desire to avoid surgery
  - Failed prior surgical attempts
  - Preservation of surgical options

**Ellipsys Vascular Access System (Avenu Medical):**
- **FDA-approved** 2018
- Percutaneous arteriovenous anastomosis
- Proximal radial artery to perforating vein
- **Technique:**
  - Ultrasound-guided catheter placement
  - Radiofrequency energy creates anastomosis
  - No thermal resistance wire (vs WavelinQ)
  - Pure percutaneous approach
- **Advantages:**
  - More proximal location (less steal risk)
  - Preserves radial artery for future use
  - Quick procedure (30-45 minutes)
- **Clinical Data (2024):**
  - NEAT study: 95% technical success
  - 12-month patency: 75-85%
  - Lower intervention rates vs WavelinQ
  - Excellent safety profile

#### Novel Graft Materials
**Early Cannulation Grafts:**
- Acuseal (Gore), Flixene (Atrium)
- Can cannulate 24-72 hours post-placement
- Immediate dialysis capability
- Higher cost but reduce catheter dependence

**Heparin-Bonded Grafts:**
- Propaten (Gore) - heparin-bonded ePTFE
- Reduced early thrombosis
- Improved patency rates
- 20-30% reduction in interventions

#### Advanced Catheter Technologies
**Antimicrobial Lock Solutions (2024 Protocols):**
- **Taurolidine-Citrate (TauroLock):**
  - Broad-spectrum antimicrobial
  - Anticoagulant properties
  - Reduces CRBSI by 60-80%
  - FDA-cleared, widely used
- **Ethanol Locks:**
  - 70% ethanol solution
  - Highly effective against biofilms
  - 70-90% reduction in catheter infections
  - May damage catheter materials (check compatibility)
- **Citrate-Methylene Blue-Parabens (C-MB-P):**
  - Antibiotic-free antimicrobial lock
  - Reduces CRBSI rates
  - Preserves catheter integrity

**Novel Catheter Designs:**
- Palindrome catheter (symmetric tips, reduced recirculation)
- VectorFlow catheter (optimized flow dynamics)
- Surface modifications (antimicrobial coatings)

#### Access Creation
**Pre-operative Evaluation:**
- Vein mapping with ultrasound
- Physical examination
- Central venous stenosis assessment
- Patient education

**Surgical Techniques:**
- Radiocephalic fistula (wrist)
- Brachiocephalic fistula (elbow)
- Brachiobasilic transposition
- Graft placement techniques

#### Maturation Assessment
**Maturation Criteria (Rule of 6s):**
- Flow >600 mL/minute
- Diameter >6 mm
- Depth <6 mm
- Length >6 cm for cannulation

**Maturation Time:**
- 6-8 weeks minimum
- May require 12+ weeks
- 20-30% fail to mature
- Intervention may be needed

#### Access Surveillance
**Monitoring Techniques:**
- Physical examination (monthly)
- Flow measurements (quarterly)
- Pressure monitoring
- Duplex ultrasound

**Intervention Thresholds:**
- Flow <500-600 mL/minute
- Recirculation >10-15%
- Static pressure ratios >0.5
- Clinical stenosis signs

#### Complications Management
**Stenosis:**
- Percutaneous angioplasty
- Stent placement if needed
- Surgical revision if recurrent
- Surveillance post-intervention

**Thrombosis:**
- Mechanical thrombectomy
- Pharmacologic thrombolysis
- Surgical thrombectomy
- Access revision if recurrent

**Infection:**
- Catheter removal (mandatory)
- Long-term antibiotics
- Graft infection may require removal
- Fistula infections rare but serious

## 7. Complications Management

### Intradialytic Complications

#### Hypotension
**Causes:**
- Rapid ultrafiltration
- Autonomic dysfunction
- Dialysate sodium too low
- Antihypertensive medications

**Prevention:**
- Sodium modeling
- Cool dialysate (35-36°C)
- Gradual UF rate
- Medication timing

**Treatment:**
- Reduce or stop UF
- Administer saline bolus
- Trendelenburg position
- Reduce blood flow

#### Muscle Cramps
**Etiology:**
- Volume depletion
- Electrolyte shifts
- Rapid ultrafiltration
- Hypoosmolality

**Prevention:**
- Adequate sodium modeling
- Stretching exercises
- Quinine (limited use)
- Optimize dry weight

**Treatment:**
- Hypertonic saline
- Hypertonic glucose
- Calcium administration
- Massage/stretching

#### Nausea and Vomiting
**Causes:**
- Hypotension
- Disequilibrium syndrome
- Anxiety
- Medication side effects

**Management:**
- Treat underlying cause
- Antiemetics (ondansetron)
- Reduce blood flow
- Anxiety management

#### Disequilibrium Syndrome
**Pathophysiology:**
- Rapid urea removal
- Cerebral edema
- Osmotic gradients
- Increased intracranial pressure

**Risk Factors:**
- First treatments
- High BUN levels
- Elderly patients
- Pediatric patients

**Prevention:**
- Gradual initiation
- Shorter initial treatments
- Higher dialysate sodium
- Close monitoring

**Treatment:**
- Mannitol infusion
- Hypertonic saline
- Reduce treatment intensity
- Supportive care

### Access Complications

#### Infiltration/Extravasation
**Prevention:**
- Proper needle placement
- Secure fixation
- Regular site assessment
- Patient education

**Management:**
- Immediate needle removal
- Elevate extremity
- Cold compresses
- Monitor for compartment syndrome

#### Stenosis
**Detection:**
- Decreased clearance
- High venous pressures
- Prolonged bleeding
- Access flow changes

**Treatment:**
- Angioplasty (PTA)
- Stent placement
- Surgical revision
- Access creation at new site

#### Thrombosis
**Prevention:**
- Adequate anticoagulation
- Proper cannulation technique
- Avoid prolonged compression
- Treat stenosis early

**Management:**
- Urgent declotting
- Thrombolytic therapy
- Surgical thrombectomy
- Access revision

### Long-term Complications

#### Amyloidosis
**Pathophysiology:**
- β2-microglobulin accumulation
- Bone and joint deposition
- Carpal tunnel syndrome
- Arthropathy

**Prevention:**
- High-flux dialysis
- Hemodiafiltration
- Adequate clearance
- Consider transplantation

#### Cardiovascular Disease
**Risk Factors:**
- Volume overload
- Hypertension
- Mineral bone disease
- Inflammation

**Prevention:**
- Optimal volume control
- Blood pressure management
- Mineral metabolism control
- Anti-inflammatory strategies

#### Anemia Management (2024 Update)

**Traditional Erythropoiesis-Stimulating Agents (ESAs):**
- Epoetin alfa, darbepoetin alfa
- Subcutaneous or IV administration
- Target hemoglobin: 10-11.5 g/dL
- Higher targets increase cardiovascular risk

**HIF-Prolyl Hydroxylase Inhibitors (HIF-PHIs) - FDA 2023-2024:**
**Novel oral anemia agents now available for dialysis patients**

**Roxadustat (Evrenzo):**
- **FDA Approval:** February 2024 for anemia in CKD (including dialysis)
- **Mechanism:** Inhibits HIF-prolyl hydroxylase, stabilizes HIF
  - Stimulates endogenous EPO production
  - Improves iron utilization
  - Increases iron absorption
- **Dosing (Dialysis Patients):**
  - Start: 70 mg TID (weight-based dosing)
  - Titrate to hemoglobin target (10-11.5 g/dL)
  - Maximum: 120 mg TID
- **Clinical Trials:**
  - SIERRAS (2021): Non-inferior to darbepoetin for Hgb maintenance
  - Reduced IV iron requirements by 30-40%

**Daprodustat (Jesduvroq):**
- **FDA Approval:** February 2024 for anemia in CKD on dialysis
- **Dosing:** 2-4 mg once daily (titrate to Hgb target)
- **Advantages:** Once-daily oral dosing
- **ASCEND Trials (2021):**
  - Non-inferior to ESA for Hgb correction and maintenance
  - Similar cardiovascular safety profile

**Vadadustat (Vafseo):** ⭐ **FDA APPROVED MARCH 26, 2024**
- **FDA Approval:** March 26, 2024 for anemia in adults with CKD on dialysis
- **Mechanism:** Hypoxia-inducible factor prolyl hydroxylase (HIF-PH) inhibitor - oral alternative to ESAs
- **Dosing:**
  - Starting dose: 300 mg PO once daily
  - Titration: Adjust in 300 mg increments every 4 weeks based on Hgb response
  - Maximum: 600 mg once daily
  - Take without regard to meals
- **PRO2TECT Trials (2021-2023):**
  - PRO2TECT-DD (dialysis): Non-inferior to darbepoetin for Hgb maintenance
  - Effective for Hgb correction and maintenance
  - 20-30% reduction in IV iron requirements vs ESAs
  - Similar cardiovascular safety profile to darbepoetin
- **Advantages over ESAs:**
  - Oral administration (convenient, no injections)
  - Reduced IV iron needs (improves iron utilization)
  - More physiologic EPO stimulation
  - May benefit ESA-hyporesponsive patients
- **Monitoring:**
  - Hemoglobin every 2 weeks until stable, then monthly
  - Iron studies (TSAT, ferritin) monthly
  - Monitor for polycythemia (dose reduce if Hgb >12 g/dL)
- **Drug Interactions:**
  - CYP2C8 inhibitors (gemfibrozil, clopidogrel) - may increase vadadustat levels
  - CYP2C8 inducers (rifampin) - may decrease vadadustat levels
- **Cost:** Comparable to ESAs (~$300-400/month)

**Clinical Use of HIF-PHIs (2024 Guidelines):**
- **Advantages:**
  - Oral administration (vs IV/SC ESAs)
  - Reduced IV iron requirements
  - More physiologic EPO production
  - Potentially lower cost
- **Considerations:**
  - Requires good medication adherence
  - Drug interactions (CYP2C8 inhibitors/inducers)
  - Monitor for polycythemia
- **Patient Selection:**
  - Preference for oral therapy
  - Difficult IV access
  - High IV iron requirements
  - ESA hyporesponsiveness

**Iron Management (2024):**
- **IV Iron Formulations:**
  - Iron sucrose (Venofer)
  - Sodium ferric gluconate (Ferrlecit)
  - Iron dextran (INFeD, Dexferrum)
  - Ferumoxytol (Feraheme)
  - Ferric carboxymaltose (Injectafer)
- **Targets:**
  - Transferrin saturation (TSAT): 20-50%
  - Ferritin: 200-500 ng/mL
- **Monitoring:** Monthly iron studies
- **Ferric Citrate (Auryxia):**
  - Dual-action: phosphate binder + iron supplement
  - Increases hemoglobin and iron stores
  - Reduces ESA/HIF-PHI requirements
  - See Document 12 (Renal Pharmacology)

#### Mineral Bone Disease Management (2024)

**Phosphate Binders (Hemodialysis-Specific Management):**

**Calcium-Based Binders:**
- **Calcium carbonate (Tums, generic)**
  - Dosing: 500-1000 mg elemental calcium with meals TID
  - **HD Consideration**: May be adequate in early dialysis with residual renal function
  - Risk: Hypercalcemia, vascular calcification (especially with high-calcium dialysate)
  - Limit: <1500 mg elemental calcium/day from binders
- **Calcium acetate (PhosLo, Phoslyra)**
  - Dosing: 667-1334 mg (2-4 tablets) with meals TID
  - More efficient than carbonate (better phosphate binding per mg calcium)
  - **HD Consideration**: Monitor calcium-phosphate product, avoid if >55
  - Elemental calcium: 169 mg per 667 mg tablet

**Non-Calcium-Based Binders (Preferred in HD):**
- **Sevelamer hydrochloride (Renagel)**
  - Dosing: 800-1600 mg TID with meals (typical HD patient needs 2400-4800 mg/day)
  - **HD Advantage**: No calcium loading, reduces vascular calcification progression
  - **HD Disadvantage**: May worsen metabolic acidosis (need higher dialysate bicarbonate)
  - Pill burden: 6-12 tablets/day (major adherence issue)
  - Lowers LDL cholesterol 15-30%
- **Sevelamer carbonate (Renvela)**
  - Dosing: Same as hydrochloride (800-1600 mg TID)
  - **PREFERRED over hydrochloride** in HD (does not cause acidosis)
  - Same pill burden and GI side effects
  - **HD Consideration**: Better choice if patient has low pre-dialysis bicarbonate
- **Lanthanum carbonate (Fosrenol)**
  - Dosing: 500-1000 mg elemental lanthanum TID with meals
  - Chewable tablets (may improve adherence)
  - **HD Consideration**: Not removed by dialysis, long-term tissue deposition unknown
  - Moderate pill burden (3-6 tablets/day)
  - GI upset common (nausea, vomiting)

**Iron-Based Binders (Dual Benefit in HD):**
- **Ferric citrate (Auryxia)**
  - Dosing: 210 mg ferric iron (1-2 tablets) TID, titrate up to 12 tablets/day
  - **HD Advantage**: Phosphate binding + iron supplementation (reduces IV iron needs)
  - **HD Benefit**: May increase hemoglobin, decrease ESA requirements
  - Monitor: Ferritin, TSAT (avoid iron overload - target ferritin <500 ng/mL in HD)
  - Side effects: Black stools (expected), GI upset
  - Cost: $$$$
- **Sucroferric oxyhydroxide (Velphoro)**
  - Dosing: 500 mg TID with meals
  - **Lowest pill burden** of all binders (3 tablets/day total)
  - **HD Advantage**: Best adherence due to low pill count
  - Excellent phosphate binding capacity
  - Also provides iron supplementation
  - Cost: $$$$$ (most expensive, but best adherence)

**Novel Mechanisms:**
- **Tenapanor (Xphosah)**
  - **FDA Approved**: September 2023 specifically for hyperphosphatemia in adults with CKD on dialysis
  - Dosing: 10 mg PO BID (only 2 tablets/day)
  - Mechanism: NHE3 inhibitor (NOT a binder - inhibits intestinal phosphate absorption)
  - **HD Advantage**: Very low pill burden, can be added to existing binders
  - **HD Consideration**: Diarrhea in 30% (may worsen intradialytic hypotension if dehydrated)
  - Efficacy: 30-40% phosphorus reduction
  - Cost: $$$$$ (very expensive)
  - **Board Relevance**: Know it's FDA-approved ONLY for dialysis patients (not pre-dialysis CKD)

**Magnesium-Based Binders:**
- **Magnesium carbonate (generic)**
  - Dosing: 200-400 mg elemental magnesium TID
  - **HD Consideration**: Risk of hypermagnesemia depends on dialysate magnesium concentration
  - Use ONLY with low-magnesium dialysate (0.5 mEq/L)
  - Monitor serum magnesium closely (keep <3.0 mg/dL)
  - Advantages: Cheap, laxative effect
  - **Generally AVOID in HD** unless dialysate magnesium controlled
- **Magnesium hydroxide (Milk of Magnesia)**
  - Similar profile to magnesium carbonate
  - **Avoid in HD** - hypermagnesemia risk

**Aluminum-Based Binders (HISTORICAL - AVOID):**
- **Aluminum hydroxide (Amphojel, AlternaGEL)** ⚠️ **DO NOT USE**
  - **Historical Context**: Widely used in HD 1970s-1980s, caused epidemic of dialysis dementia
  - Most potent phosphate binder but **SEVERE TOXICITY**
  - **Classic Toxicity Triad**:
    1. **CNS**: Dialysis dementia (stuttering speech, myoclonus, seizures, dementia)
    2. **Bone**: Osteomalacia, adynamic bone disease, fractures (aluminum blocks bone mineralization)
    3. **Blood**: Microcytic anemia (interferes with heme synthesis)
  - **Why Worse in HD**: Aluminum accumulates rapidly, dialysis clearance inadequate
  - **Diagnosis**: Serum aluminum >60 mcg/L (normal <10 mcg/L), DFO test, bone biopsy (gold standard)
  - **Treatment**: Deferoxamine (DFO) chelation + high-flux dialysis
  - **Rare Indication**: Short-term (2-4 weeks) for life-threatening acute hyperphosphatemia ONLY
  - **Board Exam**: Know aluminum toxicity triad, diagnosis (serum level + bone biopsy), treatment (DFO)

**Hemodialysis-Specific Binder Selection Strategy (2024):**
1. **Preferred First-Line**: Non-calcium binders (sevelamer carbonate, lanthanum, sucroferric)

### Wearable & Portable Dialysis Technologies (2024 Update) ⭐

#### Overview: Paradigm Shift in Dialysis Delivery

**Current Paradigm:**
- Conventional hemodialysis: 3× weekly, 3.5-4 hours, tethered to machine
- Home HD: More frequent/longer, but still requires dedicated space, plumbing, water treatment
- Peritoneal dialysis: Portable but different clearance profile, technique failure

**Emerging Paradigm:**
- **Wearable dialysis**: Continuous or prolonged dialysis while ambulatory
- **Miniaturized portable systems**: Battery-powered, no dedicated plumbing required
- **Implantable bioartificial kidney**: Living cell-based filtration
- **Vision**: "Normalize" dialysis—continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) for chronic patients

**Rationale for Wearable/Portable Dialysis:**
1. **Physiologic**: Native kidneys work 24/7, not 12 hours/week
   - Continuous dialysis provides more consistent solute/volume control
   - Reduced interdialytic fluctuations (BP swings, fluid overload, uremic toxin buildup)
2. **Quality of Life**: Freedom from clinic-based schedules, travel limitations
3. **Clinical Outcomes**: Potential for reduced mortality, better volume control, improved cardiovascular outcomes
4. **Economic**: Reduced hospitalization, facility costs

#### Wearable Artificial Kidney (WAK)

**Developer**: University of Washington/University of California, Los Angeles (Dr. Victor Gura)

**Design Concept:**
- **Wearable belt-mounted device** (4-5 kg total weight)
- Continuous 24-hour dialysis with ambulatory capability
- Miniaturized dialysis components:
  - Peristaltic blood pump
  - Single-pass dialysate system (no water treatment infrastructure)
  - Sorbent-based dialysate regeneration (vs. conventional batch dialysate)
  - Rechargeable battery power (8-10 hour battery life)
  - Vascular access via standard fistula or catheter

**Technical Specifications:**
- **Blood flow rate**: 80-120 mL/minute (lower than conventional HD)
- **Dialysate flow rate**: 60-100 mL/minute
- **Weight**: 4-5 kg when fully loaded
- **Dialysate volume**: 300-400 mL total (sorbent regeneration recycles)
- **Treatment time**: Continuous 24 hours or 8-12 hours daily

**Sorbent-Based Dialysate Regeneration (Key Innovation):**
- **Zirconium phosphate**: Removes ammonium (from urea breakdown by urease)
- **Urease**: Converts urea → ammonium + CO2
- **Activated charcoal**: Removes middle molecules, organic solutes
- **Hydrated zirconium oxide**: Removes phosphate
- **Total dialysate consumption**: 300-400 mL/day (vs. 120 L for conventional HD)
- Eliminates need for water treatment system

**Clinical Trials:**
- **Pilot Study (2012-2015):** 7 patients, 8-hour continuous WAK therapy
  - Adequate solute clearance (Kt/V 0.8-1.0 per 8 hours)
  - Good patient tolerance
  - Freedom of movement while dialyzing
  - Technical issues: Battery life, pump reliability
- **Phase II Trial (2016-2020):** Suspended due to adverse events
  - Several serious adverse events (SAEs) including hypotension, access issues
  - Redesign required for safety improvements
  - FDA placed clinical hold pending design modifications

**Current Status (2024):**
- **Clinical development PAUSED** (2018-2024)
- Safety concerns (hypotension, air embolism risks) led to FDA clinical hold
- Redesign underway focusing on:
  - Improved safety monitoring (pressure sensors, air detection)
  - Better battery life (target: 24-hour continuous)
  - Miniaturization (target weight: <3 kg)
  - User interface simplification
- **Anticipated Timeline**: Phase II trials resume 2025-2026 (pending FDA approval)

**Challenges:**
- Safety: Air embolism detection, blood leak detection, hypotension monitoring
- Battery life: Extending to full 24-hour operation
- Miniaturization: Reducing weight/bulk for comfortable wearing
- Cost: Manufacturing scalability, reimbursement model
- Training: Patient/caregiver education for troubleshooting

**Potential Advantages (When/If Approved):**
- Continuous dialysis (mimics native kidney function)
- Ambulatory during treatment (work, travel, exercise)
- Improved quality of life
- Better hemodynamic stability (gentle continuous UF)
- Reduced dietary restrictions (continuous clearance)

#### AWAK (Automated Wearable Artificial Kidney)

**Developer**: AWAK Technologies (Singapore), FDA Breakthrough Device Designation 2019

**Design Concept:**
- **Sorbent-based peritoneal dialysis (PD) device**
  - Hybrid approach: Uses peritoneal membrane for clearance
  - Sorbent regeneration of PD fluid (vs. discarding bags)
  - Wearable backpack design (weight: ~2 kg)
  - 8-12 hour daytime or nighttime wear

**Technical Specifications:**
- **Dialysate volume**: 200 mL dwell volume (vs. conventional PD 2 L bags)
- **Treatment time**: 8-12 hours continuous
- **Dialysate regeneration**: Sorbent cartridge regenerates spent PD fluid
  - Urease, zirconium phosphate, activated charcoal (similar to WAK)
  - Total dialysate consumption: 200-500 mL/treatment (vs. 8-12 L for conventional PD)
- **Battery-powered**: 8-12 hour battery life
- **Peritoneal catheter**: Standard Tenckhoff catheter (no new implantation required)

**Mechanism:**
- PD fluid dwells in peritoneal cavity (small volume, 200 mL)
- Device continuously recirculates PD fluid through sorbent cartridge
- Sorbent removes urea, creatinine, phosphate, middle molecules
- Regenerated PD fluid returned to peritoneal cavity
- **Advantages over conventional PD**:
  - Low dialysate volume (reduces peritoneal membrane stress)
  - Portable (no bags, can wear during work/activities)
  - Reduced environmental waste (vs. 8-12 L bags/day)

**Clinical Trials:**
- **AWAK PD Trial (2018-2020):** 10 patients, Singapore
  - Adequate solute clearance (Kt/V 2.0-2.5 weekly)
  - Good peritoneal membrane preservation
  - Patient satisfaction high (portability, reduced bag handling)
  - Technical issues: Sorbent cartridge clogging (fibrin, protein)
- **US Phase II Trial (2022-2024):** ONGOING
  - FDA Breakthrough Device Designation (expedited review)
  - Target enrollment: 30 patients
  - Primary endpoint: Safety, adequate clearance
  - Expected completion: Late 2024/Early 2025

**Current Status (2024):**
- **Phase II trials ONGOING** in United States and Singapore
- FDA Breakthrough Device Designation (2019) - expedited development pathway
- Anticipated FDA approval timeline: 2025-2027 (if trials successful)

**Potential Advantages:**
- Wearable during work, travel, daily activities
- Reduced PD fluid consumption (environmental benefit)
- Lower peritoneal membrane stress (small dwell volumes)
- No need for home water treatment system
- Preserves peritoneal membrane function longer

**Challenges:**
- Sorbent cartridge longevity (clogging with protein/fibrin)
- Peritoneal catheter complications (infection, malposition)
- Cost of sorbent cartridges (disposable vs. reusable)
- Reimbursement model unclear

#### Implantable Bioartificial Kidney

**Developer**: The Kidney Project (UCSF/Vanderbilt University) - Dr. Shuvo Roy & Dr. William Fissell

**Design Concept:**
- **Fully implantable device** (no external components)
- Two-stage design:
  1. **Hemofilter** (silicon nanopore membranes): Filters blood using patient's blood pressure (no pump)
  2. **Bioreactor** (living kidney cells): Reabsorbs water, salts, glucose (mimics tubular function)
- **Power source**: Patient's blood pressure drives filtration (no external power/batteries)
- **Goal**: Portable artificial kidney replacing dialysis and preventing transplant need

**Technical Specifications:**
- **Silicon nanopore membranes**:
  - 5-20 nanometer pores (size-selective filtration)
  - Filters small/middle molecules (urea, creatinine, β2-microglobulin)
  - Retains albumin, blood cells, large proteins
  - No anticoagulation required (hemocompatible surface)
- **Bioreactor**:
  - Living renal epithelial cells (proximal tubule cells)
  - Reabsorb 99% of filtered water, salts, glucose
  - Secrete erythropoietin, vitamin D
  - Regulate acid-base balance
- **Hemodynamics**:
  - Arterial-venous anastomosis (similar to AV fistula)
  - Blood pressure gradient drives filtration (no pump needed)
  - Target GFR: 20-30 mL/minute (sufficient to eliminate dialysis)

**Mechanism:**
1. **Filtration Stage**: Blood flows through silicon nanopore hemofilter
   - Small solutes (urea, creatinine, toxins) filtered
   - Albumin, blood cells retained
   - Ultrafiltrate (similar to glomerular filtrate) produced
2. **Bioreactor Stage**: Living kidney cells reabsorb water/electrolytes
   - Proximal tubule cells reabsorb 99% of water, glucose, sodium
   - Secretes useful hormones (EPO, calcitriol)
   - Final urine output: ~1-2 L/day (via catheter to bladder or collection device)

**Current Status (2024):**
- **Pre-clinical testing phase**
- Successfully demonstrated:
  - Silicon nanopore membranes are biocompatible (no clotting, no anticoagulation needed)
  - Membranes retain albumin while clearing uremic toxins
  - Living kidney cells survive and function in bioreactor for weeks-months
- **Challenges Remaining**:
  - Scaling to human-size device (current prototypes small-animal sized)
  - Long-term cell viability (keeping cells alive for years)
  - Immune rejection of kidney cells (may need immunosuppression)
  - Surgical implantation technique
  - Urine drainage system (bladder connection vs. external collection)
- **Anticipated Timeline**:
  - Large animal trials (pigs): 2024-2026
  - Human Phase I trials: 2027-2030
  - FDA approval (if successful): 2030s

**Potential Advantages (Long-Term Vision):**
- **Complete freedom** from dialysis machines, clinics, schedules
- No immunosuppression (vs. kidney transplant—cells may be autologous or encapsulated)
- Continuous clearance (native kidney-like function)
- Hormonal functions (EPO, vitamin D production)
- No dietary/fluid restrictions
- Improved survival vs. dialysis (likely comparable to transplant)

**Challenges:**
- Surgical complexity (implantation requires vascular surgery)
- Cell source (autologous cells vs. donor cells vs. stem cell-derived)
- Immunosuppression needs unclear (if using allogeneic cells)
- Device failure: What happens if device fails? (backup dialysis plan required)
- Cost: Manufacturing, surgical implantation, long-term maintenance

- **Currently**: No reimbursement codes for wearable dialysis (devices not approved)
- **When Approved**: Likely bundled ESRD payment model
  - May be cost-neutral or savings vs. conventional HD (reduced hospitalization, better outcomes)
  - Initial costs high (device, training) but long-term savings anticipated
  - Home dialysis payment model (similar to PD/home HD)
- **Challenges**:
  - Establishing appropriate payment rates
  - Coverage of disposables (sorbent cartridges, batteries)
  - Training reimbursement (intensive training period)

**Commercial Insurance:**
- Will likely follow CMS lead
- May require prior authorization, medical necessity documentation
- Out-of-pocket costs for patients may be barrier

#### Clinical Trials & Evidence Base (2024)

**WAK Clinical Trials:**
- **Pilot Study (2012-2015)**: 7 patients, proof-of-concept
  - Adequate clearance, patient satisfaction high
  - Technical issues: Battery, pump reliability
- **Phase II (2016-2018)**: Suspended due to safety concerns
  - SAEs: Hypotension, access issues, air detection failures
  - FDA clinical hold (ongoing 2024)

**AWAK Clinical Trials:**
- **Singapore Pilot (2018-2020)**: 10 patients, 6-month follow-up
  - Kt/V 2.0-2.5 weekly (adequate)
  - Peritoneal membrane preservation
  - Sorbent cartridge clogging issues
- **US Phase II (2022-2024)**: ONGOING
  - Target: 30 patients
  - Primary endpoint: Safety, clearance adequacy
  - Expected results: Late 2024/Early 2025

**Implantable BAK:**
- **Pre-clinical only** (animal studies)
- Silicon membrane biocompatibility demonstrated
- Cell viability in bioreactor >3 months (animal models)
- Human trials not yet initiated

**Evidence Gaps:**
- No long-term (>1 year) safety data for any wearable device
- No randomized controlled trials comparing wearable vs conventional HD
- No survival data (only surrogate endpoints: Kt/V, QOL)
- Cardiovascular outcomes unknown

#### Future Directions & Paradigm Shift Implications

**Short-Term (2025-2027):**
- **AWAK** likely first wearable device to reach FDA approval (2025-2027 projected)
- Initial rollout to highly selected patients (motivated, stable, good access)
- Post-market surveillance for long-term safety/efficacy
- Gradual expansion of indications based on real-world evidence

**Medium-Term (2027-2030):**
- **WAK** redesign and Phase II/III trials (if safety issues resolved)
- Miniaturization advances (target weight <2 kg for wearable devices)
- Battery technology improvements (48-72 hour battery life)
- Sorbent cartridge longevity (reduce replacement frequency)
- **Implantable BAK** enters Phase I human trials (late 2020s)

**Long-Term (2030+):**
- **Fully implantable bioartificial kidney** becomes reality
  - Eliminates dialysis as we know it
  - Paradigm shift comparable to insulin pump for diabetes
- **Continuous renal replacement** becomes standard of care
  - Thrice-weekly HD viewed as "legacy" treatment
  - Improved cardiovascular outcomes, survival approaching transplant
- **Integration with telehealth**: AI-powered remote monitoring, predictive algorithms
- **Personalized dialysis**: Wearable sensors adjust clearance in real-time based on solute levels

**Impact on Nephrology Practice:**
- **Nephrologists**: Shift from "dialysis prescribers" to "device managers"
- **Dialysis centers**: Decline in utilization, shift to training centers
- **Transplant**: Wearable dialysis may reduce urgency for transplant (better QOL on dialysis)
- **Home dialysis growth**: Wearable devices accelerate shift away from in-center HD

**Barriers to Adoption:**
- **Safety**: Demonstrating long-term safety equivalent to conventional HD
- **Cost**: High upfront costs, unclear reimbursement
- **Training**: Intensive patient/caregiver education required
- **Regulatory**: FDA approval pathway for novel devices lengthy
- **Cultural**: Patient and provider comfort with new technology

**Key Take-Home Points: Wearable/Portable Dialysis** ⭐
1. **WAK** (Wearable Artificial Kidney): Continuous 24-hour HD, sorbent-based, currently on FDA hold
2. **AWAK** (Automated Wearable Artificial Kidney): Sorbent-based PD, Phase II trials, FDA Breakthrough Device
3. **Implantable Bioartificial Kidney**: Silicon membrane + living cells, pre-clinical, decades from approval
4. **NxStage/Tablo**: Currently available portable HD (not wearable, but travel-friendly)
5. **Paradigm shift**: Move toward continuous dialysis mimicking native kidney function
6. **Challenges**: Safety, battery life, cost, regulatory approval, reimbursement
7. **Timeline**: AWAK possible approval 2025-2027, WAK 2027-2030, Implantable BAK 2030s
8. **Future**: Wearable/implantable dialysis may transform ESRD care like insulin pumps transformed diabetes

## 11. Clinical Algorithms & Decision Trees

### Hypotension Management Algorithm

```
Intradialytic Hypotension (SBP drop >20 mmHg or symptoms)
        ↓
Immediate Actions:
- Reduce or stop ultrafiltration
- Place patient in Trendelenburg
- Reduce blood flow to 200 mL/min
- Administer 100-200 mL normal saline
        ↓
Assess Response (5-10 minutes)
        ↓
Persistent Hypotension:
- Check dialysate sodium (increase if <140)
- Reduce dialysate temperature to 35-36°C
- Consider vasopressor support
- Evaluate dry weight
        ↓
Prevention Strategies:
- Sodium modeling for next treatment
- Cool dialysate protocol
- Reduce UF rate (<10 mL/kg/hr)
- Review antihypertensive timing
```

### Access Troubleshooting Algorithm

```
High Venous Pressure (>250 mmHg) or Poor Clearance
        ↓
Immediate Assessment:
- Check needle position
- Assess for kinking
- Verify blood pump speed
- Review patient position
        ↓
Persistent Issues:
- Perform access recirculation study
- Check flow measurement
- Duplex ultrasound evaluation
- Consider stenosis
        ↓
Intervention:
- Refer for angiography
- Percutaneous angioplasty
- Stent placement if needed
- Surgical consultation if recurrent
```

## 12. Key Take-Home Points

1. **Adequacy Standards**: Maintain Kt/V ≥1.2 per treatment; monitor monthly
2. **Vascular Access**: Fistula First initiative; monitor for stenosis quarterly
3. **Volume Management**: UF rate <10-13 mL/kg/hour; individualize dry weight
4. **Complication Prevention**: Use sodium modeling and cool dialysate for hypotension
5. **Home Dialysis**: Consider for motivated patients; superior outcomes
6. **Water Quality**: Meet AAMI standards; monitor bacterial/endotoxin levels
7. **Quality Metrics**: Track QIP measures; implement continuous improvement
8. **Patient Education**: Essential for adherence and complication prevention
9. **Individualization**: Tailor prescriptions to patient needs and lifestyle
10. **Technology Integration**: Use monitoring systems to optimize treatments
