# Vascular Disorders - Comprehensive Clinical Guide

**Core Domain:** Clinical Nephrology I  
**Difficulty Level:** Intermediate-Advanced  
**Frequency of Use:** Weekly  
**Clinical Impact:** High  
**Regulatory Requirements:** None specific  
**Prerequisites:** Vascular physiology, hypertension management  
**Competency Development Time:** 12-18 months  
**Cross-references:** Hypertension (Domain I.3), AKI (Domain I.1), Transplantation (Domain III)

## 1. Renal Vascular Anatomy & Physiology

### Normal Renal Vasculature

#### Arterial Supply
**Renal Artery:**
- Single artery in 70% (multiple in 30%)
- Branches into segmental arteries
- Lateral to renal vein at hilum
- End arteries (no significant collaterals)

**Segmental Arteries:**
- 5 segmental branches
- Supply renal segments
- No anastomoses between segments
- Occlusion causes segmental infarction

**Interlobar Arteries:**
- Travel between renal pyramids
- Give off arcuate arteries
- Supply renal cortex and medulla

**Arcuate Arteries:**
- Arch over renal pyramids
- Give off interlobular arteries
- Supply cortical glomeruli

**Interlobular Arteries:**
- Give off afferent arterioles
- Supply glomerular tufts
- Site of juxtaglomerular apparatus

#### Microcirculation
**Afferent Arteriole:**
- Supplies glomerular capillaries
- Regulates glomerular pressure
- Site of prostaglandin production
- Target of ACE inhibitors

**Glomerular Capillaries:**
- High-pressure bed
- Site of filtration
- Unique endothelial structure
- Fenestrated without diaphragms

**Efferent Arteriole:**
- Drains glomerular capillaries
- Forms peritubular capillaries
- Regulates filtration pressure
- Site of angiotensin II action

**Peritubular Capillaries:**
- Surround tubules
- Reabsorb filtered substances
- Site of exchange processes
- Low-pressure, high-flow system

#### Venous Drainage
**Peritubular Venules:**
- Collect from peritubular capillaries
- Form interlobular veins
- Drain into arcuate veins

**Renal Vein:**
- Anterior to renal artery
- Drains into inferior vena cava
- Multiple anastomoses
- Thrombosis can cause renal infarction

### Autoregulation Mechanisms

#### Myogenic Response
- Stretch-activated vascular smooth muscle
- Responds to pressure changes
- Maintains constant blood flow
- Operates over MAP 80-180 mmHg

#### Tubuloglomerular Feedback
- Macula densa senses NaCl delivery
- Adenosine mediates afferent constriction
- Prostaglandins modulate response
- Maintains GFR constant

#### Hormonal Regulation
- Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system
- Sympathetic nervous system
- Antidiuretic hormone
- Natriuretic peptides

## 2. Renal Artery Stenosis (RAS)

### Epidemiology & Etiology

#### Prevalence
- General population: 1-5%
- Hypertensive patients: 15-30%
- Patients with CAD: 15-25%
- Dialysis patients: 5-15%
- More common in elderly males

#### Etiologic Classification
**Atherosclerotic RAS (90%):**
- Typically affects proximal 2 cm
- Often bilateral or ostial lesions
- Associated with systemic atherosclerosis
- Progressive disease
- Peak incidence: 60-70 years

**Fibromuscular Dysplasia (10%):**
- Affects middle to distal artery
- "String of beads" appearance
- More common in young women
- Non-progressive after diagnosis
- Peak incidence: 30-40 years

**Rare Causes:**
- Vasculitis (Takayasu, polyarteritis)
- Radiation-induced
- Neurofibromatosis
- External compression
- Congenital stenosis

### Pathophysiology

#### Hemodynamic Effects
- Reduced renal perfusion pressure
- Activation of RAAS
- Sodium and water retention
- Systemic hypertension
- Reduced GFR

#### Ischemic Nephropathy
- Chronic hypoperfusion
- Tubulointerstitial fibrosis
- Glomerulosclerosis
- Progressive CKD
- Irreversible changes if prolonged

#### Mechanisms of Hypertension
- Renin-mediated hypertension
- Volume expansion
- Sympathetic activation
- Endothelial dysfunction
- Loss of pressure natriuresis

### Clinical Presentation

#### Hypertension Features
**Characteristics:**
- Sudden onset or worsening
- Severe or resistant hypertension
- Age >55 years (atherosclerotic)
- Age <30 years (FMD)
- Episodic flash pulmonary edema

**Medication Requirements:**
- Multiple antihypertensives needed
- Poor response to standard therapy
- Worsening renal function with ACEi/ARBs
- Refractory to 3+ medications including diuretic

#### Renal Dysfunction
**Acute Presentation:**
- Rapid rise in creatinine with ACEi/ARB
- Flash pulmonary edema
- Acute kidney injury in volume depletion
- Contrast-induced AKI

**Chronic Presentation:**
- Progressive CKD
- Asymmetric kidney size (>1.5 cm difference)
- Unexplained renal atrophy
- Recurrent pulmonary edema

#### Systemic Features
- Atherosclerotic disease elsewhere
- Carotid bruits
- Abdominal bruit (epigastric)
- Peripheral vascular disease
- Coronary artery disease

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Screening Indications
**Clinical Scenarios:**
- Resistant hypertension (3+ drugs)
- Abrupt worsening of hypertension
- Unexplained CKD with asymmetric kidneys
- Flash pulmonary edema
- AKI with ACEi/ARB initiation

**High-Risk Groups:**
- Elderly with atherosclerosis
- Young women with hypertension
- Patients with multivessel CAD
- Peripheral vascular disease

#### Imaging Studies
**Duplex Doppler Ultrasound:**
**Advantages:**
- Non-invasive, no contrast
- Relatively inexpensive
- Can assess flow dynamics
- Good screening test

**Findings:**
- Peak systolic velocity >180-200 cm/s
- Renal-aortic ratio >3.5
- Turbulent flow patterns
- Parvus et tardus waveform

**Limitations:**
- Operator dependent
- Limited in obese patients
- Cannot assess accessory arteries
- Limited visualization of distal lesions

**CT Angiography (CTA):**
**Advantages:**
- Excellent spatial resolution
- 3D reconstruction possible
- Assesses calcification
- Fast acquisition

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires iodinated contrast
- Radiation exposure
- Risk of contrast nephropathy
- May overestimate stenosis

**MRI Angiography (MRA):**
**Advantages:**
- No iodinated contrast
- No radiation
- Good for patients with contrast allergy
- Can assess renal function

**Disadvantages:**
- Expensive
- Longer acquisition time
- Gadolinium risk in severe CKD
- May overestimate stenosis

**Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA):**
**Gold Standard:**
- Most accurate assessment
- Therapeutic intervention possible
- Can measure pressure gradients
- Assesses collateral circulation

**Risks:**
- Invasive procedure
- Contrast nephropathy risk
- Vascular complications
- Cholesterol embolization risk

#### Functional Testing
**Captopril Renography:**
- Assesses functional significance
- Useful in bilateral disease
- Limited availability
- False positives common

**Renal Vein Renin Measurements:**
- Lateralization of renin production
- Predicts blood pressure response
- Invasive procedure
- Limited availability

### Treatment Strategies

#### Medical Management
**Blood Pressure Control:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs (monitor creatinine)
- Calcium channel blockers
- Diuretics (volume management)
- Beta-blockers (sympathetic blockade)

**Statin Therapy:**
- High-intensity statins
- Plaque stabilization
- Cardiovascular risk reduction
- Anti-inflammatory effects

**Antiplatelet Therapy:**
- Aspirin 81 mg daily
- Prevents atherosclerotic progression
- Reduces cardiovascular events
- Consider bleeding risk

**Risk Factor Modification:**
- Smoking cessation
- Diabetes control
- Weight management
- Exercise program

#### Revascularization Indications
**Absolute Indications:**
- Recurrent flash pulmonary edema
- Rapidly declining renal function
- Refractory hypertension despite 4+ drugs
- Unilateral total occlusion with viable kidney

**Relative Indications:**
- Resistant hypertension (3+ drugs)
- Progressive CKD with high-grade stenosis
- Difficult to control blood pressure
- Need for ACEi/ARB but contraindicated

#### Revascularization Techniques
**Percutaneous Transluminal Angioplasty (PTA):**
**Fibromuscular Dysplasia:**
- Excellent results (>90% success)
- Low restenosis rate (10%)
- First-line therapy
- Usually no stent needed

**Atherosclerotic Disease:**
- Primary stenting preferred
- Technical success >95%
- Restenosis rate 10-20%
- Requires dual antiplatelet therapy

**Stent Placement:**
- Balloon-expandable stents (ostial lesions)
- Self-expanding stents (long lesions)
- Drug-eluting stents (investigational)
- Covered stents for aneurysms

**Surgical Revascularization:**
**Indications:**
- Failed endovascular therapy
- Complex anatomy
- Multiple renal arteries
- Concomitant aortic surgery

**Procedures:**
- Endarterectomy
- Bypass grafting
- Ex vivo repair with autotransplantation
- Nephrectomy (if non-viable)

### Outcomes & Prognosis

#### Blood Pressure Response
**Predictors of Response:**
- Age <70 years
- Recent onset hypertension
- High-grade stenosis (>70%)
- Unilateral disease
- Elevated renal vein renin

**Response Rates:**
- FMD: 85-95% cure or improvement
- Atherosclerotic: 60-70% improvement
- Bilateral disease: lower response rates
- Long-standing disease: poor response

#### Renal Function Outcomes
**Improvement Predictors:**
- Baseline creatinine <2.0 mg/dL
- Recent decline in function
- Unilateral disease with contralateral normal kidney
- Absence of extensive fibrosis

**Stabilization Rates:**
- 40-50% show improvement
- 30-40% stabilize
- 10-20% continue to decline
- Better outcomes with early intervention

#### Complications
**Procedure-Related:**
- Access site complications (5-10%)
- Contrast nephropathy (5-15%)
- Cholesterol embolization (1-2%)
- Renal artery dissection (1-3%)

**Long-term:**
- Restenosis (10-20%)
- Progressive CKD
- Cardiovascular events
- Mortality (related to comorbidities)

## 3. Thrombotic Microangiopathy (TMA)

### Definition & Classification

#### Pathophysiology
**Endothelial Injury:**
- Widespread endothelial damage
- Microvascular thrombosis
- Mechanical hemolysis
- End-organ ischemia

**Laboratory Hallmarks:**
- Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia
- Thrombocytopenia
- Organ dysfunction
- Elevated LDH, low haptoglobin

#### Classification Systems
**Primary TMA:**
- Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP)
- Hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS)
- Atypical HUS
- Complement-mediated TMA

**Secondary TMA:**
- Pregnancy-associated (HELLP, pre-eclampsia)
- Drug-induced
- Transplant-associated
- Infection-associated
- Systemic disease-associated

### Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (TTP)

#### Etiology & Pathogenesis
**ADAMTS13 Deficiency:**
- Severe deficiency (<10% activity)
- Autoantibodies (acquired) or mutations (congenital)
- Impaired vWF multimer cleavage
- Platelet microthrombi formation

**Autoantibody-Mediated:**
- IgG antibodies against ADAMTS13
- Most common in adults
- May be triggered by infections, drugs
- Relapsing course common

**Congenital TTP:**
- ADAMTS13 gene mutations
- Autosomal recessive inheritance
- Presents in childhood
- Requires regular plasma infusion

#### Clinical Features
**Classic Pentad (Present in <10%):**
- Thrombocytopenia
- Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia
- Neurologic symptoms
- Renal dysfunction
- Fever

**Common Presentations:**
- Neurologic symptoms (headache, confusion, seizures)
- Purpura, petechiae, bleeding
- Fatigue, weakness (anemia)
- Abdominal pain, nausea
- Renal involvement (usually mild)

#### Diagnostic Approach
**Laboratory Findings:**
- Severe thrombocytopenia (<20,000/μL)
- Hemoglobin <10 g/dL
- Elevated LDH
- Low haptoglobin
- Schistocytes on peripheral smear
- ADAMTS13 activity <10%

**Urgent Evaluation:**
- Peripheral smear (schistocytes)
- LDH, haptoglobin, bilirubin
- CBC with differential
- ADAMTS13 activity (send immediately)
- Renal function tests

#### Treatment Strategies
**Plasma Exchange (PLEX):**
**Indications:**
- Suspected TTP (don't wait for ADAMTS13)
- Daily exchanges until platelet count >150,000
- 1.5 plasma volume exchanges
- Continue 2-3 days after recovery

**Mechanism:**
- Removes autoantibodies
- Replaces ADAMTS13
- Removes ultra-large vWF multimers
- Reduces platelet consumption

**Immunosuppression:**
**Corticosteroids:**
- High-dose prednisone 1 mg/kg/day
- Or methylprednisolone 1 g IV daily × 3 days
- Reduces autoantibody production
- Standard of care with PLEX

**Rituximab:**
- 375 mg/m² weekly × 4 doses
- For refractory or relapsing disease
- B-cell depletion
- Reduces relapse rate

**Caplacizumab:**
- Anti-vWF nanobody
- 10 mg daily subcutaneously
- Inhibits vWF-platelet interaction
- Reduces time to normalization
- Expensive but effective

**Supportive Care:**
- Avoid platelet transfusions (worsens thrombosis)
- Treat hypertension
- Renal replacement therapy if needed
- Monitor for complications

### Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS)

#### Classification
**Typical HUS (Shiga toxin-producing):**
- E. coli O157:H7 most common
- Other STEC strains
- Preceding diarrheal illness
- Usually self-limited

**Atypical HUS (Complement-mediated):**
- Complement dysregulation
- Genetic mutations (CFH, CFI, MCP, C3, CFB, THBD)
- Autoantibodies against factor H (5-10%, more common in children)
- High relapse rate without complement inhibition
- **See Document 03 (Glomerular Diseases) for comprehensive aHUS management including 2024 updates**

**Secondary HUS:**
- Pneumococcal infection
- Pregnancy (HELLP)
- Drugs (calcineurin inhibitors, quinine)
- Transplantation
- Systemic diseases

#### Clinical Features
**Typical HUS:**
- Bloody diarrhea (90%)
- Abdominal pain, cramping
- Fever (50%)
- Acute kidney injury (50-70%)
- Thrombocytopenia, anemia

**Atypical HUS:**
- No diarrheal prodrome
- More severe renal involvement
- Extrarenal manifestations common
- Relapsing course
- Family history may be present

#### Diagnostic Approach
**Microbiologic Testing:**
- Stool culture for E. coli O157:H7
- Shiga toxin PCR
- Serology for STEC
- Blood cultures if bacteremia suspected

**Complement Evaluation:**
- Complement levels (C3, C4)
- ADAMTS13 activity (to exclude TTP)
- Genetic testing for complement mutations
- Factor H autoantibodies

**Renal Evaluation:**
- Urinalysis (hematuria, proteinuria)
- Renal function tests
- Ultrasound (kidney size)
- Consider biopsy if atypical

#### Treatment Strategies
**Typical HUS:**
**Supportive Care:**
- Hemodialysis if needed (30-50% require)
- Maintain fluid/electrolyte balance
- Blood transfusions if severe anemia
- Avoid antimotility agents
- Avoid antibiotics (may increase toxin release)

**Specific Therapy:**
- No proven specific therapy
- Eculizumab not routinely recommended
- Monitor for complications
- Long-term follow-up for CKD

**Atypical HUS (2024 Standard of Care):**
**Complement Inhibition - FIRST-LINE THERAPY:**
**Eculizumab (Soliris):**
- FDA-approved for aHUS (standard of care)
- 900 mg weekly × 4 doses, then 1200 mg at week 5, then 1200 mg every 2 weeks
- Blocks terminal complement activation (C5 inhibitor)
- **Outcomes:** 80-90% TMA resolution, 80-90% dialysis independence if started early
- **Requires:** Meningococcal vaccination 2-4 weeks prior (or prophylactic antibiotics if urgent)

**Ravulizumab (Ultomiris):**
- FDA-approved for aHUS (2019)
- Long-acting C5 inhibitor
- Every 8 weeks dosing (weight-based loading, then maintenance)
- Equivalent efficacy to eculizumab
- **Advantages:** Less frequent dosing, improved patient quality of life

**Duration:** Likely lifelong for genetic mutations; individualized for acquired causes

**NOTE:** For comprehensive aHUS pathophysiology, genetics, treatment protocols, and transplant management, see **Document 03 (Glomerular Diseases), Section on Atypical HUS**

**Plasma Therapy:**
- Plasma exchange or infusion
- If eculizumab not available
- Replace complement factors
- Remove autoantibodies

**Supportive Care:**
- Dialysis as needed
- Blood pressure control
- Monitor for TMA complications
- Genetic counseling

### Drug-Induced TMA

#### Common Culprits
**Calcineurin Inhibitors:**
- Cyclosporine, tacrolimus
- Dose-dependent toxicity
- Usually reversible with dose reduction
- More common in transplant

**Chemotherapeutic Agents:**
- Gemcitabine
- Mitomycin C
- Bevacizumab
- Quinine

**Other Drugs:**
- Quetiapine
- Cocaine
- Ticlopidine
- Clopidogrel

#### Clinical Features
- Temporal relationship to drug exposure
- Similar to other TMA forms
- May have less severe renal involvement
- Often reversible with drug discontinuation

#### Management
- Discontinue offending drug
- Supportive care
- Consider plasma exchange if severe
- Avoid re-exposure
- Monitor for recovery

## 4. Renal Vein Thrombosis (RVT)

### Etiology & Risk Factors

#### Neonatal RVT
**Risk Factors:**
- Dehydration
- Sepsis
- Maternal diabetes
- Birth asphyxia
- Inherited thrombophilia

#### Adult RVT
**Nephrotic Syndrome:**
- Membranous nephropathy (most common)
- Minimal change disease
- FSGS
- Amyloidosis

**Hypercoagulable States:**
- Factor V Leiden
- Prothrombin gene mutation
- Antiphospholipid syndrome
- Protein C/S deficiency

**Other Causes:**
- Renal cell carcinoma (tumor thrombus)
- Trauma
- Pregnancy
- Oral contraceptives
- External compression

### Pathophysiology

#### Hemodynamic Effects
- Increased renal venous pressure
- Decreased renal blood flow
- Reduced GFR
- Interstitial edema

#### Ischemic Injury
- Renal congestion
- Tubular ischemia
- Glomerular hypertension
- Progressive fibrosis if chronic

### Clinical Presentation

#### Acute RVT
**Symptoms:**
- Flank pain (85%)
- Gross hematuria (70%)
- Fever (40%)
- Nausea, vomiting
- Palpable renal mass

**Signs:**
- Costovertebral angle tenderness
- Enlarged kidney
- Hypertension
- Decreased urine output

#### Chronic RVT
- Often asymptomatic
- Incidental finding
- May cause hypertension
- Progressive renal dysfunction
- Collateral circulation development

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Imaging Studies
**Doppler Ultrasound:**
- First-line imaging
- No flow in renal vein
- Enlarged kidney
- Increased resistive index

**CT Venography:**
- Excellent visualization
- Can assess extent
- Identify tumor thrombus
- Evaluate collateral vessels

**MRI Venography:**
- No radiation
- Good for contrast allergy
- Can assess tumor extension
- More expensive

**Conventional Venography:**
- Gold standard
- Therapeutic intervention possible
- Invasive procedure
- Contrast nephropathy risk

#### Laboratory Evaluation
- Urinalysis (hematuria, proteinuria)
- Renal function tests
- Coagulation studies
- Hypercoagulable workup if indicated

### Treatment Strategies

#### Anticoagulation
**Initial Therapy:**
- Heparin or LMWH
- Transition to warfarin (INR 2-3)
- Duration: 3-6 months minimum
- Longer if recurrent or hypercoagulable

**DOACs:**
- Apixaban, rivaroxaban
- Similar efficacy to warfarin
- Easier monitoring
- Limited data in RVT

#### Thrombolysis
**Indications:**
- Bilateral RVT with renal failure
- Solitary kidney involvement
- Severe symptoms
- Recent thrombosis (<7 days)

**Methods:**
- Catheter-directed thrombolysis
- Systemic thrombolysis
- Mechanical thrombectomy
- Surgical thrombectomy (rare)

#### Treatment of Underlying Cause
- Treat nephrotic syndrome
- Manage malignancy
- Correct hypercoagulable state
- Discontinue offending agents

### Outcomes & Prognosis

#### Recovery Factors
- Early diagnosis and treatment
- Unilateral vs bilateral involvement
- Baseline renal function
- Underlying cause
- Extent of thrombosis

#### Complications
- Renal infarction
- Pulmonary embolism
- Chronic kidney disease
- Hypertension
- Recurrent thrombosis

## 5. Atheroembolic Disease

### Pathophysiology & Etiology

#### Mechanism
- Cholesterol crystal embolization
- From atherosclerotic plaques
- Usually iatrogenic (vascular procedures)
- Can occur spontaneously

#### Risk Factors
**Procedural:**
- Cardiac catheterization
- Vascular surgery
- Endovascular interventions
- Anticoagulation/ thrombolysis

**Patient Factors:**
- Advanced atherosclerosis
- Hypertension
- Smoking
- Diabetes mellitus

### Clinical Features

#### Acute Presentation
- Following vascular procedure (days-weeks)
- Acute kidney injury
- Livedo reticularis
- Blue toe syndrome
- GI symptoms

#### Chronic Presentation
- Progressive CKD
- Unexplained renal failure
- Systemic embolic phenomena
- Weight loss

#### Laboratory Findings
- Eosinophilia (80%)
- Elevated ESR/CRP
- Low complement (C3, C4)
- Proteinuria (usually sub-nephrotic)
- Hematuria

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Clinical Suspicion
- Recent vascular procedure
- Characteristic skin findings
- Eosinophilia
- Multi-organ involvement

#### Laboratory Evaluation
- CBC with differential (eosinophils)
- ESR, CRP
- Complement levels
- Renal function tests
- Lipid profile

#### Imaging Studies
- Renal ultrasound (usually normal)
- CT may show infarcts
- MRI may show ischemic changes
- Angiography (usually avoided)

#### Kidney Biopsy
**Indications:**
- Uncertain diagnosis
- Need for definitive diagnosis
- Exclude other causes

**Findings:**
- Cholesterol clefts in lumen
- Giant cell reaction
- Intimal thickening
- Ischemic changes

### Treatment Strategies

#### Prevention
- Careful catheter manipulation
- Use of embolic protection devices
- Prophylactic statins
- Avoid unnecessary procedures

#### Medical Management
**Statin Therapy:**
- High-intensity statins
- Plaque stabilization
- Anti-inflammatory effects
- Cardiovascular protection

**Anti-inflammatory Agents:**
- Corticosteroids (controversial)
- Limited evidence
- Consider for severe cases
- Monitor side effects

**Supportive Care:**
- Blood pressure control
- Manage renal failure
- Treat complications
- Avoid further procedures

#### Procedural Considerations
- Avoid future vascular procedures
- Use alternative imaging
- Careful risk-benefit assessment
- Consider medical management

### Prognosis
- High mortality (50-80% at 1 year)
- Progressive renal failure common
- Cardiovascular events frequent
- Limited treatment options

## 6. Vasculitis Affecting Kidney

### ANCA-Associated Vasculitis

#### Classification
**Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA):**
- c-ANCA (PR3-ANCA) positive
- Upper airway, lung, kidney involvement
- Granulomatous inflammation
- Necrotizing vasculitis

**Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA):**
- p-ANCA (MPO-ANCA) positive
- Pulmonary-renal syndrome
- No granulomas
- Small vessel vasculitis

**Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (EGPA):**
- Asthma, eosinophilia
- p-ANCA positive (50%)
- Allergic granulomatosis
- Small to medium vessel

#### Renal Manifestations
- Rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis
- Hematuria, RBC casts
- Proteinuria (usually sub-nephrotic)
- Acute kidney injury
- May progress to ESRD

#### Treatment
**Induction:**
- High-dose corticosteroids
- Rituximab or cyclophosphamide
- Plasma exchange for severe disease
- Supportive care

**Maintenance:**
- Azathioprine or methotrexate
- Low-dose steroids
- Rituximab maintenance
- Monitor for relapse

**Steroid-Sparing Alternative - Avacopan (Tavneos) (FDA-Approved 2021):**
- **Mechanism:** Selective C5a receptor antagonist (blocks neutrophil activation)
- **Dosing:** 30 mg PO twice daily × 52 weeks (with food)
- **Indication:** Alternative to glucocorticoid taper in ANCA vasculitis
- **ADVOCATE Trial (2021):**
  - Non-inferior to prednisone for remission at 26 weeks (72.3% vs 70.1%)
  - Superior sustained remission at 52 weeks (65.7% vs 54.9%, p=0.007)
  - Greater eGFR improvement and fewer relapses
  - 60% reduction in cumulative steroid dose
- **Use in combination with:**
  - Rituximab (preferred) OR Cyclophosphamide
  - Allows avoidance of high-dose glucocorticoids
- **Advantages:**
  - Avoids long-term steroid toxicity (diabetes, osteoporosis, weight gain)
  - Better renal outcomes than prednisone taper
  - Oral administration (convenient)
- **Monitoring:**
  - LFTs monthly (hepatotoxicity risk - most important AE)
  - Baseline: HBV/HCV serology, TB screening
  - Discontinue if ALT/AST >5× ULN
- **Side Effects:**
  - Hepatotoxicity (monitor LFTs monthly)
  - Upper respiratory infections (15-20%)
  - Nausea, diarrhea, hypertension
- **Contraindications:**
  - Active hepatitis or cirrhosis
  - Severe liver impairment (Child-Pugh C)
  - Strong CYP3A4 inducers (rifampin, phenytoin)
- **Place in Therapy:**
  - Consider FIRST-LINE steroid-sparing option for:
    - Diabetes or steroid intolerance
    - Osteoporosis/fragility fractures
    - Obesity or metabolic syndrome
  - Cost: ~$200,000-250,000/year (prior authorization required)

### Immune Complex Vasculitis

#### Cryoglobulinemic Vasculitis
- Type II or III cryoglobulinemia
- Hepatitis C association
- Membranoproliferative GN pattern
- Treat underlying cause

#### IgA Vasculitis (Henoch-Schönlein)
- Usually pediatric
- Palpable purpura
- Abdominal pain
- Arthralgias
- IgA deposition

### Other Vasculitic Disorders

#### Polyarteritis Nodosa
- Medium vessel vasculitis
- ANCA negative
- Microaneurysms
- Renal infarcts common

#### Takayasu Arteritis
- Large vessel vasculitis
- Renal artery stenosis
- Hypertension
- Young women

## 7. Scleroderma Renal Crisis

### Epidemiology & Risk Factors

#### Incidence
- Occurs in 5-10% of systemic sclerosis patients
- More common in diffuse cutaneous disease
- Peak incidence: 2-5 years after disease onset
- Higher mortality without treatment

#### Risk Factors
- Diffuse cutaneous involvement
- Rapid skin progression
- Corticosteroid use (>15 mg prednisone)
- Anti-RNA polymerase III antibodies
- Pregnancy

### Pathophysiology

#### Vascular Mechanisms
- Intimal proliferation of arcuate arteries
- Luminal narrowing/obliteration
- Ischemic injury
- Juxtaglomerular hyperplasia

#### RAAS Activation
- Severe hypertension
- Hyperreninemia
- Secondary hyperaldosteronism
- Volume expansion

### Clinical Features

#### Hypertension
- Sudden severe hypertension
- Malignant hypertension features
- Headache, visual changes
- Encephalopathy

#### Renal Manifestations
- Rapidly progressive renal failure
- Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia
- Thrombocytopenia
- Proteinuria, hematuria

#### Systemic Features
- Fever, malaise
- Arthralgias, myalgias
- Cardiac involvement
- Pulmonary edema

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Laboratory Evaluation
- Severe hypertension (often >180/120)
- Elevated creatinine
- Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia
- Thrombocytopenia
- Elevated renin levels

#### Urinalysis
- Proteinuria (usually <1 g/day)
- Microscopic hematuria
- RBC casts
- Granular casts

#### Imaging
- Renal ultrasound (enlarged kidneys)
- Rule out obstruction
- Assess kidney size
- No specific findings

### Treatment Strategies

#### ACE Inhibitors
**First-Line Therapy:**
- Captopril 25-100 mg every 6 hours
- Or enalapril 5-40 mg daily
- Titrate to blood pressure control
- Continue even if renal function declines initially

**Mechanism:**
- Blocks RAAS activation
- Reduces hyperreninemia effects
- Improves renal perfusion
- Prevents further damage

#### Blood Pressure Management
- Multiple antihypertensives often needed
- Calcium channel blockers
- Beta-blockers
- Avoid bilateral renal artery stenosis

#### Supportive Care
- Dialysis if needed (often temporary)
- Treat microangiopathic hemolysis
- Manage complications
- Avoid nephrotoxins

#### Prognosis
- Dramatically improved with ACE inhibitors
- 1-year survival: 76% (vs 15% historically)
- 50% recover renal function
- 20% progress to ESRD

## 8. Clinical Algorithms & Decision Trees

### Renal Artery Stenosis Evaluation

```
Resistant Hypertension or Unexplained CKD
        ↓
Clinical Assessment: Age, Onset, Medications
        ↓
Screening: Duplex Doppler Ultrasound
        ↓
Positive Study → CTA or MRA
        ↓
High-Grade Stenosis (>70%) → Consider Revascularization
        ↓
Revascularization Indications:
- Flash pulmonary edema
- Rapidly declining GFR
- Refractory hypertension
        ↓
Intervention: PTA with stenting (atherosclerotic)
        ↓
Follow-up: BP control, renal function, duplex surveillance
```

### Thrombotic Microangiopathy Emergency Management

```
MAHA + Thrombocytopenia + Organ Dysfunction
        ↓
Urgent: Send ADAMTS13, peripheral smear, LDH
        ↓
Start Plasma Exchange Immediately (don't wait for results)
        ↓
Add High-Dose Steroids
        ↓
ADAMTS13 Results:
<10% → TTP → Continue PLEX, consider rituximab
Normal/low → HUS → Consider eculizumab (atypical)
        ↓
Supportive Care: Dialysis, transfuse PRBCs only if needed
        ↓
Monitor: Platelet count, LDH, renal function daily
```

## 9. Key Take-Home Points

1. **RAS Recognition**: Consider in resistant hypertension or asymmetric kidneys
2. **TMA Emergency**: Treat suspected TTP with plasma exchange immediately
3. **HUS Classification**: Distinguish typical (diarrheal) from atypical (complement-mediated)
4. **RVT Association**: Strong association with nephrotic syndrome, especially membranous
5. **Atheroembolism Prevention**: Careful catheter technique and statin therapy
6. **Vasculitis Patterns**: ANCA patterns help predict clinical features
7. **Scleroderma Crisis**: ACE inhibitors are life-saving, continue despite initial GFR decline
8. **Imaging Selection**: Choose appropriate imaging based on clinical scenario
9. **Multidisciplinary Care**: Collaborate with cardiology, rheumatology, interventional radiology
10. **Long-term Follow-up**: Monitor for recurrence, progression, and complications
