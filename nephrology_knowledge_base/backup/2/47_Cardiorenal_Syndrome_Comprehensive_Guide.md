## Introduction & Definition {#introduction-definition}

### Definition

**Cardiorenal Syndrome (CRS):**
- Disorders of the heart and kidneys whereby acute or chronic dysfunction in one organ may induce acute or chronic dysfunction in the other organ
- Bidirectional relationship: Heart failure ⇄ Kidney disease
- Affects 25-30% of heart failure patients
- Associated with worse prognosis, increased mortality, longer hospitalizations

### Clinical Significance

**Epidemiology:**
- **Acute Heart Failure**: 25-40% develop worsening renal function during hospitalization
- **Chronic Heart Failure**: 40-50% have CKD (eGFR <60 mL/min)
- **CKD Patients**: 2-3× higher risk of cardiovascular events, heart failure
- **Mortality**: Presence of CRS increases in-hospital mortality 2-3×

**Impact on Outcomes:**
- Longer hospital stays (median 6-8 days vs. 4-5 days without CRS)
- Higher readmission rates (30-day readmission 25-30%)
- Reduced quality of life
- Limited treatment options (medication intolerance, device contraindications)

## Ronco Classification (Types 1-5) {#ronco-classification}

### Overview

The **Ronco Classification** (2008) divides cardiorenal syndrome into 5 types based on:
1. Primary organ affected (heart vs. kidney)
2. Acuity (acute vs. chronic)
3. Direction of dysfunction

---

### Type 1 CRS: Acute Cardiorenal Syndrome ⭐

**Definition:**
- **Acute worsening of heart function** → **Acute kidney injury (AKI)**
- Most common type encountered in clinical practice

**Examples:**
- Acute decompensated heart failure (ADHF) with AKI
- Acute myocardial infarction with AKI
- Cardiogenic shock with AKI
- Acute valvular dysfunction (e.g., acute mitral regurgitation) with AKI

**Pathophysiology:**
- **Reduced Cardiac Output**: Decreased renal perfusion → prerenal AKI
- **Venous Congestion**: Elevated central venous pressure (CVP) → renal venous congestion → reduced GFR
  - **Key Concept**: Venous congestion may be MORE important than low cardiac output
  - Elevated CVP impairs renal blood flow, increases interstitial pressure
- **Neurohormonal Activation**: RAAS, sympathetic nervous system, ADH
- **Inflammation**: Inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6) contribute to both cardiac and renal dysfunction

**Clinical Presentation:**
- Acute heart failure symptoms: Dyspnea, orthopnea, edema
- Elevated jugular venous pressure (JVP >10 cm H₂O)
- Acute rise in creatinine (≥0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours or ≥50% within 7 days)
- Oliguria (<0.5 mL/kg/hr)
- Diuretic resistance

**Diagnosis:**
- Elevated BNP or NT-proBNP
- Acute increase in creatinine (KDIGO AKI criteria)
- Elevated CVP on right heart catheterization (if performed)
- Echocardiography: Low ejection fraction or diastolic dysfunction
- Renal biomarkers: NGAL, KIM-1 (elevated early)

**Management:** See Type-Specific Management section below

**Prognosis:**
- In-hospital mortality: 5-10%
- 30-day readmission: 25-30%
- Long-term mortality: 2-3× higher than heart failure without AKI

---

### Type 2 CRS: Chronic Cardiorenal Syndrome ⭐

**Definition:**
- **Chronic heart failure** → **Progressive chronic kidney disease**
- Gradual decline in renal function in setting of chronic heart failure

**Examples:**
- Chronic systolic heart failure (HFrEF) with progressive CKD
- Chronic diastolic heart failure (HFpEF) with CKD
- Ischemic cardiomyopathy with CKD
- Chronic valvular heart disease with CKD

**Pathophysiology:**
- **Chronic Low Cardiac Output**: Chronic renal hypoperfusion
- **Chronic Venous Congestion**: Persistent elevated CVP → chronic renal venous hypertension
- **Neurohormonal Activation**: Chronic RAAS activation, sympathetic overdrive
- **Inflammation & Fibrosis**: Chronic low-grade inflammation, interstitial renal fibrosis
- **Anemia**: Erythropoietin deficiency from CKD worsens heart failure
- **Volume Overload**: Difficult to manage, worsens both heart and kidney function

**Clinical Presentation:**
- Chronic heart failure symptoms (dyspnea on exertion, fatigue, peripheral edema)
- Progressive decline in eGFR (gradual, over months to years)
- Anemia (multifactorial: CKD, heart failure, hemodilution)
- Refractory edema despite diuretics
- Worsening proteinuria

**Diagnosis:**
- Elevated BNP or NT-proBNP (often chronically elevated)
- CKD: eGFR <60 mL/min for >3 months
- Anemia (Hgb <12 g/dL in women, <13 g/dL in men)
- Echocardiography: Reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) or preserved EF with diastolic dysfunction (HFpEF)

**Management:** See Type-Specific Management section below

**Prognosis:**
- 5-year mortality: 50-70% (both HF and CKD contribute)
- ESRD development: 10-20% over 5 years
- Cardiovascular events: 3-4× higher than heart failure alone

---

### Type 3 CRS: Acute Reno-Cardiac Syndrome

**Definition:**
- **Acute worsening of kidney function** → **Acute cardiac dysfunction**
- Acute kidney injury triggers cardiac complications

**Examples:**
- Acute glomerulonephritis → acute pulmonary edema, arrhythmias
- Rhabdomyolysis with AKI → myocardial depression
- Hyperkalemia from AKI → cardiac arrhythmias
- Volume overload from AKI → acute heart failure
- Uremic pericarditis

**Pathophysiology:**
- **Volume Overload**: Rapid fluid retention → pulmonary edema, cardiac strain
- **Electrolyte Disturbances**: Hyperkalemia, hypocalcemia, hyperphosphatemia → arrhythmias
- **Uremic Toxins**: Accumulation of uremic toxins → myocardial depression, pericarditis
- **Inflammation**: Cytokine release from AKI → myocardial inflammation
- **Acidosis**: Metabolic acidosis → reduced cardiac contractility

**Clinical Presentation:**
- AKI presentation (oliguria, elevated creatinine)
- Acute pulmonary edema
- Arrhythmias (especially with hyperkalemia >6.5 mEq/L)
- Pericarditis (uremic pericardial rub, chest pain)
- Cardiomyopathy (rare, with severe uremia)

**Diagnosis:**
- AKI criteria (KDIGO)
- Electrocardiogram: Arrhythmias, peaked T waves (hyperkalemia), pericarditis changes
- Chest X-ray: Pulmonary edema
- Echocardiography: Pericardial effusion (uremic pericarditis), reduced EF
- Severe electrolyte abnormalities (K >6.5 mEq/L, Ca low, Phos high)

**Management:**
- **Urgent Dialysis** (indications):
  - Severe hyperkalemia refractory to medical therapy
  - Pulmonary edema refractory to diuretics
  - Uremic pericarditis
  - Severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.1)
- Electrolyte correction
- Volume management
- Treat underlying AKI cause

**Prognosis:**
- Depends on reversibility of AKI
- Arrhythmias and pericarditis usually resolve with dialysis

---

### Type 4 CRS: Chronic Reno-Cardiac Syndrome

**Definition:**
- **Chronic kidney disease** → **Chronic cardiac dysfunction**
- CKD as primary driver of cardiac disease development

**Examples:**
- CKD → left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH)
- CKD → coronary artery disease (CAD)
- CKD → heart failure (uremic cardiomyopathy)
- CKD → valvular calcification (aortic stenosis, mitral annular calcification)
- CKD → arrhythmias (atrial fibrillation)

**Pathophysiology:**
- **Volume Overload**: Chronic fluid retention → LVH, dilation
- **Hypertension**: Difficult-to-control HTN → LVH, diastolic dysfunction
- **CKD-Mineral Bone Disorder (CKD-MBD)**:
  - Hyperphosphatemia → vascular calcification, coronary artery calcification
  - FGF-23 elevation → direct myocardial toxicity, LVH
  - Secondary hyperparathyroidism → myocardial fibrosis
- **Anemia**: Chronic anemia → high-output state, LVH
- **Uremic Toxins**: Chronic accumulation → cardiomyopathy, arrhythmias
- **Inflammation**: Chronic low-grade inflammation → atherosclerosis
- **Malnutrition**: Protein-energy wasting → cardiac cachexia

**Clinical Presentation:**
- CKD symptoms (fatigue, anemia, bone disease)
- Hypertension (often resistant)
- Progressive dyspnea on exertion
- Left ventricular hypertrophy on ECG
- Diastolic heart failure (HFpEF common)
- Coronary artery disease (often silent)
- Arrhythmias (atrial fibrillation most common)

**Diagnosis:**
- CKD: eGFR <60 mL/min for >3 months
- Echocardiography:
  - LVH (increased LV mass index)
  - Diastolic dysfunction (E/e' ratio elevated)
  - Reduced EF (systolic dysfunction if advanced)
  - Valvular calcification
- ECG: LVH criteria, arrhythmias
- Coronary artery calcium (CAC) score: Often very high (>1000)
- Elevated cardiac biomarkers: Troponin, BNP (chronically elevated in CKD)

**Management:** See Type-Specific Management section below

**Prognosis:**
- Cardiovascular disease is #1 cause of death in CKD (40-50% of deaths)
- 10-year CV event rate: 50-60% in CKD Stage 4-5

---

### Type 5 CRS: Secondary Cardiorenal Syndrome

**Definition:**
- **Systemic disease** affects both heart and kidneys simultaneously
- Not direct cardiac→renal or renal→cardiac causation

**Examples:**
**Systemic Diseases:**
- **Diabetes Mellitus**: Diabetic cardiomyopathy + diabetic nephropathy
- **Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)**: Lupus carditis + lupus nephritis
- **Amyloidosis**: Cardiac amyloidosis + renal amyloidosis
- **Sarcoidosis**: Cardiac sarcoid + granulomatous interstitial nephritis
- **Sepsis**: Septic cardiomyopathy + sepsis-induced AKI

**Systemic Conditions:**
- **Cirrhosis**: Cirrhotic cardiomyopathy + hepatorenal syndrome
- **Myeloma**: Myeloma cast nephropathy + AL amyloid heart disease
- **Vasculitis**: (ANCA, anti-GBM) → lung-heart-kidney involvement

**Toxins/Drugs:**
- **Chemotherapy**: Anthracyclines (doxorubicin) → cardiotoxicity + nephrotoxicity
- **NSAIDs**: Heart failure exacerbation + AKI
- **Cocaine**: Acute MI + rhabdomyolysis with AKI

**Pathophysiology:**
- Specific to underlying systemic disease
- Shared pathways: Inflammation, immune-mediated injury, metabolic derangements, toxin exposure

**Clinical Presentation:**
- Features of underlying systemic disease
- Both cardiac and renal manifestations present

**Diagnosis:**
- Identify underlying systemic disease
- Cardiac evaluation: Echocardiography, ECG, biomarkers
- Renal evaluation: Creatinine, urinalysis, biomarkers

**Management:**
- Treat underlying systemic disease
- Supportive care for both heart and kidney

**Prognosis:**
- Depends on underlying disease and severity

## Pathophysiology {#pathophysiology}

### Hemodynamic Mechanisms

**Reduced Cardiac Output (Forward Failure):**
- **Mechanism**: Low cardiac output → decreased renal perfusion pressure → prerenal azotemia
- **GFR Reduction**: Afferent arteriole vasoconstriction (autoregulation failure)
- **RAAS Activation**: Renin release → angiotensin II → efferent arteriole constriction (initially preserves GFR)
- **Limitation**: If cardiac output too low (<2.5 L/min/m²), GFR cannot be maintained
- **Clinical Markers**: Hypotension, narrow pulse pressure, cool extremities

**Venous Congestion (Backward Failure):** ⭐ **KEY MECHANISM**
- **Mechanism**: Elevated central venous pressure (CVP) → renal venous congestion
- **Renal Effects**:
  - Increased renal venous pressure → decreased renal blood flow (reduced arteriovenous gradient)
  - Increased renal interstitial pressure → tubular compression, reduced GFR
  - Reduced oxygen delivery to renal medulla → tubular injury
- **Evidence**: CVP >12-15 mmHg strongly predicts worsening renal function
- **Clinical Markers**: Elevated JVP, hepatojugular reflux, peripheral edema, ascites
- **KEY POINT**: Venous congestion may be MORE important than low cardiac output in CRS

**Intra-Abdominal Hypertension:**
- Occurs with severe ascites, bowel edema
- Intra-abdominal pressure (IAP) >12 mmHg → renal vein compression
- Worsens renal venous congestion
- Consider paracentesis if IAP elevated

### Neurohormonal Mechanisms

**Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS):**
- **Activation Triggers**: Low cardiac output, renal hypoperfusion, sympathetic activation
- **Effects**:
  - Angiotensin II: Efferent arteriole constriction, sodium retention, fibrosis
  - Aldosterone: Sodium/water retention, potassium loss, cardiac/renal fibrosis
- **Maladaptive**: Chronic RAAS activation worsens volume overload, hypertension, fibrosis

**Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS):**
- **Activation**: Baroreceptor response to low cardiac output
- **Renal Effects**:
  - α1-adrenergic stimulation → afferent arteriole vasoconstriction → reduced GFR
  - β1-stimulation → renin release → RAAS activation
- **Cardiac Effects**: Increased HR, contractility, arrhythmia risk

**Arginine Vasopressin (ADH/AVP):**
- Released in response to low cardiac output, increased osmolality
- V2 receptor activation → water retention → hyponatremia
- V1a receptor → vasoconstriction
- Contributes to volume overload, dilutional hyponatremia

**Natriuretic Peptides (ANP, BNP):**
- Released from atrial/ventricular stretch
- Promote natriuresis, vasodilation
- **Paradox**: Elevated in heart failure but insufficient to overcome RAAS/SNS
- **Resistance**: Renal resistance to natriuretic peptides in CRS

### Inflammatory Mechanisms

**Cytokine Activation:**
- Heart failure and AKI both trigger inflammatory cytokine release
- **Key Cytokines**: TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, IL-18
- **Bidirectional Effects**:
  - Cytokines from heart → renal tubular injury, endothelial dysfunction
  - Cytokines from kidney → myocardial depression, arrhythmias
- **Oxidative Stress**: Reactive oxygen species (ROS) cause cardiac and renal cell injury

**Endothelial Dysfunction:**
- Reduced nitric oxide (NO) bioavailability
- Increased endothelin-1 (vasoconstrictor)
- Impaired microvascular flow in both organs

### Metabolic and Kidney-Specific Mechanisms

**Anemia:**
- CKD → erythropoietin deficiency → anemia
- Anemia → high-output state → cardiac dilation, LVH
- Worsens both heart failure and CKD

**CKD-Mineral Bone Disorder (CKD-MBD):**
- Hyperphosphatemia + elevated FGF-23 → LVH, vascular calcification
- Secondary hyperparathyroidism → myocardial fibrosis
- Contributes to Type 4 CRS pathophysiology

**Uremic Toxins:**
- Accumulation in CKD: Indoxyl sulfate, p-cresyl sulfate, TMAO
- Direct myocardial toxicity
- Endothelial dysfunction
- Arrhythmogenic

## Diagnostic Approach {#diagnostic-approach}

### Clinical Assessment

**History:**
- **Cardiac History**: Heart failure type (HFrEF vs. HFpEF), ischemic heart disease, valvular disease
- **Renal History**: Baseline kidney function, CKD stage, prior AKI episodes
- **Medications**: Diuretics, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, NSAIDs
- **Volume Status**: Weight changes, edema, dyspnea, orthopnea

**Physical Examination:**
**Cardiac Exam:**
- **JVP**: Elevated (>10 cm H₂O) → venous congestion
- Hepatojugular reflux: Positive in elevated CVP
- S3 gallop: Volume overload
- Pulmonary crackles: Pulmonary edema
- Peripheral edema: Volume overload

**Renal Exam:**
- Edema: Peripheral (legs, sacral), anasarca
- Ascites: Intra-abdominal hypertension

### Laboratory Evaluation

**Renal Function:**
- **Serum Creatinine & eGFR**: Assess baseline and acute changes
  - AKI: ≥0.3 mg/dL rise in 48 hrs OR ≥50% rise in 7 days
  - CKD: eGFR <60 mL/min for >3 months
- **Urine Output**: Oliguria (<0.5 mL/kg/hr) suggests AKI
- **Urinalysis**: Rule out intrinsic renal disease (glomerulonephritis, ATN)
- **Urine Sodium & FENa**:
  - FENa <1%: Prerenal (low cardiac output, venous congestion)
  - FENa >2%: Intrinsic renal injury (ATN)

**Cardiac Biomarkers:**
- **BNP or NT-proBNP** ⭐:
  - Elevated: Heart failure, volume overload
  - **Thresholds**:
    - BNP >100 pg/mL (acute HF)
    - NT-proBNP >300 pg/mL (<50 years), >450 pg/mL (50-75 years), >900 pg/mL (>75 years)
  - **Caveat**: Chronically elevated in CKD (interpret with caution)
- **Troponin** (I or T):
  - Elevated in acute coronary syndrome, myocardial strain
  - Chronically elevated in CKD (use change from baseline, not absolute value)

**Novel Renal Biomarkers (Emerging):**
- **NGAL (Neutrophil Gelatinase-Associated Lipocalin)**:
  - Rises early in AKI (within 2-6 hours, before creatinine)
  - Predicts AKI in acute decompensated heart failure
  - Threshold: >150 ng/mL predicts AKI
- **KIM-1 (Kidney Injury Molecule-1)**: Tubular injury marker
- **TIMP-2 × IGFBP7** (NephroCheck®): FDA-approved AKI prediction biomarker
  - Score >0.3 predicts AKI risk within 12 hours

**Electrolytes:**
- **Potassium**: Monitor for hyperkalemia (especially with RAAS inhibitors)
- **Sodium**: Hyponatremia common in heart failure (dilutional, ADH excess)
- **Bicarbonate**: Metabolic acidosis in AKI/CKD

**Other:**
- **Hemoglobin**: Anemia worsens both heart failure and CKD
- **Albumin**: Hypoalbuminemia → reduced diuretic response

### Hemodynamic Assessment

**Right Heart Catheterization (RHC):**
- **Gold Standard** for hemodynamic assessment in CRS
- **Indications**: Unclear volume status, diuretic-resistant, considering advanced therapies
- **Key Measurements**:
  - **Central Venous Pressure (CVP)** / Right Atrial Pressure (RAP):
    - Normal: 2-8 mmHg
    - Elevated (>12-15 mmHg): Venous congestion → predicts worsening renal function
  - **Pulmonary Capillary Wedge Pressure (PCWP)**:
    - Normal: 6-12 mmHg
    - Elevated (>18 mmHg): Volume overload
  - **Cardiac Output (CO)** / Cardiac Index (CI):
    - Normal CI: 2.5-4.0 L/min/m²
    - Low CI (<2.2 L/min/m²): Cardiogenic shock risk
  - **Mixed Venous Oxygen Saturation (SvO₂)**:
    - Normal: 60-80%
    - Low (<60%): Inadequate tissue oxygen delivery

**Non-Invasive Hemodynamic Monitoring:**
- **Blood Volume Analysis (BVA) - Daxor System**:
  - Uses I-131 albumin to measure actual blood volume
  - Distinguishes true volume overload vs. intravascular depletion
  - Useful in diuretic-resistant patients
- **Inferior Vena Cava (IVC) Diameter on Ultrasound**:
  - IVC diameter >2.1 cm with <50% collapse → elevated CVP
  - IVC diameter <2.1 cm with >50% collapse → normal/low CVP

### Imaging

**Echocardiography (Transthoracic Echo - TTE):**
- **Ejection Fraction (EF)**:
  - HFrEF: EF <40%
  - HFpEF: EF ≥50% with diastolic dysfunction
- **Diastolic Dysfunction**: E/e' ratio >14, E/A ratio abnormalities
- **Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH)**: LV mass index elevated
- **Valvular Disease**: Assess for stenosis, regurgitation
- **Pericardial Effusion**: Uremic pericarditis (Type 3 CRS)
- **IVC Diameter**: Estimate CVP

**Chest X-Ray:**
- Pulmonary edema (Kerley B lines, cephalization)
- Cardiomegaly (cardiothoracic ratio >50%)
- Pleural effusions

**Renal Ultrasound:**
- Kidney size (small kidneys suggest chronic disease)
- Hydronephrosis (obstruction)
- Doppler: Renal artery stenosis if suspected

## General Management Principles {#general-management-principles}

### Goals of Management

1. **Optimize Cardiac Function**: Improve cardiac output, reduce venous congestion
2. **Preserve/Improve Renal Function**: Prevent or reverse AKI, slow CKD progression
3. **Manage Volume Overload**: Decongestion without worsening renal function
4. **Address Neurohormonal Activation**: RAAS inhibition, beta-blockade
5. **Prevent Complications**: Avoid nephrotoxins, electrolyte disturbances

### Volume Management ⭐ **CORNERSTONE OF THERAPY**

**Decongestion Strategy:**
- **Goal**: Euvolemia (normal CVP, no peripheral edema, no pulmonary congestion)
- **Monitoring**: Daily weights, I/O, physical exam (JVP, edema), BNP

**Diuretic Therapy:**

**Loop Diuretics (First-Line):**
- **Furosemide** (Lasix):
  - Initial IV dose: 20-40 mg (diuretic-naive), 1-2× home oral dose (diuretic-exposed)
  - Bolus vs. Continuous Infusion:
    - **Continuous Infusion**: May be more effective, less ototoxicity
    - Bolus: 40-80 mg IV every 6-12 hours
    - Continuous: 5-10 mg/hr IV (after 40 mg IV bolus)
- **Torsemide** (Demadex):
  - Better oral bioavailability than furosemide (80-100% vs. 50%)
  - 20 mg torsemide ≈ 40 mg furosemide
- **Bumetanide** (Bumex):
  - 40× more potent than furosemide by weight
  - 1 mg bumetanide ≈ 40 mg furosemide

**Diuretic Resistance:**
- **Definition**: Inadequate response despite escalating loop diuretic doses
- **Mechanisms**:
  - Reduced renal perfusion (low cardiac output, venous congestion)
  - Reduced tubular secretion (CKD, hypoalbuminemia)
  - Distal nephron compensation (sodium reabsorption)
  - Tolerance (chronic diuretic use)

**Strategies for Diuretic Resistance:**
1. **Increase Loop Diuretic Dose**:
   - Double dose if inadequate response
   - Maximum: Furosemide 160-200 mg IV bolus or 20-40 mg/hr continuous
2. **Add Thiazide Diuretic (Sequential Nephron Blockade)** ⭐:
   - **Metolazone** 2.5-10 mg PO daily (30-60 min before loop diuretic)
   - **Chlorothiazide** 500-1000 mg IV (if unable to take PO)
   - **Mechanism**: Blocks distal tubule sodium reabsorption (overcomes distal compensation)
   - **Caution**: Severe hypokalemia, hyponatremia, contraction alkalosis
3. **Add Acetazolamide (Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitor)**:
   - 250-500 mg IV once daily
   - Blocks proximal tubule bicarbonate reabsorption
   - Particularly useful if metabolic alkalosis (bicarb >32 mEq/L)
4. **Albumin Infusion** (If Hypoalbuminemia <2.5 g/dL):
   - 25% albumin 50-100 mL IV before loop diuretic
   - Increases oncotic pressure, improves loop diuretic delivery
   - Evidence mixed, expensive
5. **Switch IV Route**: Oral → IV (improves bioavailability)
6. **Continuous Infusion**: Bolus → continuous (more consistent diuresis)

**Monitoring During Diuresis:**
- **Electrolytes**: Check daily (K, Na, Mg, bicarb)
- **Renal Function**: Creatinine daily (acceptable to have transient rise if decongesting)
- **Weight**: Daily (goal: 0.5-1 kg/day weight loss)
- **Urine Output**: Goal >100-150 mL/hr with diuretics

### RAAS Inhibition

**ACE Inhibitors / ARBs:**
- **Benefits**:
  - Reduce afterload → improve cardiac output
  - Reduce RAAS activation → slow renal fibrosis
  - Mortality benefit in heart failure
- **Risks**:
  - Hyperkalemia (especially with CKD)
  - Acute GFR decline (acceptable if <30% from baseline)
  - Hypotension
- **Initiation**:
  - Start low, titrate slowly
  - **Accept 20-30% cr

eatinine rise** if patient improving clinically (decongesting)
  - Hold if Cr rises >30-50% or hyperkalemia >5.5 mEq/L
- **Target Doses** (heart failure):
  - Lisinopril 20-40 mg daily
  - Enalapril 10-20 mg BID
  - Losartan 50-150 mg daily (ARB)
  - Valsartan 160-320 mg daily (ARB)

**Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists (MRAs):**
- **Spironolactone** 12.5-25 mg daily:
  - Reduces mortality in HFrEF
  - **Caution**: Hyperkalemia (check K within 1 week, then monthly)
  - Use if K <5.0 mEq/L, eGFR >30 mL/min
- **Eplerenone** 25-50 mg daily:
  - More selective, fewer gynecomastia side effects
  - Same hyperkalemia risk

**Newer Agents:**
- **Finerenone** (Kerendia):
  - Nonsteroidal MRA, lower hyperkalemia risk than spironolactone
  - FDA-approved for diabetic CKD, also used in heart failure
  - 10-20 mg daily

### Beta-Blockade

**Indications:**
- HFrEF (EF <40%)
- Reduces mortality, hospitalizations

**Agents:**
- **Carvedilol** 3.125 mg BID → 25 mg BID (target)
- **Metoprolol succinate** 12.5-25 mg daily → 200 mg daily (target)
- **Bisoprolol** 1.25 mg daily → 10 mg daily (target)

**Initiation:**
- Start low, uptitrate slowly (every 2 weeks)
- Monitor HR, BP, signs of decompensation
- May transiently worsen heart failure initially (negative inotrope)

### SGLT2 Inhibitors ⭐ **GAME-CHANGER**

**Mechanism:**
- Inhibit SGLT2 in proximal tubule → glucosuria, natriuresis
- Reduce preload and afterload
- Improve cardiac and renal outcomes

**Agents with Cardiovascular + Renal Indications:**
- **Empagliflozin** (Jardiance) 10 mg daily
- **Dapagliflozin** (Farxiga) 10 mg daily
- **Canagliflozin** (Invokana) 100 mg daily

**Indications (2024):**
- HFrEF (EF <40%) - regardless of diabetes status
- HFpEF (EF ≥40%) - dapagliflozin, empagliflozin approved
- CKD with or without diabetes (eGFR ≥20 mL/min)

**Benefits in Cardiorenal Syndrome:**
- **Heart Failure**: 25-30% reduction in heart failure hospitalizations
- **Renal**: 30-40% reduction in CKD progression
- **Mortality**: 13-15% reduction in CV death
- **Mechanism**: Natriuresis, improved hemodynamics, metabolic benefits

**Initiation:**
- Start in stable patients (not during acute decompensation)
- eGFR ≥20 mL/min required
- Acceptable to have transient eGFR dip (5-10 mL/min) after starting

**Monitoring:**
- eGFR at 2-4 weeks (expect small dip, stabilizes)
- Genital infections (counsel on hygiene)
- Euglycemic DKA (rare, in insulin-dependent diabetes)

## Type-Specific Management {#type-specific-management}

### Type 1 CRS: Acute Cardiorenal Syndrome

**Acute Management (Inpatient):**

**Step 1: Assess Hemodynamics**
- Clinical exam: JVP, edema, perfusion (warm/cold, wet/dry)
- Consider RHC if unclear (CVP, PCWP, CO/CI)

**Step 2: Decongestion (PRIMARY GOAL)**
- **IV Loop Diuretics**:
  - Furosemide 40-80 mg IV bolus (or 2× home oral dose)
  - Assess response at 2-6 hours (urine output, symptom improvement)
  - If inadequate: Double dose or switch to continuous infusion
- **Sequential Nephron Blockade** (if diuretic-resistant):
  - Add metolazone 2.5-10 mg PO 30-60 min before loop diuretic
  - Monitor electrolytes closely (severe hypokalemia risk)
- **Monitor**:
  - Daily weights (goal: 0.5-1 kg/day loss)
  - Urine output (goal: >100-150 mL/hr)
  - Daily creatinine (accept 20-30% rise if decongesting)
  - Electrolytes daily

**Step 3: Optimize Cardiac Output (If Low CO)**
- **Inotropes** (if cardiogenic shock, CI <2.2 L/min/m²):
  - **Dobutamine** 2.5-10 mcg/kg/min IV (beta-agonist, increases contractility)
  - **Milrinone** 0.125-0.75 mcg/kg/min IV (phosphodiesterase inhibitor)
  - **Caution**: Arrhythmias, tachyphylaxis, increased mortality with prolonged use
  - Goal: Bridge to recovery or advanced therapy (LVAD, transplant)

**Step 4: Address Venous Congestion**
- If elevated CVP despite diuretics → consider ultrafiltration (see Advanced Therapies)

**Step 5: Continue GDMT (Guideline-Directed Medical Therapy)**
- **ACE inhibitor/ARB**: Continue if tolerated (BP, K, Cr acceptable)
  - May need to hold temporarily if hypotension or Cr rises >30%
- **Beta-Blocker**: Continue if tolerated (may reduce dose if decompensated)
- **MRA**: Hold if hyperkalemia >5.0 mEq/L
- **SGLT2 Inhibitor**: Hold during acute decompensation, restart when stable

**Criteria for Stability/Discharge:**
- Euvolemia (no JVD, no edema, stable weight)
- Adequate urine output on oral diuretics
- Stable creatinine (acceptable if higher than admission if euvolemic)
- Optimized GDMT

---

### Type 2 CRS: Chronic Cardiorenal Syndrome

**Chronic Management (Outpatient):**

**Step 1: GDMT Optimization**

**Quadruple Therapy for HFrEF** (2024 Standard):
1. **ACE Inhibitor/ARB or ARNI**:
   - **ARNI (Sacubitril/Valsartan - Entresto)**: Preferred over ACE/ARB
   - Reduces CV death and HF hospitalization by 20%
   - Dosing: Start 24/26 mg BID → target 97/103 mg BID
   - Contraindication: History of angioedema, within 36 hrs of ACE inhibitor
2. **Beta-Blocker**:
   - Carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, or bisoprolol
   - Uptitrate to target doses
3. **Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonist (MRA)**:
   - Spironolactone 12.5-25 mg daily OR finerenone 10-20 mg daily
   - Monitor K closely (especially with CKD)
4. **SGLT2 Inhibitor** ⭐:
   - Empagliflozin 10 mg or dapagliflozin 10 mg daily
   - START IN ALL HFrEF PATIENTS (regardless of diabetes, eGFR ≥20)

**Diuretic Titration:**
- **Goal**: Euvolemia without excessive diuresis
- **Loop Diuretic**: Lowest dose to maintain euvolemia
  - Torsemide preferred over furosemide (better bioavailability, less resistance)
- **Metolazone**: Reserve for refractory edema
- **Monitor**: Weekly weights at home, call if gain >2-3 lbs in 1 week

**Step 2: Manage CKD Complications**

**Anemia:**
- **Iron Supplementation**:
  - If iron deficiency (ferritin <100 ng/mL or TSAT <20%)
  - **IV Iron Preferred** in heart failure (better absorption than oral)
  - Ferric carboxymaltose (Injectafer) 750 mg IV × 2 doses
  - Ferric derisomaltose (Monoferric) 1000 mg IV single dose
- **ESAs** (if Hgb <10 g/dL):
  - Darbepoetin 0.45 mcg/kg SC every 2-4 weeks
  - Target Hgb 10-11.5 g/dL (avoid >11.5, increased CV risk)

**CKD-MBD:**
- **Phosphate Binders**:
  - Goal phosphate 3.5-5.5 mg/dL
  - Non-calcium binders preferred (sevelamer, lanthanum) to avoid vascular calcification
- **Vitamin D**:
  - Ergocalciferol or cholecalciferol if deficiency
  - Calcitriol if severe secondary hyperparathyroidism

**Hyperkalemia Management:**
- **Dietary K restriction** (<2-3 g/day)
- **Potassium Binders** (if K >5.5 mEq/L recurrent):
  - Patiromer (Veltassa) 8.4-25.2 g PO daily
  - Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (Lokelma) 10 g PO daily
  - Allow continuation of ACE/ARB/MRA

**Step 3: Monitor Progression**
- **Clinic Visits**: Every 3-6 months
- **Labs**: Creatinine, eGFR, electrolytes, BNP every 3-6 months
- **Echocardiography**: Annually or if clinical change
- **Nephrology Referral**: If eGFR <30 mL/min or rapid decline (>5 mL/min/year)

**Step 4: Consider Advanced Therapies**
- **Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy (CRT)**: If EF <35%, QRS >150 ms
- **Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator (ICD)**: If EF <35%
- **LVAD**: If refractory HF despite GDMT
- **Heart Transplant**: If eligible (age, comorbidities)

---

### Type 3 CRS: Acute Reno-Cardiac Syndrome

**Management:**

**Urgent Dialysis Indications:**
- **A**cidosis: Severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.1) refractory to bicarbonate
- **E**lectrolytes: Hyperkalemia (K >6.5 mEq/L) refractory to medical therapy
- **I**ngestions: Toxic alcohol, lithium, metformin
- **O**verload: Pulmonary edema refractory to diuretics
- **U**remia: Uremic pericarditis, encephalopathy, bleeding

**Electrolyte Management:**
- **Hyperkalemia** (K >6.5 mEq/L or ECG changes):
  - Calcium gluconate 1-2 g IV (cardiac membrane stabilization)
  - Insulin 10 units IV + dextrose 50 g IV (shift K intracellularly)
  - Albuterol 10-20 mg nebulized (shift K intracellularly)
  - Sodium bicarbonate 50-100 mEq IV (if metabolic acidosis)
  - Patiromer or sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (GI K removal)
  - Dialysis if refractory

**Treat Underlying AKI:**
- Volume resuscitation (if prerenal)
- Remove nephrotoxins
- Treat specific cause (glomerulonephritis, obstruction)

---

### Type 4 CRS: Chronic Reno-Cardiac Syndrome

**Management:**

**CKD Management:**
- **SGLT2 Inhibitor**: Empagliflozin or dapagliflozin 10 mg daily (if eGFR ≥20)
- **ACE Inhibitor/ARB**: If proteinuria >300 mg/day
- **BP Control**: Target <130/80 mmHg (KDIGO 2024: <120/80 if tolerated)
- **Manage CKD-MBD**: Phosphate binders, vitamin D
- **Anemia Management**: IV iron, ESAs if Hgb <10 g/dL

**Cardiovascular Risk Reduction:**
- **Statin**: High-intensity (atorvastatin 40-80 mg, rosuvastatin 20-40 mg)
- **Aspirin**: If established ASCVD (not routine primary prevention in CKD)
- **Lifestyle**: Smoking cessation, dietary sodium restriction, exercise

**Dialysis/Transplant Planning:**
- **Nephrology Referral**: eGFR <30 mL/min
- **Dialysis Access Placement**: eGFR 15-20 mL/min (AVF creation 6-12 months before anticipated dialysis)
- **Transplant Evaluation**: eGFR <20 mL/min (if eligible)
- **Advance Care Planning**: Discuss conservative management option

---

### Type 5 CRS: Secondary Cardiorenal Syndrome

**Management:**
- **Treat Underlying Systemic Disease**:
  - Diabetes: Glycemic control, SGLT2 inhibitors, GLP-1 RAs
  - Lupus: Immunosuppression (MMF, belimumab, cyclophosphamide)
  - Amyloidosis: Treat plasma cell dyscrasia (if AL), tafamidis (if ATTR cardiac)
  - Sepsis: Source control, antibiotics, hemodynamic support
- **Supportive Cardiac & Renal Care**: Same principles as other CRS types

---

## Advanced Therapies {#advanced-therapies}

### Ultrafiltration (UF)

**Aquadex® System (FDA-Approved for Heart Failure):**

**Indications:**
- **Diuretic-Resistant Volume Overload**: Adequate trial of IV loop diuretics ± thiazide failed
- **Severe Congestion**: Persistent edema, dyspnea despite escalating diuretics
- **Worsening Renal Function with Diuretics**: Cr rising despite volume overload

**Mechanism:**
- **Isolated Ultrafiltration**: Removes plasma water (isot onic fluid), NOT solutes
- Does not affect electrolytes (unlike diuretics)
- Gentler on kidneys than aggressive diuresis

**Procedure:**
- Peripheral IV access (dual-lumen catheter) or central line
- Ultrafiltration rate: 100-200 mL/hr (slower than hemodialysis)
- Duration: 8-24 hours per session
- Volume removed: 1-3 L per session

**Benefits:**
- More sodium and water removal than diuretics (liter-for-liter)
- Preserves renal function better than high-dose diuretics (less neurohormonal activation)
- Reduces rehospitalizations

**Trials:**
- **UNLOAD Trial (2007)**: UF superior to IV diuretics for weight loss, fluid removal, reduced 90-day hospitalizations
- **CARRESS-HF Trial (2012)**: UF NOT superior to stepped diuretic algorithm (but more invasive)

**Current Use:**
- **Reserve for diuretic-resistant patients**
- Not first-line (diuretics remain first-line)
- Requires specialized equipment, training

**Complications:**
- Bleeding, infection (catheter-related)
- Hypotension (if fluid removed too quickly)

---

### Mechanical Circulatory Support (MCS)

**Indications:**
- Refractory cardiogenic shock despite inotropes
- Bridge to heart transplant or decision
- Destination therapy (if not transplant candidate)

**Devices:**
- **Intra-Aortic Balloon Pump (IABP)**: Temporary support, improves coronary perfusion
- **Impella®**: Percutaneous LVAD, short-term support
- **Durable LVAD (HeartMate 3)**: Long-term support, implantable

**Impact on CRS:**
- **Improves Renal Function**: Restores cardiac output → improved renal perfusion
- **Post-LVAD**: Renal function often improves (eGFR increases 10-20 mL/min on average)

---

### Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT)

**Indications in CRS:**
- Urgent dialysis indications (AEIOU)
- Refractory volume overload despite UF
- ESRD development

**Modality Selection:**
- **Hemodialysis**: Most common, 3×/week
- **Peritoneal Dialysis**: May be better tolerated hemodynamically (gentler fluid removal)
- **Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy (CRRT)**: If ICU, hemodynamically unstable

**Heart Failure on Dialysis:**
- **Challenges**: Volume management, dry weight assessment, intradialytic hypotension
- **Ultrafiltration Rate**: Keep <10-13 mL/kg/hr to avoid intradialytic hypotension
- **Longer/More Frequent Dialysis**: Consider if volume overload difficult to manage

## Special Populations {#special-populations}

### HFpEF (Heart Failure with Preserved Ejection Fraction)

**Characteristics:**
- EF ≥50%, diastolic dysfunction
- More common in elderly, women, hypertensive patients
- **Closely Linked to CKD**: 60-70% of HFpEF patients have CKD

**Management:**
- **Diuretics**: For volume overload (same as HFrEF)
- **SGLT2 Inhibitors** ⭐:
  - **Empagliflozin** (EMPEROR-Preserved): 21% reduction in CV death/HF hospitalization
  - **Dapagliflozin** (DELIVER): Similar benefit
  - **First-line therapy for HFpEF** (2024)
- **MRA**: Spironolactone may have modest benefit (TOPCAT trial)
- **ACE/ARB, Beta-Blockers**: Less evidence than HFrEF, but often used for comorbidities

---

### Diabetic Cardiorenal Syndrome

**High-Risk Population:**
- Diabetes is leading cause of both CKD and heart failure

**Management:**
- **SGLT2 Inhibitor** ⭐ **FIRST-LINE**:
  - Empagliflozin, dapagliflozin, or canagliflozin
  - Benefits: Glycemic control + CV + renal protection
- **GLP-1 Receptor Agonist**:
  - Semaglutide (Ozempic), liraglutide (Victoza), dulaglutide (Trulicity)
  - CV benefit, weight loss, renal benefit (modest)
- **Finerenone** (Kerendia):
  - Nonsteroidal MRA for diabetic CKD
  - Reduces CV events and CKD progression
  - 10-20 mg daily
- **ACE/ARB**: If proteinuria >300 mg/day

---

### Post-Transplant Cardiorenal Syndrome

**Heart Transplant Recipients:**
- **CKD Common**: 20-30% develop CKD by 5 years post-transplant
- **Causes**: Calcineurin inhibitor (CNI) nephrotoxicity, diabetes, hypertension
- **Management**:
  - Minimize CNI (tacrolimus) dose
  - SGLT2 inhibitor (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin)
  - BP control

**Kidney Transplant Recipients:**
- **CV Disease Common**: Leading cause of death post-transplant
- **Management**:
  - Statins (all patients)
  - BP control (<130/80 mmHg)
  - SGLT2 inhibitor if diabetes or proteinuria

## Board Exam High-Yield Concepts {#board-exam-concepts}

### Key Facts for Board Exams ⭐

**Ronco Classification:**
- **Type 1**: Acute heart → acute kidney (ADHF → AKI)
- **Type 2**: Chronic heart → chronic kidney (CHF → CKD)
- **Type 3**: Acute kidney → acute heart (AKI → pulmonary edema, arrhythmias)
- **Type 4**: Chronic kidney → chronic heart (CKD → LVH, CAD)
- **Type 5**: Systemic disease → both (sepsis, diabetes, lupus)

**Pathophysiology - Key Mechanisms:**
- **Venous Congestion** (elevated CVP) may be MORE important than low cardiac output
- CVP >12-15 mmHg predicts worsening renal function
- RAAS activation, SNS activation, ADH excess
- Inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6)

**Biomarkers:**
- **BNP >100 pg/mL or NT-proBNP >300 pg/mL**: Heart failure
  - Caveat: Chronically elevated in CKD
- **NGAL >150 ng/mL**: Predicts AKI in acute heart failure

**Diuretic Resistance:**
- **First-line**: Increase loop diuretic dose or switch to continuous infusion
- **Sequential Nephron Blockade**: Add thiazide (metolazone 2.5-10 mg)
- **Acetazolamide**: If metabolic alkalosis (bicarb >32 mEq/L)

**SGLT2 Inhibitors:**
- **Empagliflozin** or **Dapagliflozin** 10 mg daily
- Indications: HFrEF, HFpEF, CKD (eGFR ≥20)
- Benefits: 25-30% reduction in HF hospitalizations, 30-40% reduction in CKD progression

**Ultrafiltration (Aquadex):**
- Indications: Diuretic-resistant volume overload
- Mechanism: Isolated ultrafiltration, removes isotonic fluid
- Not first-line (diuretics remain first-line)

**Type 1 CRS Management:**
- **Goal**: Decongestion (reduce CVP)
- Accept 20-30% creatinine rise if patient decongesting
- Continue ACE/ARB if tolerated (hold if Cr rises >30-50%)

**Type 3 CRS - Urgent Dialysis (AEIOU):**
- **A**cidosis (pH <7.1), **E**lectrolytes (K >6.5), **I**ngestions, **O**verload (pulmonary edema), **U**remia (pericarditis)

**Type 4 CRS:**
- CKD-MBD (FGF-23, hyperphosphatemia) → LVH, vascular calcification
- Leading cause of death in CKD is cardiovascular disease (40-50%)

## Key Practice Points {#key-practice-points}

1. **Cardiorenal syndrome is bidirectional**: Heart failure worsens kidney function, and kidney disease worsens heart function.

2. **Ronco Classification helps guide management**: Identify the type (1-5) to determine primary intervention target.

3. **Venous congestion (elevated CVP) may be more important than low cardiac output** in causing renal dysfunction.

4. **Decongestion is the primary goal in Type 1 CRS**: Accept transient creatinine rise (20-30%) if patient is clinically improving and decongesting.

5. **Diuretic resistance is common**: Use sequential nephron blockade (loop + thiazide) or continuous loop infusion if bolus dosing inadequate.

6. **SGLT2 inhibitors are game-changers**: Start in all HFrEF, HFpEF, and CKD patients (eGFR ≥20 mL/min). Benefits both cardiac and renal outcomes.

7. **Continue RAAS inhibitors (ACE/ARB) if tolerated**: Hold only if creatinine rises >30-50% or hyperkalemia >5.5 mEq/L.

8. **Quadruple therapy for HFrEF (2024 standard)**: ARNI or ACE/ARB + beta-blocker + MRA + SGLT2 inhibitor.

9. **Ultrafiltration (Aquadex) is for diuretic-resistant cases**: Not first-line, but effective when diuretics fail.

10. **BNP/NT-proBNP are useful but interpret with caution in CKD**: Chronically elevated in kidney disease, use change from baseline or trends.

11. **Type 3 CRS requires urgent dialysis** if AEIOU criteria met (acidosis, electrolytes, ingestions, overload, uremia).

12. **Type 4 CRS: CKD drives cardiovascular disease**: Manage CKD-MBD (phosphate binders, vitamin D), anemia (IV iron, ESAs), and CV risk factors.

13. **HFpEF is closely linked to CKD**: 60-70% of HFpEF patients have CKD. SGLT2 inhibitors are first-line therapy.

14. **Diabetic cardiorenal syndrome**: SGLT2 inhibitor + GLP-1 RA + finerenone provide triple protection (glycemic, CV, renal).

15. **Monitor for hyperkalemia with RAAS inhibitors + MRAs in CKD**: Use potassium binders (patiromer, sodium zirconium cyclosilicate) to allow continuation of life-saving therapies.
