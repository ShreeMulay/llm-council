# Glomerular Diseases - Comprehensive Clinical Guide

## Metadata
**Core Domain:** Clinical Nephrology I  
**Difficulty Level:** Intermediate-Advanced  
**Frequency of Use:** Weekly-Daily  
**Clinical Impact:** High  
**Regulatory Requirements:** None specific  
**Prerequisites:** Immunology, pathology basics, renal physiology  
**Competency Development Time:** 12-24 months  
**Cross-references:** Kidney biopsy (Domain IV.11), Immunosuppression (Domain V.12), Transplantation (Domain III)

## 1. Glomerular Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Normal Glomerular Structure

#### Cellular Components
**Podocytes:**
- Visceral epithelial cells
- Primary and secondary foot processes
- Slit diaphragm proteins (nephrin, podocin)
- Synthesize GBM components
- Limited regenerative capacity

**Glomerular Endothelial Cells:**
- Fenestrated endothelium
- Glycocalyx layer
- Produce nitric oxide, prostacyclin
- Anti-thrombotic surface
- Source of VEGF for podocytes

**Mesangial Cells:**
- Contractile properties
- Phagocytic function
- Matrix production
- Support glomerular capillaries
- Regulate glomerular filtration surface area

**Parietal Epithelial Cells:**
- Line Bowman's capsule
- Can become activated in disease
- Contribute to crescent formation
- Potential progenitor cell function

#### Extracellular Matrix
**Glomerular Basement Membrane (GBM):**
- Type IV collagen network
- Heparan sulfate proteoglycans
- Laminin, entactin, nidogen
- Charge and size barrier
- 300-350 nm thickness

**Mesangial Matrix:**
- Type IV collagen
- Fibronectin, laminin
- Proteoglycans
- Provides structural support

### Pathophysiological Mechanisms

#### Immunologic Injury
**Humoral Immunity:**
- Antibody deposition (in situ, circulating)
- Complement activation
- Immune complex formation
- Anti-GBM antibodies

**Cellular Immunity:**
- T-cell mediated injury
- Cytokine release
- Macrophage infiltration
- Direct cellular cytotoxicity

#### Non-Immunologic Injury
**Hemodynamic Factors:**
- Hyperfiltration injury
- Glomerular hypertension
- Shear stress
- RAAS activation

**Metabolic Factors:**
- Advanced glycation end-products
- Lipid toxicity
- Hyperglycemia effects
- Insulin resistance

**Genetic Factors:**
- Structural protein mutations
- Complement regulatory defects
- Podocyte protein abnormalities
- APOL1 risk variants

## 2. Clinical Syndromes Classification

### Nephrotic Syndrome

#### Definition & Diagnostic Criteria
**Core Features:**
- Proteinuria >3.5 g/24h (>3.5 g/day or >40 mg/m²/h in children)
- Serum albumin <3.0 g/dL
- Edema (peripheral, periorbital, anasarca)
- Hyperlipidemia (LDL, VLDL elevation)
- Lipiduria (oval fat bodies, fatty casts)

**Pathophysiology:**
- Massive protein loss → hypoalbuminemia
- Decreased plasma oncotic pressure → edema
- Hepatic lipoprotein synthesis ↑ → hyperlipidemia
- Loss of antithrombin III → hypercoagulable state
- Vitamin D binding protein loss → secondary hyperparathyroidism

#### Etiologies by Age
**Children (1-12 years):**
- Minimal change disease (80%)
- FSGS (10-15%)
- Membranous nephropathy (5%)
- Mesangial proliferative GN (<5%)

**Adolescents (13-18 years):**
- FSGS (30-40%)
- Minimal change disease (30-35%)
- Membranous nephropathy (15-20%)
- Lupus nephritis (10-15%)

**Adults (19-40 years):**
- FSGS (35-40%)
- Minimal change disease (20-25%)
- Membranous nephropathy (20-25%)
- Lupus nephritis (10-15%)

**Adults (40-60 years):**
- Membranous nephropathy (35-40%)
- FSGS (25-30%)
- Minimal change disease (15-20%)
- Amyloidosis (10-15%)

**Elderly (>60 years):**
- Membranous nephropathy (30-35%)
- Amyloidosis (25-30%)
- FSGS (20-25%)
- Minimal change disease (10-15%)

### Nephritic Syndrome

#### Definition & Diagnostic Criteria
**Core Features:**
- Hematuria (dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts)
- Proteinuria (sub-nephrotic, <3.5 g/day)
- Hypertension
- Reduced GFR (elevated creatinine)
- Oliguria (may be present)
- Edema (mild to moderate)

**Pathophysiology:**
- Glomerular inflammation → proliferative changes
- Capillary wall damage → RBC leakage
- Reduced filtration surface → GFR decline
- Sodium/water retention → hypertension
- Inflammatory mediators → systemic symptoms

#### Clinical Presentations
**Acute Nephritic Syndrome:**
- Sudden onset (days)
- Gross hematuria (cola-colored)
- Hypertension, edema
- Often post-infectious
- Usually self-limited

**Rapidly Progressive GN:**
- Very rapid decline (days-weeks)
- Severe renal failure
- Often requires dialysis
- Medical emergency
- Multiple etiologies

**Chronic Nephritic Syndrome:**
- Insidious onset (months-years)
- Progressive GFR decline
- Persistent hematuria
- Often leads to CKD

### Asymptomatic Urinary Abnormalities

#### Isolated Hematuria
**Definition:**
- >3 RBCs/hpf on 2+ occasions
- No proteinuria
- Normal renal function
- No hypertension

**Differential Diagnosis:**
- IgA nephropathy
- Thin basement membrane disease
- Alport syndrome (early)
- Hereditary nephritis
- Vascular malformations

#### Isolated Proteinuria
**Definition:**
- Proteinuria <3.5 g/day
- No hematuria
- Normal renal function
- No edema

**Classifications:**
- Orthostatic proteinuria
- Intermittent proteinuria
- Persistent proteinuria
- Exercise-induced

## 3. Primary Glomerular Diseases

### Minimal Change Disease (MCD)

#### Epidemiology & Pathology
**Epidemiology:**
- Most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in children
- 10-15% of adult nephrotic syndrome
- Peak incidence: 2-6 years (children), 16-24 years (adults)
- Male predominance in children (2:1)

**Pathology:**
**Light Microscopy:**
- Normal glomeruli
- No proliferation, no sclerosis
- May show mild tubular vacuolization

**Immunofluorescence:**
- Negative for immune deposits
- No IgG, IgA, IgM, C3 deposition

**Electron Microscopy:**
- Diffuse foot process effacement
- No electron-dense deposits
- No basement membrane abnormalities

#### Clinical Features
**Presentation:**
- Sudden onset nephrotic syndrome
- Selective proteinuria (albumin > IgG)
- Normal renal function
- No hypertension
- May be triggered by infections, allergies

**Laboratory Findings:**
- Proteinuria 3-10 g/day
- Serum albumin <2.5 g/dL
- Normal complement levels
- Negative serologies

#### Treatment & Prognosis
**Children:**
- Prednisone 60 mg/m²/day (max 60 mg) for 4-6 weeks
- Then alternate day for 4-6 weeks
- 80-90% complete remission
- Relapse common (60-70%)

**Adults:**
- Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day for 8-12 weeks
- Slower response than children
- 70-80% remission rate
- Higher relapse rate

**Steroid-Dependent/Resistant:**
- Calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus)
- Mycophenolate mofetil
- Rituximab (emerging)
- Cyclophosphamide (rarely)

**Prognosis:**
- Excellent long-term survival
- <5% progress to CKD/ESRD
- Most complications from treatment, not disease

### Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)

#### Classification & Pathology
**Primary (Idiopathic) FSGS:**
- No identifiable cause
- Often presents with nephrotic syndrome
- May recur after transplant

**Secondary FSGS:**
- Adaptive (hyperfiltration)
- Virus-associated (HIV, parvovirus)
- Drug-induced (pamidronate, interferon)
- Genetic forms

**Histologic Variants:**
**Collapsing Variant:**
- Collapsed glomerular tuft
- Hyperplastic podocytes
- Poor prognosis
- Associated with HIV, APOL1

**Tip Variant:**
- Segmental sclerosis at tubular pole
- Best prognosis
- High remission rate
- Nephrotic presentation

**Cellular Variant:**
- Endocapillary hypercellularity
- Foam cells, macrophages
- Intermediate prognosis
- May progress to collapsing

**Perihilar Variant:**
- Sclerosis at vascular pole
- Associated with hyperfiltration
- Common in obesity, reflux
- Moderate prognosis

**Not Otherwise Specified (NOS):**
- Mixed features
- Most common variant
- Variable prognosis

#### Clinical Features
**Presentation:**
- Nephrotic syndrome (70-80%)
- Non-nephrotic proteinuria (20-30%)
- Hypertension common
- Reduced GFR at presentation (30-40%)
- Hematuria may be present

**Risk Factors:**
- African American ethnicity
- APOL1 risk variants
- Obesity
- Low birth weight
- Premature birth

#### Treatment Strategies
**First-Line Therapy:**
- High-dose prednisone (1 mg/kg/day for 12-16 weeks)
- Consider extended course (up to 6 months)
- Monitor for steroid toxicity

**Steroid-Resistant Disease:**
**Calcineurin Inhibitors:**
- Cyclosporine 3-5 mg/kg/day
- Tacrolimus 0.05-0.1 mg/kg/day
- Therapeutic drug monitoring
- Combine with low-dose steroids

**Mycophenolate Mofetil:**
- 1000-1500 mg twice daily
- Good steroid-sparing agent
- Less nephrotoxic than CNIs
- May be combined with CNIs

**Rituximab:**
- 375 mg/m² weekly × 4 doses
- Or 1 g × 2 doses (2 weeks apart)
- Effective in some cases
- Consider in CNI-intolerant patients

**Supportive Care:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs
- SGLT2 inhibitors
- Diuretics for edema
- Statins for hyperlipidemia

#### Prognosis
**Response to Treatment:**
- Complete remission: 5-10% (primary), 20-30% (secondary)
- Partial remission: 20-30%
- No response: 60-70%

**Long-term Outcomes:**
- 50% progress to ESRD in 10 years
- Better prognosis with remission
- Worse prognosis with collapsing variant
- High recurrence after transplant (30-40%)

### Membranous Nephropathy (MN)

#### Pathogenesis & Classification
**Primary MN:**
- Anti-PLA2R antibodies (70-80%)
- Anti-THSD7A antibodies (5-10%)
- Other antigens being identified
- Autoimmune disease mechanism

**Secondary MN:**
**Infections:**
- Hepatitis B (most common)
- Hepatitis C
- Syphilis
- Malaria

**Autoimmune:**
- Lupus nephritis (Class V)
- Rheumatoid arthritis
- Sjögren's syndrome
- Sarcoidosis

**Malignancy:**
- Lung cancer (most common)
- Colon cancer
- Prostate cancer
- Hematologic malignancies

**Drugs:**
- NSAIDs
- Penicillamine
- Gold salts
- Anti-TNF agents

#### Pathology
**Light Microscopy:**
- Thickened glomerular basement membrane
- Subepithelial deposits
- "Spike and hole" appearance on silver stain
- No cellular proliferation

**Immunofluorescence:**
- Granular IgG deposition along capillary loops
- C3 often present
- IgG4 predominance in primary MN

**Electron Microscopy:**
- Subepithelial electron-dense deposits
- Podocyte foot process effacement
- Basement membrane reaction (spike formation)

#### Clinical Features
**Presentation:**
- Nephrotic syndrome (80%)
- Edema, proteinuria >3.5 g/day
- Hypertension (30-40%)
- Normal or mildly reduced GFR
- May have microscopic hematuria

**Laboratory Findings:**
- Anti-PLA2R antibodies (if primary)
- Low complement (rare, usually secondary)
- Hyperlipidemia
- Hypercoagulable state

#### Risk Stratification
**Prognostic Factors:**
- Persistent proteinuria >8 g/day
- Age >50 years
- Male gender
- Reduced GFR at presentation
- Anti-PLA2R antibody titer

**Risk Categories (KDIGO):**
- Low risk: Proteinuria <4 g/day, normal GFR
- Medium risk: Proteinuria 4-8 g/day, normal GFR
- High risk: Proteinuria >8 g/day or reduced GFR

#### Treatment Approach
**Conservative Management (Low Risk):**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs
- SGLT2 inhibitors
- Diuretics, statins
- Observe for 6 months

**Immunosuppression (Medium/High Risk):**
**Rituximab-Based:**
- Rituximab 1 g on days 1 and 15
- Or 375 mg/m² weekly × 4
- Repeat based on B-cell depletion
- First-line per KDIGO 2021

**Alternatives:**
- Cyclophosphamide + steroids (Ponticelli regimen)
- Calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus)
- Mycophenolate (less effective)

**Ponticelli Regimen:**
- Month 1: Methylprednisolone 1 g IV × 3 days + oral prednisone 0.5 mg/kg/day + cyclophosphamide 2 mg/kg/day
- Month 2: Prednisone 0.5 mg/kg/day
- Alternate months for 6 months

#### Prognosis
**Spontaneous Remission:**
- 20-30% within 2 years
- More common in low-risk patients
- Monitor anti-PLA2R titers

**Treatment Response:**
- Complete remission: 60-70% with rituximab
- Partial remission: 20-30%
- Relapse rate: 20-30%

**Long-term Outcomes:**
- 10-year renal survival: 70-80%
- Better with early remission
- Worse with persistent high proteinuria

### IgA Nephropathy (Berger's Disease)

#### Epidemiology & Pathogenesis
**Epidemiology:**
- Most common primary glomerulonephritis worldwide
- Peak incidence: 20-30 years
- Male predominance (2-3:1)
- Higher prevalence in Asian populations

**Pathogenesis:**
- Aberrant IgA1 glycosylation
- Formation of anti-glycan antibodies
- Immune complex deposition
- Complement activation (alternative pathway)
- Mucosal immunity dysfunction

#### Pathology
**Light Microscopy:**
- Mesangial hypercellularity
- Matrix expansion
- May have segmental sclerosis
- Crescent formation in severe disease

**Immunofluorescence:**
- Dominant mesangial IgA deposition
- IgG and C3 often present
- Capillary loop deposition in severe disease

**Electron Microscopy:**
- Mesangial electron-dense deposits
- Paramesangial deposits
- Foot process effacement (variable)

**Oxford Classification (MEST-C):**
**M (Mesangial hypercellularity):**
- M0: ≤50% glomeruli with >3 mesangial cells
- M1: >50% glomeruli with >3 mesangial cells

**E (Endocapillary hypercellularity):**
- E0: Absent
- E1: Present

**S (Segmental sclerosis):**
- S0: Absent
- S1: Present

**T (Tubular atrophy/interstitial fibrosis):**
- T0: ≤25% of cortical area
- T1: 26-50% of cortical area
- T2: >50% of cortical area

**C (Crescents):**
- C0: No crescents
- C1: Crescents in <25% of glomeruli
- C2: Crescents in ≥25% of glomeruli

#### Clinical Presentation
**Spectrum of Disease:**
- Asymptomatic hematuria (40-50%)
- Nephritic syndrome (30-40%)
- Nephrotic syndrome (10-15%)
- Rapidly progressive GN (5-10%)

**Typical Features:**
- Gross hematuria after URI (synpharyngitic)
- Flank pain with gross hematuria
- Normal complement levels
- May have elevated IgA levels

#### Prognostic Assessment
**Risk Factors for Progression:**
- Proteinuria >1 g/day
- Hypertension
- Reduced GFR at presentation
- Oxford MEST-C score
- Persistent hematuria

**Risk Categories:**
- Low risk: Proteinuria <0.5 g/day, normal GFR
- Medium risk: Proteinuria 0.5-1 g/day
- High risk: Proteinuria >1 g/day or reduced GFR

#### Treatment Strategies
**Supportive Care (All Patients):**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs (max tolerated dose)
- SGLT2 inhibitors (if eGFR >30)
- Blood pressure <130/80 mmHg
- Statins if indicated
- Lifestyle modifications

**Immunosuppression (High Risk):**
**Corticosteroids:**
- Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day for 2 months
- Then taper over 4-6 months
- Consider pulse steroids for RPGN

**Mycophenolate Mofetil:**
- 1000-1500 mg twice daily
- For steroid-sparing or intolerance
- 6-12 months duration

**Azathioprine:**
- 2 mg/kg/day
- Alternative to MMF
- Monitor TPMT levels

**Targeted Therapies:**
- Budesonide-targeted release (Nefecon)
- Complement inhibitors (experimental)
- Anti-APRIL antibodies (experimental)

**RPGN Treatment:**
- Pulse methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg daily × 3 days
- Cyclophosphamide or rituximab
- Plasmapheresis if anti-GBM coexistence

#### Prognosis
**Natural History:**
- 20-30% progress to ESRD in 20 years
- 10-20% have complete remission
- 30-40% have stable disease
- Variable course

**Impact of Treatment:**
- Improved outcomes with early intervention
- SGLT2 inhibitors reduce progression by 30-40%
- Better prognosis with proteinuria reduction

## 4. Secondary Glomerular Diseases

### Lupus Nephritis

#### Classification System (ISN/RPS 2003)
**Class I: Minimal Mesangial LN**
- Normal LM
- Mesangial IF deposits
- Excellent prognosis

**Class II: Mesangial Proliferative LN**
- Mesangial hypercellularity
- Mesangial immune deposits
- Good prognosis

**Class III: Focal LN**
- <50% glomeruli involved
- Active or chronic lesions
- Subdivided by activity/chronicity

**Class IV: Diffuse LN**
- ≥50% glomeruli involved
- Most severe form
- Subdivided into segmental (S) and global (G)

**Class V: Membranous LN**
- Subepithelial immune deposits
- May coexist with Class III/IV
- Variable prognosis

**Class VI: Advanced Sclerosing LN**
- >90% glomeruli sclerosed
- Irreversible damage
- Poor prognosis

#### Clinical Features
**Systemic Features:**
- Malar rash, photosensitivity
- Arthritis, serositis
- Neurologic involvement
- Hematologic abnormalities

**Renal Features:**
- Proteinuria (nephrotic in Class V)
- Hematuria, RBC casts
- Reduced GFR
- Hypertension

**Laboratory Findings:**
- Positive ANA (always)
- Anti-dsDNA antibodies
- Low complement (C3, C4)
- Elevated ESR/CRP

#### Treatment Protocols
**Induction Therapy (3-6 months):**
**Class III/IV:**
- MMF 2-3 g/day divided BID
- OR low-dose IV cyclophosphamide (Euro-Lupus)
- PLUS high-dose steroids
- Consider belimumab addition

**Class V:**
- MMF or calcineurin inhibitors
- Plus steroids
- Longer induction period

**Maintenance Therapy (2-3 years):**
- MMF 1-2 g/day
- OR azathioprine 2 mg/kg/day
- Low-dose steroids (5-10 mg prednisone)
- Consider belimumab

**Refractory Disease:**
- Rituximab 1 g × 2 doses (2 weeks apart)
- Consider cyclophosphamide retreatment
- Voclosporin (new CNI option)

#### Monitoring & Prognosis
**Response Criteria:**
- Complete response: Proteinuria <0.5 g/day, normal GFR
- Partial response: ≥50% proteinuria reduction, stable GFR
- No response: <50% proteinuria reduction

**Long-term Outcomes:**
- 10-year renal survival: 80-90%
- Better with early treatment
- Worse with African American ethnicity
- Flare rate: 30-40% in 5 years

### Diabetic Nephropathy

#### Natural History
**Stage 1: Hyperfiltration**
- GFR >120 mL/min
- No structural changes
- Reversible with good control

**Stage 2: Silent Stage**
- Microalbuminuria (30-300 mg/day)
- Normal GFR
- Early structural changes

**Stage 3: Overt Nephropathy**
- Macroalbuminuria (>300 mg/day)
- GFR decline begins
- Hypertension common

**Stage 4: Advanced Nephropathy**
- GFR 15-29 mL/min
- Nephrotic-range proteinuria
- Hypertension severe

**Stage 5: ESRD**
- GFR <15 mL/min
- Need for renal replacement

#### Pathology
**Light Microscopy:**
- Mesangial expansion
- Basement membrane thickening
- Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules (advanced)
- Arteriolar hyalinosis

**Immunofluorescence:**
- Linear IgG along GBM (non-specific)
- IgM in mesangium (hyalinosis)
- No immune complex deposition

**Electron Microscopy:**
- GBM thickening (400-1000 nm)
- Mesangial matrix expansion
- Podocyte foot process effacement

#### Treatment Strategies
**Glycemic Control:**
- Target HbA1c 7-8%
- SGLT2 inhibitors (first-line)
- GLP-1 receptor agonists
- Consider early insulin

**Blood Pressure Control:**
- Target <130/80 mmHg
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs
- SGLT2 inhibitors
- Multiple agents often needed

**SGLT2 Inhibitors:**
- Dapagliflozin, empagliflozin, canagliflozin
- Cardiovascular and renal benefits
- Can be used to eGFR 20 mL/min
- Reduce progression by 30-40%

**GLP-1 Receptor Agonists:**
- Liraglutide, semaglutide, dulaglutide
- Cardiovascular benefits
- Weight loss
- Modest renal benefits

**Non-Steroidal MRAs:**
- Finerenone (FIDELIO-DKD, FIGARO-DKD)
- Cardio-renal benefits
- Hyperkalemia monitoring
- Use with ACEi/ARB

#### Prognosis
**Risk Factors for Progression:**
- Poor glycemic control
- Uncontrolled hypertension
- High proteinuria
- Genetic factors (APOL1)
- Smoking

**Outcomes:**
- Leading cause of ESRD worldwide
- 20-40% progress to ESRD in 20 years
- Better outcomes with modern therapy
- Cardiovascular mortality higher than ESRD

### Amyloidosis

#### Types & Classification
**AL Amyloidosis (Primary):**
- Light chain production by plasma cells
- Lambda light chains more common
- Associated with multiple myeloma
- Rapid progression

**AA Amyloidosis (Secondary):**
- Serum amyloid A protein
- Chronic inflammatory conditions
- Rheumatoid arthritis, IBD, infections
- Slower progression

**ATTR Amyloidosis:**
- Transthyretin protein
- Hereditary or wild-type
- Cardiac involvement common
- New therapies available

#### Clinical Features
**Renal Presentation:**
- Nephrotic syndrome (90%)
- Progressive renal failure
- Hypertension (less common)
- May have restrictive cardiomyopathy

**Systemic Features:**
- Macroglossia (AL)
- Periorbital purpura (AL)
- Hepatomegaly
- Neuropathy
- Cardiac involvement

#### Diagnosis
**Laboratory Findings:**
- Proteinuria (nephrotic range)
- Serum/urine protein electrophoresis
- Free light chain assay
- Serum amyloid A (AA type)

**Biopsy Findings:**
**Congo Red Stain:**
- Apple-green birefringence under polarized light
- Amyloid deposition in mesangium, vessels
- Congo red positivity required for diagnosis

**Immunofluorescence:**
- Light chain restriction (AL)
- Negative for AA in AL type
- Mass spectrometry for typing

#### Treatment
**AL Amyloidosis:**
- Autologous stem cell transplant (if eligible)
- Bortezomib-based regimens
- Daratumumab (anti-CD38)
- Supportive care

**AA Amyloidosis:**
- Treat underlying inflammatory condition
- Anti-IL-1 agents (canakinumab)
- Tocilizumab (anti-IL-6)
- Eprodisate (experimental)

**ATTR Amyloidosis:**
- Tafamidis (TTR stabilizer)
- Patisiran, inotersen (RNA silencing)
- Liver transplantation (hereditary)

**Renal-Specific:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs
- Diuretics for edema
- Anticoagulation if nephrotic
- Early transplant evaluation

#### Prognosis
**AL Amyloidosis:**
- Median survival 12-18 months (untreated)
- Improved to 5+ years with modern therapy
- Cardiac involvement determines prognosis
- Renal response predicts overall response

**AA Amyloidosis:**
- Better prognosis than AL
- Dependent on underlying disease control
- 5-year survival 50-70%
- Renal survival variable

## 5. Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN)

### Classification & Etiology

#### Type I: Anti-GBM Disease
**Pathogenesis:**
- Anti-glomerular basement membrane antibodies
- Linear IgG deposition along GBM
- Associated with pulmonary involvement (Goodpasture's)
- No immune complex deposition

**Clinical Features:**
- Rapid renal failure (days-weeks)
- Pulmonary hemorrhage (50-60%)
- Hemoptysis, dyspnea
- Severe anemia

**Laboratory Findings:**
- Positive anti-GBM antibodies
- Linear IF on biopsy
- No complement consumption
- High creatinine at presentation

#### Type II: Immune Complex GN
**Etiologies:**
- Post-infectious GN
- Lupus nephritis
- IgA nephropathy
- Cryoglobulinemic GN
- Essential mixed cryoglobulinemia

**Pathology:**
- Granular immune complex deposition
- "Lumpy-bumpy" IF pattern
- Complement activation
- Crescent formation

#### Type III: Pauci-Immune GN
**ANCA-Associated Vasculitis:**
**Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA):**
- c-ANCA (PR3-ANCA) positive
- Upper airway, lung, kidney involvement
- Granulomatous inflammation

**Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA):**
- p-ANCA (MPO-ANCA) positive
- Pulmonary-renal syndrome
- No granulomas

**Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (EGPA):**
- Asthma, eosinophilia
- p-ANCA positive (50%)
- Allergic granulomatosis

### Clinical Presentation

#### Renal Manifestations
- Rapid rise in creatinine (days)
- Oliguria or anuria
- Active urinary sediment
- Hematuria, RBC casts
- Proteinuria (usually sub-nephrotic)

#### Extrarenal Manifestations
**Pulmonary:**
- Hemoptysis (anti-GBM, ANCA)
- Pulmonary infiltrates
- Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage

**Systemic:**
- Fever, weight loss
- Arthralgias, myalgias
- Skin lesions (purpura, ulcers)
- Neurologic involvement

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Urgent Evaluation
**Laboratory Tests:**
- Anti-GBM antibodies
- ANCA panel (PR3, MPO)
- Complement levels (C3, C4)
- Hepatitis serologies
- Cryoglobulins

**Imaging:**
- Chest X-ray/CT (pulmonary involvement)
- Sinus CT (GPA)
- Consider PET scan for systemic disease

#### Kidney Biopsy
**Indications:**
- All suspected RPGN cases
- Guide treatment decisions
- Determine prognosis
- Rule out other causes

**Findings:**
- Cellular crescents (>50% glomeruli)
- Fibrinoid necrosis
- Breaks in GBM
- IF pattern determines type

### Treatment Protocols

#### General Principles
- Urgent treatment required
- Plasmapheresis for severe cases
- High-dose immunosuppression
- Supportive care (dialysis if needed)

#### Anti-GBM Disease
**Induction:**
- Plasmapheresis daily × 14 days, then every 3 days × 3 weeks
- Methylprednisolone 1 g IV daily × 3 days
- Cyclophosphamide 2 mg/kg/day
- Then oral prednisone taper

**Maintenance:**
- No maintenance therapy needed
- Monitor for relapse (rare)
- Consider transplant after 6-12 months

#### ANCA-Associated Vasculitis
**Induction:**
**CYCLOPS Regimen:**
- Pulse cyclophosphamide 15 mg/kg every 2-3 weeks × 6 doses
- Plus high-dose steroids
- Equivalent efficacy to daily oral

**Rituximab Regimen:**
- Rituximab 375 mg/m² weekly × 4 doses
- Or 1 g × 2 doses (2 weeks apart)
- Non-inferior to cyclophosphamide
- Preferred for relapsing disease

**Plasmapheresis:**
- Consider for creatinine >5.6 mg/dL
- Pulmonary hemorrhage
- Anti-GBM overlap

**Maintenance:**
- Azathioprine 2 mg/kg/day for 18-24 months
- OR methotrexate 20-25 mg weekly
- OR rituximab every 6 months
- Low-dose steroids (5-10 mg)

#### Immune Complex RPGN
- Treat underlying disease
- High-dose steroids
- Cyclophosphamide or MMF
- Plasmapheresis if anti-GBM overlap

### Prognosis & Outcomes

#### Predictors of Poor Outcome
- Creatinine >5.6 mg/dL at presentation
- Oliguria/anuria
- >50% crescents on biopsy
- Advanced age
- Delay in treatment

#### Recovery Rates
**Anti-GBM Disease:**
- 40% recover renal function if treated early
- <10% if creatinine >5.6 mg/dL
- Pulmonary recovery better than renal

**ANCA Vasculitis:**
- 70-80% achieve remission
- 50% maintain renal function
- Relapse rate 30-50%

**Immune Complex:**
- Variable based on underlying disease
- Better prognosis than anti-GBM
- Dependent on early treatment

## 6. Special Situations & Complications

### Pregnancy and Glomerular Disease

#### Pre-conception Counseling
- Disease quiescence >6 months preferred
- Medication safety review
- Prognosis discussion
- Genetic counseling if indicated

#### Medication Adjustments
**Continue:**
- Prednisone (lowest effective dose)
- Azathioprine
- Calcium channel blockers
- Hydroxychloroquine (lupus)

**Discontinue:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs
- Mycophenolate mofetil
- Cyclophosphamide
- Methotrexate

**Alternatives:**
- Azathioprine instead of MMF
- Low-dose steroids
- Plasma exchange if needed

#### Monitoring
- Monthly renal function
- Blood pressure control
- Proteinuria monitoring
- Fetal growth assessment

### Infection-Related GN

#### Post-Infectious GN
**Typical Pathogens:**
- Streptococcus pyogenes (pharyngitis, skin)
- Staphylococcus aureus
- Hepatitis B/C
- HIV

**Clinical Features:**
- 1-3 weeks after infection
- Nephritic syndrome
- Low complement (C3)
- Usually self-limited

**Treatment:**
- Supportive care
- Treat underlying infection
- Consider steroids if severe
- Monitor for progression

#### Hepatitis-Related GN
**Hepatitis B:**
- Membranous nephropathy pattern
- Polyarteritis nodosa (HBV-PAN)
- Antiviral therapy essential
- Consider interferon, nucleos(t)ide analogs

**Hepatitis C:**
- Membranoproliferative GN pattern
- Cryoglobulinemic GN
- Direct-acting antivirals
- Consider rituximab for cryoglobulinemia

### Monoclonal Gammopathy-Related GN

#### Types of Disease
**AL Amyloidosis:**
- Light chain deposition
- Systemic involvement
- Rapid progression

**Light Chain Deposition Disease:**
- Non-amyloid deposits
- Nodular sclerosis
- Better prognosis than AL

**Immunotactoid Glomerulopathy:**
- Organized deposits
- Associated with lymphoproliferative disorders
- Variable prognosis

**Fibrillary GN:**
- Randomly oriented fibrils
- Congo red negative
- Often progresses to ESRD

#### Diagnostic Approach
- Serum/urine protein electrophoresis
- Free light chain assay
- Bone marrow biopsy
- Kidney biopsy with EM
- Flow cytometry if needed

#### Treatment Strategies
- Treat underlying plasma cell disorder
- Bortezomib-based regimens
- Daratumumab (anti-CD38)
- Consider autologous stem cell transplant
- Supportive renal care

## 7. Treatment Monitoring & Toxicity

### Immunosuppressive Monitoring

#### Corticosteroids
**Efficacy Monitoring:**
- Proteinuria reduction
- Renal function stabilization
- Serologic markers (anti-PLA2R, ANCA)
- Clinical symptoms

**Toxicity Monitoring:**
- Blood glucose, HbA1c
- Blood pressure
- Bone density (DEXA scan)
- Mood changes, cataracts
- Infection surveillance

#### Calcineurin Inhibitors
**Therapeutic Drug Monitoring:**
- Cyclosporine: trough 100-200 ng/mL
- Tacrolimus: trough 5-10 ng/mL
- Draw 12 hours post-dose
- Monitor levels weekly initially

**Toxicity Monitoring:**
- Serum creatinine (nephrotoxicity)
- Blood pressure
- Magnesium, potassium
- Lipid profile
- Neurologic symptoms

#### Mycophenolate Mofetil
**Monitoring:**
- CBC weekly for first month
- Liver function tests
- GI side effects
- Consider level monitoring in transplant

**Toxicity:**
- Bone marrow suppression
- GI intolerance
- Increased infection risk
- Potential teratogenicity

#### Cyclophosphamide
**Monitoring:**
- CBC weekly
- Urinalysis for hemorrhagic cystitis
- Liver function tests
- Pregnancy testing

**Long-term Toxicity:**
- Gonadal toxicity (sperm banking)
- Hemorrhagic cystitis (mesna protection)
- Secondary malignancies
- Pulmonary fibrosis

#### Rituximab
**Monitoring:**
- B-cell counts (CD19/20)
- IgG levels
- Hepatitis B reactivation
- Infusion reactions

**Toxicity:**
- Infusion reactions
- Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (rare)
- Hepatitis B reactivation
- Hypogammaglobulinemia

### Response Assessment

#### Proteinuria Response
**Complete Remission:**
- Proteinuria <0.3 g/day (nephrotic diseases)
- <0.5 g/day (non-nephrotic diseases)
- Stable or improved GFR

**Partial Remission:**
- ≥50% reduction in proteinuria
- Proteinuria <3.5 g/day (if initially nephrotic)
- Stable GFR (<25% decline)

**No Response:**
- <50% proteinuria reduction
- Progressive GFR decline
- Persistent active sediment

#### Time to Response
- Minimal change disease: 2-8 weeks
- Membranous nephropathy: 3-6 months
- FSGS: 3-6 months
- Lupus nephritis: 3-6 months
- ANCA vasculitis: 2-4 months

#### Relapse Monitoring
- Regular proteinuria testing
- Clinical symptom review
- Medication adherence assessment
- Consider biomarker trends

- Danicopan (oral alternative)

- Rituximab (established)

- Inebilizumab

## 9. Clinical Algorithms & Decision Trees

### Nephrotic Syndrome Evaluation

```
Nephrotic Syndrome (Proteinuria >3.5 g/day, Albumin <3.0 g/dL)
        ↓
Initial Evaluation:
- Age, ethnicity, comorbidities
- Medication review
- Infection screen
- Autoimmune serologies
        ↓
Secondary Cause Workup:
- Hepatitis B/C, HIV
- ANA, dsDNA (lupus)
- Serum/urine electrophoresis
- Blood glucose, HbA1c
        ↓
Primary Disease Suspicion → Kidney Biopsy
        ↓
Pathology-Directed Treatment:
- MCD → High-dose steroids
- FSGS → Steroids → CNI/MMF
- MN → Anti-PLA2R testing → Rituximab/Conservative
- Amyloidosis → Type → Disease-specific therapy
```

### RPGN Emergency Management

```
Rapidly Progressive GN (Creatinine rise >0.5 mg/day × 2+ days)
        ↓
Urgent Laboratory Evaluation:
- Anti-GBM antibodies
- ANCA panel (PR3, MPO)
- Complement levels
- Hepatitis serologies
        ↓
Empiric Therapy While Awaiting Results:
- Methylprednisolone 1 g IV daily × 3 days
- Consider plasmapheresis if severe
- Prepare for kidney biopsy
        ↓
Targeted Therapy Based on Results:
- Anti-GBM positive → Plasmapheresis + cyclophosphamide
- ANCA positive → Rituximab or cyclophosphamide
- Immune complex → Treat underlying disease
        ↓
Maintenance Therapy (18-24 months)
```

## 10. Key Take-Home Points

1. **Syndrome Recognition**: Differentiate nephrotic vs nephritic syndromes for initial approach
2. **Age-Specific Etiologies**: Consider age when evaluating nephrotic syndrome causes
3. **Biopsy Indications**: Most glomerular diseases require kidney biopsy for definitive diagnosis
4. **Treatment Individualization**: Tailor therapy to specific pathology, not just syndrome
5. **SGLT2 Inhibitors**: Use across most glomerular diseases for cardio-renal protection
6. **RPGN as Emergency**: Rapid recognition and treatment essential for renal recovery
7. **Toxicity Monitoring**: Regular monitoring essential for all immunosuppressive therapy
8. **Relapse Planning**: Anticipate and monitor for disease relapse after remission
9. **Multidisciplinary Care**: Collaborate with rheumatology, hepatology, oncology as needed
10. **Emerging Therapies**: Stay current with new biologic agents and targeted treatments
