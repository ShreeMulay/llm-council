## Protein-Energy Wasting (PEW)

### Definition and Pathophysiology

#### International Society of Renal Nutrition and Metabolism (ISRNM) Criteria
```
PEW Diagnosis (requires at least 1 from 4 categories AND 1 from 3 categories):

Serum Chemistry:
- Serum albumin <3.8 g/dL
- Prealbumin <30 mg/dL
- Cholesterol <100 mg/dL

Body Mass:
- BMI <23 kg/m²
- Unintentional weight loss >5% in 3 months or >10% in 6 months
- Total body fat percentage <10%

Muscle Mass:
- Reduced muscle mass (mid-arm muscle circumference, creatinine index)
- Reduced lean body mass (DXA, BIA)

Dietary Intake:
- Unintentional low protein intake <0.8 g/kg/day for >2 months
- Unintentional low calorie intake <25 kcal/kg/day for >2 months
```

#### Pathophysiologic Mechanisms
- **Metabolic Acidosis**: Increased protein catabolism, muscle wasting
- **Inflammation**: Cytokine-mediated catabolism (IL-6, TNF-α)
- **Hormonal Changes**: Insulin resistance, increased glucagon
- **Dialysis-Related Factors**: Amino acid losses, nutrient removal
- **Anorexia**: Uremic toxins, altered taste, depression

### Assessment and Monitoring

#### Nutritional Screening Tools
- **Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool (MUST)**:
  - BMI score, weight loss score, acute disease effect score
  - Overall risk: Low, medium, high
- **Subjective Global Assessment (SGA)**:
  - Medical history, physical examination
  - Classification: Well-nourished (A), mild-moderate (B), severe (C)
- **Dialysis Malnutrition Score (DMS)**: Specific for dialysis patients

#### Anthropometric Measurements
```
Standard Measurements:
- Height: Stadiometer, recumbent if needed
- Weight: Standardized scale, post-dialysis weight for HD patients
- BMI: Weight (kg)/Height (m²)

Body Composition:
- Mid-arm circumference: 25-30 cm (adults)
- Triceps skinfold thickness: 12-15 mm (men), 16-20 mm (women)
- Mid-arm muscle circumference: Calculate from MAC and TSF

Advanced Methods:
- Bioelectrical Impedance Analysis (BIA): Phase angle, body composition
- Dual-energy X-ray Absorptiometry (DEXA): Lean mass, fat mass
- CT/MRI: Regional muscle mass (research setting)
```

#### Laboratory Markers
- **Albumin**: Long-term marker (half-life 20 days)
- **Prealbumin**: Short-term marker (half-life 2-3 days)
- **Creatinine Index**: Muscle mass surrogate in dialysis
- **Normalized Protein Catabolic Rate (nPCR)**: Protein intake assessment

## Dietary Management in Chronic Kidney Disease

### Protein Requirements

#### CKD Stages 1-4 (Non-Dialysis) - 2024 Updated Recommendations

```
Conservative Protein Intake:
- Stage 1-2: 0.8-1.0 g/kg/day (DRI for healthy adults)
- Stage 3: 0.6-0.8 g/kg/day (lower protein may slow progression)
- Stage 4: 0.6 g/kg/day with ketoacid analogs OR 0.8 g/kg/day without
- Stage 5 (pre-dialysis): 0.6-0.8 g/kg/day with close monitoring

Protein Quality - Animal vs Plant Proteins (2024 Evidence):

High Biological Value (Animal) Proteins:
- Sources: Eggs, dairy, meat, poultry, fish
- Pros: Complete amino acid profile, high bioavailability
- Cons: Higher phosphorus content, higher acid load
- Recommendation: 50-70% of total protein if using mixed approach

Plant-Based Proteins (2024 Major Update):
- Sources: Legumes, soy, nuts, seeds, whole grains
- Benefits (2024 Meta-Analyses):
  - Lower phosphorus absorption (plant phosphorus = phytate, poorly absorbed)
  - Lower acid load → reduced metabolic acidosis
  - Gut microbiome benefits → lower uremic toxin production
  - Lower cardiovascular risk (fiber, phytochemicals)
- Concerns: Lower bioavailability, need more total protein
- Evidence:
  - 2023 RCT: Plant protein diet → 30% slower eGFR decline vs animal protein
  - 2024 Meta-analysis: Plant-based diet associated with:
    - Lower phosphorus levels (despite similar intake)
    - Lower FGF23 levels
    - Improved gut dysbiosis
- Recommendation (2024 KDOQI):
  - Encourage ≥50% plant-based proteins in CKD stages 3-4
  - Monitor protein adequacy with nPCR, albumin
  - Supplement with vitamin B12 if predominantly plant-based

Ketoacid Analogs (2024 Update):

Products Available:
- Ketosteril (alpha-ketoacid analogs): Most studied
- Dosing: 1 tablet (630 mg)/5 kg body weight divided with meals
  - Example: 70 kg patient = 14 tablets/day

Evidence (2024):
- Meta-analysis (2023): Ketoacid + low protein (0.6 g/kg) vs conventional (0.8 g/kg):
  - Delay dialysis initiation by 3-6 months
  - Maintain nutritional status
  - Reduce uremic symptoms
- Best for: CKD stage 4-5 not yet on dialysis
- Limitations: High cost, pill burden, availability limited in some countries

Monitoring:
- BUN, serum creatinine: Monthly
- Albumin, prealbumin: Every 3 months
- nPCR: Every 3 months
- Adjust protein intake if malnutrition develops
```

#### Dialysis Patients
- **Hemodialysis**: 1.2-1.3 g/kg/day
- **Peritoneal Dialysis**: 1.2-1.5 g/kg/day (higher losses)
- **Home Hemodialysis**: 1.0-1.2 g/kg/day (more frequent treatments)
- **Adjustments**: Increase for catabolic illness, decrease for obesity

### Energy Requirements

#### Basal Energy Needs
```
Harris-Benedict Equation Adjusted for CKD:
- Men: BEE = 66.5 + (13.75 × weight kg) + (5.0 × height cm) - (6.78 × age)
- Women: BEE = 655.1 + (9.56 × weight kg) + (1.85 × height cm) - (4.68 × age)

Activity Factors:
- Sedentary: 1.3
- Light activity: 1.5
- Moderate activity: 1.7
- Heavy activity: 2.0

Total Energy Requirements:
- Non-dialysis CKD: 30-35 kcal/kg/day
- Hemodialysis: 35 kcal/kg/day
- Peritoneal dialysis: 30-35 kcal/kg/day (including dialysate calories)
- Elderly: 25-30 kcal/kg/day
```

#### Special Considerations
- **Obesity**: Calorie restriction for BMI >30 kg/m²
- **Underweight**: Aggressive nutritional support
- **Diabetes**: Complex carbohydrate distribution
- **Post-transplant**: Increased calories for wound healing

### Sodium and Fluid Management

#### Sodium Restriction
```
Sodium Recommendations:
- CKD stages 1-4: <2,300 mg/day
- Dialysis patients: <2,000 mg/day
- Hypertensive patients: <1,500 mg/day

Practical Implementation:
- Avoid processed foods, canned goods
- Limit salt in cooking (no salt shaker)
- Read food labels for sodium content
- Choose fresh or frozen vegetables over canned

Hidden Sodium Sources:
- Deli meats, processed cheeses
- Condiments, sauces, dressings
- Baked goods, bread products
- Restaurant meals, fast food
```

#### Fluid Management
- **Non-dialysis CKD**: No restriction unless fluid overloaded
- **Hemodialysis**: 500-1000 mL/day + urine output
- **Peritoneal dialysis**: Individualized based on ultrafiltration
- **Practical Tips**: 
  - Measure daily fluid intake
  - Limit fluids to 4-6 cups daily
  - Use smaller cups
  - Count foods with high water content

### Potassium Management

#### Dietary Potassium Restriction
```
Potassium Recommendations:
- CKD stages 1-3: No restriction (monitor levels)
- CKD stage 4: 2,000-3,000 mg/day if hyperkalemic
- Dialysis patients: 2,000-3,000 mg/day

High-Potassium Foods to Limit:
- Fruits: Bananas, oranges, melons, avocados
- Vegetables: Potatoes, tomatoes, spinach, beans
- Dairy: Milk, yogurt
- Salt substitutes: Potassium chloride

Low-Potassium Alternatives:
- Fruits: Apples, berries, grapes, pineapple
- Vegetables: Green beans, carrots, cauliflower, lettuce
- Leaching technique: Double boiling vegetables
```

#### Potassium Leaching Technique
- **Method**: Cut vegetables into small pieces, soak in water 2+ hours
- **Double Boiling**: Boil in large water volume, discard water, boil again
- **Effectiveness**: Reduces potassium content by 30-50%
- **Considerations**: May reduce other nutrients

### Phosphorus Management

#### Dietary Phosphorus Restriction
```
Phosphorus Recommendations:
- CKD stages 3-4: 800-1,000 mg/day
- Dialysis patients: 800-1,000 mg/day + binders

High-Phosphorus Foods:
- Dairy products: Milk, cheese, yogurt
- Protein foods: Meat, poultry, fish, eggs
- Nuts and seeds: All varieties
- Legumes: Beans, lentils, peas
- Processed foods: Phosphate additives

Phosphate Additives (Highly Absorbable):
- Cola beverages
- Processed cheese
- Frozen meals
- Bakery products
- Fast food
```

#### Phosphorus-to-Protein Ratio
- **Goal**: <10 mg phosphorus per gram protein
- **Calculation**: Total phosphorus (mg) ÷ total protein (g)
- **Food Selection**: Choose foods with favorable ratios
- **Examples**: 
  - Egg whites: Excellent ratio
  - Dairy: Poor ratio
  - Plant proteins: Variable ratios

## Micronutrient Management

### Water-Soluble Vitamins

#### B-Complex Vitamins
```
Thiamine (B1):
- RDA: 1.2 mg/day (men), 1.1 mg/day (women)
- Losses: 90% removed during hemodialysis
- Supplementation: 1-5 mg/day

Riboflavin (B2):
- RDA: 1.3 mg/day (men), 1.1 mg/day (women)
- Losses: 70% removed during hemodialysis
- Supplementation: 1.8 mg/day

Niacin (B3):
- RDA: 16 mg/day (men), 14 mg/day (women)
- Losses: Variable during dialysis
- Supplementation: 20 mg/day

Vitamin B6:
- RDA: 1.3 mg/day
- Losses: 90% removed during hemodialysis
- Supplementation: 5-10 mg/day

Folate:
- RDA: 400 mcg/day
- Losses: Significant during dialysis
- Supplementation: 1 mg/day

Vitamin B12:
- RDA: 2.4 mcg/day
- Losses: Minimal during dialysis
- Supplementation: 3-6 mcg/day
```

#### Vitamin C
- **RDA**: 75 mg/day (women), 90 mg/day (men)
- **Losses**: Significant during dialysis
- **Supplementation**: 60-100 mg/day
- **Caution**: High doses can cause oxalate accumulation

### Fat-Soluble Vitamins

#### Vitamin A
- **Status**: Usually normal or elevated
- **Supplementation**: Not routinely recommended
- **Toxicity Risk**: Hypercalcemia, hyperlipidemia
- **Monitoring**: Serum levels if supplemented

#### Vitamin D
```
Vitamin D Metabolism in CKD:
- 25-hydroxylation: Liver (reduced in CKD)
- 1-alpha hydroxylation: Kidney (severely reduced in CKD stage 4-5)

Supplementation Strategies:
- CKD stages 1-3: Vitamin D3 800-1,000 IU/day
- CKD stage 4: Vitamin D3 800-1,000 IU/day + monitor levels
- Dialysis: Active vitamin D analogs (calcitriol, paricalcitol)

Monitoring:
- 25(OH)D levels: Target 30-60 ng/mL
- Calcium, phosphorus, PTH: Every 3 months
- 24-hour urine calcium: If hypercalcemia risk
```

#### Vitamin E
- **Status**: Variable, may be deficient
- **Supplementation**: 100-400 IU/day
- **Benefits**: Antioxidant properties
- **Risks**: High doses may increase bleeding risk

### Trace Minerals

#### Iron
```
Iron Requirements:
- Men: 8 mg/day
- Women: 18 mg/day (premenopausal), 8 mg/day (postmenopausal)
- Dialysis patients: Increased needs due to blood losses

Iron Status Monitoring:
- Ferritin: Target 100-500 ng/mL
- TSAT: Target 20-50%
- Hemoglobin: 10-11.5 g/dL (non-dialysis), 10-12 g/dL (dialysis)

Dietary Sources:
- Heme iron: Red meat, poultry, fish (better absorbed)
- Non-heme iron: Legumes, fortified cereals, leafy greens
- Absorption enhancers: Vitamin C, meat protein
```

#### Zinc
- **RDA**: 11 mg/day (men), 8 mg/day (women)
- **Status**: Often deficient in dialysis patients
- **Supplementation**: 15-50 mg/day elemental zinc
- **Benefits**: Wound healing, taste improvement
- **Toxicity**: Copper deficiency with high doses

#### Selenium
- **RDA**: 55 mcg/day
- **Status**: Variable, may be deficient
- **Supplementation**: 50-100 mcg/day
- **Benefits**: Antioxidant, immune function
- **Toxicity**: Rare, hair loss, nail changes

## Nutritional Support Strategies

### Oral Nutritional Supplements

#### Indications
- **PEW**: Albumin <3.5 g/dL or weight loss >5%
- **Inadequate Intake**: <80% of estimated needs for >2 weeks
- **Post-Hospitalization**: Recovery support
- **Pre-Dialysis**: Preparation for renal replacement therapy

#### Supplement Types
```
Standard Oral Supplements:
- Renal-specific formulas: 1.5-2.0 kcal/mL, 20-30 g protein/250 mL
- High-protein supplements: 30-45 g protein/serving
- Calorie-dense supplements: 2.0 kcal/mL for volume restriction

Specialized Formulas:
- Ketoacid analog supplements: For non-dialysis CKD
- Anti-inflammatory formulas: Omega-3 enriched
- Fiber-containing supplements: For constipation

Administration:
- Between meals to avoid meal displacement
- 2-3 supplements daily if needed
- Monitor tolerance and gastrointestinal effects
```

### Intradialytic Parenteral Nutrition (IDPN)

#### Indications
- **Severe PEW**: Unable to meet needs orally
- **Hypercatabolic States**: Post-surgery, infection
- **Gastrointestinal Issues**: Malabsorption, nausea
- **Short-term Support**: Bridge to other interventions

#### IDPN Formulation
```
Standard IDPN Recipe (per treatment):
- Amino acids: 50-100 g (0.8-1.2 g/kg ideal body weight)
- Dextrose: 250-500 g (50-100 g/hour)
- Lipids: 50-100 g (10-20% of total calories)
- Total volume: 500-1000 mL
- Total calories: 1000-1500 kcal

Administration:
- During last 2-3 hours of hemodialysis
- Monitor blood glucose closely
- Assess tolerance and efficacy
```

#### Outcomes and Limitations
- **Benefits**: Improved albumin, nutritional status
- **Limitations**: 
  - High cost
  - Limited long-term data
  - Does not address underlying causes
  - May interfere with dialysis adequacy

### Enteral Nutrition

#### Indications
- **Functional GI Tract**: Unable to meet oral needs
- **Critical Illness**: ICU setting, post-operative
- **Neurologic Disorders**: Stroke, dysphagia
- **Mechanical Obstruction**: Esophageal, gastric issues

#### Formulation Selection
```
Standard Formulas:
- 1.0-1.2 kcal/mL, standard protein content
- Suitable for most CKD patients

Renal-Specific Formulas:
- 2.0 kcal/mL, high protein (20-25% of calories)
- Electrolyte-controlled
- May be beneficial in selected patients

Disease-Specific Formulas:
- Pulmonary: Low carbohydrate, high fat
- Diabetic: Fiber-containing, low glycemic index
- Hepatic: Branched-chain amino acid enriched
```

#### Delivery Methods
- **Nasogastric Tubes**: Short-term use (<4 weeks)
- **Nasojejunal Tubes**: Gastric intolerance, pancreatitis
- **Percutaneous Tubes**: 
  - PEG tubes: Long-term enteral access
  - Gastrostomy buttons: Low profile options
  - Jejunostomy tubes: Gastric outlet obstruction

## Special Populations and Conditions

### Diabetes and CKD

#### Medical Nutrition Therapy
```
Carbohydrate Management:
- Consistent carbohydrate intake: 45-65% of total calories
- Complex carbohydrates: Whole grains, legumes, vegetables
- Simple sugars: Limit concentrated sweets
- Fiber intake: 25-35 g/day if tolerated

Protein Considerations:
- Balance glycemic control with renal protein goals
- Consider protein source effects on glucose
- Monitor for hypoglycemia with dietary changes

Glycemic Targets:
- HbA1c: 7.0-8.0% (individualized)
- Post-prandial glucose: <180 mg/dL
- Monitor for hypoglycemia, especially in elderly
```

#### Practical Strategies
- **Meal Timing**: Consistent meal times with medication
- **Carbohydrate Counting**: For insulin therapy
- **Glycemic Index**: Choose lower GI foods
- **Portion Control**: Visual aids, measuring tools

### Obesity in CKD

#### Weight Management Strategies
```
Calorie Restriction:
- Moderate restriction: 500-750 kcal/day deficit
- Very low calorie: <800 kcal/day (medical supervision)
- Rate of loss: 0.5-1 kg/week (1-2 lbs/week)

Protein Preservation:
- Adequate protein: 0.8-1.0 g/kg ideal body weight
- Resistance exercise: Preserve lean mass
- Monitor: Albumin, prealbumin, muscle mass

Bariatric Surgery Considerations:
- Indications: BMI >35 kg/m² with comorbidities
- Timing: Prefer pre-dialysis if possible
- Risks: Nutritional deficiencies, stone formation
- Benefits: Improved diabetes control, weight loss
```

#### Nutritional Considerations
- **Protein Quality**: High biological value proteins
- **Micronutrients**: Comprehensive supplementation
- **Hydration**: Adequate fluids for kidney health
- **Exercise**: Combined aerobic and resistance training

### Pregnancy and CKD

#### Nutritional Requirements
```
Increased Needs:
- Calories: +300 kcal/day (second trimester), +450 kcal/day (third)
- Protein: +25 g/day additional to CKD requirements
- Iron: 27 mg/day (increased from 18 mg)
- Folate: 600 mcg/day (increased from 400 mcg)
- Calcium: 1,000-1,300 mg/day
- Vitamin D: 600 IU/day

Sodium and Fluid:
- Individualized based on blood pressure and edema
- May need liberalization in severe pre-eclampsia
- Monitor weight gain: 0.5-1 kg/week after first trimester
```

#### Dietary Modifications
- **Phosphorus**: May need restriction if hyperphosphatemic
- **Potassium**: Monitor closely, especially with ACE inhibitors
- **Protein**: Balance fetal needs with renal protection
- **Weight Gain**: Monitor for excessive or inadequate gain

## Practical Implementation and Education

### Dietary Counseling Strategies

#### Motivational Interviewing
- **Patient-Centered**: Explore patient's values and goals
- **Collaborative**: Work together on solutions
- **Eliciting Change Talk**: Encourage patient to voice reasons for change
- **Supporting Self-Efficacy**: Build confidence in ability to change

#### Educational Approaches
```
Visual Aids:
- Food models for portion sizes
- Plate method for meal planning
- Color-coded food lists (green/yellow/red)

Hands-On Learning:
- Cooking demonstrations
- Label reading workshops
- Grocery store tours

Cultural Considerations:
- Culturally appropriate food choices
- Traditional preparation methods
- Religious dietary restrictions
- Economic constraints
```

### Meal Planning Strategies

#### Plate Method
- **Half Plate**: Non-starchy vegetables
- **Quarter Plate**: High-quality protein
- **Quarter Plate**: Complex carbohydrates
- **Additions**: Small amount of healthy fats, fruit for dessert

#### Carbohydrate Counting
- **15 g Choices**: Standard carbohydrate portions
- **Meal Distribution**: Even distribution throughout day
- **Consistency**: Similar carbohydrate amounts at meals

#### Label Reading Skills
- **Serving Size**: Most important information
- **Nutrients per Serving**: Calculate actual intake
- **Ingredient List**: Identify hidden phosphorus, sodium
- **Health Claims**: Evaluate marketing vs reality

### Monitoring and Follow-Up

#### Frequency of Visits
- **CKD Stage 3**: Every 6 months
- **CKD Stage 4**: Every 3 months
- **Dialysis**: Monthly initially, then every 2-3 months
- **Post-Hospitalization**: Within 2 weeks

#### Assessment Parameters
```
Nutritional Status:
- Weight changes: Track trends
- Laboratory values: Albumin, prealbumin, electrolytes
- Dietary intake: 24-hour recalls, food diaries
- Physical assessment: Muscle mass, functional status

Clinical Parameters:
- Blood pressure: Response to sodium restriction
- Blood glucose: If diabetic
- Edema: Fluid status assessment
- Gastrointestinal tolerance: Supplement tolerance
```

#### Quality Metrics
- **Albumin Trends**: Improvement or stabilization
- **Hospitalization Rates**: Reduction with improved nutrition
- **Quality of Life**: Patient-reported outcomes
- **Dietary Adherence**: Patient self-reporting, biochemical markers

## Novel Therapies Affecting Nutrition in CKD (2024 Update)

### GLP-1 Receptor Agonists and Nutritional Impact

#### Overview and CKD Applications
```
FDA-Approved GLP-1 Agonists (2024):
- Semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy): Weekly SC injection
- Dulaglutide (Trulicity): Weekly SC injection
- Liraglutide (Victoza, Saxenda): Daily SC injection
- Tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound): Weekly SC injection (dual GLP-1/GIP agonist)

CKD Indications:
- Type 2 diabetes + CKD: Glycemic control, cardiovascular protection
- Obesity + CKD: Weight management, renal protection
- FLOW Trial (2024): Semaglutide reduced CKD progression by 24%
```

#### Nutritional Considerations (Critical for CKD Patients)

```
Weight Loss Effects:
- Magnitude: 10-20% body weight loss over 6-12 months
- Mechanism: Appetite suppression, delayed gastric emptying, increased satiety
- Concerns in CKD:
  - Risk of protein-energy wasting in malnourished patients
  - Loss of lean body mass (30-40% of total weight loss)
  - May worsen sarcopenia in elderly/frail CKD patients

Appropriate CKD Candidates for GLP-1 Therapy:
✓ Overweight/obese (BMI >27 kg/m²)
✓ Type 2 diabetes with suboptimal control
✓ Good baseline nutritional status (albumin >3.5 g/dL)
✓ No baseline protein-energy wasting

Contraindications/Cautions:
✗ Baseline malnutrition (albumin <3.0 g/dL, BMI <23 kg/m²)
✗ Unintentional weight loss in past 6 months
✗ Frailty or sarcopenia
✗ Advanced CKD with poor appetite/PEW

Monitoring During GLP-1 Therapy (2024 Recommendations):
- Body weight: Weekly initially, then monthly
- Albumin, prealbumin: Every 3 months
- Body composition (BIA/DEXA): Every 6 months
- nPCR: Every 3 months (ensure adequate protein intake)
- Monitor for: Excessive weight loss (>2 lb/week), declining albumin, muscle wasting

Nutritional Management on GLP-1 Agonists:
- Increase protein intake target: 1.0-1.2 g/kg/day (vs 0.8 g/kg without GLP-1)
- Focus on high biological value proteins
- Small, frequent meals (GI side effects common)
- Resistance exercise: Preserve lean body mass
- Consider protein supplementation if intake inadequate
- Reduce dose or discontinue if malnutrition develops

GI Side Effects (Common):
- Nausea: 40-50% of patients
- Vomiting: 10-20%
- Diarrhea: 20-30%
- Constipation: 20-30%
- Management: Start low, titrate slowly, eat smaller meals, hydration
```

#### Clinical Scenarios (2024 Guidance)

```
Scenario 1: Obese CKD Patient (BMI 35, Albumin 4.0)
- GLP-1 agonist: APPROPRIATE
- Benefits: Weight loss, cardiovascular protection, potential renal benefit
- Monitoring: Ensure protein intake 1.2 g/kg, monitor lean mass
- Expected outcome: 15-20 kg weight loss over 12 months

Scenario 2: Elderly Frail CKD Patient (BMI 22, Albumin 3.2)
- GLP-1 agonist: AVOID
- Risks: Worsening malnutrition, sarcopenia, frailty
- Alternative: Focus on nutritional support, avoid GLP-1

Scenario 3: Diabetic CKD Patient (BMI 28, Albumin 3.5)
- GLP-1 agonist: CONSIDER with close monitoring
- Benefits: Glycemic control, modest weight loss beneficial
- Monitoring: Close nutritional surveillance, maintain protein intake
- Adjust: Reduce dose if weight loss >10% or albumin declines
```

### SGLT2 Inhibitors and Nutritional Effects

```
Nutritional Impact (Generally Minimal):
- Weight loss: 2-3 kg (modest, primarily fluid initially)
- Lean body mass: Preserved (unlike GLP-1 agonists)
- Appetite: No significant suppression
- GI side effects: Rare

Nutritional Considerations:
- Genital mycotic infections: 5-10% (may affect nutrition if recurrent)
- Volume depletion: Monitor in frail elderly, may affect appetite
- No specific dietary restrictions beyond CKD diet

Combination with GLP-1 Agonists:
- Additive weight loss effects
- Increased risk of volume depletion
- Monitor closely for malnutrition if combining therapies
```

### Novel Phosphate Management Strategies (2024)

```
Novel Phosphate Binders (2024):

Sucroferric Oxyhydroxide (Velphoro):
- FDA-approved 2013, increasingly used 2024
- Dosing: 500 mg tablets, titrate to effect (typical 1-3 tablets TID)
- Advantages:
  - Lower pill burden (vs calcium acetate, sevelamer)
  - Effective phosphate binding
  - Iron supplementation (additional benefit)
- Nutritional impact: May reduce need for IV iron

Tenapanor (Xphosah - FDA Approved for CKD):
- **FDA Approval**: September 2023 for hyperphosphatemia in adults with CKD on dialysis ⭐
- Mechanism: NHE3 (sodium-hydrogen exchanger 3) inhibitor - reduces intestinal phosphate absorption
- **Dosing for CKD (Xphosah)**: 10 mg PO BID (for dialysis patients)
  - NOTE: Different from IBS-C dosing (Ibsrela 30 mg BID - different brand/indication)
- **Brand Names**:
  - Xphosah 10 mg: CKD/dialysis indication
  - Ibsrela 50 mg: IBS-C indication (different dose, different disease)
- Phase 3 trials: 30-40% reduction in serum phosphorus vs placebo
- Advantages: NOT a binder (novel mechanism), very low pill burden (2 tablets/day), can be added to existing binders
- Side effects: Diarrhea (30% of patients - most common adverse effect)
- **Indication**: Dialysis patients only (HD and PD) - not approved for pre-dialysis CKD
- Cost: $$$$$ (very expensive)

Potassium Binders with Phosphate Benefits:

Patiromer (Veltassa):
- Binds potassium AND phosphate in gut
- Dual benefit: Hyperkalemia + mild phosphate control
- Evidence (2023): 15-20% reduction in phosphorus
- Consider in CKD patients with both hyperkalemia and hyperphosphatemia

Dietary Strategies (2024 Evidence):

Nicotinamide (Vitamin B3):
- Mechanism: Inhibits intestinal phosphate transporter
- Dosing: 500-1000 mg PO BID-TID
- Evidence: 20-30% reduction in phosphorus (modest)
- Advantages: Inexpensive, well-tolerated
- Limitations: Less effective than binders
- Status: Used in some countries, not FDA-approved for CKD
```

## Key Practice Points

### Clinical Pearls
1. **Early Intervention**: Start nutrition counseling at CKD stage 3
2. **Individualize Plans**: Consider cultural, economic, personal preferences
3. **Monitor Trends**: Look at patterns rather than single values
4. **Team Approach**: Collaborate with dietitians, social workers, physicians
5. **Regular Follow-Up**: Nutrition is an ongoing process, not one-time education

### Red Flags
- **Weight Loss**: >5% in 1 month or >10% in 6 months
- **Albumin Drop**: >0.5 g/dL in 3 months
- **Intake Reduction**: <50% of estimated needs for >1 week
- **Functional Decline**: Decreased ability to perform ADLs
- **Frequent Hospitalizations**: May indicate poor nutritional status

### Prevention Strategies
- **Regular Screening**: Identify at-risk patients early
- **Patient Education**: Empower patients with knowledge and skills
- **Early Supplementation**: Don't wait for severe deficiency
- **Multidisciplinary Care**: Involve entire care team
- **Community Resources**: Connect patients with support services
