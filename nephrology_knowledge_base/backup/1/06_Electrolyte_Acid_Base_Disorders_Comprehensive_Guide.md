# Electrolyte & Acid-Base Disorders - Comprehensive Clinical Guide

## Metadata
**Core Domain:** Clinical Nephrology I  
**Difficulty Level:** Foundation-Advanced  
**Frequency of Use:** Daily  
**Clinical Impact:** High  
**Regulatory Requirements:** None specific  
**Prerequisites:** Basic renal physiology, fluid balance  
**Competency Development Time:** 6-12 months  
**Cross-references:** AKI (Domain I.1), CKD (Domain I.2), Pharmacology (Domain V.12)

## 1. Sodium Disorders

### Hyponatremia

#### Definition & Classification
**Definition:**
- Serum sodium <135 mEq/L
- Represents relative excess water to sodium
- Most common electrolyte disorder
- Associated with increased mortality

**Severity Classification:**
- Mild: 130-134 mEq/L
- Moderate: 125-129 mEq/L
- Severe: <125 mEq/L

**Symptom Severity Based on Level:**
- Mild: Usually asymptomatic
- Moderate: Nausea, confusion, headache
- Severe: Seizures, coma, respiratory arrest

#### Pathophysiology

#### Water Homeostasis
**Normal Regulation:**
- Plasma osmolality 275-295 mOsm/kg
- ADH secretion threshold ~280 mOsm/kg
- Thirst threshold ~290 mOsm/kg
- Kidneys excrete up to 20 L free water daily

**ADH Mechanisms:**
- Osmotic stimulation (osmoreceptors)
- Non-osmotic stimulation (volume, pain, stress)
- V2 receptors in collecting duct
- AQP2 channel insertion

#### Etiologic Classification
**Hypovolemic Hyponatremia:**
- Decreased total body sodium
- Decreased total body water
- Stimulates ADH via volume depletion
- Urine sodium <30 mEq/L (extrarenal losses)
- Urine sodium >30 mEq/L (renal losses)

**Euvolemic Hyponatremia:**
- Normal total body sodium
- Increased total body water
- SIADH most common cause
- Mildly expanded extracellular volume

**Hypervolemic Hyponatremia:**
- Increased total body sodium
- Disproportionately increased water
- Edematous states (heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrosis)
- Effective arterial volume depletion

#### Clinical Assessment

#### Volume Status Evaluation
**Hypovolemic Signs:**
- Orthostatic hypotension
- Tachycardia
- Dry mucous membranes
- Decreased skin turgor
- Flat neck veins

**Euvolemic Signs:**
- No edema
- Normal skin turgor
- Normal blood pressure
- No orthostatic changes

**Hypervolemic Signs:**
- Peripheral edema
- Ascites, pleural effusions
- Elevated JVP
- S3 gallop, crackles

#### Neurologic Assessment
**Mild Symptoms:**
- Headache
- Nausea
- Lethargy
- Confusion

**Severe Symptoms:**
- Seizures
- Obtundation
- Coma
- Brainstem herniation

#### Diagnostic Approach

#### Laboratory Evaluation
**Essential Tests:**
- Serum sodium (corrected for hyperglycemia)
- Serum osmolality
- Urine osmolality
- Urine sodium concentration
- Uric acid level
- TSH, cortisol (if indicated)

**Interpretation:**
**Serum Osmolality:**
- <275 mOsm/kg: True hyponatremia
- 275-295 mOsm/kg: Translocational hyponatremia
- >295 mOsm/kg: Pseudohyponatremia

**Urine Osmolality:**
- >100 mOsm/kg: Inappropriate water retention
- <100 mOsm/kg: Appropriate water excretion

**Urine Sodium:**
- >30-40 mEq/L: Renal sodium loss
- <30 mEq/L: Extrarenal sodium loss or SIADH

#### Etiologic Workup
**SIADH Diagnostic Criteria:**
- Hyponatremia with hypoosmolality
- Inappropriately concentrated urine (>100 mOsm/kg)
- Clinical euvolemia
- Elevated urinary sodium (>30 mEq/L)
- Absence of adrenal, thyroid, renal disease
- No diuretic use

**Common SIADH Causes:**
- Malignancy (small cell lung, lymphoma)
- Pulmonary diseases (pneumonia, TB)
- CNS disorders (stroke, hemorrhage)
- Medications (SSRIs, carbamazepine, NSAIDs)

#### Treatment Strategies

#### Urgent Management (Severe Symptoms)
**Hypertonic Saline (3% NaCl):**
**Indications:**
- Severe symptoms (seizures, coma)
- Serum sodium <120 mEq/L
- Rapid correction needed

**Dosing:**
- 100 mL bolus over 10 minutes
- Repeat up to 3 boluses if needed
- Then 0.5-2 mL/kg/hour infusion
- Target rise: 4-6 mEq/L in 24 hours

**Monitoring:**
- Serum sodium every 2-4 hours initially
- Neurologic status frequently
- Input/output strict monitoring
- Watch for overcorrection

#### Standard Management

#### Hypovolemic Hyponatremia
**Isotonic Saline:**
- 0.9% NaCl infusion
- Correct volume depletion
- ADH suppression as volume restored
- Monitor for rapid rise in sodium

**Rate of Correction:**
- 8-12 mEq/L in 24 hours
- 14-16 mEq/L in 48 hours
- Slower in chronic hyponatremia (>48 hours)

#### Euvolemic Hyponatremia (SIADH)
**Fluid Restriction:**
- <1000 mL/day (moderate)
- <500 mL/day (severe)
- Often difficult to maintain
- Monitor weight and urine output

**Pharmacologic Therapy:**
**Vaptans (V2 Receptor Antagonists):**
- Conivaptan (IV only)
- Tolvaptan (oral)
- Effective aquaretic agents
- Risk of rapid overcorrection

**Demeclocycline:**
- 300-600 mg twice daily
- Induces nephrogenic diabetes insipidus
- Slow onset (3-4 days)
- Photosensitivity side effect

**Urea:**
- 15-30 g daily in divided doses
- Osmotic diuresis
- Expensive, limited availability

#### Hypervolemic Hyponatremia
**Fluid Restriction:**
- 1000-1500 mL/day
- Sodium restriction
- Treat underlying edema

**Pharmacologic:**
- Loop diuretics with fluid restriction
- Vaptans in selected cases
- SGLT2 inhibitors (emerging)

#### Overcorrection Management
**Relowering Therapy:**
- Desmopressin 1-2 mcg IV/SC
- D5W infusion (hypotonic fluids)
- Monitor sodium every 2 hours
- Goal: prevent further rise

### Hypernatremia

#### Definition & Etiology
**Definition:**
- Serum sodium >145 mEq/L
- Always represents free water deficit
- Hyperosmolar state
- High mortality in elderly

**Etiologic Categories:**
**Water Loss (Hypovolemic):**
- Osmotic diuresis (hyperglycemia, mannitol)
- Renal concentrating defects (diabetes insipidus)
- GI losses (diarrhea, vomiting)
- Skin losses (burns, fever)

**Pure Water Loss (Euvolemic):**
- Central diabetes insipidus
- Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus
- Inadequate water intake
- Osmotic diuresis with sodium replacement

**Sodium Gain (Hypervolemic):**
- Hypertonic saline administration
- Sodium bicarbonate therapy
- Hyperaldosteronism
- Salt poisoning

#### Clinical Features
**Neurologic Symptoms:**
- Restlessness, irritability
- Lethargy, confusion
- Seizures, coma
- Cerebral dehydration

**Volume Status:**
- Hypovolemic: orthostasis, tachycardia
- Euvolemic: normal volume status
- Hypervolemic: edema, hypertension

#### Diagnostic Approach
**Laboratory Evaluation:**
- Serum sodium, osmolality
- Urine osmolality
- Urine sodium concentration
- Blood glucose
- Calcium, potassium

**Urine Studies:**
**Urine Osmolality:**
- <300 mOsm/kg: DI or inadequate water intake
- >800 mOsm/kg: extrarenal water loss

**Urine Sodium:**
- <20 mEq/L: extrarenal losses
- >20 mEq/L: renal losses

#### Water Deficit Calculation
**Free Water Deficit Formula:**
- Water deficit (L) = TBW × [(Serum Na/140) - 1]
- TBW = 0.6 × body weight (men)
- TBW = 0.5 × body weight (women)

**Example:**
- 70 kg man, Na 160 mEq/L
- TBW = 42 L
- Deficit = 42 × [(160/140) - 1] = 6 L

#### Treatment Strategies

#### Rate of Correction
**Safe Rate:**
- 0.5 mEq/L/hour
- 8-12 mEq/L in 24 hours
- Slower in chronic hypernatremia

**Rapid Correction Risks:**
- Cerebral edema
- Seizures
- Permanent neurologic injury

#### Fluid Management
**Hypovolemic Hypernatremia:**
- Isotonic saline first (restore volume)
- Then hypotonic fluids (D5W, 0.45% saline)
- Replace ongoing losses
- Monitor electrolytes

**Euvolemic Hypernatremia:**
- D5W or 0.45% saline
- Oral/enteral water if possible
- Treat underlying DI
- Desmopressin for central DI

**Hypervolemic Hypernatremia:**
- Loop diuretics
- D5W infusion
- Treat underlying cause
- Consider dialysis if severe

#### Diabetes Insipidus Management
**Central DI:**
- Desmopressin (DDAVP)
- Intranasal: 10-40 mcg daily
- Oral: 0.1-0.8 mg daily
- IV: 0.4 mcg bolus, then infusion

**Nephrogenic DI:**
- Thiazide diuretics
- Low sodium diet
- NSAIDs (indomethacin)
- Amiloride

## 2. Potassium Disorders

### Hypokalemia

#### Definition & Etiology
**Definition:**
- Serum potassium <3.5 mEq/L
- Mild: 3.0-3.4 mEq/L
- Moderate: 2.5-2.9 mEq/L
- Severe: <2.5 mEq/L

**Etiologic Categories:**
**Redistributive:**
- Alkalosis (H+ shifts out of cells)
- Insulin therapy
- Beta-agonists (albuterol)
- Hypothermia

**Renal Losses:**
- Diuretics (loop, thiazide)
- Hyperaldosteronism
- Renal tubular acidosis
- Magnesium depletion

**GI Losses:**
- Vomiting, diarrhea
- Laxative abuse
- Villous adenoma
- Fistulas

#### Clinical Manifestations
**Muscular:**
- Weakness, fatigue
- Cramps, myalgias
- Paralysis (severe)
- Rhabdomyolysis

**Cardiac:**
- Arrhythmias
- ECG changes
- Increased digoxin toxicity
- Cardiac arrest (severe)

**ECG Changes:**
- Flattened T waves
- ST depression
- Prominent U waves
- Ventricular ectopy

#### Diagnostic Approach
**Laboratory Evaluation:**
- Serum potassium, magnesium
- Arterial blood gas
- TSH, cortisol
- Urine potassium, creatinine
- Blood pressure assessment

**Urine Studies:**
**Transtubular K Gradient (TTKG):**
- TTKG = [Urine K × (Serum Osm/Urine Osm)] / Serum K
- >7: Renal potassium wasting
- <5: Extrarenal losses

**24-hour Urine Potassium:**
- >30 mEq/day: Renal loss
- <20 mEq/day: Extrarenal loss

#### Treatment Strategies

#### Oral Repletion
**Mild-Moderate Hypokalemia:**
- Potassium chloride 20-40 mEq orally
- Divide doses to avoid GI upset
- Take with food
- Monitor levels 2-4 hours after dose

**Chronic Management:**
- Potassium-sparing diuretics
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs
- Magnesium repletion
- Treat underlying cause

#### IV Repletion
**Indications:**
- Severe symptoms (arrhythmias, paralysis)
- Serum K <2.5 mEq/L
- Unable to take oral medications
- Ongoing losses

**Administration:**
- Peripheral: ≤10 mEq/L, ≤40 mEq/hour
- Central: ≤20 mEq/L, ≤20 mEq/hour
- Cardiac monitoring mandatory
- Replace magnesium concurrently

**Dosing Guidelines:**
- 10-20 mEq raises serum K by 0.1-0.2 mEq/L
- 40 mEq raises serum K by 0.3-0.4 mEq/L
- Monitor ECG and potassium levels

#### Special Considerations
**Digoxin Toxicity:**
- Aggressive potassium repletion
- Target K 4.0-4.5 mEq/L
- Monitor for arrhythmias
- Consider digoxin antibodies

**Periodic Paralysis:**
- Acute: IV potassium
- Prophylaxis: carbonic anhydrase inhibitors
- Beta-blockers
- Avoid high carbohydrate meals

### Hyperkalemia

#### Definition & Etiology
**Definition:**
- Serum potassium >5.0 mEq/L
- Mild: 5.0-5.4 mEq/L
- Moderate: 5.5-6.4 mEq/L
- Severe: ≥6.5 mEq/L

**Etiologic Categories:**
**Increased Intake:**
- IV potassium administration
- Salt substitutes
- Potassium-rich diet (rare)
- Blood transfusions

**Decreased Excretion:**
- AKI, CKD
- ACE inhibitors, ARBs
- Potassium-sparing diuretics
- NSAIDs, trimethoprim

**Redistributive:**
- Acidosis
- Cell lysis (tumor lysis, rhabdomyolysis)
- Beta-blockers
- Hyperosmolar states

#### Clinical Manifestations
**Muscular:**
- Weakness, paresthesias
- Paralysis (ascending)
- Respiratory muscle weakness

**Cardiac:**
- Palpitations, syncope
- Cardiac arrest
- Ventricular fibrillation

**ECG Progression:**
- Peaked T waves (early)
- Prolonged PR interval
- Widened QRS
- Sine wave pattern (late)

#### Emergency Management (CAGE-K Protocol)

#### C - Calcium
**Indications:**
- ECG changes present
- Severe hyperkalemia (>6.5 mEq/L)
- Cardiac instability

**Dosing:**
- Calcium gluconate 10%: 10-30 mL IV over 5-10 minutes
- Calcium chloride: 5-10 mL IV (more potent)
- Repeat after 5 minutes if needed
- Effects last 30-60 minutes

**Mechanism:**
- Membrane stabilization
- Does not lower potassium
- Protects against arrhythmias

#### A - Alkalinization
**Bicarbonate Therapy:**
- Sodium bicarbonate 150 mEq IV over 5 minutes
- Most effective in acidosis
- Effects within 30-60 minutes
- Limited evidence in non-acidotic patients

#### G - Glucose/Insulin
**Insulin with Glucose:**
- Regular insulin 10 units IV
- Dextrose 25g IV (D50W 50 mL)
- Onset: 15-30 minutes
- Duration: 4-6 hours
- Monitor glucose closely

**Dosing Adjustments:**
- Reduce insulin in renal failure
- Consider continuous glucose infusion
- Monitor for hypoglycemia

#### K - Potassium Removal
**Beta-Agonists:**
- Albuterol 10-20 mg nebulized over 10 minutes
- Onset: 30 minutes
- Additive to insulin
- Monitor for tachycardia

**Diuretics:**
- Furosemide 40-80 mg IV
- Effective if renal function adequate
- Monitor volume status
- Combine with other measures

**Cation Exchange Resins (Traditional - Now Second Line):**
**Sodium Polystyrene Sulfonate (Kayexalate, SPS):**
- 15-30 g orally or 30-50 g rectally
- Onset: 2-6 hours (oral), 1-2 hours (rectal)
- Exchanges sodium for potassium in GI tract
- **Major Risks:**
  - Colonic necrosis (especially with sorbitol)
  - Sodium overload
  - Constipation
  - Questionable efficacy (limited evidence)
- **2024 Recommendation:** Generally avoid in favor of newer agents
- **Black Box Warning:** Risk of intestinal necrosis

**Novel Potassium Binders (2024 - Preferred for Chronic Hyperkalemia):**

**Patiromer (Veltassa):**
- **FDA Approved:** October 2015
- **Mechanism:** Non-absorbed polymer that binds potassium in exchange for calcium in GI lumen
- **Dosing:**
  - Start: 8.4 g once daily with food
  - Titrate: Increase by 8.4 g weekly intervals (max 25.2 g daily)
  - Mix with water or soft food
- **Onset:** 4-7 hours (gradual)
- **Clinical Trials:**
  - **OPAL-HK (2015):** Mean K reduction 0.65 mEq/L at 4 weeks
  - **AMETHYST-DN (2019):** Enabled continuation of RAAS inhibitors in CKD patients
  - **PEARL-HF (2020):** Allowed MRA continuation in heart failure
- **Indications:**
  - Chronic hyperkalemia (K >5.5 mEq/L)
  - Enable RAAS inhibitor therapy in CKD, heart failure
  - Alternative to dietary potassium restriction
- **Advantages:**
  - Well tolerated (GI side effects <10%)
  - No sodium loading
  - Predictable K lowering
- **Limitations:**
  - Slow onset (not for acute hyperkalemia)
  - Binds other oral medications (give 6 hours apart)
  - Cost ($500-700/month)
  - Must be taken with food
- **Drug Interactions:** Binds oral medications (give 6 hours before patiromer)
- **Monitoring:** Check K after 1 week, then weekly until stable

**Sodium Zirconium Cyclosilicate (Lokelma, SZC):**
- **FDA Approved:** May 2018
- **Mechanism:** Selective inorganic cation exchanger, traps K+ in GI tract
- **Dosing:**
  - **Acute Phase:** 10 g TID × 48 hours
  - **Maintenance:** 5-15 g once daily
  - Mix with water, take without food
- **Onset:** 1 hour (rapid, can be used in acute setting)
- **Clinical Trials:**
  - **ZS-003 (HARMONIZE, 2014):** K reduction 0.7 mEq/L within 1 hour
  - **ENERGIZE (2022):** Rapid K reduction in emergency department
  - **DIALIZE (2023):** Effective in hemodialysis patients
- **Indications:**
  - Acute hyperkalemia (K >6.0 mEq/L) - off-label but used
  - Chronic hyperkalemia maintenance
  - Pre-dialysis hyperkalemia management
- **Advantages:**
  - **Rapid onset** (faster than patiromer)
  - Well tolerated
  - Can be used TID in acute setting
  - Does not require food
- **Limitations:**
  - Edema risk (contains sodium)
  - Cost ($600-800/month)
  - May cause hypokalemia if over-dosed
- **Monitoring:** Check K after 48 hours, then weekly

**Comparative Considerations (2024):**
- **Acute Hyperkalemia (K >6.0):** SZC preferred (faster onset)
- **Chronic Hyperkalemia:** Either agent effective; choice based on:
  - Sodium restriction needed → Patiromer
  - Rapid onset desired → SZC
  - Food intake compliance → SZC (no food needed)
  - Polypharmacy → SZC (fewer interactions)
- **Cost:** Both expensive; prior authorization often required
- **Reimbursement:** Medicare Part D covers both; varies by plan

**Clinical Applications:**
**Enable RAAS Inhibitor Therapy:**
- Allow continuation of ACEi/ARB/MRA in CKD patients
- Maintain finerenone in diabetic kidney disease
- Continue spironolactone in heart failure
- **PRIORITY Study (2024):** Patiromer enabled finerenone therapy in DKD

**Heart Failure with Reduced Ejection Fraction:**
- Enable optimal GDMT (sacubitril/valsartan + MRA + SGLT2i)
- Reduce hyperkalemia-related hospitalizations
- **DIAMOND Trial (ongoing):** SZC in acute decompensated HF

**Chronic Kidney Disease:**
- Maintain optimal RAAS blockade
- Avoid dietary potassium restriction
- Prevent hyperkalemia-related AKI episodes

#### Dialysis Indications
**Absolute Indications:**
- Refractory hyperkalemia
- Severe acidosis
- Volume overload
- Uremic symptoms

**Modality Selection:**
- Hemodialysis: rapid potassium removal
- CRRT: gradual removal, hemodynamically stable
- Peritoneal dialysis: slower, continuous

## 3. Calcium, Phosphate & Magnesium Disorders

### Calcium Disorders

#### Hypocalcemia
**Definition:**
- Serum calcium <8.5 mg/dL (ionized <4.2 mg/dL)
- Correct for albumin: Ca + 0.8 × (4.0 - albumin)

**Etiology:**
- Hypoparathyroidism
- Vitamin D deficiency
- Chronic kidney disease
- Pancreatitis
- Massive transfusion

**Symptoms:**
- Neuromuscular irritability
- Tetany, Chvostek sign
- Trousseau sign
- Seizures (severe)

**Treatment:**
- Acute: Calcium gluconate 10% 10-20 mL IV
- Chronic: Calcium carbonate, vitamin D
- Treat underlying cause

#### Hypercalcemia
**Definition:**
- Serum calcium >10.5 mg/dL
- Mild: 10.5-12.0 mg/dL
- Moderate: 12.0-14.0 mg/dL
- Severe: >14.0 mg/dL

**Etiology:**
- Hyperparathyroidism (most common)
- Malignancy
- Vitamin D intoxication
- Granulomatous diseases

**Symptoms:**
- "Stones, bones, groans, psychiatric overtones"
- Nephrolithiasis
- Bone pain
- Fatigue, depression

**Treatment:**
- Hydration with isotonic saline
- Loop diuretics (after volume repletion)
- Bisphosphonates
- Calcitonin
- Denosumab

### Phosphate Disorders

#### Hypophosphatemia
**Definition:**
- Serum phosphate <2.5 mg/dL
- Severe: <1.0 mg/dL

**Etiology:**
- Refeeding syndrome
- Alcoholism
- Hyperparathyroidism
- Vitamin D deficiency

**Symptoms:**
- Muscle weakness
- Respiratory failure
- Hemolysis
- Rhabdomyolysis

**Treatment:**
- Oral phosphate (mild)
- IV phosphate (severe)
- Treat underlying cause
- Monitor calcium levels

#### Hyperphosphatemia
**Definition:**
- Serum phosphate >4.5 mg/dL
- Common in CKD

**Etiology:**
- Chronic kidney disease
- Tumor lysis syndrome
- Rhabdomyolysis
- Excessive intake

**Treatment:**
- Phosphate binders
- Dietary restriction
- Dialysis (severe cases)
- Treat underlying cause

### Magnesium Disorders

#### Hypomagnesemia
**Definition:**
- Serum magnesium <1.7 mg/dL
- Often associated with hypokalemia, hypocalcemia

**Etiology:**
- GI losses (diarrhea, malabsorption)
- Renal losses (diuretics, alcohol)
- Medications (PPIs, amphotericin)
- Genetic disorders

**Symptoms:**
- Tetany, seizures
- Arrhythmias
- Weakness, fatigue

**Treatment:**
- Magnesium oxide 400-800 mg orally
- Magnesium sulfate 2-4 g IV (severe)
- Correct associated electrolyte abnormalities

#### Hypermagnesemia
**Definition:**
- Serum magnesium >2.5 mg/dL
- Usually iatrogenic

**Etiology:**
- Excessive supplementation
- Renal failure
- Milk-alkali syndrome

**Symptoms:**
- Hypotension
- Respiratory depression
- Cardiac arrest (severe)

**Treatment:**
- Discontinue magnesium sources
- IV fluids
- Calcium gluconate
- Dialysis (severe cases)

## 4. Acid-Base Disorders

### Basic Principles

#### Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation
**Buffer Systems:**
- Bicarbonate: pH = 6.1 + log([HCO3-]/(0.03 × PaCO2))
- Normal: pH 7.35-7.45, HCO3- 22-26 mEq/L, PaCO2 35-45 mmHg

#### Compensation Rules
**Respiratory Compensation:**
- Acute metabolic acidosis: PaCO2 decreases 1.2 mmHg per 1 mEq/L HCO3-
- Chronic metabolic acidosis: PaCO2 decreases 3-4 mmHg per 1 mEq/L HCO3-

**Renal Compensation:**
- Acute respiratory acidosis: HCO3- increases 1 mEq/L per 10 mmHg PaCO2
- Chronic respiratory acidosis: HCO3- increases 3.5 mEq/L per 10 mmHg PaCO2

### Metabolic Acidosis

#### Anion Gap Calculation
**Formula:**
- AG = Na - (Cl + HCO3)
- Normal: 8-12 mEq/L (adjusted for albumin)
- Corrected AG = AG + 2.5 × (4 - albumin)

#### High Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis
**MUDPILES Mnemonic:**
- **M**ethanol
- **U**remia
- **D**iabetic ketoacidosis
- **P**araldehyde
- **I**ron, isoniazid
- **L**actic acidosis
- **E**thylene glycol
- **S**alicylates

**Lactic Acidosis:**
- Type A: Tissue hypoxia (sepsis, shock)
- Type B: No hypoxia (liver disease, malignancy)
- Treatment: Address underlying cause

**DKA:**
- Hyperglycemia, ketonemia, metabolic acidosis
- Treatment: Fluids, insulin, electrolyte replacement

**Toxic Ingestions:**
- Ethylene glycol: Oxalate crystals, renal failure
- Methanol: Formic acid, visual disturbances
- Treatment: Fomepizole, hemodialysis

#### Normal Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis
**Causes:**
- Diarrhea (bicarbonate loss)
- Renal tubular acidosis
- Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors
- Early renal failure

**Renal Tubular Acidosis:**
- Type 1: Distal RTA, inability to acidify urine
- Type 2: Proximal RTA, bicarbonate wasting
- Type 4: Hyperkalemic RTA, hypoaldosteronism

### Metabolic Alkalosis

#### Etiology
**Contraction Alkalosis:**
- Diuretic therapy
- Volume depletion

**Chloride-Responsive:**
- Vomiting, NG suction
- Diuretics
- Post-hypercapnia

**Chloride-Resistant:**
- Hyperaldosteronism
- Cushing's syndrome
- Severe hypokalemia

#### Treatment
**Volume Repletion:**
- Isotonic saline
- Correct chloride depletion
- Monitor electrolytes

**Acid Administration:**
- Acetazolamide
- HCl infusion (severe cases)
- Treat underlying cause

### Respiratory Acidosis

#### Acute Respiratory Acidosis
**Causes:**
- Airway obstruction
- CNS depression
- Neuromuscular disease
- Chest wall disorders

**Treatment:**
- Improve ventilation
- Treat underlying cause
- Consider mechanical ventilation

#### Chronic Respiratory Acidosis
**Causes:**
- COPD
- Obesity hypoventilation
- Chest wall deformities

**Treatment:**
- Supplemental oxygen
- Non-invasive ventilation
- Weight loss

### Respiratory Alkalosis

#### Causes
- Hyperventilation
- Anxiety, pain
- Early sepsis
- Pregnancy

#### Treatment
- Address underlying cause
- Rebreathing techniques
- Sedation if severe

## 5. Mixed Acid-Base Disorders

#### Recognition
**Stepwise Approach:**
1. Check pH (acidemia or alkalemia)
2. Evaluate primary disturbance
3. Assess compensation
4. Calculate anion gap
5. Look for mixed disorders

#### Common Combinations
- Metabolic acidosis + respiratory alkalosis (sepsis)
- Metabolic acidosis + respiratory acidosis (cardiopulmonary arrest)
- Metabolic alkalosis + respiratory acidosis (COPD with diuretics)

#### Delta-Delta Gap
**Formula:**
- Delta gap = Measured AG - Normal AG
- Delta HCO3 = Normal HCO3 - Measured HCO3
- If delta gap ≈ delta HCO3: pure AG acidosis
- If delta gap > delta HCO3: mixed AG + normal gap acidosis

## 6. Electrolyte Management in Special Populations

### Chronic Kidney Disease

#### Sodium
- Tend toward volume overload
- Restrict to 2-3 g/day
- Monitor for hypertension
- Adjust diuretics

#### Potassium
- Hyperkalemia common
- Dietary restriction
- Potassium binders
- Adjust medications

#### Calcium/Phosphate
- Hyperphosphatemia, secondary hyperparathyroidism
- Phosphate binders
- Active vitamin D
- Calcimimetics

### Dialysis Patients

#### Sodium
- Individualized dialysate sodium
- Avoid rapid shifts
- Monitor interdialytic weight gain

#### Potassium
- Dialysate potassium 2-3 mEq/L
- Monitor pre/post dialysis
- Dietary education

#### Acid-Base
- Correct metabolic acidosis
- Bicarbonate dialysate 35 mEq/L
- Monitor for alkalosis

### Heart Failure

#### Sodium
- Restrict to 2 g/day
- Monitor diuretic response
- Avoid hyponatremia

#### Potassium
- Monitor with diuretics, ACEi
- Replace losses
- Avoid hyperkalemia

#### Magnesium
- Depletion common with diuretics
- Replace if <2 mg/dL
- Monitor arrhythmia risk

## 7. Clinical Algorithms & Decision Trees

### Hyponatremia Evaluation Algorithm

```
Serum Na <135 mEq/L
        ↓
Assess Volume Status
        ↓
Hypovolemic → Check Urine Na
- <30 mEq/L: Extrarenal losses → Isotonic saline
- >30 mEq/L: Renal losses → Treat cause

Euvolemic → Check Urine Osmolality
- <100 mEq/L: Primary polydipsia → Fluid restriction
- >100 mEq/L: SIADH → Fluid restriction ± vaptans

Hypervolemic → Check Urine Na
- <30 mEq/L: Cirrhosis/CHF → Fluid restriction, diuretics
- >30 mEq/L: AKI/CKD → Treat underlying disease
```

### Hyperkalemia Emergency Protocol

```
Serum K >6.0 mEq/L OR ECG Changes
        ↓
CAGE-K Protocol:
C - Calcium gluconate 10% 10-30 mL IV
A - Consider bicarbonate if acidotic
G - Glucose 25g + Insulin 10 units IV
K - Kayexalate or patiromer for chronic management
        ↓
Recheck K and ECG in 1 hour
        ↓
Persistent >6.5 mEq/L → Repeat or prepare dialysis
```

---

## 8. Key Take-Home Points

1. **Hyponatremia**: Always assess volume status first; treat symptoms, not numbers
2. **Hypernatremia**: Calculate free water deficit; correct slowly to prevent cerebral edema
3. **Hypokalemia**: Always check magnesium level; replace both concurrently
4. **Hyperkalemia**: Use CAGE-K protocol; calcium protects heart, doesn't lower K
5. **Acid-Base**: Use systematic approach; calculate anion gap and compensation
6. **CKD Patients**: Different electrolyte targets; anticipate and prevent abnormalities
7. **Medication Effects**: Review medications in all electrolyte disorders
8. **Monitoring**: Frequent monitoring during correction; prevent overcorrection
9. **Mixed Disorders**: Common in critically ill; use delta-delta gap
10. **Prevention**: Identify at-risk patients; monitor high-risk medications
