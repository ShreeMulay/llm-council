# Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) - Comprehensive Clinical Guide

## Metadata
**Core Domain:** Clinical Nephrology I  
**Difficulty Level:** Foundation-Advanced  
**Frequency of Use:** Daily  
**Clinical Impact:** High  
**Regulatory Requirements:** QIP reporting, CMS documentation  
**Prerequisites:** Basic renal physiology, fluid/electrolyte balance  
**Competency Development Time:** 6-12 months  
**Cross-references:** Electrolyte disorders (Domain I.2), CRRT (Domain II.6), ICU nephrology (Domain VI.19)

---

## 1. Definition & Pathophysiology

### Clinical Definition
Acute Kidney Injury is defined as a rapid decrease in kidney function occurring over hours to days, characterized by:
- Rise in serum creatinine
- Decrease in urine output
- Accumulation of metabolic waste products
- Disturbances in fluid, electrolyte, and acid-base balance

### KDIGO 2012 Definition & Staging (Updated 2024 Consensus)
**Stage 1:**
- Serum creatinine rise ≥0.3 mg/dL (≥26.5 μmol/L) within 48 hours, OR
- Serum creatinine rise to 1.5-1.9 times baseline (within prior 7 days), OR
- Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/h for 6-12 hours

**Stage 2:**
- Serum creatinine rise to 2.0-2.9 times baseline, OR
- Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/h for ≥12 hours

**Stage 3:**
- Serum creatinine rise to ≥3.0 times baseline, OR
- Serum creatinine ≥4.0 mg/dL (≥353.6 μmol/L) with acute rise ≥0.5 mg/dL, OR
- Initiation of renal replacement therapy, OR
- In patients <18 years, decrease in eGFR to <35 mL/min/1.73m², OR
- Urine output <0.3 mL/kg/h for ≥24 hours, OR
- Anuria for ≥12 hours

**2024 Updates & Clarifications:**
- Baseline creatinine: Use lowest value within 7 days prior or up to 3 months prior
- Hospital-acquired AKI: Compare to admission creatinine if baseline unknown
- Community-acquired AKI: May use eGFR-back-calculation for baseline
- Functional AKI vs Structural AKI distinction increasingly emphasized

### Pathophysiological Mechanisms
**Hemodynamic Alterations:**
- Renal autoregulation failure
- Afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction
- Efferent arteriolar vasodilation
- Glomerular hydrostatic pressure reduction
- Reduced renal blood flow (RBF)

**Tubular Injury Mechanisms:**
- Ischemic injury to proximal tubules
- Cytoskeletal disruption and cell polarity loss
- Tubular obstruction from cell debris
- Back-leak of glomerular filtrate
- Inflammatory cascade activation

**Inflammatory Response:**
- Endothelial activation and leukocyte adhesion
- Cytokine release (TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β)
- Complement activation
- Oxidative stress and reactive oxygen species
- Microvascular dysfunction

## 2. Etiology Classification

### Pre-renal Azotemia (Functional AKI)

#### Volume Depletion States
**Absolute Hypovolemia:**
- Hemorrhage (trauma, GI bleeding, surgical)
- Severe diarrhea/vomiting
- Burns (>20% BSA)
- Pancreatitis (third spacing)
- Diuretic overuse
- Osmotic diuresis (hyperglycemia, mannitol)

**Relative Hypovolemia:**
- Cirrhosis with ascites
- Nephrotic syndrome
- Sepsis with vasodilation
- Anaphylaxis
- Cardiac tamponade

#### Cardiogenic Causes
**Decreased Cardiac Output:**
- Acute decompensated heart failure
- Cardiogenic shock
- Myocardial infarction
- Severe arrhythmias
- Pulmonary embolism

**Cardiorenal Syndromes:**
- Type 1: Acute cardiac dysfunction leading to AKI
- Type 2: Chronic cardiac dysfunction exacerbating CKD
- Type 5: Systemic disorders affecting both heart and kidneys

#### Hepatorenal Syndrome
**HRS-AKI (Type 1):**
- Rapid rise in creatinine (≥2.5 mg/dL)
- Occurs in setting of acute liver injury
- No improvement after volume expansion
- Absence of shock
- No nephrotoxic drug exposure
- No parenchymal kidney disease

**HRS-CKD (Type 2):**
- Gradual creatinine rise
- Stable or slowly progressive
- Refractory ascites
- Sodium retention

### Intrinsic AKI (Structural)

#### Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)
**Ischemic ATN:**
- Prolonged hypotension (>60 minutes MAP <65 mmHg)
- Major surgery (cardiac, vascular, abdominal)
- Severe trauma
- Sepsis with hemodynamic compromise
- Burns

**Nephrotoxic ATN:**
**Contrast Media:**
- High-osmolar contrast > low-osmolar > iso-osmolar
- Risk factors: CKD, diabetes, dehydration, high contrast volume
- Prevention: IV isotonic saline 1 mL/kg/h for 12 hours pre/post
- N-acetylcysteine not proven effective

**Aminoglycosides:**
- Gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin
- Accumulation in proximal tubular cells
- Dose-dependent toxicity
- Therapeutic drug monitoring essential
- Once-daily dosing reduces toxicity

**Amphotericin B:**
- Liposomal formulations less toxic
- Vasoconstriction of afferent arterioles
- Direct tubular cell toxicity
- Electrolyte wasting (K+, Mg++)

**Calcineurin Inhibitors:**
- Cyclosporine, tacrolimus
- Afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction
- Chronic interstitial fibrosis
- Therapeutic drug monitoring

**Other Nephrotoxins:**
- Acyclovir (crystal nephropathy)
- Methotrexate (precipitation)
- Tenofovir (proximal tubulopathy)
- Ifosfamide (Fanconi syndrome)
- Cisplatin (magnesium wasting)

#### Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN)
**Drug-Induced AIN:**
**Common Culprits:**
- Antibiotics: β-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins), quinolones, sulfonamides, rifampin
- PPIs: Omeprazole, pantoprazole, esomeprazole (most common cause)
- NSAIDs: Ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac
- Diuretics: Thiazides, furosemide
- Allopurinol
- 5-ASA compounds

**Clinical Features:**
- Fever (70-80%)
- Rash (30-50%)
- Eosinophilia (60-80%)
- Arthralgias
- Timing: 7-14 days after drug exposure

**Laboratory Findings:**
- Sterile pyuria with WBC casts
- Eosinophiluria (Hansel's stain)
- Mild proteinuria (<1 g/day)
- Elevated ESR/CRP

**Infection-Related AIN:**
- Bacterial: Leptospirosis, Brucellosis
- Viral: EBV, CMV, HIV, hepatitis B/C
- Fungal: Histoplasmosis
- Parasitic: Toxoplasmosis

**Systemic Disease AIN:**
- Sarcoidosis (non-caseating granulomas)
- Sjögren's syndrome
- IgG4-related disease
- Tubulointerstitial nephritis with uveitis (TINU)

#### Glomerular Diseases
**Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN):**
- Anti-GBM disease (Goodpasture's)
- ANCA-associated vasculitis
- Immune complex GN (lupus, IgA, post-infectious)
- Pauci-immune GN

**Clinical Presentation:**
- Active urinary sediment (RBC casts)
- Nephritic syndrome
- Rapid decline in GFR
- Systemic symptoms (vasculitis)

#### Vascular Causes
**Thrombotic Microangiopathy (TMA):**
- Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP)
- Hemolytic uremic syndrome (STEC-HUS, atypical HUS)
- Drug-induced (calcineurin inhibitors, quinine)
- Pregnancy-associated (HELLP, pre-eclampsia)
- Shiga-toxin producing E. coli

**Atheroembolic Disease:**
- Recent vascular procedure (catheterization, surgery)
- Cholesterol crystal emboli
- Livedo reticularis
- Eosinophilia
- Low complement levels

**Renal Vein Thrombosis:**
- Nephrotic syndrome (membranous nephropathy)
- Hypercoagulable states
- Malignancy
- Dehydration

### Post-renal AKI (Obstructive)

#### Anatomical Obstruction
**Ureteral Obstruction:**
- Calculi (most common)
- Blood clots
- Tumors (transitional cell carcinoma)
- Strictures
- External compression (retroperitoneal fibrosis, lymphadenopathy)
- Papillary necrosis

**Bladder Outlet Obstruction:**
- Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH)
- Prostate cancer
- Urethral stricture
- Neurogenic bladder
- Phimosis

**Urethral Obstruction:**
- Catheter obstruction/kinking
- Meatal stenosis
- Foreign bodies

#### Functional Obstruction
- Neurogenic bladder
- Medications (anticholinergics, α-agonists)
- Spinal cord injury
- Diabetes mellitus (autonomic neuropathy)

## 3. Diagnostic Approach

### Initial Assessment

#### History Taking
**Timeline of Events:**
- Onset and duration of symptoms
- Recent procedures/surgeries
- New medication exposures
- Contrast administration
- Recent infections
- Volume status changes

**Symptoms Review:**
- Decreased urine output
- Edema (peripheral, pulmonary)
- Shortness of breath
- Fatigue, nausea, confusion
- Flank pain (obstruction)
- Rash, fever, arthralgias (AIN)

**Past Medical History:**
- Baseline kidney function
- Chronic kidney disease
- Diabetes, hypertension
- Heart failure, liver disease
- Autoimmune diseases

#### Physical Examination
**Volume Status Assessment:**
- Skin turgor, mucous membranes
- Jugular venous pressure
- Pulmonary exam (crackles, effusions)
- Peripheral edema
- Orthostatic vitals

**Cardiovascular Exam:**
- Heart sounds, murmurs
- Peripheral pulses
- Capillary refill

**Abdominal Exam:**
- Bladder distension
- Flank tenderness
- Costovertebral angle tenderness
- Organomegaly

**Skin Examination:**
- Rash (drug reactions)
- Livedo reticularis (atheroemboli)
- Purpura (vasculitis)
- Pallor (anemia)

### Laboratory Evaluation

#### Basic Metabolic Panel
**Creatinine Kinetics:**
- Baseline creatinine comparison
- Creatinine rise rate
- Fractional excretion calculations
- Cystatin C if muscle mass abnormal

**BUN/Creatinine Ratio:**
- >20:1 suggests pre-renal azotemia
- <10-15:1 suggests intrinsic AKI
- Exceptions: steroids, protein catabolism, GI bleeding

#### Urine Studies
**Urinalysis:**
- Specific gravity (>1.020 suggests pre-renal)
- pH (acidic in ATN, alkaline in RTA)
- Protein (nephrotic range in glomerular disease)
- Blood (RBCs vs myoglobin vs hemoglobin)

**Urine Microscopy:**
**Pre-renal:**
- Hyaline casts
- Few cells/crystals

**ATN:**
- Muddy brown granular casts
- Renal tubular epithelial cells
- WBC casts

**AIN:**
- WBC casts
- Eosinophils (Hansel's stain)
- RBC casts (less common)

**Glomerular Disease:**
- Dysmorphic RBCs
- RBC casts
- Lipiduria (nephrotic syndrome)

**Urine Electrolytes:**
**FENa (Fractional Excretion of Sodium):**
- <1%: Pre-renal azotemia
- >2%: Intrinsic AKI (ATN)
- Limitations: Diuretic use, CKD, contrast nephropathy

**FEUrea (Fractional Excretion of Urea):**
- <35%: Pre-renal azotemia
- >50%: Intrinsic AKI
- More reliable with diuretic use

**Urine Osmolality:**
- >500 mOsm/kg: Pre-renal
- <350 mOsm/kg: Intrinsic AKI

#### Advanced Biomarkers (2024 Update)

**Damage Biomarkers:**
**NGAL (Neutrophil Gelatinase-Associated Lipocalin):**
  - Rises within 2-6 hours of injury
  - Predicts need for RRT (AUC 0.78-0.82)
  - Distinguishes pre-renal vs structural AKI
  - Plasma and urine forms available
  - Limitations: Sepsis, CKD can elevate levels
  - FDA-cleared test available
  - Cutoff: >150 ng/mL (varies by assay)

**KIM-1 (Kidney Injury Molecule-1):**
  - Proximal tubule injury marker
  - Highly specific for tubular damage
  - Rises 6-12 hours post-injury
  - Predicts progression to Stage 2-3 AKI
  - Not affected by CKD or sepsis
  - Mainly urinary marker

**IL-18 (Interleukin-18):**
  - Pro-inflammatory cytokine
  - Predictor of mortality and RRT need
  - Rises 4-6 hours post-injury
  - Additive to NGAL for prediction

**L-FABP (Liver-type Fatty Acid Binding Protein):**
  - Proximal tubular stress marker
  - Early detection (2-4 hours)
  - Approved in Japan for clinical use
  - Predicts AKI before creatinine rise

**TIMP-2·IGFBP7 (NephroCheck®):**
  - Cell cycle arrest markers (G1 phase)
  - **FDA-cleared test** for AKI risk stratification
  - Predicts moderate-severe AKI within 12 hours
  - Test result: [TIMP-2]·[IGFBP7]
  - Cutoff: >0.3 (ng/mL)²/1000 = elevated risk
  - NPV >95% (excellent rule-out test)
  - Most validated in cardiac surgery, ICU patients
  - Does not detect established AKI

**Emerging Biomarkers (Research Stage):**
**Urinary Angiotensinogen:**
  - Marker of intrarenal RAS activation
  - Predicts progression to CKD post-AKI

**Clusterin:**
  - Tubular injury marker
  - Early rise in cisplatin nephrotoxicity

**Cystatin C:**
  - Functional marker (GFR estimation)
  - Less affected by muscle mass
  - More expensive than creatinine
  - Useful in cirrhosis, malnutrition
  - Rises earlier than creatinine (24-48h vs 48-72h)
  - eGFR-cystatin C equations available

**Urinary Calprotectin:**
  - Marker of tubular inflammation
  - Predicts AKI in sepsis

**Proenkephalin (PENK):**
  - Filtration marker
  - Stable in blood samples
  - Predicts AKI and mortality

**Cell-Free DNA:**
  - Marker of cell death and injury
  - Research applications

**Multi-Biomarker Panels:**
  - Combinations improve prediction
  - Example: NGAL + KIM-1 + IL-18
  - Machine learning algorithms incorporating multiple biomarkers
  - Point-of-care devices in development

**Clinical Implementation Challenges:**
  - Cost and reimbursement
  - Lack of therapeutic interventions to prevent AKI
  - Need for point-of-care testing
  - Standardization across platforms
  - Integration into clinical workflows

### Imaging Studies

#### Renal Ultrasound
**Indications:**
- All AKI patients without obvious cause
- Suspected obstruction
- Baseline kidney size assessment

**Findings:**
- Kidney size (normal 10-12 cm)
- Cortical echogenicity
- Hydronephrosis grading
- Renal artery/vein Doppler
- Bladder volume

**Limitations:**
- Operator dependent
- Limited in obesity
- Cannot differentiate AKI causes

#### CT Scanning
**Non-contrast CT:**
- Nephrolithiasis
- Retroperitoneal fibrosis
- Mass lesions

**Contrast CT:**
- Vascular abnormalities
- Mass characterization
- Contraindicated in AKI unless essential

#### MRI
**Indications:**
- Renal artery stenosis
- Renal vein thrombosis
- Complex cysts (Bosniak classification)
- Contrast allergy (gadolinium caution in GFR <30)

## 4. Management Strategies

### General Principles

#### Hemodynamic Optimization
**Fluid Resuscitation:**
- Isotonic crystalloids (0.9% NS, LR)
- Avoid hypotonic fluids
- Target MAP >65 mmHg
- Avoid fluid overload

**Vasopressor Support:**
- Norepinephrine first-line
- Vasopressin as adjunct
- Avoid excessive vasoconstriction
- Maintain renal perfusion pressure

**Diuretic Strategy:**
- Loop diuretics (furosemide 40-80 mg IV)
- Continuous infusion vs bolus
- Avoid in hypovolemia
- Monitor for ototoxicity

#### Nephrotoxin Avoidance
**Medication Review:**
- Hold nephrotoxic medications
- Adjust doses for GFR
- Avoid contrast when possible
- Alternative imaging modalities

**High-Risk Medications:**
- NSAIDs (hold all)
- ACEi/ARBs (hold in AKI)
- Diuretics (use cautiously)
- Aminoglycosides (avoid)
- Amphotericin B (avoid)

### Specific Etiology Management

#### Pre-renal Azotemia
**Volume Repletion:**
- Crystalloid bolus 250-500 mL
- Repeat based on response
- Monitor for pulmonary edema
- Consider albumin in cirrhosis

**Cardiac Support:**
- Inotropes if low cardiac output
- Afterload reduction
- Mechanical support if needed

**Hepatorenal Syndrome:**
- Vasoconstrictors (terlipressin, midodrine + octreotide)
- Albumin infusion 1 g/kg
- Avoid diuretics
- Liver transplant evaluation

#### Acute Tubular Necrosis
**Supportive Care:**
- Maintain euvolemia
- Electrolyte management
- Nutritional support
- Avoid further nephrotoxins

**Diuretic Challenge:**
- Furosemide 1-2 mg/kg IV
- Continuous infusion if responsive
- Goal: Prevent fluid overload
- Does not prevent RRT need

#### Acute Interstitial Nephritis
**Discontinue Offending Agent:**
- Immediate cessation
- All medications reviewed
- Rechallenge only if essential

**Corticosteroid Therapy:**
- Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day
- Duration: 4-6 weeks
- Taper gradually
- Consider if no improvement in 72 hours

**Supportive Care:**
- Fluid management
- Electrolyte correction
- Monitor for recovery

#### Glomerular Disease (RPGN)
**Urgent Treatment:**
- High-dose steroids (methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg daily × 3 days)
- Plasma exchange (anti-GBM, ANCA)
- Cyclophosphamide or rituximab
- Supportive care

**Diagnostic Workup:**
- Urgent kidney biopsy
- Autoimmune serologies
- Complement levels
- Infectious workup

#### Obstructive AKI
**Relieve Obstruction:**
- Foley catheter placement
- Urological consultation
- Stent placement if needed
- Percutaneous nephrostomy

**Post-Obstructive Diuresis:**
- Monitor electrolytes
- Replace losses
- Avoid dehydration
- May last 24-72 hours

### Renal Replacement Therapy Indications

#### Absolute Indications (AEIOU)
**A - Acidosis:**
- Severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.1-7.2)
- Refractory to bicarbonate therapy

**E - Electrolytes:**
- Severe hyperkalemia (>6.5 mEq/L)
- Refractory to medical therapy
- ECG changes

**I - Intoxication:**
- Toxic ingestions (lithium, metformin)
- Rhabdomyolysis
- Tumor lysis syndrome

**O - Overload:**
- Refractory pulmonary edema
- Severe fluid overload
- Unable to tolerate fluids

**U - Uremia:**
- Symptomatic uremia (pericarditis, encephalopathy)
- BUN >100 mg/dL with symptoms
- Intractable nausea/vomiting

#### Relative Indications
- Refractory hyperkalemia (>5.5-6.0 mEq/L)
- Moderate acidosis (pH 7.1-7.2)
- Fluid overload compromising oxygenation
- Need for nutrition/medications
- Temperature control

#### Modality Selection
**Intermittent Hemodialysis (IHD):**
- Hemodynamically stable patients
- Rapid solute clearance needed
- Drug intoxication
- Resource-limited settings

**Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy (CRRT):**
- Hemodynamically unstable
- ICP concerns
- Gradual solute removal
- 24/7 ICU availability

**Sustained Low-Efficiency Dialysis (SLED):**
- Intermediate option
- Hemodynamic stability
- Resource efficiency
- Anticoagulation flexibility

## 5. Special Populations

### Cardiac Surgery Patients

#### Risk Factors
- Pre-existing CKD
- Diabetes mellitus
- Advanced age
- Low cardiac output syndrome
- Cardiopulmonary bypass time >120 minutes
- Postoperative low cardiac output

#### Prevention Strategies
**Preoperative:**
- Optimize volume status
- Hold nephrotoxic medications
- Baseline creatinine/cystatin C
- Risk stratification

**Intraoperative:**
- Maintain MAP >65 mmHg
- Avoid excessive hemodilution
- Minimize bypass time
- Consider off-pump surgery

**Postoperative:**
- Optimize cardiac output
- Maintain euvolemia
- Early nephrology consultation
- Biomarker monitoring (NGAL)

#### Management
- Hemodynamic optimization
- Avoid hypotension
- Diuretic challenge if fluid overloaded
- Early RRT if indicated
- Consider remote ischemic preconditioning

### Sepsis-Associated AKI

#### Pathophysiology
- Global renal hypoperfusion
- Microcirculatory dysfunction
- Inflammatory mediator injury
- Metabolic reprogramming
- Cellular bioenergetic failure

#### Clinical Features
- Often non-oliguric initially
- High urinary output despite AKI
- Rapid creatinine rise
- Fluid overload common
- Multi-organ dysfunction

#### Management Principles
**Sepsis Management:**
- Early appropriate antibiotics
- Source control
- Hemodynamic support
- Vasopressor optimization

**Renal-Specific Care:**
- Conservative fluid strategy after initial resuscitation
- Avoid nephrotoxins
- Early biomarker monitoring
- Consider early RRT for fluid control

**RRT Timing:**
- No clear consensus
- Consider for severe fluid overload
- Earlier intervention may improve outcomes
- Individualized decision-making

### Contrast-Associated AKI

#### Risk Stratification
**High-Risk Patients:**
- eGFR <45 mL/min/1.73m²
- Diabetes mellitus
- Dehydration
- High contrast volume (>100 mL)
- Multiple contrast exposures

**Risk Scores:**
- Mehran risk score
- Chen risk score
- CIN risk calculator

#### Prevention Strategies
**Pre-Procedure:**
- IV isotonic saline 1 mL/kg/h for 12 hours
- Hold nephrotoxic medications
- Consider alternative imaging
- Use low-osmolar or iso-osmolar contrast

**Intra-Procedure:**
- Minimize contrast volume
- Use iso-osmolar contrast
- Avoid repeat injections
- Consider CO2 angiography

**Post-Procedure:**
- Continue hydration 6-12 hours
- Monitor creatinine 48-72 hours
- Avoid nephrotoxins
- Outpatient follow-up

#### Management
- Supportive care
- Monitor for progression
- Usually self-limited
- Rarely requires RRT
- Long-term follow-up for CKD development

### Liver Disease Patients

#### Hepatorenal Syndrome (HRS) - Comprehensive Management (2024 Update)

**2024 Nomenclature (Revised Classification):**
- **HRS-AKI**: Hepatorenal syndrome with acute kidney injury
  - Formerly "Type 1 HRS"
  - Rapid creatinine rise (≥0.3 mg/dL within 48h OR ≥50% from baseline within 7 days)
  - Rapidly progressive, high mortality without treatment
- **HRS-NAKI (Non-AKI)**: Hepatorenal syndrome without AKI criteria
  - Formerly "Type 2 HRS"
  - Stable or slowly progressive renal dysfunction (Cr 1.5-2.5 mg/dL)
  - Associated with refractory ascites
  - Better prognosis than HRS-AKI

**Pathophysiology:**
- Splanchnic vasodilation → reduced effective arterial blood volume
- Renal vasoconstriction (increased sympathetic activity, RAAS activation)
- Low cardiac output despite hyperdynamic circulation
- Functional renal failure (kidneys structurally normal)

**Diagnostic Criteria (International Club of Ascites 2024):**

**Major Criteria (All Required):**
1. Cirrhosis with ascites (or acute liver failure)
2. AKI per ICA-AKI criteria:
   - Increase in SCr ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours, OR
   - Increase in SCr ≥50% from baseline (within prior 7 days)
3. **No response to diuretic withdrawal AND volume expansion**:
   - Hold diuretics ×2 days
   - Albumin 1 g/kg (maximum 100 g/day) × 2 consecutive days
   - Serum creatinine does NOT decrease to <1.5 mg/dL

**Exclusions (Must Rule Out):**
1. Shock (septic, cardiogenic, hypovolemic)
2. Recent nephrotoxic drug use (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast, vancomycin)
3. Parenchymal kidney disease:
   - Proteinuria >500 mg/day
   - Hematuria >50 RBC/hpf
   - Abnormal renal ultrasound (unless pre-existing CKD)
4. Ongoing bacterial infection (treat first, reassess)

**Additional Supportive Criteria:**
- Urine sodium <10 mEq/L
- Urine osmolality > plasma osmolality
- Fractional excretion of sodium (FENa) <1%
- Oliguria (<500 mL/day)
- Low urinary biomarkers (NGAL, KIM-1)

**Treatment Protocols (2024 Guidelines):**

**1. Vasoconstrictor Therapy (First-Line):**

**Option A: Terlipressin + Albumin (PREFERRED - FDA Approved 2022)**
- **Terlipressin (Terlivaz®)**:
  - Initial: 0.5-1 mg IV bolus every 4-6 hours
  - Titrate up by 0.5-1 mg every 3 days if Cr not decreasing
  - Maximum: 2 mg IV every 4 hours (12 mg/day)
  - **Alternative**: Continuous infusion 2-12 mg/day
  - Duration: Until Cr <1.5 mg/dL OR maximum 14 days
- **Albumin**:
  - Day 1: 1 g/kg IV (maximum 100 g)
  - Day 2 onward: 20-40 g/day IV
  - Continue throughout terlipressin course
- **Response Assessment**:
  - Complete response: Cr <1.5 mg/dL (50-60% of patients)
  - Partial response: Cr decrease ≥50% but still >1.5 mg/dL
  - No response: <25-30% Cr reduction by day 3 → consider alternative
- **Contraindications**:
  - Ischemic heart disease, arrhythmias
  - Peripheral vascular disease
  - Cerebrovascular disease
- **Adverse Effects**:
  - Ischemic events (5-10%): abdominal pain, skin necrosis, MI
  - Bradycardia, hypertension
  - Monitor: ECG, troponin if chest pain

**Option B: Midodrine + Octreotide + Albumin (Alternative if terlipressin unavailable/contraindicated)**
- **Midodrine**: 7.5-15 mg PO TID (alpha-1 agonist)
  - Start 7.5 mg TID, increase to 12.5-15 mg TID as tolerated
- **Octreotide**: 100-200 mcg SC TID (or 50 mcg/hour continuous IV infusion)
- **Albumin**: Same as above (1 g/kg day 1, then 20-40 g/day)
- **Efficacy**: Less effective than terlipressin (~30-40% response vs 50-60%)
- **Duration**: 5-14 days

**Option C: Norepinephrine + Albumin (ICU Setting)**
- **Norepinephrine**: 0.5-3 mg/hour IV continuous infusion
- Titrate to MAP goal 80-85 mmHg
- **Albumin**: As above
- **Setting**: Only in ICU with arterial line
- **Efficacy**: Similar to terlipressin in small trials

**2. Albumin Administration Details:**
- **Rationale**: Volume expansion + oncotic support + anti-inflammatory effects
- **Dosing**:
  - Initial diagnostic trial: 1 g/kg × 2 days (max 100 g/day)
  - Therapeutic (with vasoconstrictors): 20-40 g/day IV
- **Monitoring**: CVP, JVP (avoid volume overload)

**3. Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT):**
- **Indications**:
  - Severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.1)
  - Hyperkalemia refractory to medical therapy
  - Severe volume overload with respiratory compromise
  - Uremic symptoms (encephalopathy, pericarditis)
  - Bridge to liver transplantation
- **Modality**:
  - CRRT preferred (CVVH or CVVHDF) - hemodynamic stability
  - Avoid intermittent HD (hypotension worsens HRS)
- **Caution**: RRT does NOT treat underlying HRS pathophysiology

**4. Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt (TIPS):**
- **Indications**:
  - HRS-AKI refractory to medical therapy
  - Recurrent HRS after initial treatment
  - Bridge to transplant
- **Requirements**:
  - MELD <18-20 (better outcomes)
  - Bilirubin <5 mg/dL
  - No severe hepatic encephalopathy
  - Adequate cardiac function
- **Efficacy**: 60-75% improvement in renal function
- **Risks**: Hepatic encephalopathy (30-50%), heart failure

**5. Liver Transplantation:**
- **Timing**:
  - Urgent evaluation for all HRS-AKI patients
  - HRS presence increases MELD score (consider MELD-Na)
- **Simultaneous Liver-Kidney (SLK) Transplant Indications**:
  - AKI requiring dialysis >6 weeks, OR
  - CKD with eGFR <25 mL/min for >3 months, OR
  - Metabolic disease requiring both organs
- **Renal Recovery Post-Liver Transplant**:
  - 60-70% of HRS patients recover renal function after liver transplant alone
  - Recovery may take 3-12 months

**Prevention Strategies:**
1. **Avoid precipitants**:
   - Nephrotoxic drugs (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides)
   - Aggressive diuresis
   - Large-volume paracentesis without albumin
2. **Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP) prophylaxis**:
   - High-risk cirrhosis: norfloxacin 400 mg daily or ciprofloxacin 500 mg weekly
   - Albumin after SBP diagnosis: 1.5 g/kg day 1, 1 g/kg day 3
3. **Albumin after large-volume paracentesis (LVP)**:
   - 6-8 g albumin per liter of ascites removed (if >5 L removed)

**Prognosis:**
- **Untreated HRS-AKI**: 80-90% mortality at 2 weeks
- **Treated with terlipressin**: 40-50% mortality at 3 months
- **Post-liver transplant**: Renal recovery in 60-70%
- **Predictors of poor outcome**:
  - MELD score >30
  - Bilirubin >10 mg/dL
  - No response to vasoconstrictors by day 3
  - Need for mechanical ventilation

**Monitoring During Treatment:**
- Daily: SCr, BUN, electrolytes, urine output
- Every 2-3 days: Liver function tests, coagulation panel
- Hemodynamics: CVP, MAP (target MAP 80-85 mmHg)
- Terlipressin patients: Daily ECG, troponin if chest pain
- Assess response by day 3-4 (consider alternative if no improvement)

### COVID-19-Associated AKI

#### Epidemiology & Pathophysiology
- **Incidence:** 20-40% in hospitalized patients, up to 50-80% in ICU
- **Mechanisms:**
  - Direct viral injury to tubular and endothelial cells (ACE2 receptor)
  - Cytokine storm and systemic inflammatory response
  - Endothelial dysfunction and microangiopathy
  - Hemodynamic instability from sepsis/ARDS
  - Hypercoagulability and microthrombi
  - Rhabdomyolysis in severe cases
  - Collapsing glomerulopathy (APOL1 high-risk genotypes)

#### Clinical Features
- Often severe, prolonged AKI
- High rates of RRT requirement (20-30% in ICU)
- Collapsing FSGS (particularly in African ancestry with APOL1 variants)
- Proteinuria common (30-60%)
- High mortality when RRT required (50-70%)
- Electrolyte abnormalities (hypokalemia from GI losses, hyperkalemia in AKI)

#### Diagnostic Considerations
- Standard AKI workup
- Consider kidney biopsy in:
  - Nephrotic-range proteinuria
  - Prolonged AKI without clear cause
  - Suspected collapsing glomerulopathy
- APOL1 genotyping in high-risk patients
- Urinalysis: proteinuria, hematuria common

#### Management
- **Supportive Care:**
  - Hemodynamic optimization
  - Avoid nephrotoxins
  - Prone positioning for ARDS (may improve renal perfusion)
  - Conservative fluid strategy after resuscitation
- **RRT Considerations:**
  - Standard indications (AEIOU)
  - Regional citrate anticoagulation preferred
  - Extended RRT sessions common due to clotting
  - Consider higher CRRT dose in cytokine storm
- **Specific Therapies:**
  - No proven renal-specific treatments
  - Remdesivir: requires eGFR >30 (monitor for AKI)
  - Dexamethasone: beneficial in severe COVID (may help AKI)
  - Anticoagulation per institutional protocols
- **Long-term Outcomes:**
  - Many patients recover renal function
  - 20-30% develop CKD
  - Higher risk with prolonged AKI, need for RRT
  - Close nephrology follow-up essential

### Heat Stress-Associated AKI (Climate Change Impact)

#### Mesoamerican Nephropathy (Chronic Kidney Disease of Non-Traditional Causes)
**Epidemiology:**
- Epidemic in Central America (El Salvador, Nicaragua, Guatemala)
- Agricultural workers (sugarcane, cotton)
- Young men (20-50 years) predominantly affected
- Hot, humid climate with strenuous labor

**Pathophysiology:**
- Recurrent episodes of volume depletion and heat stress
- Repeated AKI leading to CKD
- Fructose metabolism in heat stress (uric acid nephropathy)
- Tubular injury and interstitial fibrosis
- Possible environmental toxin co-factors (pesticides)

**Clinical Features:**
- Progressive CKD without traditional risk factors
- Minimal proteinuria
- Tubulointerstitial disease on biopsy
- Hyperuricemia common
- Early onset ESRD

**Prevention Strategies:**
- Adequate hydration (water breaks every 15-30 min)
- Electrolyte replacement
- Shade and rest periods
- Reduce workload during peak heat
- Occupational health programs
- Early detection programs

#### Acute Heat Stress AKI
**Risk Populations:**
- Military personnel
- Athletes (especially in hot climates)
- Outdoor workers
- Elderly during heat waves
- Lack of air conditioning access

**Prevention & Management:**
- Aggressive hydration
- Electrolyte repletion
- Cooling measures
- Monitor renal function during heat waves
- Public health interventions

### Pregnancy-Related AKI

#### Unique Considerations
**Physiological Changes:**
- Increased GFR (40-50% above baseline)
- Lower baseline creatinine (0.4-0.6 mg/dL)
- Creatinine >0.8 mg/dL may indicate impairment
- Increased renal blood flow

**Causes by Trimester:**
**First Trimester:**
- Hyperemesis gravidarum (volume depletion)
- Septic abortion
- Ectopic pregnancy

**Second/Third Trimester:**
- Preeclampsia/eclampsia
- HELLP syndrome
- Acute fatty liver of pregnancy
- Thrombotic microangiopathy
- Urinary tract obstruction

**Postpartum:**
- Hemorrhage
- Sepsis
- Postpartum preeclampsia
- Atypical HUS

#### Preeclampsia-Associated AKI
- Proteinuria (often >300 mg/24h)
- Hypertension (>140/90 mmHg)
- Endotheliosis on biopsy
- Management: delivery when indicated
- May require RRT in severe cases

#### Management Principles
- Multidisciplinary care (OB/GYN, nephrology, MFM)
- Avoid nephrotoxic medications
- ACEi/ARBs contraindicated
- Safe medications: methyldopa, labetalol, nifedipine
- Consider early delivery in severe cases
- RRT if standard indications met

## 9. Clinical Algorithms

### Initial AKI Evaluation Algorithm

```
AKI Detected (↑Cr or ↓UO)
        ↓
Urgent Assessment (ABCs, Volume Status)
        ↓
Focused History & Physical
        ↓
Laboratory Evaluation:
- BMP, CBC, UA with microscopy
- Urine electrolytes (FENa, FEUrea)
- Medication review
        ↓
Imaging:
- Renal ultrasound (if no obvious cause)
- Consider CT/MRI if indicated
        ↓
Determine Etiology:
- Pre-renal? → Volume optimization
- Intrinsic? → Specific treatment
- Post-renal? → Relieve obstruction
        ↓
Monitor & Reassess
```

### RRT Initiation Algorithm

```
AKI Patient Assessment
        ↓
Check AEIOU Indications:
A - Acidosis (pH <7.1)
E - Electrolytes (K+ >6.5)
I - Intoxication
O - Overload (pulmonary edema)
U - Uremia (symptomatic)
        ↓
Assess Hemodynamic Status:
- Stable → IHD
- Unstable → CRRT
- Intermediate → SLED
        ↓
Vascular Access:
- IJ femoral, or subclavian
- Prefer right IJ for CRRT
- Consider tunneled catheter if >7 days
        ↓
Initiate RRT
        ↓
Daily Reassessment
```

## 11. Recent Clinical Trials & Evidence Updates

### RRT Timing Trials

**STARRT-AKI Trial (2020):**
- **Design:** Multicenter RCT, 3019 patients
- **Question:** Early vs delayed RRT initiation strategy
- **Early strategy:** RRT within 12 hours of eligibility
- **Delayed strategy:** RRT only for absolute indications or 72 hours post-eligibility
- **Results:**
  - No difference in 90-day mortality (43.9% early vs 43.7% delayed)
  - More patients in delayed group avoided RRT (38%)
  - RRT dependence similar at 90 days
  - Catheter-related bloodstream infections higher in early group
- **Conclusion:** Delayed strategy is reasonable in hemodynamically stable patients

**AKIKI Trials:**
**AKIKI (2016):**
- Early vs delayed RRT in severe AKI
- No mortality benefit with early initiation
- 49% in delayed group avoided RRT

**AKIKI-2 (2021):**
- More delayed strategy (wait-and-see up to 7 days)
- vs delayed strategy from AKIKI (2-3 days)
- No mortality difference
- 37% avoided RRT in more-delayed group

**IDEAL-ICU Trial (2018):**
- Early (within 12h) vs delayed (48h) RRT
- No mortality benefit with early initiation
- Trend toward harm with early strategy

**Clinical Implications:**
- No benefit to early "pre-emptive" RRT
- Watchful waiting approach appropriate if:
  - Hemodynamically stable
  - No absolute indications (AEIOU)
  - Close monitoring possible
  - Electrolytes manageable medically
- ~40% patients may avoid RRT with delayed approach

### Fluid Management Trials

**BICAR-ICU Trial (2018):**
- Sodium bicarbonate for severe metabolic acidosis in AKI
- No mortality benefit
- Trend toward lower need for RRT

**PLUS Trial (2022):**
- Plasma-Lyte vs 0.9% saline in ICU patients
- No difference in 90-day mortality
- No difference in AKI incidence or severity
- Buffered crystalloids not superior to saline

**Conservative Fluid Strategy:**
- After initial resuscitation, conservative approach preferred
- Avoid fluid overload (associated with worse outcomes)
- Target neutral or negative balance once hemodynamically stable

### Prevention Trials

**PRESERVE Trial (JAMA 2020):**
- High-dose vs low-dose statin before cardiac surgery
- No reduction in AKI incidence
- Statins not effective for AKI prevention

**PrevAKI Trial (2023):**
- TIMP-2·IGFBP7-guided intervention bundle
- Bundle: optimization of volume status, hemodynamics, nephrotoxin avoidance
- 35% relative risk reduction in AKI (Stage 2-3)
- First trial showing biomarker-guided intervention can prevent AKI

## 13. Novel and Emerging Therapies

### Pharmacologic Interventions (Investigational)

**Alkaline Phosphatase:**
- Recombinant enzyme therapy
- Detoxifies LPS, reduces inflammation
- Phase 2 trials in sepsis-associated AKI
- Mixed results to date

**Remote Ischemic Preconditioning (RIPC):**
- Brief ischemia cycles to arm/leg before surgery
- May reduce cardiac surgery-associated AKI
- ERICCA and RIPHeart trials: no benefit
- Ongoing research in specific populations

**Mesenchymal Stem Cells (MSCs):**
- Anti-inflammatory and regenerative properties
- Preclinical promise
- Early phase clinical trials ongoing
- Safety appears acceptable
- Efficacy not yet proven

**SGLT2 Inhibitors:**
- Not for acute AKI treatment
- May reduce AKI risk in CKD patients
- Mechanisms: hemodynamic, anti-inflammatory, metabolic
- Ongoing trials (EMPULSE, EMPA-KIDNEY)

**Complement Inhibitors:**
- For complement-mediated AKI (aHUS, C3GN)

- Investigational for other AKI etiologies

## 15. Key Take-Home Points (2025 Update)

1. **Early Recognition**: Use KDIGO criteria; biomarkers (TIMP-2·IGFBP7, NGAL) can detect AKI before creatinine rises
2. **Systematic Approach**: Classify as pre-renal, intrinsic, or post-renal using clinical, laboratory, and imaging data
3. **Rapid Evaluation**: Urgent assessment, focused labs (FENa, microscopy), timely ultrasound
4. **Prevention Focus**: Avoid nephrotoxins, optimize hemodynamics, consider biomarker-guided care bundles
5. **Individualized Care**: Tailor treatment to etiology - pre-renal (volume), ATN (supportive), AIN (steroids), RPGN (immunosuppression)
6. **RRT Timing**: Use AEIOU for absolute indications; delayed strategy reasonable if hemodynamically stable (STARRT-AKI, AKIKI trials)
7. **Special Populations**: COVID-19, heat stress, pregnancy require specific management considerations
8. **Novel Biomarkers**: FDA-cleared NephroCheck (TIMP-2·IGFBP7) for risk stratification; NGAL, KIM-1 increasingly available
9. **AI & Machine Learning**: Emerging prediction models can identify high-risk patients 24-48 hours early
10. **Long-term Follow-up**: 30-50% develop CKD; monitor eGFR, proteinuria, cardiovascular risk at 3, 6, 12 months
11. **Quality Documentation**: Accurate KDIGO staging, clear etiology, prevention measures documented
12. **Multidisciplinary Care**: Collaborate with ICU, cardiology, urology, obstetrics as appropriate
13. **Patient Education**: Medication safety (avoid NSAIDs, nephrotoxins), hydration, warning signs for recurrence
14. **Climate Change Awareness**: Heat stress-associated AKI increasingly recognized; prevention critical for outdoor workers
15. **Evidence-Based Practice**: Delayed RRT, conservative fluids post-resuscitation, biomarker-guided interventions show promise
