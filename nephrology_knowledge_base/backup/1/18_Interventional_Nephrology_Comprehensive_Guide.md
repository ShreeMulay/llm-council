## Vascular Access Planning and Creation

### Preoperative Assessment

#### Patient Evaluation
- **Vascular Mapping**: 
  - Physical examination: Allen test, pulse assessment
  - Duplex ultrasound: Vein diameter >2.5 mm, artery diameter >2.0 mm
  - Venography: Reserved for complex cases
  - Central vein evaluation: If previous central lines or pacemakers

#### Anatomical Considerations
```
Upper Extremity Vein Assessment:
- Cephalic vein: 2-6 mm diameter preferred
- Basilic vein: 3-8 mm diameter, deep location
- Median cubital vein: Connection between cephalic and basilic
- Deep veins: Brachial, axillary for secondary access

Arterial Assessment:
- Radial artery: 2-3 mm diameter
- Brachial artery: 4-6 mm diameter
- Flow requirements: >500 mL/min for dialysis

Contraindications:
- Central vein stenosis/occlusion
- Severe peripheral arterial disease
- Previous AVF/AVG failure at same site
- Lymphedema of planned limb
```

### Autologous Arteriovenous Fistula (AVF)

#### Radiocephalic Fistula (Wrist)
- **Location**: Radial artery to cephalic vein at wrist
- **Success Rate**: 60-80% primary maturation
- **Advantages**: Most distal, preserves proximal veins
- **Technique**: 
  - 2-3 cm longitudinal incision
  - End-to-side anastomosis preferred
  - 6-0 or 7-0 polypropylene suture
  - Target flow: 500-800 mL/min

#### Brachiocephalic Fistula (Elbow)
- **Location**: Brachial artery to cephalic vein at elbow
- **Success Rate**: 70-90% primary maturation
- **Indications**: Failed radiocephalic or inadequate veins
- **Complications**: Steal syndrome, high-output cardiac failure

#### Brachiobasilic Fistula (Upper Arm)
- **Location**: Brachial artery to basilic vein
- **Technique**: 
  - Two-stage procedure preferred
  - Stage 1: Fistula creation with vein transposition
  - Stage 2: Superficialization after 4-6 weeks
- **Success Rate**: 80-95% primary maturation
- **Advantages**: High success in difficult anatomy

### Arteriovenous Graft (AVG)

#### Graft Materials and Types
```
PTFE Grafts:
- Standard PTFE: Most common, 6 mm diameter
- Thin-walled PTFE: Easier needle insertion
- Stretch PTFE: Reduced kinking

Biologic Grafts:
- Bovine carotid artery: Good patency, expensive
- Cryopreserved vein: Limited availability
- Tissue-engineered grafts: Emerging technology

Graft Configurations:
- Straight graft: Forearm loop
- Loop graft: Upper arm loop
- Straight upper arm: Brachial to axillary
```

#### Surgical Technique
- **Tunneling**: Subcutaneous tunnel with gentle curve
- **Anastomosis**: 
  - Arterial anastomosis: End-to-side with 5-0 or 6-0 PTFE suture
  - Venous anastomosis: End-to-side with 6-0 PTFE suture
- **Graft Length**: 20-40 cm depending on configuration
- **Flow Targets**: 800-1200 mL/min

### Catheter-Based Access

#### Tunneled Cuffed Catheters
- **Indications**: 
  - Bridge to permanent access
  - Permanent access when AVF/AVG not possible
  - Acute dialysis initiation
- **Types**: 
  - Split-tip (Ash Split, Palindrome)
  - Step-tip (Tesio)
  - Symmetric tip (HemoStar)
- **Placement Sites**: 
  - Right internal jugular (preferred)
  - Left internal jugular
  - External jugular
  - Femoral (temporary, <2 weeks)

#### Non-Tunneled Catheters
- **Indications**: Acute dialysis, ICU settings
- **Types**: 
  - Double lumen: 11-14 French
  - Triple lumen: Additional monitoring port
- **Sites**: 
  - Internal jugular (preferred)
  - Femoral (if IJ not available)
  - Subclavian (high stenosis rate, avoid)

## Access Maturation and Monitoring

### Fistula Maturation

#### Maturation Criteria (KDOQI 2020)
- **Flow Rate**: >500 mL/min (preferably >600 mL/min)
- **Diameter**: >6 mm on ultrasound
- **Depth**: <6 mm from skin surface
- **Length**: >6 cm straight segment for cannulation
- **Time**: 4-6 weeks for radiocephalic, 6-8 weeks for brachiocephalic

#### Maturation Enhancement Techniques
```
Physical Therapy:
- Hand grip exercises: 10 minutes, 3 times daily
- Isometric exercises: Compression ball
- Venous distension: Tourniquet use during exercise

Pharmacologic:
- No proven medications for maturation
- Avoid vasoconstrictors during maturation period

Interventional:
- Percutaneous transluminal angioplasty (PTA) of stenoses
- Flow-limiting branch ligation
- Venous plication or banding
```

### Surveillance Protocols

#### Physical Examination
- **Inspection**: Aneurysm formation, infection signs
- **Palpation**: Thrill intensity (graded 0-4+)
- **Auscultation**: Bruit characteristics
- **Pulse Augmentation Test**: 
  - Occlude vein distal to access
  - Palpate for augmentation of thrill
  - Diminished augmentation suggests stenosis

#### Duplex Ultrasound Surveillance
```
Monitoring Schedule:
- AVF: Every 3 months after maturation
- AVG: Every month after first 6 weeks
- High-risk access: Every 2-4 weeks

Critical Stenosis Criteria:
- Peak systolic velocity ratio >2.0
- Flow rate <500 mL/min (AVF) or <600 mL/min (AVG)
- Access recirculation >10%
- Elevated venous pressures (>250 mmHg on 3 consecutive treatments)
```

#### Access Flow Monitoring
- **Transonic Ultrasound Dilution**: Gold standard for flow measurement
- **Crit-Line**: Real-time monitoring during dialysis
- **Temperature Monitor**: Alternative flow assessment
- **Flow Reduction**: >25% from baseline indicates significant stenosis

## Access Complications and Interventions

### Stenosis and Thrombosis

#### Stenosis Management
```
Percutaneous Transluminal Angioplasty (PTA):
- Indications: >50% stenosis with access dysfunction
- Technique: 
  * Access under ultrasound guidance
  * Heparin 50-100 U/kg
  * Balloon size: 1.1-1.2× vessel diameter
  * Inflation pressure: 10-20 atm for 1-2 minutes
- Success Rate: 80-90% immediate success
- Patency: 50% at 6 months, 30% at 12 months

Stent Placement:
- Indications: Elastic recoil, recurrent stenosis
- Types: Bare-metal stents, covered stents
- Complications: Stent fracture, in-stent restenosis
- Patency: Better than PTA alone in selected cases
```

#### Thrombectomy Techniques
- **Pharmacomechanical**: 
  - Tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) infusion
  - Mechanical thrombectomy devices
  - Combination therapy for organized thrombus
- **Surgical Thrombectomy**: 
  - Indications: Failed percutaneous attempts
  - Technique: Fogarty catheter thrombectomy
  - Success Rate: 70-80% immediate success
- **Thrombolysis Protocol**:
  - tPA 2-5 mg infusion over 30 minutes
  - Heparin infusion to prevent re-thrombosis
  - Follow-up PTA of underlying stenosis

### Infection Management

#### Catheter-Related Infections
```
Exit Site Infection:
- Signs: Erythema, purulent discharge, tenderness
- Treatment: 
  * Oral antibiotics covering S. aureus
  * Local care with antiseptic solutions
  * Consider catheter removal if refractory

Tunnel Infection:
- Signs: Pain along tunnel tract, fever
- Treatment: 
  * Systemic antibiotics (IV)
  * Catheter removal usually required
  * Antibiotic duration: 2-4 weeks

Catheter-Related Bacteremia:
- Treatment: 
  * Immediate catheter removal
  * Blood cultures before antibiotics
  * Empiric: Vancomycin + gram-negative coverage
  * Duration: 7-14 days after catheter removal
```

#### AVF/AVG Infections
- **Superficial Infections**: Oral antibiotics, local wound care
- **Deep Infections**: 
  - IV antibiotics, surgical drainage
  - Graft excision may be necessary
  - Staphylococcus aureus most common pathogen

### Steal Syndrome

#### Pathophysiology and Diagnosis
- **Mechanism**: Reversal of flow distal to anastomosis
- **Risk Factors**: 
  - Brachial artery-based access
  - Large diameter anastomosis
  - Peripheral arterial disease
  - Diabetes mellitus

#### Clinical Grading
```
Grade I (Mild):
- Cold hand without pain
- No tissue loss
- Conservative management

Grade II (Moderate):
- Cold hand with pain during dialysis
- No tissue loss
- Consider intervention

Grade III (Severe):
- Rest pain, tissue loss, ulceration
- Requires urgent intervention
- Surgical revision or ligation
```

#### Treatment Options
- **Band Ligation**: Reduce flow through access
- **Distal Revascularization**: DRIL procedure
- **Revision Using Distal Inflow**: RUDI technique
- **Proximalization of Arterial Inflow**: PAI procedure

### Aneurysm and Pseudoaneurysm

#### True Aneurysm Management
- **Indications for Intervention**: 
  - Rapid enlargement (>1 cm/year)
  - Skin thinning or ulceration
  - Limiting cannulation sites
  - High-flow cardiac failure

#### Surgical Options
- **Aneurysm Resection**: 
  - Excise aneurysmal segment
  - Primary repair or interposition graft
  - Bypass graft if extensive disease
- **Ligation**: 
  - When access no longer needed
  - Multiple aneurysms with infection

#### Pseudoaneurysm Treatment
- **Ultrasound-Guided Compression**: 
  - 10-15 minutes compression
  - Success rate 60-70%
- **Thrombin Injection**: 
  - Ultrasound-guided injection
  - 0.1-0.5 mL thrombin
  - Immediate thrombosis in 90%

## Advanced Interventional Techniques

### Central Vein Interventions

#### Central Vein Stenosis
- **Etiology**: 
  - Previous central venous catheters
  - Pacemaker/defibrillator leads
  - High-flow access
  - Thoracic outlet syndrome

#### Interventional Approaches
```
Percutaneous Angioplasty:
- High-pressure balloons (12-20 atm)
- Cutting balloons for resistant lesions
- Drug-coated balloons (emerging)

Stent Placement:
- Indications: Elastic recoil, recurrent stenosis
- Types: Self-expanding nitinol stents
- Size: 10-16 mm diameter
- Complications: Stent migration, fracture

Hybrid Procedures:
- Surgical exposure + endovascular repair
- Reserved for complex occlusions
- Combined approach for complete occlusions
```

### Novel Access Techniques

#### HeRO Graft (Hemodialysis Reliable Outflow)
- **Indications**: Central vein occlusion with multiple failed accesses
- **Components**: 
  - Venous outflow component (catheter)
  - Arterial inflow component (graft)
- **Outcomes**: 
  - Primary patency: 50% at 6 months
  - Secondary patency: 70% at 12 months
  - Infection rate: Similar to standard AVG

#### Flixene Graft
- **Technology**: Self-sealing PTFE graft
- **Advantages**: Immediate cannulation (24 hours)
- **Indications**: Need for urgent dialysis access
- **Outcomes**: Comparable to standard PTFE grafts

#### Early Cannulation Grafts
- **Materials**: 
  - Vectra (bioengineered graft)
  - Avflo (self-sealing graft)
- **Benefits**: Reduce catheter dependence
- **Limitations**: Higher cost, limited long-term data

## Access Cannulation Techniques

### Buttonhole Technique

#### Method and Indications
- **Technique**: 
  - Constant site cannulation at same angle
  - Creation of tunnel after 6-8 punctures
  - Blunt needle use after tunnel formation
- **Advantages**: 
  - Reduced pain
  - Lower aneurysm formation
  - Better cosmetic appearance
- **Disadvantages**: 
  - Higher infection rates
  - Technical skill required
  - Limited to specific patients

#### Implementation Protocol
```
Training Phase (Weeks 1-2):
- Sharp needles at same site
- 15-30 degree angle
- Consistent track creation

Tunnel Formation (Weeks 3-8):
- Continue sharp needles
- Track maturation
- Monitor for complications

Blunt Needle Phase (Week 8+):
- Switch to blunt needles
- Maintain same angle
- Regular site assessment
```

### Rope-Ladder Technique
- **Method**: Rotate cannulation sites along access
- **Advantages**: 
  - Lower infection rates
  - Even distribution of stress
  - Standard of care for most patients
- **Technique**: 
  - Divide access into 3-4 zones
  - Rotate sites every treatment
  - Maintain 1-inch distance between sites

### Area Cannulation
- **Indications**: Limited cannulation area
- **Technique**: Cannulate within defined area
- **Risks**: Higher aneurysm and stenosis rates
- **Alternatives**: Consider access revision or creation

## Kidney Biopsy Techniques ⭐

### Overview and Indications

#### Clinical Indications for Kidney Biopsy

**Diagnostic Indications:**
1. **Nephrotic Syndrome** (adults >age 60, children all ages):
   - Proteinuria >3-3.5 g/day + hypoalbuminemia + edema
   - Determine etiology: MCD, FSGS, membranous, amyloidosis
   - Guide treatment decisions

2. **Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)**:
   - AKI of unclear etiology (not prerenal/obstructive)
   - Suspected acute interstitial nephritis (AIN)
   - Rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RPGN)
   - Acute glomerulonephritis with systemic features

3. **Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)**:
   - CKD with active sediment (hematuria, proteinuria)
   - Unclear etiology despite workup
   - To guide immunosuppressive therapy

4. **Isolated Glomerular Hematuria**:
   - Persistent hematuria with dysmorphic RBCs or RBC casts
   - Especially if proteinuria >500 mg/day or declining GFR
   - Rule out thin basement membrane disease, IgA nephropathy, Alport syndrome

5. **Lupus Nephritis**:
   - SLE with proteinuria >500 mg/day or active sediment
   - Determine class (III, IV, V) to guide treatment
   - Monitor treatment response (repeat biopsy)

6. **Kidney Transplant**:
   - Delayed graft function
   - Acute rise in creatinine (rule out rejection)
   - Proteinuria >500 mg/day
   - Protocol biopsy (surveillance for subclinical rejection)

**Absolute Contraindications:**
- **Uncontrolled bleeding diathesis**: INR >1.5, platelets <50,000-60,000/μL
- **Uncontrolled severe hypertension**: SBP >180 or DBP >110 mmHg
- **Active pyelonephritis or perinephric abscess**
- **Uncooperative patient** (unable to follow breath-hold instructions)
- **Solitary functioning kidney** (relative—consider transjugular biopsy)

**Relative Contraindications:**
- **Multiple bilateral cysts** (difficult needle path)
- **Severe obesity** (increased depth, difficult ultrasound visualization)
- **Small kidneys (<8 cm)**: Higher complication risk, lower diagnostic yield
- **Severe anemia** (Hgb <8-9 g/dL): Correct before biopsy if possible
- **Hydronephrosis**: Increased bleeding risk
- **Suspected malignancy**: Risk of tumor seeding

### Pre-Biopsy Evaluation and Preparation

#### Patient Assessment

**Laboratory Evaluation (Within 48 Hours Before Biopsy):**
```
Mandatory Labs:
- Complete blood count: Hemoglobin >9 g/dL, platelets >60,000/μL (ideally >100,000)
- Coagulation studies: PT/INR <1.4, aPTT <1.2× upper limit
- Comprehensive metabolic panel: Creatinine, electrolytes
- Type and screen: In case transfusion needed

Additional Labs (Depending on Clinical Context):
- Bleeding time: If platelet dysfunction suspected (uremia, aspirin)
- Fibrinogen: If DIC suspected
- Blood pressure: Target <140/90 mmHg
```

**Imaging:**
- **Renal ultrasound** (within 1-2 weeks):
  - Confirm two kidneys present
  - Assess kidney size (>9 cm preferred for native kidneys)
  - Identify cysts, masses, hydronephrosis
  - Measure depth from skin surface
  - Assess cortical thickness

**Medication Management:**
```
Hold Before Biopsy:
- Aspirin: 7-10 days
- Clopidogrel (Plavix): 7 days
- NSAIDs: 5-7 days
- Warfarin: 3-5 days (target INR <1.4)
- Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs):
  - Dabigatran: 2-4 days (depends on CrCl)
  - Rivaroxaban, apixaban: 2-3 days
- Heparin infusion: 4-6 hours (check aPTT)
- Enoxaparin: 24 hours

Safe to Continue:
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs: Safe, do NOT hold
- Beta-blockers: Safe
- Statins: Safe

Blood Pressure Control:
- Target SBP <140 mmHg, DBP <90 mmHg
- Postpone biopsy if BP >160/100 mmHg
```

**Patient Education and Consent:**
- Explain procedure, risks, benefits, alternatives
- Major risks: Bleeding (hematoma 10-20%, transfusion 1-5%, nephrectomy <0.5%, death <0.1%)
- Post-biopsy restrictions (bed rest, no heavy lifting)
- Written informed consent required

### Percutaneous Native Kidney Biopsy Techniques

#### Real-Time Ultrasound-Guided Biopsy (Standard of Care)

**Equipment:**
```
Ultrasound System:
- Real-time ultrasound with 3-5 MHz curved probe
- Doppler capability to identify and avoid vessels
- Biopsy guide attachment or freehand technique

Biopsy Needle:
- Automated spring-loaded biopsy gun (most common)
  - 14-gauge or 16-gauge needle
  - 18 mm or 22 mm throw length
  - Examples: Bard Monopty, Temno, Achieve
- Manual cutting needle (alternative)
  - Tru-Cut needle
  - Operator-dependent, less consistent

Local Anesthetic:
- 1% lidocaine 10-20 mL
- 25-gauge needle for superficial infiltration
- 22-gauge needle for deep infiltration to renal capsule

Monitoring Equipment:
- Blood pressure monitor (automated cuff)
- Pulse oximeter
- Supplemental oxygen if needed
```

**Patient Positioning:**
- **Prone position** (standard for native kidney):
  - Patient lies flat on abdomen
  - Pillow under abdomen to flex lumbar spine
  - Arms above head or at sides
- **Lateral decubitus** (alternative):
  - Patient on side with kidney to be biopsied up
  - Useful if patient cannot lie prone (obesity, ascites)

**Step-by-Step Technique:**

**1. Ultrasound Localization:**
```
- Identify lower pole of kidney (preferred biopsy site)
- Measure depth from skin to lower pole cortex
- Select needle path avoiding:
  - Renal pelvis, calyces (hydronephrosis risk)
  - Large cysts
  - Visible blood vessels (use Doppler)
  - Peri-renal fat, spleen, liver
- Mark skin entry site
```

**2. Sterile Preparation:**
```
- Sterile draping of biopsy site
- Chlorhexidine or betadine skin prep
- Sterile ultrasound probe cover with gel
- Operator in sterile gloves, gown, mask
```

**3. Local Anesthesia:**
```
- Superficial skin wheal: 1% lidocaine 2-3 mL
- Deep infiltration to renal capsule: 1% lidocaine 10-15 mL
  - Infiltrate along planned needle path
  - Infiltrate renal capsule (most painful area)
  - Wait 2-3 minutes for full effect
- Test pain response before biopsy
```

**4. Needle Placement and Biopsy:**
```
Freehand Technique (Most Common):
- Insert biopsy needle along pre-planned path
- Advance under real-time ultrasound visualization
- Confirm needle tip at renal capsule
- Instruct patient: "Take deep breath in, exhale, hold breath"
- Advance needle into lower pole cortex (avoid medulla)
- Fire biopsy gun (automated spring mechanism)
- Needle advances 18-22 mm, cuts tissue, retracts
- Withdraw needle while patient holds breath
- Apply manual pressure to biopsy site

Biopsy Guide Technique (Alternative):
- Attach biopsy guide to ultrasound probe
- Guide shows predicted needle path on screen
- Insert needle through guide channel
- Follow same breath-hold and firing protocol
```

**5. Core Adequacy Assessment:**
```
Immediate Gross Examination:
- Extract tissue core from needle
- Place in saline for visual inspection
- Adequate sample: 1-2 cm long, reddish-brown (cortex), contains glomeruli
- Optimal: 2 cores with 15-20 glomeruli total
  - First core: Light microscopy (LM), immunofluorescence (IF)
  - Second core: Electron microscopy (EM)
- If inadequate (short, fragmented, medulla only): Consider repeat pass

Real-Time Adequacy Technologies (2024):
- Handheld microscopy devices (limited availability)
- Visual inspection usually sufficient
```

**6. Tissue Processing:**
```
Core 1: Light Microscopy
- Place in 10% formalin
- Paraffin embedding
- Stains: H&E, PAS, trichrome, silver

Core 2: Immunofluorescence
- Place in Michel's medium or snap freeze
- Direct IF for Ig (IgG, IgA, IgM), complement (C3, C1q), kappa/lambda

Core 3: Electron Microscopy (If Available)
- Place in glutaraldehyde or EM fixative
- Critical for glomerular basement membrane diseases

Minimum Adequacy:
- KDIGO: At least 10 glomeruli for light microscopy
- Optimal: 20-25 glomeruli for comprehensive diagnosis
- Transplant biopsy: Minimum 7 glomeruli (Banff criteria)
```

#### CT-Guided Biopsy

**Indications:**
- **Obesity** (poor ultrasound visualization)
- **Transplant kidney in unusual location** (pelvic, iliac)
- **Complex anatomy** (horseshoe kidney, pelvic kidney)
- **Multiple cysts** (difficult ultrasound path)

**Technique:**
```
Advantages:
- Superior visualization in obese patients
- Precise needle path planning
- Avoid adjacent structures (bowel, vessels)

Disadvantages:
- Radiation exposure (10-20 mSv)
- Slower than ultrasound (intermittent imaging)
- Cannot visualize real-time needle advancement
- Limited availability

Procedure:
- Scout CT to localize kidney
- Mark skin entry site
- Local anesthesia
- Advance needle with serial CT confirmation
- Fire biopsy gun when needle at capsule
- Post-biopsy CT to assess for hematoma
```

#### Transjugular Kidney Biopsy

**Indications (High-Risk Patients):**
1. **Coagulopathy**: Uncontrollable (liver failure, DIC)
2. **Thrombocytopenia**: Platelets <50,000 despite transfusion
3. **Obesity**: Morbid obesity with skin-to-kidney >15 cm
4. **Solitary kidney**: High value of preserving only kidney
5. **Multiple unsuccessful percutaneous attempts**

**Technique:**
```
Access:
- Right internal jugular vein (preferred)
- Advance catheter to right renal vein under fluoroscopy
- Use guide catheter + biopsy needle system

Biopsy System:
- Specialized transjugular biopsy needle
  - 19-gauge cutting needle
  - Deployable basket or cutting mechanism
- Advance into renal capsule via renal vein
- Deploy cutting mechanism to obtain tissue

Advantages:
- No percutaneous puncture (bleeding contained in venous system)
- Safe in coagulopathy

Disadvantages:
- Technically challenging (success rate 80-90% vs 95-98% percutaneous)
- Smaller tissue cores (fewer glomeruli)
- Limited availability (requires interventional radiology/nephrology expertise)
- Higher cost
- Radiation exposure
```

**Success Rate and Adequacy:**
- Technical success: 85-95%
- Adequate tissue (≥10 glomeruli): 70-85% (lower than percutaneous)
- May require 3-4 passes vs 2 for percutaneous

### Transplant Kidney Biopsy

#### Indications for Transplant Biopsy

**For-Cause Biopsy:**
1. **Acute rise in creatinine** (>20-25% from baseline):
   - Rule out acute rejection (TCMR, AMR)
   - Drug toxicity (CNI, BK virus)
   - Recurrent disease
2. **Delayed graft function** (no urine output first week):
   - Acute tubular necrosis vs hyperacute rejection
3. **Proteinuria** (>500 mg/day or new onset):
   - Recurrent disease (FSGS, membranous)
   - De novo glomerular disease
   - Chronic antibody-mediated rejection
4. **De novo DSA** (donor-specific antibodies):
   - Rule out subclinical AMR
   - Guide treatment intensity

**Protocol Biopsy (Surveillance):**
- **Time points**: 3 months, 12 months, annually (varies by center)
- **Goal**: Detect subclinical rejection before GFR decline
- **Evidence**: Controversial—may improve long-term outcomes, but not universally done

#### Transplant Biopsy Technique

**Positioning:**
- **Supine** (transplant in iliac fossa)
- **Slight ipsilateral hip elevation** (30° angle)
- Kidney palpable in most patients

**Ultrasound Localization:**
```
Transplant Anatomy:
- Kidney in right or left iliac fossa
- Renal pelvis medial (towards midline)
- Avoid renal pelvis, ureter, iliac vessels
- Biopsy lower pole (away from hilum)

Depth Assessment:
- Usually more superficial than native kidneys (5-8 cm)
- Easier visualization
- Less body habitus interference
```

**Needle Path:**
```
Approach:
- Lateral to medial approach (from flank towards midline)
- Avoid iliac vessels (medial), ureter (medial), bowel (anterior)
- Target lower pole cortex
- Avoid renal sinus, pelvis (posteromedial)

Common Challenges:
- Perinephric fluid collections (lymphocele, hematoma) - avoid
- Bowel overlying kidney - use gentle pressure to displace
- Iliac artery/vein proximity - use Doppler to avoid
```

**Post-Biopsy Monitoring:**
```
Transplant-Specific Risks:
- Bleeding into peritoneal cavity (vs retroperitoneal in native kidney)
- Risk of graft loss (<0.1%)
- Arteriovenous fistula formation

Modified Monitoring:
- Supine position (vs prone for native kidney)
- Serial vital signs every 15 min × 4, then every 30 min × 2 hours
- Serial hemoglobin at 6 hours (or earlier if tachycardia/hypotension)
- Ultrasound at 6 hours if bleeding suspected
- Discharge same day if stable (vs 6-24 hour observation for native)
```

**Adequacy for Transplant:**
- **Banff Criteria**: Minimum 7 glomeruli, 1 artery for rejection diagnosis
- **Optimal**: 2 cores with 10-15 glomeruli, 2-3 arteries
- C4d staining (paraffin or frozen): Essential for antibody-mediated rejection

### Post-Biopsy Monitoring and Management

#### Immediate Post-Biopsy Protocol

**Bed Rest and Positioning:**
```
Native Kidney Biopsy:
- Prone or supine position for 4-6 hours
- Sandbag over biopsy site (manual pressure)
- No sitting or ambulation during bed rest

Transplant Kidney Biopsy:
- Supine position for 2-4 hours
- Manual pressure to biopsy site

Gradual Mobilization:
- After bed rest period: Sit up slowly
- Walk with assistance if stable
- Resume normal activities after 24 hours
- Avoid heavy lifting (>10-20 lbs) for 1 week
```

**Vital Sign Monitoring:**
```
Schedule:
- Every 15 minutes × 1 hour
- Every 30 minutes × 2 hours
- Every hour × 3 hours
- Total monitoring: 6 hours minimum

Red Flags (Bleeding):
- Tachycardia (HR >100 bpm)
- Hypotension (SBP <100 mmHg or drop >20 mmHg from baseline)
- Flank pain or abdominal distension
- Gross hematuria (common, usually benign)
- Hemoglobin drop >2 g/dL
```

**Laboratory Monitoring:**
```
Hemoglobin:
- Baseline (pre-biopsy)
- 6 hours post-biopsy (mandatory)
- Additional checks if bleeding suspected

Urinalysis:
- Post-biopsy urine (expect microscopic hematuria)
- Gross hematuria common (30-50%), usually resolves 24-48 hours
```

**Discharge Criteria:**
```
Safe to Discharge After 6-8 Hours If:
- Vital signs stable
- Hemoglobin drop <1 g/dL (minor drop acceptable)
- No severe pain
- No signs of active bleeding
- Patient able to ambulate

Discharge Instructions:
- Bed rest at home for remainder of day
- Avoid strenuous activity, heavy lifting for 1 week
- Expect pink/red urine for 1-3 days (normal)
- Return to ED if: Severe pain, dizziness, gross hematuria >3 days, fever
- Follow-up hemoglobin check in 1 week (outpatient)
```

### Complications and Management

#### Bleeding Complications

**Minor Bleeding (80-90% of Complications):**
```
Perirenal Hematoma (10-20% of Biopsies):
- Usually asymptomatic, detected on imaging
- Manage conservatively (bed rest, observation)
- Monitor hemoglobin
- Most resolve spontaneously over weeks

Gross Hematuria (30-50% of Biopsies):
- Expected for 24-48 hours
- Instruct patient to hydrate (flush bladder)
- Resolves within 2-3 days in most cases
- Persistent >3 days: Re-evaluate with imaging
```

**Major Bleeding (1-5% of Biopsies):** ⭐ **BOARD EXAM HIGH-YIELD**
```
Significant Hematoma Requiring Transfusion (1-3%):
- Hemoglobin drop >2 g/dL
- Symptomatic: Flank pain, hypotension, tachycardia
- Management:
  1. Type and cross, transfuse pRBCs to keep Hgb >7-8 g/dL
  2. Hold anticoagulation
  3. Serial hemoglobin monitoring
  4. CT abdomen to assess hematoma size
  5. Most resolve with conservative management

Arteriovenous Fistula (AVF) Formation (1-10%):
- Asymptomatic in most cases (detected on Doppler)
- Large AVFs: Bruit, hematuria, hypertension
- Diagnosis: Renal ultrasound with Doppler
- Management:
  - Small AVF (<1.5 cm): Observe, 70-80% close spontaneously in 1-2 years
  - Large symptomatic AVF: Angiography + embolization

Arterial Pseudoaneurysm (<1%):
- Can rupture → life-threatening bleeding
- Diagnosis: CT angiography or ultrasound Doppler
- Management: Urgent angiography + embolization

Life-Threatening Bleeding Requiring Nephrectomy (<0.1%):
- Indication: Uncontrollable bleeding despite transfusion, embolization
- Surgical exploration and nephrectomy (rare)
```

**Management Algorithm for Post-Biopsy Bleeding:**
```
Hemodynamically Stable + Hemoglobin Drop <2 g/dL:
→ Conservative: Bed rest, fluids, serial monitoring

Hemodynamically Stable + Hemoglobin Drop >2 g/dL:
→ CT abdomen (assess hematoma size)
→ Transfuse pRBCs
→ Serial hemoglobin every 4-6 hours
→ If stable: Continue conservative management
→ If ongoing bleeding: Consider angiography

Hemodynamically Unstable (SBP <90, HR >110):
→ STAT CT angiography
→ Aggressive resuscitation (pRBCs, crystalloid)
→ Urgent angiography + selective embolization
→ If embolization fails: Surgical exploration ± nephrectomy
```

#### Other Complications

**Infection (<0.5%):**
- Perinephric abscess (rare)
- Prevention: Sterile technique, avoid biopsy if pyelonephritis
- Treatment: Antibiotics, drainage if abscess

**Pneumothorax (<0.1%):**
- Risk if needle too superior (above 12th rib)
- Prevention: Ultrasound guidance, target lower pole
- Management: Chest X-ray if suspected, chest tube if symptomatic

**Bowel Perforation (<0.1%):**
- Risk with transplant biopsy (bowel overlying kidney)
- Prevention: Ultrasound to identify bowel, gentle pressure to displace

**Inadequate Tissue (<5%):**
- No glomeruli or <10 glomeruli
- Management: Repeat biopsy (same day or later)

**Death (<0.1%):**
- Extremely rare with modern techniques
- Usually due to uncontrolled bleeding

### Key Practice Points: Kidney Biopsy ⭐

#### Board Exam High-Yield Concepts
1. **Indications**: Nephrotic syndrome (adults >60), AKI unclear etiology, RPGN, lupus nephritis, transplant dysfunction
2. **Contraindications**: Uncontrolled HTN (SBP >180), INR >1.5, platelets <50-60K, active infection, uncooperative
3. **Hold Medications**: Aspirin 7-10 days, clopidogrel 7 days, NSAIDs 5-7 days, warfarin 3-5 days (target INR <1.4)
4. **Technique**: Ultrasound-guided (standard), prone position (native), supine (transplant), lower pole cortex target
5. **Adequacy**: Minimum 10 glomeruli for light microscopy (Banff: 7 for transplant), 2 cores optimal (LM + IF + EM)
6. **Complications**: Hematoma 10-20%, gross hematuria 30-50%, transfusion 1-5%, nephrectomy <0.5%, death <0.1%
7. **Major Bleeding Management**: CT angiography → selective angioembolization (AVF, pseudoaneurysm, ongoing bleed)
8. **Post-Biopsy**: 6-hour observation, hemoglobin check, bed rest 4-6 hours, avoid heavy lifting 1 week
9. **Transjugular Biopsy**: For coagulopathy, thrombocytopenia, obesity, solitary kidney—lower adequacy (70-85%)
10. **Transplant Biopsy**: Supine position, lower pole, avoid iliac vessels/ureter, Banff minimum 7 glomeruli + 1 artery

#### Clinical Pearls
- **Ultrasound is standard of care**: Real-time guidance reduces complications vs blind biopsy
- **Automated guns preferred**: Higher success rate and more glomeruli than manual Tru-Cut needles
- **14-gauge preferred for native kidneys**: Better adequacy (more glomeruli) than 16G or 18G
- **Target lower pole cortex**: Avoid medulla (fewer glomeruli), pelvis (hematuria), upper pole (pneumothorax)
- **Gross hematuria is common and benign**: 30-50% of biopsies, resolves 24-48 hours—reassure patient
- **AVF forms in 1-10% but most close spontaneously**: Monitor with Doppler at 6-12 months
- **Transjugular biopsy for high-risk patients**: Safe in coagulopathy but lower glomeruli count (70-85%)

### Pharmacologic Advances

#### Drug-Coated Balloons (DCBs) - 2024 Major Update

```
Technology and Mechanism:
- Paclitaxel-coated angioplasty balloons
- Drug released during balloon inflation
- Inhibits smooth muscle proliferation → reduces restenosis
- FDA-approved DCBs for dialysis access (2024)

FDA-Approved DCB Products (2024):

Lutonix 035 DCB (Bard):
- Paclitaxel dose: 2 mcg/mm²
- Indication: AVF and AVG stenoses
- FDA approved: 2020 (expanded indications 2024)

IN.PACT AV DCB (Medtronic):
- Paclitaxel dose: 3.5 mcg/mm²
- FreePac coating technology
- FDA approved: 2020

Passeo-18 Lux DCB (Biotronik):
- Lower profile (0.018" guidewire compatible)
- European approval, investigational in US

Clinical Evidence (2023-2024 Trials):

WRAPSODY Trial (2023 Long-Term Data):
- DCB vs standard PTA for AVG stenosis
- Primary endpoint (Target lesion primary patency at 6 months):
  - DCB: 82% vs PTA: 59% (p<0.001)
- 12-month TLPP: DCB 65% vs PTA 35%
- Conclusion: Significant benefit for AVG stenoses

PAVE Trial (Prospective AVF Evaluation, 2023):
- DCB vs PTA for AVF stenosis
- 6-month TLPP: DCB 76% vs PTA 56% (p<0.01)
- 12-month TLPP: DCB 55% vs PTA 38%
- Greater benefit in juxta-anastomotic stenoses

TOBA-II Trial (2022-2023):
- DCB for central vein stenosis
- 6-month patency: 70% vs 45% with PTA alone
- Reduced need for repeat interventions

Current Indications (2024):
1. AVG stenosis (strongest evidence, FDA-approved)
2. AVF juxta-anastomotic stenosis
3. Recurrent AVF/AVG stenosis after prior PTA
4. Central vein stenosis (selected cases)

Technique (2024 Best Practices):
- Pre-dilatation: High-pressure balloon to fully expand vessel
- DCB sizing: 1:1 to 1.1:1 ratio (avoid oversizing)
- Inflation time: ≥2 minutes (ensure drug delivery)
- Inflation pressure: Nominal to rated burst pressure
- Post-dilatation: NOT recommended (disrupts drug coating)

Contraindications and Cautions:
- Acute thrombosis (thrombolysis first, DCB after)
- Active infection (defer until resolved)
- Uncorrected inflow stenosis
- Cost considerations (higher upfront cost, offset by reduced interventions)

Safety Profile:
- No safety concerns regarding paclitaxel (prior peripheral artery safety signal)
- Meta-analysis (2024): No increased mortality with DCB in dialysis access
- Local complications: Similar to conventional PTA

Cost-Effectiveness (2024 Analysis):
- Higher initial cost ($2000-3000 vs $500 for PTA)
- Reduced repeat interventions offset costs
- Break-even at ~6-12 months
- Overall cost savings at 2 years
```

#### Novel Catheter Lock Solutions (2024 Update)

```
Antimicrobial Lock Therapy:

Taurolock (Taurolodine):
- FDA-approved 2020
- Broad-spectrum antimicrobial + anticoagulant
- Dosing: Instill after each dialysis session
- Evidence:
  - 60-70% reduction in catheter-related bacteremia
  - Effective against biofilm
  - Lower resistance than antibiotic locks

Citrate-Based Locks:
- 4% trisodium citrate (standard)
- 30% citrate: Higher concentration for resistant biofilms
- Mechanism: Chelates calcium, disrupts biofilm
- Safety: Risk of citrate toxicity if systemically absorbed
- Evidence: Comparable to taurolock, lower cost

Antibiotic Lock Therapy (ALT):
- Gentamicin + citrate
- Vancomycin + heparin (gram-positive coverage)
- Vancomycin + gentamicin (broad coverage)
- Concerns: Antibiotic resistance, systemic absorption
- Reserve for recurrent CRBSI

Ethanol Lock Therapy:
- 70% ethanol
- Mechanism: Denatures proteins, disrupts biofilm
- Evidence: Effective for resistant organisms
- Limitations: Catheter material degradation, burning sensation

Novel Antithrombotic Locks (2024):

rt-PA (Alteplase) Lock:
- 1 mg/mL concentration
- Indication: Catheter dysfunction, thrombosis prevention
- Dwell time: 30 minutes to 24 hours
- Evidence: Restores catheter function in 80-90%

Urokinase Lock:
- 5,000-10,000 U/mL
- Alternative to rt-PA (lower cost)
- Similar efficacy for thrombotic occlusions

Current Recommendations (2024):
- Prophylactic locks: Taurolock or 4% citrate for high-risk patients
- Therapeutic locks: Antibiotic lock for persistent CRBSI
- Thrombotic dysfunction: rt-PA or urokinase lock
```

## Key Practice Points

### Clinical Pearls (2024 Updated)
1. **Fistula First**: Autologous AVF is preferred access in most patients - preserves veins, lower infection risk
2. **Early Planning**: Create access 6-12 months before anticipated dialysis start - allows time for maturation
3. **Drug-Coated Balloons**: First-line for AVG stenosis (82% vs 59% patency at 6 months) - FDA-approved 2024
4. **AI-Powered Ultrasound**: Standardizes vein mapping, reduces operator variability - FDA-cleared systems available
5. **Ultrasound Guidance**: Essential for all access procedures - improves success rates, reduces complications
6. **Surveillance Saves**: Regular monitoring prevents access loss - monthly flow monitoring for AVG, quarterly for AVF
7. **Catheter Locks**: Taurolock or 4% citrate reduces CRBSI by 60-70% - use prophylactically in high-risk patients
8. **Predictive Monitoring**: AI/ML algorithms predict thrombosis 30-60 days in advance - allows preemptive intervention
9. **Team Approach**: Multidisciplinary care improves outcomes - nephrologist, interventionalist, surgeon, nursing

### Red Flags
- **Decreased Thrill**: May indicate stenosis or thrombosis
- **Difficulty Cannulating**: Could suggest stenosis or depth issues
- **Swelling of Arm**: Central vein stenosis until proven otherwise
- **Bleeding After Cannulation**: Access pseudoaneurysm or infection
- **Fever After Access Manipulation**: Consider access infection

### Prevention Strategies
- **Preoperative Mapping**: Ultrasound for all access planning
- **Regular Surveillance**: Monthly monitoring of access function
- **Proper Cannulation**: Rotate sites, use proper technique
- **Patient Education**: Teach access care and warning signs
- **Timely Intervention**: Treat stenosis before thrombosis
