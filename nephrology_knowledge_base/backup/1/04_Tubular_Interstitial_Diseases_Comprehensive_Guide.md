# Tubular & Interstitial Diseases - Comprehensive Clinical Guide

## Metadata
**Core Domain:** Clinical Nephrology I  
**Difficulty Level:** Intermediate-Advanced  
**Frequency of Use:** Weekly  
**Clinical Impact:** High  
**Regulatory Requirements:** None specific  
**Prerequisites:** Basic renal physiology, acid-base balance  
**Competency Development Time:** 6-12 months  
**Cross-references:** AKI (Domain I.1), Electrolytes (Domain I.2), Pharmacology (Domain V.12)

## 1. Renal Tubular Physiology & Pathophysiology

### Normal Tubular Function

#### Proximal Tubule (65% of reabsorption)
**Reabsorption Functions:**
- 65% of filtered sodium and water
- 100% of filtered glucose and amino acids
- 80-90% of bicarbonate
- 50% of filtered phosphate
- 65% of filtered potassium
- 20% of filtered calcium
- 90% of filtered uric acid

**Transport Mechanisms:**
- Na+/K+ ATPase on basolateral membrane
- Na+/H+ exchanger (NHE3) on apical membrane
- Na+/glucose cotransporters (SGLT2, SGLT1)
- Na+/phosphate cotransporters (NaPi-IIa, NaPi-IIc)
- Na+/amino acid cotransporters

#### Loop of Henle (25% of reabsorption)
**Thick Ascending Limb:**
- Na+/K+/2Cl- cotransporter (NKCC2)
- Reabsorbs 25% of filtered sodium
- Generates medullary concentration gradient
- Site of loop diuretic action
- Paracellular calcium and magnesium reabsorption

**Thin Descending Limb:**
- High water permeability (AQP1)
- Passive water reabsorption
- Concentrates tubular fluid

**Thin Ascending Limb:**
- Low water permeability
- Passive sodium chloride reabsorption
- Dilutes tubular fluid

#### Distal Tubule & Collecting Duct (10% of reabsorption)
**Distal Convoluted Tubule:**
- Na+/Cl- cotransporter (NCC)
- Site of thiazide diuretic action
- Calcium reabsorption via TRPV5 channel
- Early potassium secretion

**Collecting Duct:**
- Principal cells: sodium reabsorption (ENaC), potassium secretion
- Intercalated cells: acid-base regulation
- Water reabsorption via AQP2 (ADH-dependent)
- Final site of urine concentration

### Pathophysiological Mechanisms

#### Tubular Injury Patterns
**Acute Tubular Injury:**
- Ischemic or toxic insults
- Cell swelling and necrosis
- Loss of brush border
- Tubular obstruction
- Back-leak of filtrate

**Chronic Tubulointerstitial Fibrosis:**
- Persistent inflammation
- Fibroblast activation
- Extracellular matrix deposition
- Tubular atrophy
- Vascular rarefaction

#### Transport Defects
**Genetic Transport Defects:**
- Mutations in transport proteins
- Congenital syndromes
- Often present in childhood
- May be progressive

**Acquired Transport Defects:**
- Autoantibodies against transporters
- Drug-induced inhibition
- Metabolic disturbances
- Secondary to other renal diseases

## 2. Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)

### Classification & Etiology

#### Ischemic ATN
**Causes:**
- Prolonged hypotension (>60 minutes MAP <65 mmHg)
- Major surgery (cardiac, vascular, abdominal)
- Severe trauma with hemorrhage
- Sepsis with hemodynamic compromise
- Burns (>20% BSA)
- Cardiogenic shock

**Pathophysiology:**
- Medullary hypoxia (high metabolic demand)
- ATP depletion
- Cytoskeletal disruption
- Cell polarity loss
- Inflammatory cascade activation

**Risk Factors:**
- Pre-existing CKD
- Diabetes mellitus
- Advanced age
- Atherosclerotic disease
- Volume depletion

#### Nephrotoxic ATN
**Contrast-Induced Nephropathy:**
**Risk Stratification:**
- Baseline eGFR <45 mL/min/1.73m²
- Diabetes mellitus
- Dehydration
- High contrast volume (>100 mL)
- Multiple contrast exposures

**Prevention Strategies:**
- Pre- and post-procedure isotonic saline
- Minimize contrast volume
- Use low-osmolar or iso-osmolar contrast
- Consider alternative imaging
- N-acetylcysteine not proven effective

**Aminoglycoside Nephrotoxicity:**
**Mechanism:**
- Accumulation in proximal tubular cells
- Phospholipidosis
- Lysosomal dysfunction
- Cell necrosis

**Risk Factors:**
- High peak levels
- Prolonged therapy (>7 days)
- Pre-existing CKD
- Volume depletion
- Concurrent nephrotoxins

**Prevention:**
- Therapeutic drug monitoring
- Once-daily dosing
- Adequate hydration
- Avoid concurrent nephrotoxins
- Limit duration of therapy

**Amphotericin B Toxicity:**
**Mechanism:**
- Direct tubular cell membrane damage
- Renal vasoconstriction
- Distal RTA development
- Magnesium and potassium wasting

**Risk Reduction:**
- Liposomal formulations
- Aggressive hydration
- Electrolyte supplementation
- Sodium loading

**Other Nephrotoxins:**
- Acyclovir (crystal nephropathy)
- Methotrexate (precipitation)
- Tenofovir (proximal tubulopathy)
- Ifosfamide (Fanconi syndrome)
- Cisplatin (magnesium wasting)
- Calcineurin inhibitors (afferent vasoconstriction)

### Clinical Presentation

#### Early Phase (1-3 days)
- Rising serum creatinine
- Decreased urine output (oliguric in 50%)
- Urine sediment: muddy brown granular casts
- Fractional excretion of sodium >2%
- Urine osmolality <350 mOsm/kg

#### Maintenance Phase (3-10 days)
- Stable but elevated creatinine
- Continued oliguria or polyuria phase
- Fluid overload common
- Electrolyte abnormalities
- Need for renal replacement therapy in 20-30%

#### Recovery Phase (7-21 days)
- Gradual creatinine decline
- Polyuria with sodium wasting
- Tubular function recovery lags GFR
- Risk of hypovolemia and electrolyte loss
- Most recover baseline function

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Urine Sediment Analysis
**Characteristic Findings:**
- Muddy brown granular casts
- Renal tubular epithelial cells
- Occasional WBC casts
- Few RBCs unless glomerular involvement

**Differential Features:**
- Pre-renal: hyaline casts, bland sediment
- AIN: WBC casts, eosinophils
- Glomerular: RBC casts, dysmorphic RBCs

#### Urine Chemistries
**FENa (Fractional Excretion of Sodium):**
- >2%: Typical for ATN
- <1%: Suggests pre-renal azotemia
- Limitations: diuretic use, CKD, contrast nephropathy

**FEUrea (Fractional Excretion of Urea):**
- >50%: ATN
- <35%: Pre-renal
- More reliable with diuretic use

**Urine Osmolality:**
- <350 mOsm/kg: ATN
- >500 mOsm/kg: Pre-renal

#### Biomarkers
**NGAL (Neutrophil Gelatinase-Associated Lipocalin):**
- Rises within 2-6 hours
- Predicts need for RRT
- Distinguishes pre-renal vs ATN
- Higher in ischemic vs nephrotoxic

**KIM-1 (Kidney Injury Molecule-1):**
- Proximal tubule injury marker
- Specific for AKI
- Predicts outcomes

**IL-18 (Interleukin-18):**
- Inflammatory marker
- Predicts mortality
- Rises early in ATN

### Management Strategies

#### General Principles
- Remove/avoid nephrotoxins
- Optimize hemodynamics
- Maintain euvolemia
- Prevent complications
- Consider early nephrology consultation

#### Hemodynamic Support
**Fluid Management:**
- Isotonic crystalloids
- Avoid hypervolemia
- Monitor central venous pressure
- Consider diuretic challenge

**Blood Pressure Support:**
- Maintain MAP >65 mmHg
- Norepinephrine first-line
- Avoid excessive vasoconstriction
- Consider invasive monitoring

#### Specific Therapies
**Diuretic Challenge:**
- Furosemide 1-2 mg/kg IV
- Continuous infusion if responsive
- Goal: prevent fluid overload
- Does not prevent RRT need

**Loop Diuretic Resistance:**
- Add thiazide diuretic
- Consider metolazone
- High-dose continuous infusion
- Monitor for ototoxicity

#### Renal Replacement Therapy Indications
**Absolute Indications:**
- Refractory hyperkalemia (>6.5 mEq/L)
- Severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.1)
- Volume overload with pulmonary edema
- Uremic complications (pericarditis, encephalopathy)
- Certain toxin removal

**Timing Considerations:**
- Early vs delayed RRT controversy
- Individualized decision-making
- Consider biomarker trends
- Factor in comorbidities

### Prevention Strategies

#### High-Risk Patient Identification
- Pre-existing CKD
- Diabetes mellitus
- Advanced age
- Volume depletion
- Planned nephrotoxin exposure

#### Pharmacologic Prevention
- N-acetylcysteine (limited evidence)
- Statins (some benefit in cardiac surgery)
- Theophylline (contrast nephropathy)
- Sodium bicarbonate (controversial)

#### Non-Pharmacologic Prevention
- Adequate hydration
- Minimize contrast volume
- Avoid concurrent nephrotoxins
- Optimize hemodynamics
- Close monitoring

## 3. Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN)

### Etiology & Classification

#### Drug-Induced AIN (70-80% of cases)
**Common Culprits:**
**Antibiotics:**
- β-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins)
- Quinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin)
- Sulfonamides (TMP-SMX)
- Rifampin
- Macrolides (rare)

**Proton Pump Inhibitors:**
- Omeprazole (most common)
- Pantoprazole
- Esomeprazole
- Lansoprazole
- Rabeprazole

**NSAIDs:**
- Ibuprofen
- Naproxen
- Diclofenac
- Indomethacin
- COX-2 inhibitors

**Diuretics:**
- Thiazides (HCTZ, chlorthalidone)
- Furosemide
- Spironolactone

**Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors (ICIs) - EMERGING MAJOR CAUSE:**
- **PD-1 Inhibitors:**
  - Nivolumab (Opdivo)
  - Pembrolizumab (Keytruda)
  - Cemiplimab
- **PD-L1 Inhibitors:**
  - Atezolizumab
  - Durvalumab
  - Avelumab
- **CTLA-4 Inhibitors:**
  - Ipilimumab
- **Characteristics:**
  - Incidence: 2-5% of ICI-treated patients
  - Median onset: 3-4 months after initiation (range: weeks to >1 year)
  - Can occur with combination therapy (higher risk)
  - Granulomatous AIN more common
  - May require prolonged steroid course
  - Often necessitates ICI discontinuation

**SGLT2 Inhibitors (Newly Recognized Cause - 2020s):**
- Empagliflozin (Jardiance)
- Dapagliflozin (Farxiga)
- Canagliflozin (Invokana)
- Ertugliflozin
- **Characteristics:**
  - Rare but increasingly reported
  - Euglycemic DKA may coexist
  - Usually occurs within first 6 months
  - Good response to drug discontinuation
  - Generally favorable prognosis

**Other Medications:**
- Allopurinol (dose-related risk)
- 5-ASA compounds (mesalamine, sulfasalazine)
- Phenytoin
- Carbamazepine
- Cimetidine
- Acetazolamide

#### Infection-Related AIN
**Bacterial:**
- Leptospirosis
- Brucellosis
- Legionella
- Salmonella
- Streptococcal infections

**Viral:**
- Epstein-Barr virus (EBV)
- Cytomegalovirus (CMV)
- HIV
- Hepatitis B/C
- Hantavirus

**Fungal:**
- Histoplasmosis
- Candidiasis (rare)
- Aspergillosis (rare)

#### Systemic Disease-Associated AIN
**Autoimmune Diseases:**
- Sarcoidosis (non-caseating granulomas)
- Sjögren's syndrome
- Systemic lupus erythematosus
- IgG4-related disease
- Tubulointerstitial nephritis with uveitis (TINU)

**Other:**
- Inflammatory bowel disease
- Celiac disease
- Primary biliary cirrhosis

### Clinical Presentation

#### Classic Triad (Present in 30-40%)
- Fever (70-80%)
- Rash (30-50%)
- Arthralgias (40-60%)

#### Renal Manifestations
- Acute rise in creatinine (days to weeks)
- Non-nephrotic proteinuria (<1 g/day)
- Sterile pyuria with WBC casts
- Microscopic hematuria (50%)
- Eosinophiluria (Hansel's stain)

#### Temporal Relationship
- Typical onset: 7-14 days after drug exposure
- Can occur earlier on re-exposure
- May continue after drug discontinuation
- Variable recovery time (weeks to months)

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Laboratory Evaluation
**Urinalysis:**
- WBC casts (pathognomonic)
- Eosinophils (Hansel's stain)
- RBCs (occasional)
- Proteinuria (mild)

**Blood Tests:**
- Elevated ESR/CRP
- Eosinophilia (60-80%)
- Elevated IgE (occasional)
- Normal complement (usually)

**Drug Levels:**
- Consider if applicable
- Therapeutic drug monitoring
- Toxicity assessment

#### Imaging Studies
**Renal Ultrasound:**
- Usually normal
- May show enlarged kidneys
- Rule out obstruction
- Assess kidney size

**CT/MRI:**
- Not typically required
- Consider if atypical presentation
- Rule out other causes

#### Kidney Biopsy Indications
- Atypical presentation
- No improvement after drug discontinuation
- Need for definitive diagnosis
- Exclusion of other diseases
- Persistent renal dysfunction

**Biopsy Findings:**
- Interstitial edema and inflammation
- Lymphocyte and eosinophil infiltration
- Tubulitis (inflammatory cells in tubules)
- Granuloma formation (sarcoid, drug-induced)
- Minimal glomerular changes

### Management Strategies

#### Immediate Actions
**Drug Discontinuation:**
- Stop all potentially offending agents
- Review complete medication list
- Consider drug holidays
- Document reaction

**Supportive Care:**
- Adequate hydration
- Electrolyte management
- Monitor renal function
- Avoid further nephrotoxins

#### Corticosteroid Therapy
**Indications:**
- No improvement after 5-7 days off drug
- Severe renal failure
- Biopsy-proven AIN
- Systemic disease involvement

**Regimens:**
- Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day
- Duration: 4-6 weeks
- Taper gradually over 2-4 weeks
- Consider pulse steroids for severe cases

**Response:**
- Improvement usually within 1-2 weeks
- Complete recovery in 60-80%
- Partial recovery in 15-20%
- Persistent CKD in 5-10%

#### Special Situations (2024 Update)

**Immune Checkpoint Inhibitor-Induced AIN (ICI-AIN):**
- **Incidence:** 2-5% of ICI-treated patients (rising with increased ICI use)
- **Presentation:**
  - Often severe, acute kidney injury
  - May be asymptomatic (detected on routine labs)
  - Classic triad (fever, rash, eosinophilia) RARE
  - Can occur months after ICI initiation
- **Pathology:**
  - Granulomatous interstitial nephritis (30-50%)
  - Acute tubular injury may coexist
  - May have concurrent immune-related adverse events
- **Management:**
  - **Discontinue ICI** (discuss with oncology)
  - **Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day** (higher dose than traditional AIN)
  - Taper over 6-8 weeks (longer than traditional)
  - Consider mycophenolate or infliximab if steroid-refractory
  - Multidisciplinary decision on ICI resumption
- **Outcomes:**
  - 60-70% achieve renal recovery
  - May require prolonged immunosuppression
  - ICI resumption controversial (30-50% recurrence risk)

**NSAID-Induced AIN:**
- Often presents with **nephrotic syndrome**
- **Minimal change disease** component on biopsy
- May require longer steroid course (8-12 weeks)
- Higher risk of incomplete recovery
- Avoid all NSAIDs permanently

**PPI-Induced AIN:**
- **Most common cause** of drug-induced AIN currently
- Often severe presentation with marked AKI
- May have delayed recovery (weeks to months)
- **Management:**
  - Discontinue ALL PPIs (consider H2 blockers alternative)
  - Steroid course if no improvement in 5-7 days
  - Consider permanent PPI avoidance
- **Note:** Can occur after years of PPI use

**SGLT2 Inhibitor-Induced AIN:**
- **Newly recognized entity** (case reports 2020-2024)
- May present with concurrent euglycemic DKA
- Usually occurs within first 6 months of therapy
- **Management:**
  - Discontinue SGLT2 inhibitor
  - Supportive care often sufficient
  - Steroids if severe or no improvement
  - Generally favorable prognosis

**Re-challenge Considerations:**
- Generally **CONTRAINDICATED**
- Exceptions:
  - ICIs: Case-by-case with oncology (cancer vs. kidney risk)
  - Essential medications with no alternatives
- If re-challenge attempted:
  - Desensitization protocols (very rare, limited data)
  - Close monitoring (weekly labs initially)
  - Pre-emptive steroid prophylaxis (controversial)
  - Document informed consent

### Prognosis & Follow-up

#### Recovery Predictors
- Early drug discontinuation
- Younger age
- Absence of interstitial fibrosis on biopsy
- No baseline CKD
- Prompt steroid initiation

#### Long-term Outcomes
- Complete recovery: 60-80%
- Partial recovery: 15-20%
- Chronic kidney disease: 5-10%
- Risk of recurrence with re-exposure

#### Monitoring Protocol
- Serum creatinine weekly for 4 weeks
- Then monthly for 3 months
- Urinalysis at each visit
- Monitor for steroid side effects
- Document medication allergies

## 4. Renal Tubular Acidosis (RTA)

### Classification & Pathophysiology

#### Type 1 (Distal) RTA
**Pathophysiology:**
- Impaired distal acidification
- Inability to excrete hydrogen ions
- Defective H+-ATPase in collecting duct
- Urine pH cannot be lowered below 5.5

**Etiologies:**
**Genetic:**
- Autosomal dominant (SLC4A1 mutation)
- Autosomal recessive (ATP6V1B1, ATP6V0A4)
- Associated with sensorineural deafness

**Acquired:**
- Autoimmune diseases (Sjögren's, SLE, RA)
- Hypercalciuria and nephrocalcinosis
- Obstructive uropathy
- Renal transplantation
- Amphotericin B toxicity

**Clinical Features:**
- Growth failure in children
- Nephrocalcinosis and nephrolithiasis
- Bone demineralization (rickets/osteomalacia)
- Muscle weakness
- Polyuria and polydipsia

#### Type 2 (Proximal) RTA
**Pathophysiology:**
- Impaired bicarbonate reabsorption
- Defect in proximal tubule Na+/H+ exchange
- Bicarbonate wasting until serum level falls to threshold
- Urine pH can be acidified when serum HCO3- low

**Etiologies:**
**Isolated:**
- Usually sporadic
- May be genetic (rare)
- Transient in infants

**Secondary:**
- Fanconi syndrome (multiple transport defects)
- Cystinosis
- Wilson's disease
- Tyrosinemia
- Ifosfamide toxicity
- Heavy metals (lead, cadmium)
- Multiple myeloma

**Clinical Features:**
- Growth retardation
- Bone disease (less severe than Type 1)
- Polyuria
- Associated with other proximal defects

#### Type 3 (Mixed) RTA
**Features:**
- Combined distal and proximal defects
- Rare, usually genetic
- Carbonic anhydrase II deficiency
- Associated with osteopetrosis
- Cerebral calcifications

#### Type 4 (Hyperkalemic) RTA
**Pathophysiology:**
- Aldosterone deficiency or resistance
- Impaired potassium and hydrogen secretion
- Mild metabolic acidosis
- Hyperkalemia is prominent feature

**Etiologies:**
**Hyporeninemic Hypoaldosteronism:**
- Diabetic nephropathy (most common)
- Chronic interstitial nephritis
- NSAID use
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs

**Aldosterone Resistance:**
- Obstructive uropathy
- Sickle cell disease
- Pseudohypoaldosteronism type 1
- Amiloride-sensitive ENaC defects

**Clinical Features:**
- Hyperkalemia (often asymptomatic)
- Mild metabolic acidosis
- Normal anion gap
- May have hypertension

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Laboratory Evaluation
**Basic Metabolic Panel:**
- Low serum bicarbonate (<22 mEq/L)
- Normal anion gap (Types 1, 2, 4)
- Hyperkalemia (Type 4)
- Hypokalemia (Types 1, 2)

**Urine Studies:**
**Urine pH:**
- Type 1: >5.5 despite acidosis
- Type 2: Variable, can be <5.5
- Type 4: <5.5 (can acidify)

**Urine Anion Gap:**
- Positive: impaired NH4+ excretion (Types 1, 4)
- Negative: normal NH4+ excretion (Type 2)

**Fractional Excretion of Bicarbonate:**
- >15%: Type 2 RTA
- <5%: Type 1 RTA

#### Specialized Testing
**Ammonium Chloride Loading Test:**
- Confirm distal RTA
- Measure urine pH after acid load
- Cannot lower pH below 5.5 in Type 1

**Bicarbonate Titration:**
- Identify proximal RTA
- Give bicarbonate until serum normal
- Measure urinary bicarbonate loss

**Aldosterone Levels:**
- Evaluate Type 4 RTA
- Check renin activity
- Assess adrenal function

### Treatment Strategies

#### Type 1 RTA
**Alkali Therapy:**
- Sodium bicarbonate or potassium citrate
- Goal: serum HCO3- 22-24 mEq/L
- Dose: 1-3 mEq/kg/day divided
- Monitor for volume overload

**Potassium Management:**
- Potassium citrate preferred
- Corrects hypokalemia and acidosis
- Reduces stone formation
- Monitor serum potassium

**Stone Prevention:**
- High fluid intake
- Low sodium diet
- Thiazide diuretics if hypercalciuria
- Citrate supplementation

#### Type 2 RTA
**Bicarbonate Supplementation:**
- Higher doses needed (Type 1)
- 5-15 mEq/kg/day divided
- May need continuous supplementation
- Monitor for volume overload

**Treat Underlying Cause:**
- Ifosfamide: discontinue drug
- Cystinosis: cysteamine therapy
- Wilson's disease: chelation therapy
- Multiple myeloma: chemotherapy

**Address Associated Defects:**
- Phosphate supplementation
- Vitamin D therapy
- Growth hormone in children

#### Type 4 RTA
**Hyperkalemia Management:**
**Dietary Measures:**
- Low potassium diet
- Avoid potassium supplements
- Limit salt substitutes

**Medications:**
- Loop diuretics (furosemide)
- Thiazide diuretics
- Sodium bicarbonate
- Fludrocortisone (if aldosterone deficient)

**Avoid Potassium-Raising Drugs:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs (if possible)
- NSAIDs
- Potassium-sparing diuretics
- Beta-blockers (high doses)

#### Monitoring
- Serum electrolytes weekly initially
- Then monthly once stable
- Urine pH monitoring
- Growth parameters in children
- Bone density assessment

## 5. Fanconi Syndrome

### Definition & Pathophysiology

#### Definition
- Generalized proximal tubular dysfunction
- Multiple transport defects
- Can be inherited or acquired
- Affects solute and water reabsorption

#### Pathophysiology
- Proximal tubular cell injury
- Mitochondrial dysfunction
- Transport protein defects
- Energy metabolism impairment
- Secondary to various systemic diseases

### Etiology

#### Genetic Causes
**Cystinosis:**
- Autosomal recessive
- CTNS gene mutation
- Cystine crystal accumulation
- Most common inherited cause

**Hereditary Fructose Intolerance:**
- Aldolase B deficiency
- Accumulation of fructose-1-phosphate
- Symptoms after fructose ingestion

**Tyrosinemia Type I:**
- FAH gene mutation
- Succinylacetone accumulation
- Liver and kidney involvement

**Wilson's Disease:**
- ATP7B gene mutation
- Copper accumulation
- Liver disease, Kayser-Fleischer rings

**Dent Disease:**
- CLCN5 gene mutation
- X-linked inheritance
- Low molecular weight proteinuria

#### Acquired Causes
**Multiple Myeloma:**
- Light chain toxicity
- Cast nephropathy component
- Paraprotein-related injury

**Ifosfamide Toxicity:**
- Chemotherapy-induced
- Dose-dependent
- May be irreversible

**Heavy Metal Toxicity:**
- Lead, cadmium, mercury
- Occupational exposure
- Environmental contamination

**Tenofovir Toxicity:**
- HIV medication
- Mitochondrial toxicity
- Usually reversible

**Autoimmune Diseases:**
- Sjögren's syndrome
- Systemic lupus erythematosus
- Sarcoidosis

### Clinical Features

#### Proximal Tubular Defects
**Glycosuria:**
- Normal blood glucose
- Glucose in urine
- Polyuria, polydipsia

**Phosphaturia:**
- Hypophosphatemia
- Bone pain, fractures
- Rickets in children

**Bicarbonaturia:**
- Type 2 RTA
- Growth failure
- Bone demineralization

**Uric Acid Wasting:**
- Hypouricemia
- Gout symptoms (paradoxically)

**Aminoaciduria:**
- Generalized amino acid loss
- Low molecular weight proteinuria

**Other Defects:**
- Sodium wasting
- Potassium wasting
- Calcium wasting
- Water wasting (polyuria)

#### Systemic Manifestations
- Growth retardation (children)
- Bone disease (rickets/osteomalacia)
- Muscle weakness
- Fatigue
- Dehydration risk

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Laboratory Evaluation
**Urinalysis:**
- Glycosuria with normal glucose
- Phosphate crystals
- Amino acids (urine chromatography)
- Low molecular weight proteins
- Bicarbonate in urine

**Blood Tests:**
- Hypophosphatemia
- Hypouricemia
- Metabolic acidosis
- Hypokalemia
- Normal blood glucose

**Specialized Tests:**
- Urine amino acid chromatography
- Beta-2 microglobulin (LMW protein)
- Retinol binding protein
- Fractional excretion studies

#### Etiologic Workup
**Genetic Testing:**
- Cystinosis gene panel
- Wilson's disease testing
- Tyrosinemia screening
- Dent disease mutation analysis

**Systemic Disease Evaluation:**
- Serum/urine protein electrophoresis
- Autoimmune serologies
- Heavy metal levels
- Medication review

#### Kidney Biopsy
- Indications: unclear etiology
- Findings: proximal tubular degeneration
- May show crystal deposition
- Helps exclude other diseases

### Treatment Strategies

#### General Principles
- Treat underlying cause
- Replace lost substances
- Prevent complications
- Monitor growth and development

#### Specific Replacement
**Phosphate Supplementation:**
- Oral phosphate preparations
- Goal: serum phosphate 2.5-4.5 mg/dL
- Divide doses to avoid diarrhea
- Monitor calcium levels

**Bicarbonate Therapy:**
- Sodium or potassium bicarbonate
- Goal: serum HCO3- 22-24 mEq/L
- Higher doses than isolated RTA
- Monitor for volume overload

**Vitamin D Therapy:**
- Calcitriol (active vitamin D)
- Improves calcium absorption
- Helps bone mineralization
- Monitor calcium and phosphate

**Water and Electrolytes:**
- Adequate hydration
- Sodium supplementation if needed
- Potassium supplementation
- Monitor urine output

#### Disease-Specific Therapy
**Cystinosis:**
- Cysteamine therapy
- Dose: 60-90 mg/kg/day divided
- Improves growth and renal outcomes
- Monitor white blood cell cystine levels

**Ifosfamide Toxicity:**
- Discontinue if possible
- Supportive care
- May be irreversible
- Consider dialysis if ESRD

**Multiple Myeloma:**
- Chemotherapy for myeloma
- Bortezomib-based regimens
- Consider autologous stem cell transplant
- Supportive renal care

**Tenofovir Toxicity:**
- Discontinue tenofovir
- Switch to alternative ART
- Usually reversible
- Monitor renal function recovery

#### Monitoring
- Growth parameters (children)
- Bone age assessment
- Renal function tests
- Electrolytes weekly initially
- Bone density testing

## 6. Inherited Tubular Disorders

### Bartter Syndrome

#### Classification & Genetics
**Type I (SLC12A1):**
- NKCC2 gene mutation
- Neonatal presentation
- Severe polyhydramnios

**Type II (KCNJ1):**
- ROMK gene mutation
- Neonatal presentation
- Hyperkalemia in neonatal period

**Type III (CLCNKB):**
- CLC-Kb gene mutation
- Classic presentation
- Variable severity

**Type IV (BSND):**
- Barttin gene mutation
- Sensorineural deafness
- Severe presentation

**Type V (CASR):**
- Calcium-sensing receptor mutation
- Autosomal dominant
- Milder presentation

#### Pathophysiology
- Impaired sodium chloride reabsorption
- Volume depletion mimicking diuretic use
- Secondary hyperaldosteronism
- Increased prostaglandin E2 production
- Juxtaglomerular hyperplasia

#### Clinical Features
**Neonatal Form:**
- Polyhydramnios
- Premature birth
- Severe dehydration
- Growth failure
- Hypercalciuria, nephrocalcinosis

**Classic Form:**
- Polyuria, polydipsia
- Salt craving
- Growth retardation
- Muscle weakness, cramps
- Normal blood pressure

#### Laboratory Findings
- Hypokalemia
- Metabolic alkalosis
- Normal blood pressure
- Elevated renin and aldosterone
- Hypercalciuria (Types I, II)

- 1-3 mg/kg/day divided
- Reduces prostaglandin production
- Improves growth and symptoms
- Monitor for GI side effects

- Potassium chloride
- Sodium chloride
- Magnesium supplementation
- Monitor levels frequently

**Other Measures:**

- High fluid intake
- Monitor renal function
- Growth monitoring

### Dent Disease ⭐

#### Genetics & Pathophysiology
**Genetic Basis:**
- X-linked recessive inheritance
- **CLCN5 gene** (Dent Disease 1 - 60% of cases):
  - Encodes voltage-gated chloride channel (ClC-5)
  - Critical for endosomal acidification in proximal tubule
  - Impaired receptor-mediated endocytosis
- **OCRL gene** (Dent Disease 2 - 15% of cases):
  - Encodes inositol polyphosphate 5-phosphatase
  - Overlaps with Lowe syndrome (oculocerebrorenal syndrome)
  - May have milder phenotype
- Remaining 25% of cases: Genetic cause unidentified

**Pathophysiology:**
- Defective proximal tubular endocytosis
- Impaired reabsorption of low molecular weight proteins, calcium, phosphate
- Hypercalciuria leads to nephrocalcinosis, kidney stones
- Progressive CKD due to chronic tubulointerstitial injury

#### Clinical Features
**Classic Triad (Diagnostic):**
1. **Low Molecular Weight Proteinuria (LMWP)**:
   - β2-microglobulin, retinol-binding protein elevated
   - Not albumin (differentiates from glomerular disease)
   - Urine protein usually <1-2 g/day
2. **Hypercalciuria**:
   - Urine calcium >4 mg/kg/day (children) or >300 mg/day (adults)
   - Persistent, not dietary
3. **Nephrocalcinosis and/or Kidney Stones**:
   - Calcium oxalate or calcium phosphate stones
   - Medullary nephrocalcinosis on ultrasound/CT
   - Recurrent kidney stones starting in childhood/adolescence

**Additional Features:**
- Males predominantly affected (X-linked)
- Female carriers may have mild manifestations (hypercalciuria)
- Rickets or osteomalacia (due to phosphate wasting in some patients)
- Hematuria
- Progressive CKD developing in 30-80% of patients by age 40-60

**Extrarenal Manifestations (Dent Disease 2/OCRL):**
- Cataracts
- Mild intellectual disability
- Hypotonia (Lowe syndrome overlap)

#### Diagnostic Approach
**Laboratory Evaluation:**
- **Urinalysis**: Proteinuria, hematuria (microscopic)
- **Urine Protein Electrophoresis**: Low molecular weight proteinuria
- **Specific LMW Proteins**:
  - β2-microglobulin >0.5 mg/L (elevated)
  - Retinol-binding protein elevated
- **24-Hour Urine Collection**:
  - Hypercalciuria (>4 mg/kg/day or >300 mg/day)
  - Protein excretion 1-2 g/day
  - Phosphate wasting (variable)
- **Serum**: Creatinine (assess GFR), calcium (usually normal), phosphate (may be low)
- **Imaging**:
  - Renal ultrasound: Nephrocalcinosis (80-90% of patients)
  - CT (if ultrasound negative): More sensitive for nephrocalcinosis
- **Genetic Testing**: CLCN5 and OCRL gene sequencing (confirmatory)

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- LMW proteinuria + hypercalciuria + nephrocalcinosis/stones in male patient → strongly suspect Dent disease
- Genetic confirmation recommended

#### Treatment Strategies
**No Curative Therapy** - Focus on preventing complications:

**1. Reduce Hypercalciuria and Stone Formation:**
- **Thiazide Diuretics (First-Line):**
  - Hydrochlorothiazide 12.5-25 mg daily OR chlorthalidone 25 mg daily
  - Mechanism: Increase renal calcium reabsorption in distal tubule
  - Reduces urine calcium by 30-50%
  - Monitor for hypokalemia, hyponatremia
- **High Fluid Intake**:
  - Goal: Urine output >2 L/day
  - Dilutes urine, reduces stone risk
- **Dietary Modifications**:
  - Moderate sodium restriction (<2 g/day) - reduces calcium excretion
  - Moderate animal protein restriction
  - Maintain normal calcium intake (do NOT restrict calcium - worsens oxalate absorption)
- **Potassium Citrate** (if hypocitraturia or acidosis):
  - 10-20 mEq PO BID-TID
  - Alkalinizes urine, inhibits stone formation
  - Monitor serum potassium (especially with thiazides)

**2. Slow CKD Progression:**
- **ACE Inhibitor or ARB**:
  - May reduce proteinuria and slow CKD progression
  - Limited evidence specific to Dent disease
  - Standard use for CKD management
- **BP Control**: Target <130/80 mmHg
- **Avoid nephrotoxic agents**

**3. Monitor and Treat Rickets/Osteomalacia (if present):**
- Vitamin D supplementation (if deficiency)
- Oral phosphate supplementation (if severe hypophosphatemia)

**4. Kidney Stones:**
- Medical expulsive therapy (tamsulosin) for acute stones
- Urologic intervention (ESWL, ureteroscopy) as needed
- Preventive measures as above

#### Prognosis
- **Variable progression to ESRD**:
  - 30-80% develop ESRD by age 40-60
  - Mean age of ESRD: 40-50 years
  - CLCN5 mutations may have worse prognosis than OCRL
- **Factors associated with faster progression**:
  - Nephrocalcinosis severity
  - Recurrent kidney stones
  - Severity of proteinuria
- **Post-Transplant**: Disease does not recur (tubular defect corrected by donor kidney)

#### Genetic Counseling
- X-linked recessive inheritance
- **Male offspring of carrier mothers**: 50% risk of disease
- **Female offspring of carrier mothers**: 50% risk of being carriers
- **Female carriers**: Usually asymptomatic, may have mild hypercalciuria
- Prenatal genetic testing available

### Cystinosis ⭐

#### Genetics & Pathophysiology
**Genetic Basis:**
- Autosomal recessive inheritance
- **CTNS gene** (chromosome 17p13):
  - Encodes cystinosin, a lysosomal cystine transporter
  - Mutation → cystine accumulation in lysosomes
  - Most common: 57-kb deletion in Northern Europeans

**Pathophysiology:**
- Cystine crystal accumulation in lysosomes of all cells
- Kidney: Proximal tubular dysfunction (Fanconi syndrome), progressive CKD
- Eye: Corneal cystine crystal deposition (pathognomonic)
- Other organs: Thyroid, pancreas, muscle, brain (in late-onset disease)

**Clinical Forms:**
1. **Infantile Nephropathic Cystinosis** (95% of cases):
   - Most severe form
   - Onset 6-12 months of age
   - Fanconi syndrome, ESRD by age 10 without treatment
2. **Juvenile/Late-Onset Cystinosis** (rare):
   - Milder phenotype
   - Onset in adolescence
   - ESRD in 20s-30s
3. **Ocular Non-Nephropathic Cystinosis** (very rare):
   - Only corneal crystals, no kidney disease

#### Clinical Features
**Infantile Nephropathic Cystinosis:**

**Renal Manifestations (Present by 6-12 months):**
- **Fanconi Syndrome** (hallmark):
  - Polyuria, polydipsia (renal water wasting)
  - Failure to thrive, poor growth
  - Rickets (renal phosphate wasting, metabolic acidosis)
  - Hypokalemia (potassium wasting)
  - Metabolic acidosis (proximal RTA - Type 2)
  - Glycosuria (normal serum glucose)
  - Aminoaciduria, phosphaturia
- **Progressive CKD**:
  - Without treatment: ESRD by age 10
  - With cysteamine: Delayed ESRD to late teens/20s

**Ocular Manifestations:**
- **Corneal cystine crystals** (pathognomonic, present by age 1):
  - Detected by slit-lamp examination
  - Causes photophobia (first symptom in many patients)
  - Progressive, leads to corneal erosions, pain
- Retinopathy (later, usually after age 10)

**Extrarenal Manifestations (Develop Over Time):**
- **Hypothyroidism** (60-80% by adolescence)
- **Diabetes mellitus** (rare, pancreatic β-cell damage)
- **Myopathy** (muscle weakness, difficulty swallowing - teens/adults)
- **Neurologic**: Cerebral atrophy, cognitive decline (untreated patients)
- **Growth retardation** (multifactorial: Fanconi syndrome, hypothyroidism)

#### Diagnostic Approach
**Laboratory Evaluation:**
- **Fanconi Syndrome Findings**:
  - Urine: Glycosuria, phosphaturia, aminoaciduria, low molecular weight proteinuria
  - Serum: Hypophosphatemia, hypokalemia, metabolic acidosis (normal anion gap), normal glucose
- **Elevated White Blood Cell Cystine Level (Diagnostic):**
  - Normal: <0.2 nmol half-cystine/mg protein
  - **Cystinosis**: >2 nmol half-cystine/mg protein (10-100× normal)
  - Gold standard for diagnosis
- **Genetic Testing**: CTNS gene sequencing (confirmatory)
- **Slit-Lamp Examination**: Corneal cystine crystals (pathognomonic if present)
- **Kidney Biopsy** (rarely needed):
  - "Swan neck" deformity of proximal tubules
  - Cystine crystals visible on frozen sections (dissolve in formalin)

#### Treatment Strategies

**1. Cysteamine Therapy (Disease-Modifying - CRITICAL):** ⭐

**Oral Cysteamine (Cystagon®, Procysbi®):**
- **Mechanism**: Reacts with cystine in lysosomes, forming cysteine-cysteamine mixed disulfide that can exit lysosomes via alternate transporter
- **Dosing**:
  - **Immediate-Release (Cystagon®)**: 60-90 mg/kg/day divided every 6 hours (QID)
  - **Delayed-Release (Procysbi®)**: 60-90 mg/kg/day divided every 12 hours (BID) - better adherence
  - Start low, titrate up to avoid GI side effects
- **Goal**: White blood cell cystine level <1 nmol half-cystine/mg protein (checked 5-6 hours post-dose)
- **Benefits**:
  - **Delays ESRD by 10-15 years** (from age 10 to age 20-25)
  - Improves growth
  - Reduces hypothyroidism incidence
  - Reduces muscle weakness
  - **START AS EARLY AS POSSIBLE** (ideally before age 2) for maximum benefit
- **Monitoring**: WBC cystine levels every 3-6 months
- **Adverse Effects**:
  - **GI upset** (nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain) - most common
  - **Breath/body odor** (sulfur smell - from metabolism) - often limits adherence
  - Rash (rare)
  - Gastric ulcers (rare)
  - Benign intracranial hypertension (rare)
- **Adherence Challenges**: QID dosing, odor, GI side effects → use delayed-release formulation

**Topical Cysteamine Eye Drops (Cystaran®):**
- **Indication**: Corneal cystine crystals causing photophobia
- **Dosing**: 0.44% cysteamine eye drops, 1 drop every waking hour (10-12×/day)
- **Effect**: Dissolves corneal crystals, reduces photophobia
- **Limitation**: Does not prevent retinopathy
- **Adherence**: Difficult (frequent dosing, refrigeration required)

**2. Management of Fanconi Syndrome:**

**Electrolyte and Mineral Replacement:**
- **Phosphate Supplementation**:
  - Oral phosphate (Neutra-Phos, K-Phos) 1-3 g/day divided TID-QID
  - Goal: Serum phosphate >3 mg/dL
  - Prevents rickets, improves growth
- **Potassium Supplementation**:
  - Potassium citrate or potassium chloride 1-2 mEq/kg/day divided
  - Goal: Serum potassium >3.5 mEq/L
- **Bicarbonate Replacement**:
  - Sodium or potassium bicarbonate 1-3 mEq/kg/day divided
  - Goal: Serum bicarbonate >22 mEq/L (correct metabolic acidosis)
- **Vitamin D** (if deficiency):
  - Ergocalciferol or cholecalciferol supplementation
  - Calcitriol (active vitamin D) if severe rickets
- **Carnitine** (if deficiency):
  - L-carnitine 50-100 mg/kg/day divided (proximal tubule wasting)

**Hydration:**
- High fluid intake (compensate for polyuria, prevent dehydration)
- Free access to water for children
- Monitor for dehydration, especially during illness

**3. Treatment of Extrarenal Complications:**

**Hypothyroidism:**
- Screen thyroid function annually (TSH, free T4)
- Levothyroxine replacement if hypothyroid
- Common by adolescence (60-80%)

**Growth Hormone Deficiency:**
- Growth hormone therapy if deficiency documented
- Improves height velocity in cystinosis children

**Myopathy:**
- Physical therapy
- Cysteamine continuation essential
- May require feeding tube if severe dysphagia

**4. Renal Replacement Therapy:**
- **Hemodialysis or Peritoneal Dialysis**: When ESRD develops
- **Kidney Transplantation**:
  - **Excellent outcomes** - cystinosis does NOT recur in transplant
  - Fanconi syndrome resolves (corrected by donor kidney)
  - **Continue cysteamine post-transplant** for extrarenal complications (cornea, muscle, etc.)

#### Prognosis
**Untreated:**
- ESRD by age 10
- Death in childhood from uremia (before dialysis era)

**With Cysteamine Treatment:**
- **Delays ESRD** from age 10 to age 20-25 (10-15 year delay)
- Improved growth (near-normal adult height if started early)
- Reduced hypothyroidism
- Reduced myopathy and neurologic complications
- **Early treatment critical**: Starting before age 2 years provides maximal benefit
- **Adherence is key**: Poor adherence → faster CKD progression

**Post-Transplant:**
- Excellent kidney transplant outcomes
- Cystinosis does NOT recur in transplanted kidney
- Must continue cysteamine for extrarenal disease
- Normal lifespan with transplant and cysteamine

#### Genetic Counseling
- Autosomal recessive inheritance
- 25% recurrence risk for siblings
- Carrier testing available for at-risk family members
- Prenatal diagnosis available (chorionic villus sampling, amniocentesis)

### Primary Hyperoxaluria ⭐

#### Genetics & Pathophysiology
**Genetic Basis:**
- Autosomal recessive inheritance
- Three types based on enzyme deficiency:
  1. **Primary Hyperoxaluria Type 1 (PH1) - 80% of cases**:
     - **AGXT gene** (chromosome 2q37.3)
     - Encodes alanine-glyoxylate aminotransferase (AGT)
     - AGT deficiency in peroxisomes of hepatocytes
     - Most severe form
  2. **Primary Hyperoxaluria Type 2 (PH2) - 10% of cases**:
     - **GRHPR gene**
     - Encodes glyoxylate reductase/hydroxypyruvate reductase (GRHPR)
     - Milder than PH1
  3. **Primary Hyperoxaluria Type 3 (PH3) - Rare**:
     - **HOGA1 gene**
     - Encodes 4-hydroxy-2-oxoglutarate aldolase
     - Mildest form, may not progress to ESRD

**Pathophysiology:**
- Enzyme deficiency → excess hepatic glyoxylate production
- Glyoxylate converted to oxalate (instead of glycine)
- **Massive endogenous oxalate overproduction** (3-5× normal)
- Oxalate excreted renally → calcium oxalate supersaturation
- **Nephrocalcinosis** and **recurrent calcium oxalate kidney stones**
- Progressive CKD/ESRD from chronic crystal deposition
- Once ESRD develops → systemic oxalosis (oxalate deposits in bones, heart, retina, vessels)

#### Clinical Features
**Renal Manifestations:**
- **Recurrent Calcium Oxalate Kidney Stones**:
  - Often begins in childhood (mean age 5-7 years, but can be infancy or adulthood)
  - Frequent stones (multiple per year)
  - Bilateral stones common
- **Nephrocalcinosis**:
  - Calcium oxalate crystal deposition in renal parenchyma
  - Detectable on ultrasound or CT
  - Progressive renal injury
- **Progressive CKD → ESRD**:
  - PH1: ESRD by age 15-30 years (50% by age 15 without treatment)
  - PH2: ESRD by age 30-50 years (milder)
  - PH3: Variable, may never progress to ESRD

**Systemic Oxalosis (After ESRD):**
- Once GFR <30-40 mL/min, oxalate cannot be excreted → systemic accumulation
- **Bone disease**: Oxalate deposits in bones → fractures, pain, osteosclerosis
- **Cardiac**: Conduction defects, cardiomyopathy
- **Retinopathy**: Oxalate crystal deposition in retina
- **Peripheral vascular disease**
- **Life-threatening** if untreated

#### Diagnostic Approach
**Laboratory Evaluation:**
- **24-Hour Urine Oxalate (Diagnostic):**
  - Normal: <40-45 mg/day (adults), <1 mg/kg/day (children)
  - **PH1**: 80-300 mg/day (2-8× normal)
  - **PH2**: 80-150 mg/day
  - **PH3**: 40-80 mg/day (mild elevation)
  - **Must exclude secondary hyperoxaluria** (dietary, enteric, vitamin C)
- **24-Hour Urine Glycolate** (helps differentiate types):
  - **Elevated in PH1** (specific marker)
  - Normal in PH2, PH3
- **Plasma Oxalate**:
  - Useful when GFR <30-40 mL/min (urine measurements unreliable)
  - Elevated in all PH types once CKD advanced
- **Genetic Testing**: AGXT, GRHPR, HOGA1 sequencing (confirmatory)
- **Liver Biopsy** (rarely needed):
  - Measure AGT enzyme activity (PH1 diagnosis if genetics inconclusive)
  - Invasive, not routinely performed

**Imaging:**
- **Renal Ultrasound or CT**: Nephrocalcinosis, kidney stones
- **Bone X-rays** (if systemic oxalosis suspected): Osteosclerosis

**Exclude Secondary Hyperoxaluria:**
- Dietary (high oxalate foods, vitamin C supplements)
- Enteric hyperoxaluria (inflammatory bowel disease, short gut, bariatric surgery)
- Medication (ethylene glycol, high-dose vitamin C)

#### Treatment Strategies

**PARADIGM SHIFT: RNA Interference (RNAi) Therapy - FDA Approved 2020-2021** ⭐

**1. Lumasiran (Oxlumo®) - FDA Approved November 2020** ⭐⭐⭐

**Indication:**
- **Primary Hyperoxaluria Type 1 (PH1)** - all ages, all CKD stages
- First FDA-approved treatment specifically for PH1

**Mechanism:**
- Small interfering RNA (siRNA) targeting HAO1 mRNA in hepatocytes
- HAO1 encodes glycolate oxidase (GO), the enzyme that converts glycolate → glyoxylate
- Inhibiting GO → reduces glyoxylate → reduces oxalate production
- **Reduces hepatic oxalate production by 60-70%**

**Dosing:**
- **Subcutaneous injection** (administered by healthcare provider or at home)
- **Weight-Based Dosing**:
  - Loading doses: Monthly × 3 doses
  - Maintenance: Every 3 months (quarterly)
  - Specific mg/kg dosing varies by weight (<10 kg, 10-20 kg, >20 kg)
- **All CKD stages**, including post-transplant

**Efficacy:**
- **ILLUMINATE-A Trial (2020)**:
  - 65% mean reduction in urine oxalate from baseline
  - 84% of patients achieved urine oxalate <1.5× ULN
  - Sustained benefit through 12+ months
- Reduces stone events
- Improves nephrocalcinosis (some regression)
- Slows CKD progression

**Adverse Effects:**
- Injection site reactions (most common)
- Elevated transaminases (rare, mild)
- Generally well-tolerated

**Cost:** ~$500,000-700,000/year (prior authorization required)

**Monitoring:**
- Urine oxalate every 3-6 months
- Liver enzymes periodically
- Stone recurrence, eGFR

**2. Nedosiran (Rivfloza®) - FDA Approved December 2023** ⭐

**Indication:**
- **Primary Hyperoxaluria Type 1, 2, or 3** with preserved kidney function (eGFR ≥30 mL/min)
- Broader indication than lumasiran (covers PH2 and PH3)

**Mechanism:**
- siRNA targeting LDHA mRNA (lactate dehydrogenase A) in hepatocytes
- LDHA converts glyoxylate → oxalate (final step)
- Inhibiting LDHA → reduces oxalate production
- Works for PH1, PH2, and PH3 (all converge at LDHA step)

**Dosing:**
- **Subcutaneous injection** monthly
- **160 mg SC monthly** (adults and children ≥9 years)

**Efficacy:**
- **PHYOX2 Trial (2023)**:
  - Mean 63% reduction in urine oxalate
  - 80% of patients achieved urine oxalate <1.5× ULN
- Effective for PH1, PH2, PH3

**Advantage Over Lumasiran:**
- Works for PH2 and PH3 (lumasiran only for PH1)
- Monthly dosing vs. quarterly (more frequent, but possibly more consistent levels)

**Cost:** Similar to lumasiran (~$500,000-700,000/year)

**3. Conservative (Non-RNAi) Management:**

**High Fluid Intake (Essential for All PH Patients):**
- Goal: Urine output >3 L/day (adults), 2-3 L/day (children)
- Dilutes urine, reduces calcium oxalate supersaturation
- Critical even with RNAi therapy

**Pyridoxine (Vitamin B6) - PH1 Only:**
- **Dosing**: 5-20 mg/kg/day PO (up to 300-600 mg/day in adults)
- **Mechanism**: Cofactor for AGT enzyme (may improve residual enzyme activity)
- **Response Rate**: 10-30% of PH1 patients (responsive mutations: G170R, F152I)
- **Trial Period**: 3-6 months, monitor urine oxalate
  - If urine oxalate decreases by ≥30% → pyridoxine-responsive, continue
  - If no response → discontinue
- **Monitoring**: Peripheral neuropathy (rare at doses <600 mg/day)
- NOT effective in PH2 or PH3

**Potassium Citrate:**
- 10-20 mEq PO BID-TID
- Alkalinizes urine, inhibits calcium oxalate crystallization
- Increases oxalate solubility
- May reduce stone events

**Low Oxalate Diet (Controversial):**
- Minimal impact (90-95% of oxalate is endogenously produced in PH)
- Avoid extremely high oxalate foods (spinach, rhubarb, nuts)
- NOT a primary therapy

**4. Renal Replacement Therapy:**

**Hemodialysis (If ESRD):**
- **Intensive dialysis required**: Daily or nocturnal HD
  - Standard 3×/week HD is insufficient to clear endogenous oxalate production
  - Oxalate accumulates → systemic oxalosis
- **Bridge to transplant**, not long-term solution

**Kidney Transplantation (PH1 - Special Considerations):**
- **Isolated kidney transplant** (for PH1):
  - **High risk of recurrence** (50-80% graft failure at 5 years)
  - Liver still produces excess oxalate → recurrent nephrocalcinosis in graft
  - Only option if:
    - Lumasiran/nedosiran available pre-transplant to reduce oxalate
    - Intensive post-transplant oxalate monitoring
- **Combined liver-kidney transplant** (PH1 - traditional approach):
  - Replaces defective liver (cures oxalate overproduction)
  - Kidney transplant corrects ESRD
  - **Curative** for PH1
  - Downside: Requires two organs, liver transplant immunosuppression
- **NOW: Lumasiran/Nedosiran may allow isolated kidney transplant** (emerging data):
  - Start RNAi therapy before ESRD
  - Maintain post-transplant
  - May avoid need for liver transplant (paradigm shift 2020+)

**PH2 and PH3 Kidney Transplantation:**
- **Isolated kidney transplant** generally successful
- Lower recurrence risk than PH1 (less severe oxalate overproduction)
- Liver transplant NOT required

#### Prognosis
**Untreated PH1:**
- 50% ESRD by age 15
- Systemic oxalosis after ESRD → high mortality

**With Lumasiran/Nedosiran (New Era - 2020+):**
- **Dramatic improvement**
- Reduces urine oxalate by 60-70%
- Slows or halts CKD progression
- May allow isolated kidney transplant (avoid liver transplant)
- Long-term data still emerging, but highly promising

**PH2 and PH3:**
- Milder than PH1
- Slower progression to ESRD
- Good transplant outcomes

#### Genetic Counseling
- Autosomal recessive
- 25% recurrence risk for siblings
- Prenatal diagnosis available

**Board Exam Key Points:** ⭐
- Primary Hyperoxaluria Type 1 (PH1) most common and severe
- Elevated urine oxalate (80-300 mg/day) + elevated urine glycolate (PH1 specific)
- **Lumasiran (Oxlumo®)** = first FDA-approved RNAi therapy for PH1 (2020)
  - siRNA targeting glycolate oxidase (HAO1)
  - 60-70% reduction in urine oxalate
  - SC injection quarterly
- **Nedosiran (Rivfloza®)** = FDA-approved for PH1, PH2, PH3 (2023)
  - siRNA targeting LDHA (final step)
  - SC injection monthly
- Combined liver-kidney transplant historically standard for PH1, but RNAi may change paradigm

### Nephronophthisis & Medullary Cystic Kidney Disease

#### Definition & Classification
**Nephronophthisis (NPHP):**
- Autosomal recessive cystic kidney disease
- Most common genetic cause of ESRD in children and young adults
- Affects renal medulla and corticomedullary junction
- Part of ciliopathy spectrum (defective primary cilia)

**Medullary Cystic Kidney Disease (MCKD):**
- Autosomal dominant inheritance
- Adult-onset (vs. NPHP pediatric/adolescent)
- Similar pathology to NPHP
- **MCKD now often referred to as "Autosomal Dominant Tubulointerstitial Kidney Disease (ADTKD)"**

**Together referred to as "Nephronophthisis-Medullary Cystic Kidney Disease (NPHP-MCKD) Complex"**

#### Genetics & Pathophysiology
**Nephronophthisis (NPHP) Genetics:**
- Over 20 genes identified (NPHP1-20+)
- **NPHP1** (deletion) = most common (~25% of cases)
- All encode proteins of primary cilia or centrosomes
- Ciliopathies → defective tubular epithelial function

**Pathophysiology:**
- **Cysts** at corticomedullary junction (vs. ADPKD cortical cysts)
- Tubulointerstitial fibrosis, tubular atrophy
- Thickened and disrupted tubular basement membranes
- Loss of renal concentrating ability (polyuria, polydipsia)
- Progressive CKD → ESRD

#### Clinical Features
**Nephronophthisis (NPHP) - Pediatric/Young Adult:**

**Renal Manifestations:**
- **Polyuria and Polydipsia** (earliest symptom, age 2-6 years):
  - Due to impaired urine concentrating ability (medullary dysfunction)
  - Enuresis (bedwetting) common
- **Salt Wasting** (some patients):
  - Hyponatremia, volume depletion
- **Anemia** (out of proportion to CKD stage):
  - Due to tubulointerstitial disease affecting erythropoietin production
- **Progressive CKD → ESRD**:
  - **Infantile form** (NPHP2): ESRD by age 1-3 years
  - **Juvenile form** (NPHP1 - most common): ESRD by age 10-15 years
  - **Adolescent form** (NPHP3): ESRD by age 15-25 years

**Extrarenal Manifestations (30-40% of NPHP patients):**
- **Ocular** (most common):
  - Retinitis pigmentosa (Senior-Løken syndrome)
  - Oculomotor apraxia
  - Blindness in some cases
- **Hepatic**: Congenital hepatic fibrosis
- **Skeletal**: Cone-shaped epiphyses, short ribs
- **Neurologic**: Cerebellar hypoplasia, developmental delay (Joubert syndrome)

**Medullary Cystic Kidney Disease (MCKD/ADTKD) - Adult-Onset:**
- **Later onset** (30-60 years) vs. NPHP
- Polyuria, polydipsia
- Slowly progressive CKD → ESRD by age 40-70
- **Hyperuricemia and gout** (MCKD Type 2/ADTKD-UMOD - uromodulin gene):
  - Gout often precedes kidney disease by years
  - May respond poorly to urate-lowering therapy
- **Bland urine sediment** (no hematuria, minimal proteinuria)

#### Diagnostic Approach
**Laboratory Evaluation:**
- **Urinalysis**:
  - **Bland sediment** (key feature - differentiates from glomerular disease)
  - No/minimal proteinuria (<1 g/day)
  - No hematuria (vs. ADPKD often has hematuria)
- **Urine Concentrating Defect**:
  - Low urine osmolality despite dehydration
  - Inability to concentrate urine (medullary dysfunction)
- **Anemia** (out of proportion to CKD stage)
- **Serum Uric Acid**: Elevated in MCKD Type 2/ADTKD-UMOD

**Imaging:**
- **Renal Ultrasound**:
  - **Normal or small kidneys** (vs. ADPKD enlarged kidneys)
  - Increased echogenicity
  - **Cysts may be absent or few** (vs. ADPKD numerous cysts)
    - Cysts at corticomedullary junction (not always visible on ultrasound)
  - Key: Normal-sized or small kidneys + CKD → think NPHP/MCKD
- **MRI**: More sensitive for detecting small medullary cysts

**Genetic Testing:**
- NPHP gene panel (NPHP1-20+)
- MCKD: UMOD gene (uromodulin), MUC1 gene
- Essential for diagnosis (cysts may not be visible)

**Kidney Biopsy** (if diagnosis unclear):
- Tubulointerstitial fibrosis, tubular atrophy
- Thickened tubular basement membranes
- Cysts in medulla (may not be present in biopsy sample)

#### Treatment Strategies
**No Disease-Specific Treatment:**

**1. Manage Complications:**
- **Polyuria/Polydipsia**:
  - Free access to water (prevent dehydration)
  - Avoid water restriction (dangerous in NPHP)
- **Anemia**:
  - Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs) - start early (Hgb <10 g/dL)
  - Iron supplementation as needed
- **Hyperuricemia/Gout** (MCKD/ADTKD-UMOD):
  - Allopurinol or febuxostat
  - May not fully respond (due to underexcretion)

**2. Slow CKD Progression:**
- BP control (target <130/80 mmHg)
- ACE inhibitor or ARB (if hypertension or proteinuria, though proteinuria usually minimal)
- Avoid nephrotoxic agents
- SGLT2 inhibitors may be considered (emerging evidence for ADTKD)

**3. Manage Extrarenal Complications:**
- Ophthalmology evaluation for retinitis pigmentosa (NPHP)
- Hepatic fibrosis monitoring (if present)
- Developmental screening (if neurologic features)

**4. Renal Replacement Therapy:**
- Dialysis or transplantation when ESRD develops
- **Kidney transplantation**:
  - Excellent outcomes
  - Disease does NOT recur in transplant (genetic defect in native kidneys only)
  - Standard immunosuppression

#### Prognosis
**NPHP:**
- Juvenile form (NPHP1): ESRD by age 10-15
- Adolescent form: ESRD by age 15-25
- Infantile form: ESRD by age 1-3 (rare)

**MCKD/ADTKD:**
- ESRD by age 40-70 (highly variable)
- Slowly progressive

**Post-Transplant:**
- Excellent outcomes
- No recurrence

#### Genetic Counseling
**NPHP:**
- Autosomal recessive
- 25% recurrence risk for siblings
- Carrier testing for family members

**MCKD/ADTKD:**
- Autosomal dominant
- 50% risk for offspring
- Penetrance high (most affected individuals develop ESRD)

**Board Exam Key Points:** ⭐
- Nephronophthisis = most common genetic cause of ESRD in children/young adults
- Autosomal recessive (NPHP) vs. autosomal dominant (MCKD/ADTKD)
- **Medullary cysts** at corticomedullary junction (vs. ADPKD cortical cysts)
- **Bland urine sediment** (no hematuria, minimal proteinuria) - key differentiator
- **Small or normal-sized kidneys** (vs. ADPKD enlarged)
- Polyuria, polydipsia (concentrating defect)
- Anemia out of proportion to CKD stage
- MCKD Type 2/ADTKD-UMOD: Hyperuricemia, gout
- Extrarenal: Retinitis pigmentosa (Senior-Løken syndrome), hepatic fibrosis
- No disease-specific treatment, transplant does not recur

## 7. Drug-Induced Tubular Disorders

### Common Nephrotoxic Agents

#### Aminoglycosides
**Mechanism:**
- Accumulation in proximal tubular cells
- Phospholipidosis
- Lysosomal dysfunction
- Cell necrosis

**Risk Factors:**
- High peak levels
- Prolonged therapy
- Pre-existing CKD
- Volume depletion
- Concurrent nephrotoxins

**Prevention:**
- Therapeutic drug monitoring
- Once-daily dosing
- Adequate hydration
- Limit duration

#### Amphotericin B
**Mechanism:**
- Direct tubular cell membrane damage
- Renal vasoconstriction
- Distal RTA
- Magnesium and potassium wasting

**Risk Reduction:**
- Liposomal formulations
- Aggressive hydration
- Electrolyte supplementation
- Sodium loading

#### Cisplatin
**Mechanism:**
- Proximal tubular uptake via transporters
- DNA cross-linking
- Cell death
- Magnesium wasting

**Prevention:**
- Aggressive hydration
- Magnesium supplementation
- Amifostine (controversial)
- Alternative agents if possible

#### Ifosfamide
**Mechanism:**
- Metabolite chloroacetaldehyde toxicity
- Mitochondrial dysfunction
- Fanconi syndrome
- May be irreversible

**Risk Factors:**
- Cumulative dose >60 g/m²
- Prior cisplatin exposure
- Young age
- Pre-existing CKD

#### Tenofovir
**Mechanism:**
- Mitochondrial DNA polymerase gamma inhibition
- Proximal tubular dysfunction
- Fanconi syndrome
- Usually reversible

**Monitoring:**
- Serum creatinine every 3-6 months
- Urine protein and glucose
- Phosphate levels
- Consider alternative if toxicity

### Prevention Strategies

#### Risk Assessment
- Identify high-risk patients
- Review medication list
- Assess baseline renal function
- Consider alternative agents

#### Monitoring Protocols
- Baseline and serial creatinine
- Therapeutic drug monitoring
- Urine output monitoring
- Electrolyte assessment

#### Protective Measures
- Adequate hydration
- Avoid concurrent nephrotoxins
- Dose adjustments for GFR
- Early nephrology consultation

## 8. Clinical Algorithms & Decision Trees

### Acute Interstitial Nephritis Evaluation

```
Acute Kidney Injury + Urine Abnormalities
        ↓
Medication Review (Recent New Drugs?)
        ↓
Clinical Features: Fever, Rash, Arthralgias?
        ↓
Urinalysis: WBC Casts, Eosinophils?
        ↓
Laboratory: Eosinophilia, Elevated ESR/CRP?
        ↓
Action: Stop Suspected Drugs
        ↓
Re-evaluate in 5-7 days
        ↓
Improving? → Continue monitoring
Not improving? → Consider steroids + biopsy
```

### Renal Tubular Acidosis Workup

```
Metabolic Acidosis + Normal Anion Gap
        ↓
Serum Potassium Level
        ↓
Low K+ → Consider Type 1 or 2 RTA
High K+ → Consider Type 4 RTA
        ↓
Urine pH Assessment
        ↓
pH >5.5 → Type 1 RTA (distal)
pH <5.5 → Type 2 or 4 RTA
        ↓
Bicarbonate Titration Test
        ↓
FE HCO3- >15% → Type 2 RTA (proximal)
FE HCO3- <5% → Type 1 RTA (distal)
        ↓
Aldosterone/Renin Levels (if Type 4 suspected)
```

## 9. Key Take-Home Points

1. **ATN Recognition**: Identify muddy brown casts and FENa >2% for diagnosis
2. **AIN Suspicion**: Always consider drug-induced AIN in AKI with fever, rash, or eosinophils
3. **RTA Classification**: Use urine pH and serum potassium to differentiate RTA types
4. **Fanconi Syndrome**: Look for multiple proximal tubular defects together
5. **Drug Causation**: Always review medications in tubular disorders
6. **Genetic Considerations**: Consider inherited syndromes in children and young adults
7. **Treatment Principles**: Replace lost substances and treat underlying cause
8. **Monitoring Importance**: Close electrolyte monitoring essential in all tubular disorders
9. **Recovery Expectations**: ATN and AIN usually recover, but may progress to CKD
10. **Prevention Focus**: Avoid nephrotoxins and maintain adequate hydration
