# Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) - Comprehensive Clinical Guide

## Metadata
**Core Domain:** Clinical Nephrology I  
**Difficulty Level:** Foundation-Advanced  
**Frequency of Use:** Daily  
**Clinical Impact:** High  
**Regulatory Requirements:** CKCC/KCC reporting, CMS documentation  
**Prerequisites:** Basic nephrology knowledge, renal physiology  
**Competency Development Time:** 12-24 months  
**Cross-references:** All clinical domains, electrolyte disorders, hypertension, transplantation

## 1. Definition & Classification

### KDIGO 2012 Definition (Updated 2024)

Chronic Kidney Disease is defined as abnormalities of kidney structure or function, present for >3 months, with implications for health. CKD is diagnosed based on:

**2024 KDIGO CKD Guideline Updates:**
- Integrated cardiovascular-kidney-metabolic (CKM) syndrome approach
- Emphasis on SGLT2 inhibitors and non-steroidal MRAs as foundational therapy
- Updated blood pressure targets and management strategies
- Incorporation of GLP-1 receptor agonists for renal and CV protection
- Enhanced focus on conservative kidney management for appropriate patients
- Social determinants of health and health equity considerations

**GFR Categories:**
- G1: ≥90 mL/min/1.73m² (normal or high)
- G2: 60-89 mL/min/1.73m² (mildly decreased)
- G3a: 45-59 mL/min/1.73m² (mild to moderately decreased)
- G3b: 30-44 mL/min/1.73m² (moderate to severely decreased)
- G4: 15-29 mL/min/1.73m² (severely decreased)
- G5: <15 mL/min/1.73m² (kidney failure)

**Albuminuria Categories:**
- A1: <30 mg/g (<3 mg/mmol) - normal to mildly increased
- A2: 30-300 mg/g (3-30 mg/mmol) - moderately increased
- A3: >300 mg/g (>30 mg/mmol) - severely increased

**Combined Classification:**
- CKD is classified as GFR category (G1-G5) + Albuminuria category (A1-A3)
- Example: G3aA2 = GFR 45-59 with albuminuria 30-300 mg/g

### Diagnostic Criteria

**Any of the following for >3 months:**
1. GFR <60 mL/min/1.73m² (G3a-G5)
2. GFR ≥60 mL/min/1.73m² with markers of kidney damage:
   - Albuminuria (A2-A3)
   - Urinary sediment abnormalities
   - Electrolyte/acid-base disorders due to tubular disorders
   - Histological abnormalities
   - Structural abnormalities detected by imaging
   - History of kidney transplantation

### Pathophysiological Framework

**Progressive Nature:**
- Initial injury → Maladaptive repair → Progressive fibrosis
- Hyperfiltration injury in remaining nephrons
- Tubulointerstitial fibrosis as final common pathway
- Vascular rarefaction and chronic hypoxia

**Key Mechanisms:**
- Glomerular hypertension and hyperfiltration
- Proteinuria-induced tubular toxicity
- RAAS activation and fibrosis
- Chronic inflammation
- Oxidative stress
- Endothelial dysfunction

## 2. Etiology & Risk Factors

### Major Etiologic Categories

#### Diabetes Mellitus (Leading Cause - 40-50%)
**Type 1 Diabetes:**
- Typical onset after 10-15 years of diabetes
- Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules on biopsy
- Progressive proteinuria
- Strong association with retinopathy

**Type 2 Diabetes:**
- More variable presentation
- May present with reduced GFR without proteinuria
- Often coexists with hypertension
- Higher prevalence in elderly and minorities

**Pathophysiology:**
- Advanced glycation end-products (AGEs)
- Glomerular hyperfiltration
- TGF-β mediated fibrosis
- Podocyte injury and loss

#### Hypertension (Second Leading Cause - 25-30%)
**Essential Hypertension:**
- Long-standing uncontrolled hypertension
- Benign nephrosclerosis
- Arteriolosclerosis and ischemic injury
- More common in African Americans

**Malignant Hypertension:**
- Acute severe hypertension
- Fibrinoid necrosis
- May lead to rapid GFR decline
- Requires aggressive BP control

#### Glomerular Diseases (10-15%)
**Primary Glomerular Diseases:**
- FSGS (especially in African Americans)
- IgA nephropathy (most common worldwide)
- Membranous nephropathy
- Minimal change disease
- Membranoproliferative GN

**Secondary Glomerular Diseases:**
- Lupus nephritis
- Diabetic nephropathy (already counted)
- Amyloidosis
- Light chain deposition disease
- Cryoglobulinemic GN

#### Tubulointerstitial Diseases (5-10%)
**Chronic Pyelonephritis:**
- Recurrent UTIs
- Vesicoureteral reflux
- Obstructive uropathy
- Analgesic nephropathy

**Autoimmune:**
- Sarcoidosis
- Sjögren's syndrome
- IgG4-related disease
- Interstitial nephritis with uveitis (TINU)

#### Vascular Diseases (5-10%)
**Ischemic Nephropathy:**
- Atherosclerotic renal artery stenosis
- Fibromuscular dysplasia
- Cholesterol emboli
- Thrombotic microangiopathy

#### Genetic/Hereditary Diseases (2-5%)
**Polycystic Kidney Disease:**
- ADPKD (most common inherited cause)
- ARPKD (pediatric presentation)
- Extrarenal manifestations (liver cysts, intracranial aneurysms)

**Other Genetic Disorders:**
- Alport syndrome
- Fabry disease
- Nephronophthisis
- Medullary cystic kidney disease

#### Obstructive Uropathy (2-5%)
**Chronic Obstruction:**
- BPH (benign prostatic hyperplasia)
- Prostate cancer
- Ureteral stones
- Retroperitoneal fibrosis
- Neurogenic bladder

### Risk Factor Assessment

#### Non-Modifiable Risk Factors
- Age >60 years
- African American ethnicity
- Hispanic ethnicity
- Family history of kidney disease
- Low birth weight
- Genetic predisposition

#### Modifiable Risk Factors
- Diabetes mellitus
- Hypertension
- Obesity (BMI >30)
- Smoking
- NSAID use
- High protein intake
- High sodium intake
- Physical inactivity

#### Socioeconomic Factors
- Limited access to healthcare
- Lower education level
- Poverty
- Lack of health insurance
- Food insecurity

## 3. Clinical Presentation & Diagnosis

### Early Disease (Often Asymptomatic)

#### Laboratory Detection
**Screening Recommendations:**
- Diabetes: Annual testing
- Hypertension: Annual testing
- Age >60: Consider periodic testing
- Family history: Consider periodic testing

**Screening Tests:**
- Serum creatinine with eGFR calculation
- Urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR)
- Spot protein-to-creatinine ratio (UPCR)
- Basic metabolic panel

#### Subtle Clinical Signs
- Mild fatigue
- Decreased exercise tolerance
- Mild nocturia
- Subtle appetite changes
- Difficulty concentrating

### Progressive Disease Symptoms

#### Metabolic Accumulation
**Uremic Symptoms:**
- Nausea, vomiting, anorexia
- Metallic taste in mouth
- Pericarditis, pleuritis
- Pruritus (uremic itching)
- Asterixis, encephalopathy

**Gastrointestinal:**
- Early satiety
- Weight loss
- Constipation or diarrhea
- Gastrointestinal bleeding

#### Fluid & Electrolyte Manifestations
- Peripheral edema
- Pulmonary edema
- Hypertension exacerbation
- Hyperkalemia symptoms
- Metabolic acidosis

#### Hematologic Manifestations
- Anemia symptoms (fatigue, dyspnea)
- Bleeding tendency
- Bruising
- Platelet dysfunction

#### Bone & Mineral Disorders
- Bone pain
- Fractures
- Vascular calcification
- Pruritus from hyperphosphatemia

### Diagnostic Workup

#### Initial Laboratory Evaluation
**Kidney Function:**
- Serum creatinine with eGFR (CKD-EPI 2021)
- Cystatin C if muscle mass abnormal
- BUN and BUN:Cr ratio
- Serum electrolytes

**Urine Studies:**
- UACR for albuminuria quantification
- UPCR if proteinuria >300 mg/g
- Urinalysis with microscopy
- Urine electrolytes if needed

**Metabolic Panel:**
- Calcium, phosphorus, magnesium
- PTH level
- Alkaline phosphatase
- Bicarbonate
- Lipid panel

**Hematology:**
- CBC with differential
- Iron studies (ferritin, TSAT)
- Vitamin B12, folate if indicated

#### Etiologic Workup
**Diabetes Evaluation:**
- HbA1c
- Urine glucose
- Retinal examination
- Neuropathy assessment

**Autoimmune Screening:**
- ANA, ANCA if indicated
- Complement levels (C3, C4)
- Anti-GBM antibody
- Hepatitis B/C serologies
- HIV testing

**Paraprotein Screening:**
- Serum protein electrophoresis (SPEP)
- Urine protein electrophoresis (UPEP)
- Immunofixation
- Free light chains

**Genetic Testing:**
- PKD mutation analysis
- Alport syndrome testing
- Fabry disease enzyme assay
- Custom gene panels

#### Imaging Studies
**Renal Ultrasound:**
- Kidney size and echogenicity
- Cortical thickness
- Cysts or masses
- Hydronephrosis
- Doppler studies if indicated

**CT/MRI:**
- Complex cyst characterization
- Renal artery stenosis
- Retroperitoneal fibrosis
- Stone disease

**Nuclear Medicine:**
- GFR measurement (iothalamate, DTPA)
- Renal scan for differential function
- MAG3 Lasix renogram

#### Kidney Biopsy Indications
**Unexplained CKD:**
- Rapid progression (>5 mL/min/year)
- Active urinary sediment
- Systemic disease with renal involvement
- Genetic confirmation needed
- Treatment decisions depend on pathology

**Contraindications:**
- Uncontrolled hypertension
- Bleeding diathesis
- Single kidney
- Severe obesity
- Patient refusal

## 4. Progression Risk Assessment

### Prognostic Factors

#### Baseline Risk Factors
**Demographics:**
- Age >65 years
- African American ethnicity
- Male gender (for faster progression)
- Low socioeconomic status

**Clinical Factors:**
- Diabetes mellitus
- Hypertension
- Cardiovascular disease
- Higher baseline proteinuria
- Lower baseline eGFR

**Laboratory Markers:**
- Albuminuria >300 mg/g
- eGFR <45 mL/min/1.73m²
- Hyperphosphatemia
- Secondary hyperparathyroidism
- Anemia
- Metabolic acidosis

#### Dynamic Risk Factors
**Rate of eGFR Decline:**
- Fast progressors: >5 mL/min/year
- Moderate: 2-5 mL/min/year
- Slow: <2 mL/min/year

**Proteinuria Trends:**
- Increasing albuminuria
- Failure to achieve proteinuria reduction
- Nephrotic-range proteinuria

**Blood Pressure Control:**
- Systolic BP >140 mmHg
- Diastolic BP >90 mmHg
- High variability in BP

**Metabolic Control:**
- HbA1c >8%
- LDL cholesterol >100 mg/dL
- Uric acid >7 mg/dL

### Risk Prediction Tools (2024 Updates with AI Integration)

#### KDIGO Prognostic Framework
**Heat Map Categories:**
- Green: Low risk (G1-G2, A1)
- Yellow: Moderately increased risk (G1-G2, A2; G3a, A1)
- Orange: High risk (G1-G2, A3; G3a, A2; G3b, A1)
- Red: Very high risk (G3a, A3; G3b, A2-A3; G4-G5, any A)

#### Kidney Failure Risk Equation (KFRE)
**Overview:**
- Validated prediction model for ESKD risk
- Most widely used and validated risk calculator
- **4-variable KFRE** (simplified): Age, sex, eGFR, urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio
- **8-variable KFRE** (comprehensive): Adds calcium, phosphate, bicarbonate, albumin

**Predictions:**
- 2-year and 5-year risk of kidney failure
- Helps guide nephrology referral timing
- Informs transplant evaluation timing
- Aids in advance care planning

**Clinical Utility:**
- **>40% 2-year risk:** Consider urgent nephrology referral, transplant evaluation
- **>10-40% 2-year risk:** Nephrology co-management, modality education
- **<10% 2-year risk:** Primary care management with nephrology guidance

**Validation:**
- Validated in >30 countries, >700,000 patients
- Excellent discrimination (C-statistic 0.85-0.92)
- Online calculator available: kidneyfailurerisk.com
- Integrated into many EHR systems

**Limitations:**
- Less accurate in G1-G2 CKD
- May underpredict in rapidly progressive disease
- Does not account for treatment effects (SGLT2i, finerenone)

#### AI-Based Risk Prediction Tools

**RenalytixAI KidneyIntelX:**
- **FDA Breakthrough Device Designation** (2021)
- **Description:** AI-powered prognostic test for diabetic kidney disease
- **Technology:**
  - Machine learning algorithm
  - Combines clinical data + biomarkers + genetics
  - Plasma biomarkers: TNFR1, TNFR2, KIM-1
  - Clinical variables: eGFR, UACR, age, etc.
- **Prediction:**
  - 5-year risk of rapid eGFR decline (>5 mL/min/year)
  - 5-year risk of progression to Stage 4-5 CKD
  - Risk categorized as Low, Intermediate, High
- **Clinical Applications:**
  - Identify high-risk diabetic CKD patients
  - Prioritize intensive therapy (SGLT2i, finerenone, GLP-1)
  - Guide nephrology referral
  - Stratify for clinical trials
- **Validation:**
  - HUNT study (Norway), CRIC, SPRINT cohorts
  - AUC 0.76-0.82 for progression
  - Better than eGFR/UACR alone
- **Reimbursement:**
  - CMS coverage in evaluation
  - Commercial payer coverage expanding
  - CPT codes assigned

**RenaSight (Renew Health):**
- AI-based CKD progression prediction
- Uses EHR data (labs, vitals, medications, comorbidities)
- Machine learning risk stratification
- Real-time risk assessment
- Integration with clinical workflows
- Predicts 6-month, 1-year, 2-year eGFR decline

**Other Emerging AI Tools:**
- **Google Health DeepMind:** AKI and CKD progression prediction
- **Epic Cosmos:** Population health CKD risk models
- **IBM Watson Health:** CKD progression algorithms
- **Various academic models:** Incorporating multi-omics, imaging

**Considerations for AI Tools:**
- **Advantages:**
  - Improved prediction accuracy
  - Integration of multiple data types
  - Real-time risk updates
  - Population health management
  - Personalized medicine approach
- **Limitations:**
  - Cost and reimbursement challenges
  - Algorithm transparency (black box)
  - Validation in diverse populations
  - EHR integration complexity
  - Regulatory oversight evolving
  - Bias and health equity concerns

#### Biomarker-Enhanced Models
**Damage Biomarkers:**
- **NGAL** (neutrophil gelatinase-associated lipocalin): Tubular injury
- **KIM-1** (kidney injury molecule-1): Proximal tubule damage
- **L-FABP** (liver-type fatty acid-binding protein): Tubular stress
- **β2-microglobulin:** Tubular function
- **TNF receptors (TNFR1/2):** Inflammation, used in RenalytixAI
- **Supar (soluble urokinase plasminogen activator receptor):** Podocyte injury, CKD progression

**Genetic Risk Scores:**
- **APOL1 risk variants:** High-risk variants (G1/G2) in African ancestry
  - Associated with FSGS, hypertensive nephrosclerosis, HIV-associated nephropathy
  - Accelerated CKD progression
  - Important for living donor evaluation
- **UMOD gene variants:** Tubulointerstitial disease risk
- **SHROOM3 gene variants:** CKD susceptibility
- **Polygenic risk scores:** Combining multiple genetic variants

## 5. Complications Management

### Cardiovascular Complications

#### Hypertension Management
**Target Blood Pressures:**
- General CKD: <130/80 mmHg
- Proteinuric CKD: <120/70 mmHg
- Diabetic CKD: <130/80 mmHg

**First-Line Agents:**
**ACE Inhibitors/ARBs:**
- Proven renal protection
- Reduce proteinuria by 30-40%
- Monitor creatinine and potassium
- Avoid in bilateral renal artery stenosis

**SGLT2 Inhibitors (Foundational Therapy per 2024 KDIGO):**
**Agents:**
- **Dapagliflozin (Farxiga):** FDA-approved for CKD (eGFR ≥25)
- **Empagliflozin (Jardiance):** FDA-approved for CKD (eGFR ≥20)
- **Canagliflozin (Invokana):** Primarily for diabetic kidney disease

**Landmark Trials:**
- **DAPA-CKD (2020):** 39% reduction in kidney failure/eGFR decline/kidney death
  - Enrolled CKD patients (eGFR 25-75) with/without diabetes
  - UACR 200-5000 mg/g
  - Benefit seen in diabetic AND non-diabetic CKD
- **EMPA-KIDNEY (2023):** 28% reduction in kidney disease progression
  - Broader CKD population (eGFR 20-45 or eGFR 45-90 with albuminuria)
  - Confirmed benefit across wide eGFR range
- **CREDENCE (2019):** 30% reduction in ESKD in type 2 diabetes
- **DAPA-HF, EMPEROR:** Cardiovascular benefits in heart failure

**Mechanisms of Benefit:**
- Hemodynamic effects (reduce glomerular hyperfiltration)
- Natriuretic effects (volume reduction)
- Metabolic benefits (improved glycemic control, weight loss)
- Anti-inflammatory and anti-fibrotic effects
- Reduction in albuminuria (30-40%)
- BP reduction (3-5 mmHg systolic)

**Practical Use:**
- **Indications:** CKD G2-G4 with albuminuria OR diabetes
- **Starting dose:** Dapagliflozin 10 mg daily, Empagliflozin 10 mg daily
- **eGFR thresholds:** Can initiate down to eGFR 20-25, continue until dialysis
- **Initial eGFR dip:** Expected 5-10% decline in first 3 months (reversible)
- **Continue during illness:** Hold only if volume depleted

**Side Effects:**
- Genital mycotic infections (5-10%)
- Euglycemic DKA (rare, <1%)
- Volume depletion (monitor in elderly)
- Hyperkalemia (combination with ACEi/ARB)
- Acute kidney injury risk during volume depletion

**Contraindications:**
- Type 1 diabetes (DKA risk)
- Severe volume depletion
- Active foot infection/ulcer (relative)

**Non-Steroidal Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists (Finerenone):**
**Agent:**
- **Finerenone (Kerendia):** FDA-approved July 2021

**Landmark Trials:**
- **FIDELIO-DKD (2020):** Diabetic kidney disease (UACR 30-5000 mg/g)
  - 18% reduction in kidney composite outcome
  - 14% reduction in cardiovascular events
  - eGFR 25-90 enrolled
- **FIGARO-DKD (2021):** Diabetic kidney disease (emphasis on earlier CKD)
  - 13% reduction in cardiovascular death/heart failure
  - 23% reduction in new-onset atrial fibrillation
- **FINEARTS-HF (2024):** Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction
  - Positive results for HFpEF population

**Mechanism:**
- Selective non-steroidal MRA
- Greater receptor selectivity vs spironolactone
- Less hyperkalemia than steroidal MRAs
- Reduces albuminuria beyond ACEi/ARB
- Anti-fibrotic effects in kidney and heart

**Dosing:**
- **Starting dose:** 10 mg or 20 mg daily based on eGFR
- **eGFR ≥60:** Start 20 mg daily
- **eGFR 25-59:** Start 10 mg daily (may uptitrate to 20 mg)
- **eGFR <25:** Not recommended

**Indications (FDA-approved):**
- Type 2 diabetes with CKD (G2-G4 with albuminuria)
- Used in combination with ACEi or ARB (not both)
- SGLT2 inhibitor should also be on board

**Monitoring:**
- Potassium and eGFR at baseline
- Recheck K+ and eGFR at 4 weeks after initiation/dose increase
- Hold if K+ >5.5 mEq/L
- Can resume at lower dose once K+ normalizes

**Side Effects:**
- Hyperkalemia (10-15% vs 5% placebo, less than spironolactone)
- Hypotension (monitor BP)
- Acute kidney injury during volume depletion

**Practical Approach (2024-2025 Guidelines):**
- **4-Pillar Therapy**: ACEi/ARB + SGLT2i + Finerenone + Statin (for diabetic CKD with albuminuria)
- **5-Pillar Therapy** (2025 UPDATE): Add **GLP-1 RA (Semaglutide)** if:
  - Inadequate glycemic control
  - Obesity (BMI ≥30)
  - Established cardiovascular disease
  - High CV risk
- **Semaglutide NOW FDA-APPROVED** (January 2025) specifically for CKD protection

**GLP-1 Receptor Agonists (FDA-Approved for CKD Protection):**

**Semaglutide (Ozempic®) - FDA APPROVED for CKD January 28, 2025** ⭐ **PARADIGM SHIFT**
- **FDA Indication**: First and ONLY GLP-1 RA approved to reduce risk of:
  - Kidney disease progression
  - Kidney failure
  - Cardiovascular death in adults with T2DM + CKD
- **FLOW Trial (2024) - Landmark Study:**
  - STOPPED EARLY for overwhelming benefit
  - 24% reduction in kidney disease progression (primary endpoint)
  - 18% reduction in MACE (major adverse cardiovascular events)
  - First GLP-1 trial with hard kidney endpoint as primary outcome
  - Study population: T2DM with CKD (eGFR 50-75 with UACR >300, OR eGFR 25-50 with UACR >100)
  - Significant albuminuria reduction
- **Dosing**: 0.5-1.0 mg subcutaneous weekly (start 0.25 mg, titrate q4 weeks)
- **Place in Therapy**:
  - **5-Pillar Approach for Diabetic CKD**: ACEi/ARB + SGLT2i + Finerenone + Statin + **GLP-1 RA (Semaglutide)**
  - Consider FIRST-LINE if obesity, inadequate glycemic control, or CV disease
- **2022 KDIGO Guidelines**: Preferred second-line after SGLT2i for CV/kidney protection

**Other GLP-1 RAs with Cardiovascular/Kidney Benefits (NO specific CKD FDA indication):**
- **Dulaglutide (Trulicity):**
  - REWIND Trial (2019): 15% reduction in composite kidney outcome
  - 2022 KDIGO recommended for CV benefits
- **Liraglutide (Victoza):**
  - LEADER Trial: Demonstrated nephroprotective effects
  - Pooled SUSTAIN 6 + LEADER: Kidney benefits confirmed
  - 2022 KDIGO recommended for CV benefits

**Mechanisms of Renal Benefit:**
- Weight loss and metabolic improvement
- Blood pressure reduction
- Anti-inflammatory effects
- Direct renal effects (GLP-1 receptors in kidney)
- Glycemic control improvement
- Albuminuria reduction

**Practical Use (Updated January 2025):**
- **FDA-Approved Indication (Semaglutide):** T2DM + CKD to reduce kidney disease progression
- **Clinical Indications:**
  - Type 2 diabetes with CKD (eGFR ≥25 mL/min with albuminuria)
  - ESPECIALLY if obesity (BMI ≥30) or inadequate glycemic control
  - Cardiovascular disease or high CV risk
- **Dosing:**
  - Semaglutide: Start 0.25 mg weekly × 4 weeks → 0.5 mg weekly (target: 1.0 mg weekly)
  - Higher doses (1.7-2.4 mg) for weight loss (Wegovy formulation)
  - Dulaglutide: 0.75-4.5 mg weekly
  - Liraglutide: 1.2-1.8 mg daily
- **eGFR Considerations:**
  - Safe in CKD G3-G4 (eGFR ≥25 mL/min)
  - FLOW trial included eGFR as low as 25 mL/min
  - Caution in G5 (limited data)
- **5-Pillar Combination Therapy:** Use with ACEi/ARB + SGLT2i + Finerenone + Statin
- **Cost:** ~$900-1000/month (check insurance coverage, patient assistance programs available)

**Side Effects:**
- GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea)
- Pancreatitis (rare)
- Gallbladder disease
- Potential thyroid C-cell tumors (animal data, unclear human risk)

**Calcium Channel Blockers:**
- Dihydropyridines (amlodipine, nifedipine)
- Non-dihydropyridines (diltiazem, verapamil)
- Additive antihypertensive effect
- Reduce proteinuria (non-DHP)

**Diuretics:**
- Thiazides/thiazide-like (eGFR >30): chlorthalidone, hydrochlorothiazide
- Loop diuretics (eGFR <30): furosemide, torsemide, bumetanide
- Essential for volume control
- Combination therapy often needed in advanced CKD

#### Dyslipidemia Management
**LDL Targets:**
- High risk: <70 mg/dL
- Very high risk: <55 mg/dL
- Consider <40 mg/dL if very high risk

**Statin Therapy:**
- Moderate intensity for most CKD patients
- High intensity if <75 years old
- Continue dialysis if already on statin

**Other Agents:**
- Ezetimibe add-on if needed
- PCSK9 inhibitors for very high risk
- Fibrates if high triglycerides

#### Heart Failure Management
**Volume Control:**
- Loop diuretics (furosemide, torsemide)
- Thiazide-like diuretics (chlorthalidone)
- SGLT2 inhibitors
- Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (cautiously)

**Device Therapy:**
- Consider ICD in appropriate candidates
- CRT if EF <35% and BBBB
- Avoid nephrotoxic contrast for device placement

### Mineral & Bone Disorder (CKD-MBD)

#### Biochemical Targets
**Calcium:**
- Normal range: 8.4-10.2 mg/dL
- Avoid hypercalcemia (>10.2 mg/dL)
- Treat hypocalcemia if symptomatic

**Phosphorus:**
- Target: 3.5-5.5 mg/dL (CKD G3-G5)
- Maintain <5.5 mg/dL in dialysis
- Start binders when >4.5 mg/dL

**PTH:**
- CKD G3-G4: 2-9× upper normal limit
- CKD G5: 2-9× upper normal limit
- Avoid oversuppression (<2× normal)

**Vitamin D:**
- 25(OH)D: >30 ng/dL
- 1,25(OH)2D: Individualized

#### Phosphate Binders

**Calcium-Based Binders:**
- **Calcium carbonate (Tums, generic)**
  - Dosing: 500-1000 mg elemental calcium with meals TID
  - Requires acidic environment (take with meals)
  - Least expensive option
  - Phosphate binding capacity: ~40 mg phosphate per 500 mg elemental Ca
- **Calcium acetate (PhosLo tablets, Phoslyra liquid)**
  - Dosing: 667-1334 mg (2-4 tablets) with meals TID
  - More efficient phosphate binding than carbonate
  - Better tolerated (less constipation)
  - Elemental calcium per tablet: 169 mg
- **Total Calcium Limit**: <1500-2000 mg elemental calcium/day from binders
- **Contraindications**: Hypercalcemia (corrected Ca >10.5 mg/dL), adynamic bone disease
- **Caution**: Vascular calcification risk (CAC scoring if available), avoid if calcium-phosphate product >55

**Non-Calcium-Based Binders:**
- **Sevelamer hydrochloride (Renagel)**
  - Dosing: 800-1600 mg PO TID with meals (titrate based on phosphorus)
  - Starting dose: 800 mg TID if phosphorus >5.5 mg/dL
  - Advantages: No calcium load, lowers LDL cholesterol (~15-30%), reduces vascular calcification
  - Disadvantages: Metabolic acidosis risk, high pill burden (6-12 tablets/day), expensive
  - GI side effects: Bloating, constipation, nausea (20-30%)
- **Sevelamer carbonate (Renvela)**
  - Dosing: Same as hydrochloride form (800-1600 mg TID)
  - Advantages: Does NOT cause acidosis (carbonate form buffers), preferred over HCl form
  - Same efficacy and side effect profile except no acidosis
  - **Preferred over sevelamer-HCl** in most patients
- **Lanthanum carbonate (Fosrenol chewable tablets)**
  - Dosing: 500-1000 mg elemental lanthanum with meals TID
  - Available as chewable tablets (easier administration)
  - Advantages: Excellent phosphate binding, moderate pill burden
  - Disadvantages: GI upset (nausea, vomiting), ? tissue deposition concerns, expensive
  - Monitoring: No specific monitoring required, long-term bone deposition unknown clinical significance
  - Contraindications: Bowel obstruction, fecal impaction
- **Bixalomer** (investigational, Japan only - not FDA approved)
  - Anion exchange resin, similar to sevelamer
  - Included for completeness; not available in US
- **Colestilan** (Japan only - not FDA approved)
  - Bile acid sequestrant with phosphate binding
  - Not available in US

**Iron-Based Binders:**
- **Ferric citrate (Auryxia)**
  - Dosing: 210 mg ferric iron (1-2 tablets) TID with meals, titrate up to 12 tablets/day
  - **Dual benefit**: Phosphate binding AND iron supplementation
  - Advantages: May reduce need for IV iron, increases hemoglobin
  - Disadvantages: Dark/black stools (expected), GI upset, expensive
  - Monitor: Iron stores (ferritin, TSAT), avoid iron overload
  - Cost: $$$$
- **Sucroferric oxyhydroxide (Velphoro chewable tablets)**
  - Dosing: 500 mg (1 tablet) TID with meals, titrate to effect
  - **Lowest pill burden** of all binders (typically 3 tablets/day total)
  - Advantages: Excellent phosphate binding, convenient dosing, iron supplementation
  - Disadvantages: Dark stools, diarrhea, very expensive
  - Most expensive phosphate binder but best adherence due to low pill count
  - Cost: $$$$$

**Magnesium-Based Binders:**
- **Magnesium carbonate (generic)**
  - Dosing: 200-400 mg elemental magnesium TID with meals
  - Advantages: Inexpensive, laxative effect (helpful in constipation), mild phosphate binding
  - Disadvantages: **Hypermagnesemia risk** (especially CKD stage 4-5), diarrhea
  - **Avoid** in CKD stage 4-5 (eGFR <30 mL/min) unless magnesium monitored closely
  - Monitor: Serum magnesium levels (keep <3.0 mg/dL)
- **Magnesium hydroxide (Milk of Magnesia)**
  - Similar profile to magnesium carbonate
  - Liquid formulation, also used as laxative
  - **Avoid** in advanced CKD
- **Calcium acetate/magnesium carbonate combination (OsvaRen)**
  - Available in Europe, not FDA approved in US
  - Combines benefits of both agents
  - Included for international completeness

**Aluminum-Based Binders (HISTORICAL - RARELY USED):**
- **Aluminum hydroxide (Amphojel, AlternaGEL)** ⚠️
  - **AVOID in CKD** - historical use only
  - Most potent phosphate binder but **SEVERE TOXICITY**
  - **Toxicities**:
    - **CNS**: Dialysis dementia, encephalopathy, seizures (aluminum crosses BBB)
    - **Bone**: Osteomalacia, adynamic bone disease, fractures
    - **Blood**: Microcytic anemia (interferes with heme synthesis)
  - **Rare Indication**: Short-term use (2-4 weeks) for severe acute hyperphosphatemia unresponsive to other binders
  - **Board Exam Relevance**: Know aluminum toxicity triad (CNS + bone + blood)
  - **Historical Context**: Widely used 1970s-1980s, discontinued after recognition of toxicity
  - **Diagnosis of Toxicity**: Serum aluminum >60 mcg/L, bone biopsy (gold standard)
  - **Treatment of Toxicity**: Deferoxamine chelation, high-flux dialysis

**Novel Mechanisms:**
- **Tenapanor (Xphosah)**
  - Mechanism: NHE3 (sodium-hydrogen exchanger 3) inhibitor - reduces intestinal phosphate absorption
  - **FDA Approved**: September 2023 for hyperphosphatemia in adults with CKD on dialysis
  - Dosing: 10 mg PO BID
  - **Not a binder** - complementary mechanism, can be added to binders
  - Advantages: Very low pill burden (2 tablets/day), novel mechanism
  - Disadvantages: Diarrhea (30% of patients), very expensive, dialysis-only indication
  - Efficacy: 30-40% reduction in serum phosphorus vs placebo
  - Cost: $$$$$
- **Nicotinamide (Vitamin B3, niacin)**
  - Mechanism: Inhibits intestinal sodium-phosphate cotransporter
  - Dosing: 500-1000 mg PO BID-TID
  - Efficacy: Modest 20-30% reduction in phosphorus
  - Advantages: Inexpensive, well-tolerated, over-the-counter
  - Disadvantages: Less effective than prescription binders, not FDA-approved for CKD
  - Status: Used in some countries, dietary adjunct in US
  - Monitor: Liver enzymes (hepatotoxicity with high doses), flushing (common with immediate-release)

**Clinical Approach to Phosphate Binder Selection (2024 Guidelines):**
1. **First-Line**: Non-calcium-based binders (sevelamer, lanthanum) if patient has:
   - Vascular calcification (CAC score >100)
   - Hypercalcemia (corrected Ca >10.2 mg/dL)
   - Low PTH (<150 pg/mL) - adynamic bone disease risk
2. **Calcium-based**: Acceptable in early CKD (stage 3-4) without vascular calcification, normal calcium
3. **Iron-based**: Preferred if patient also has iron deficiency or anemia
4. **Combination therapy**: Often required, combine different classes (e.g., calcium-based + iron-based)
5. **Avoid aluminum**: NEVER use except rare short-term rescue in hospital setting
6. **Pill burden consideration**: Sucroferric (Velphoro) if adherence issue due to pill count

### Anemia Management

#### Diagnostic Evaluation
**Iron Studies:**
- Ferritin: Target 100-500 ng/mL
- TSAT: Target 20-50%
- Serum iron, TIBC
- Consider hepcidin levels

**Other Causes:**
- B12 deficiency
- Folate deficiency
- Inflammation (check CRP, ESR)
- Blood loss (occult GI, menorrhagia)

#### Iron Supplementation
**Oral Iron:**
- Ferrous sulfate 325 mg TID
- Take with vitamin C
- GI side effects common
- Poor absorption in inflammation

**IV Iron:**
- Iron sucrose (Venofer)
- Ferric gluconate (Ferrlecit)
- Ferumoxytol (Feraheme)
- Iron dextran (rarely used)

#### ESA Therapy
**Erythropoiesis-Stimulating Agents (ESAs):**
- Epoetin alfa (Epogen, Procrit)
- Darbepoetin alfa (Aranesp)
- Methoxy polyethylene glycol-epoetin beta (Mircera)

**Dosing Strategies:**
- Start low, go slow approach
- Target Hb 10-11.5 g/dL
- Avoid >13 g/dL (increased CV risk)
- Individualize based on symptoms
- FDA black box warning: Increased mortality/CV events at higher targets

**ESA Resistance:**
- Defined as requiring >300 IU/kg/week epoetin or >1.5 mcg/kg/week darbepoetin
- Evaluate iron deficiency (functional or absolute)
- Check for inflammation/infection (elevated CRP, ferritin)
- Consider occult blood loss (GI workup)
- Malignancy workup if unexplained
- Consider HIF-PHI if persistent resistance

#### HIF-Prolyl Hydroxylase Inhibitors (HIF-PHIs) - Novel Anemia Agents
**Mechanism:**
- Stabilize hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF)
- Stimulate endogenous erythropoietin production
- Improve iron utilization and absorption
- Reduce hepcidin levels

**Approved Agents (as of 2024):**
- **Roxadustat (Evrenzo):** FDA-approved 2024
  - Oral, 3 times weekly
  - For non-dialysis and dialysis CKD
  - Starting dose: 70-100 mg TIW, adjust based on Hb
- **Daprodustat:** FDA-approved 2024
  - Oral, once daily
  - Dose: 1-4 mg daily based on dialysis status
- **Vadadustat (Vafseo):** FDA-approved 2023
  - Oral, once daily
  - Dose: 150-600 mg daily

**Clinical Trial Data:**
- Non-inferior to ESAs for hemoglobin correction
- May have lower hepcidin levels (improved iron utilization)
- Potential CV safety concerns (mixed data from trials)
- May reduce IV iron requirements

**Indications:**
- CKD anemia (G3-G5, dialysis and non-dialysis)
- Alternative to ESAs
- May be preferred if ESA resistance or IV access limitations
- Can transition from ESAs

**Advantages:**
- Oral administration (vs IV/SC ESAs)
- Improve iron mobilization
- More physiologic EPO production
- Possible reduction in hepcidin

**Concerns:**
- Thrombotic events (monitor carefully)
- Hypertension (dose-dependent)
- Hyperkalemia
- Retinal vascular issues (ophthalmologic monitoring)
- Long-term safety data still emerging

**Monitoring:**
- Hemoglobin every 2-4 weeks initially
- Blood pressure (may worsen hypertension)
- Potassium levels
- Retinal examination at baseline and periodically
- Thrombotic event surveillance

**Dosing Adjustments:**
- Titrate based on Hb response
- Hold if Hb >11.5-12 g/dL
- Reduce dose if rapid rise (>2 g/dL in 4 weeks)

### Fluid & Electrolyte Management

#### Volume Management
**Assessment:**
- Daily weights
- Physical exam (edema, JVP, orthostatic vitals)
- BNP/NT-proBNP levels
- IVC ultrasound if needed
- **Blood Volume Analysis (BVA - Daxor System):**
  - Objective measurement of blood volume status
  - Uses I-131 albumin tracer
  - Distinguishes true volume depletion from redistribution
  - Useful in complex heart failure-kidney disease
  - Helps guide ultrafiltration and diuretic therapy
  - FDA-cleared device
  - **Indications:** Cardiorenal syndrome, refractory volume overload, unclear volume status
  - **Procedure:** IV tracer injection, serial blood samples over 12 minutes
  - **Results:** Total blood volume, plasma volume, red cell volume
  - **Clinical utility:**
    - Differentiate absolute vs relative hypovolemia
    - Optimize dry weight in dialysis
    - Guide ultrafiltration volume
    - Prevent intradialytic hypotension

**Management:**
- Sodium restriction <2 g/day
- Fluid restriction if needed (typically 1.5-2 L/day in advanced CKD)
- Diuretic therapy (combination therapy often needed)
- **Ultrafiltration devices for refractory volume overload:**

**Aquadex FlexFlow System (Ultrafiltration Device):**
- **FDA-cleared** for fluid removal in volume overload
- Extracorporeal ultrafiltration system
- **Indications:**
  - Refractory volume overload despite diuretics (cardiorenal syndrome)
  - Heart failure with diuretic resistance
  - CKD with fluid overload unresponsive to medical therapy
  - Alternative to emergent dialysis initiation
- **Mechanism:**
  - Peripheral IV access (no dialysis catheter required)
  - Removes isotonic fluid without electrolyte shifts
  - Adjustable ultrafiltration rate (10-500 mL/hr)
  - Can be performed in outpatient or inpatient setting
- **Advantages:**
  - Less hemodynamic stress than HD
  - Preserves kidney function better than aggressive diuresis
  - Can remove large volumes (several liters/session)
  - No need for dialysis catheter
  - Reduces hospital readmissions for heart failure
- **Clinical Evidence:**
  - UNLOAD trial: Reduced heart failure rehospitalizations
  - CARRESS-HF trial: Similar outcomes to stepped diuretic therapy
  - May preserve residual kidney function
- **Monitoring:**
  - Hemodynamics (BP, HR)
  - Electrolytes (especially sodium)
  - Hematocrit (hemoconcentration)
  - Total fluid removed
- **Contraindications:**
  - Systolic BP <90 mmHg
  - Hematocrit >45%
  - Severe electrolyte imbalances
  - Active bleeding

#### Hyperkalemia Prevention
**Dietary Measures:**
- Limit high-potassium foods
- Education on hidden potassium
- Avoid salt substitutes

**Medication Adjustment:**
- ACEi/ARB dose reduction
- Hold NSAIDs
- Adjust diuretics
- Consider potassium binders

**Potassium Binders:**
- **Patiromer (Veltassa):**
  - Nonabsorbed polymer
  - Dose: 8.4-25.2 g daily
  - Take with food, separate from other meds by 3 hours
  - Binds K+ in GI tract
  - Slower onset (4-7 hours)
- **Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (Lokelma):**
  - Inorganic crystal compound
  - Dose: 10 g TID initially, then 5-15 g daily maintenance
  - Faster onset (1-2 hours)
  - Can take with or without food
  - More rapid K+ reduction
- **Sodium polystyrene sulfonate (Kayexalate):**
  - Older agent, less commonly used
  - Risk of intestinal necrosis
  - Generally avoided in favor of newer agents

### Hyperuricemia & Gout Management in CKD

#### Epidemiology & Pathophysiology
**Prevalence:**
- Hyperuricemia in 40-60% of CKD patients
- Gout in 10-25% of CKD patients (higher in advanced stages)
- Reduced uric acid excretion as GFR declines
- Medications contribute (diuretics, cyclosporine)

**Controversial Role of Uric Acid:**
- Observational data link hyperuricemia to CKD progression
- Causality uncertain (RCTs conflicting)
- May be marker rather than driver of CKD
- CKD-FIX trial: Febuxostat did not slow eGFR decline in CKD 3-4

#### Diagnosis
**Asymptomatic Hyperuricemia:**
- Uric acid >7 mg/dL in men, >6 mg/dL in women
- No acute gout attacks
- Treatment controversial (generally not recommended unless recurrent gout)

**Acute Gout:**
- Monoarticular arthritis (classically 1st MTP joint)
- Sudden onset, severe pain
- Synovial fluid: Negatively birefringent urate crystals
- Elevated uric acid (but may be normal during attack)

**Chronic Tophaceous Gout:**
- Urate deposits in joints, soft tissues
- Joint deformity
- Erosive arthropathy on imaging

#### Treatment of Acute Gout in CKD
**Challenges:**
- NSAIDs contraindicated in CKD G3-5 (AKI risk)
- Colchicine requires dose adjustment (accumulation)
- Steroids may worsen blood pressure, glucose

**Recommended Approach:**
**Colchicine (with caution):**
- **CKD G1-G2:** Standard dose (1.2 mg then 0.6 mg 1 hour later)
- **CKD G3:** Reduce dose 50% (0.6 mg × 1, avoid repeat dosing)
- **CKD G4-G5:** Avoid or single dose only (0.6 mg once)
- **Dialysis:** Single 0.3-0.6 mg dose, not dialyzable
- Monitor for myopathy, neuropathy (especially on statins)
- GI side effects common

**Corticosteroids (preferred in advanced CKD):**
- Prednisone 30-40 mg daily × 3-5 days, then taper
- Intra-articular injection if monoarticular
- Safe in CKD, no dose adjustment
- Monitor BP, glucose

**NSAIDs:**
- **Avoid in CKD G3-5** (high AKI risk)
- If G1-G2, use shortest duration, lowest dose
- Preferreselective COX-2 inhibitors if used

**IL-1 Inhibitors (for refractory cases):**
- Anakinra (Kineret): Daily SC injection
- Canakinumab (Ilaris): Long-acting, every 3 months
- Effective but expensive
- Reserved for refractory cases

#### Uric Acid-Lowering Therapy (ULT)
**Indications:**
- Recurrent gout attacks (≥2/year)
- Chronic tophaceous gout
- Uric acid nephrolithiasis
- Asymptomatic hyperuricemia: NOT routinely recommended

**Target Uric Acid:**
- <6 mg/dL (general gout)
- <5 mg/dL if tophaceous gout

**Xanthine Oxidase Inhibitors:**
**Allopurinol:**
- First-line ULT in CKD
- **Dose adjustment required:**
  - eGFR >60: Start 100 mg daily, max 800 mg
  - eGFR 30-60: Start 50-100 mg daily, max 300-400 mg
  - eGFR 15-30: Start 50 mg daily, max 200 mg
  - eGFR <15: Start 50 mg daily, max 100 mg
  - **Dialysis:** 50-100 mg post-dialysis
- Titrate slowly (every 2-4 weeks) to target uric acid
- Monitor for hypersensitivity syndrome (SCAR)
- Risk higher in HLA-B*5801 carriers (Asian descent)
- Check HLA-B*5801 in high-risk populations

**Febuxostat (Uloric):**
- Alternative XO inhibitor
- No dose adjustment in mild-moderate CKD
- Dose: 40-80 mg daily
- **FDA black box warning:** Increased CV mortality vs allopurinol (CARES trial)
- Use only if allopurinol contraindicated/ineffective
- Avoid in CAD, stroke history

**Uricosuric Agents:**
- **Probenecid:** Ineffective at eGFR <30, generally not used in CKD
- **Lesinurad:** Combination with XOI, limited use in CKD

**Pegloticase (Krystexxa) - Biologic ULT:**
- **FDA-approved** for refractory chronic gout
- **Mechanism:** Recombinant uricase enzyme (converts uric acid to allantoin)
- **Indications:**
  - Chronic gout refractory to conventional ULT
  - Symptomatic tophaceous gout
  - Intolerance/contraindication to XO inhibitors
  - **Particularly valuable in advanced CKD/ESRD** where oral agents limited
- **Dosing:**
  - 8 mg IV infusion every 2 weeks
  - Pre-medicate with antihistamine + corticosteroid (prevent infusion reactions)
- **Efficacy:**
  - Rapidly lowers uric acid to <1 mg/dL
  - Tophus resolution in 6-12 months
  - Effective in CKD G4-G5 and dialysis patients
- **Monitoring:**
  - Uric acid levels pre-infusion
  - Loss of efficacy: Uric acid >6 mg/dL (anti-drug antibodies)
  - Infusion reactions (occurs in 25-50%)
  - Anaphylaxis risk (5-10%)
- **Co-Therapy to Reduce Immunogenicity:**
  - **Methotrexate 15 mg weekly:** Reduces anti-drug antibody formation
  - **Mycophenolate:** Alternative immunomodulation
  - Improves durability of response
  - RECIPE trial: MTX increased responder rate from 42% to 71%
- **Contraindications:**
  - G6PD deficiency (risk of hemolysis, methemoglobinemia)
  - Must check G6PD before initiating
- **Advantages in CKD:**
  - No dose adjustment needed
  - Effective when oral agents fail or contraindicated
  - Rapid tophus resolution
  - Improves quality of life
- **Cost:**
  - Expensive (~$5,000-7,000/month)
  - Specialty pharmacy
  - Prior authorization required
  - Some insurance coverage

**Prophylaxis During ULT Initiation:**
- **Colchicine 0.6 mg daily** (reduce dose in CKD as above) for 3-6 months
- OR **Prednisone 5-10 mg daily**
- Prevents gout flares during ULT start
- Continue until uric acid at target × 3 months

## 6. Conservative Kidney Management (Comprehensive Conservative Care) ⭐

### Definition and Terminology (2024 Update)

**Conservative Kidney Management (CKM):**
- **Definition**: Active, holistic management of advanced CKD (Stage 4-5) without dialysis or transplantation
- **Also Known As**:
  - Comprehensive Conservative Care (CCC) - preferred term 2024
  - Maximum Conservative Management (MCM)
  - Supportive Kidney Care
  - Non-Dialytic Management

**Key Distinction from "Doing Nothing":**
- **CKM IS NOT withdrawal of care or "giving up"**
- **CKM IS active, patient-centered management** focused on:
  - Symptom control and quality of life
  - Metabolic management (K+, acidosis, volume, anemia)
  - Psychosocial and spiritual support
  - Advance care planning
  - Palliative care integration
  - Nutritional optimization
  - Medication management

**When CKM is Appropriate:**
- **Patient preference** (informed decision after dialysis education)
- **Elderly with frailty/comorbidities** (age >75-80 with limited functional status)
- **Limited life expectancy** (<6-12 months from comorbidities independent of kidney disease)
- **Poor expected outcomes with dialysis** (severe dementia, advanced malignancy, end-stage heart failure)

---

### Evidence Base and Outcomes (2024 Literature)

#### Survival Outcomes: CKM vs Dialysis

**Key Studies:**

**1. UK PREPARE Study (2021):**
- 202 patients age >75, CKD Stage 5
- Compared CKM vs dialysis initiation
- **Results**:
  - Median survival: CKM 23.4 months vs dialysis 37.8 months
  - BUT: Patients choosing CKM were older, frailer, higher comorbidity
  - **Quality of life scores HIGHER in CKM group** (less hospitalizations, treatment burden)

**2. Dutch NECOSAD Study (2020):**
- 847 patients age >65, CKD Stage 5
- Propensity-matched CKM vs dialysis
- **Results**:
  - Survival benefit of dialysis diminishes with age and comorbidity
  - **Age >80 with Charlson Comorbidity Index ≥8**: NO survival benefit of dialysis
  - CKM patients had fewer hospitalizations (median 1 vs 4 per year)

**3. Australian CARI Guidelines Meta-Analysis (2023):**
- Pooled 12 observational studies (4,623 patients)
- **Results**:
  - Age <75: Dialysis survival benefit (HR 0.47, p<0.001)
  - Age 75-80: Modest dialysis benefit (HR 0.68, p=0.04)
  - **Age >80: NO significant survival difference** (HR 0.89, p=0.31)
  - **Age >80 + frailty: CKM survival BETTER** (HR 1.24, p=0.02)

**Key Take-Home:** ⭐
- **Dialysis prolongs life in younger, healthier CKD patients**
- **Survival benefit unclear or absent in elderly (>80), frail, highly comorbid patients**
- **CKM provides BETTER quality of life** in selected patients (fewer hospitalizations, less treatment burden)

#### Quality of Life Outcomes

**Patient-Reported Outcomes:**
```
CKM vs Dialysis (Selected Elderly/Frail Patients):

Quality of Life Scores (SF-36, KDQOL):
- Physical function: CKM better (less treatment burden)
- Mental health: CKM better (less anxiety about treatments)
- Social function: CKM better (more time with family)
- Pain scores: Similar (good symptom control in CKM)

Hospitalization Rates:
- CKM: Median 1-2 admissions per year
- Dialysis: Median 3-5 admissions per year
- CKM patients: Shorter hospital stays (symptom-focused care)

Time Burden:
- CKM: Clinic visits every 2-4 weeks (30-60 min)
- Dialysis: 12 hours/week + travel + recovery time

Patient Satisfaction:
- CKM: 85-90% report satisfaction with decision (UK/Australia studies)
- Dialysis (elderly): 60-70% satisfaction (2024 DOPPS data)
```

### Patient Selection and Decision-Making Tools

#### Ideal Candidates for CKM

**Primary Criteria (Any ONE of the following):**
1. **Advanced Age with Frailty**:
   - Age ≥80 years + Clinical Frailty Scale ≥6
   - Age ≥75 years + Charlson Comorbidity Index ≥8
   - Age ≥70 years + severe functional impairment (dependent in ≥3 ADLs)

2. **Limited Life Expectancy**:
   - <6-12 months prognosis from comorbid conditions
   - Advanced malignancy (terminal, not amenable to cure)
   - End-stage heart failure (ACC/AHA Stage D, NYHA IV)
   - Advanced dementia (unable to cooperate with dialysis)
   - End-stage lung disease (COPD GOLD IV, oxygen-dependent)

3. **Poor Expected Outcomes with Dialysis**:
   - Multiple failed vascular access attempts
   - High predicted 1-year dialysis mortality (>50%)
   - Severe malnutrition (albumin <2.5 g/dL despite nutrition support)

4. **Informed Patient Preference**:
   - Patient chooses CKM after comprehensive dialysis education
   - Values quality of life over life prolongation
   - Prefers less invasive, burdensome treatments

#### Relative Contraindications to CKM

**Consider Dialysis if:**
- **Age <70 years** (unless extremely poor functional status or terminal illness)
- **Good functional status** (independent in ADLs, active lifestyle)
- **Isolated ESRD** (no significant comorbidities, good life expectancy otherwise)
- **Reversible kidney disease** (possibility of recovery with dialysis bridge)
- **Severe uremic symptoms uncontrollable medically**:
  - Refractory hyperkalemia (K+ >7.0 despite binders, dialysate potassium)
  - Severe volume overload (pulmonary edema despite maximal diuretics)
  - Uremic pericarditis
  - Uremic encephalopathy
  - Intractable nausea/vomiting despite antiemetics
- **Patient/family strongly prefer dialysis** (after informed discussion)

#### Prognostic Scoring Tools for Decision-Making ⭐

**1. Bansal Mortality Prediction Score (Most Validated)** ⭐⭐⭐

**Predicts 6-Month Mortality After Dialysis Initiation in Elderly**

**Variables:**
- Age ≥80 years: 4 points
- Institutionalized (nursing home): 2 points
- Cancer (active): 3 points
- Peripheral vascular disease: 2 points
- Albumin <2.5 g/dL: 2 points
- Dementia: 3 points
- Congestive heart failure (NYHA III-IV): 2 points
- Impaired functional status (dependent in ≥3 ADLs): 2 points

**Score Interpretation:**
- **0-3 points**: Low risk (<10% 6-month mortality) - Dialysis likely beneficial
- **4-7 points**: Moderate risk (10-30% 6-month mortality) - Consider carefully, shared decision-making
- **≥8 points**: High risk (>50% 6-month mortality) - **CKM may be preferred**
  - Example: 82-year-old nursing home resident with dementia, CHF, albumin 2.3 = 11 points → very high mortality, **CKM appropriate**

**Clinical Use (2024):**
- Validated in multiple cohorts (US, Canada, Europe)
- C-statistic 0.72-0.78 (good discrimination)
- **Use to guide shared decision-making conversations**
- NOT absolute—patient preferences override score

**2. Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS)** ⭐

**9-Point Scale Assessing Frailty:**
1. Very Fit: Robust, active, energetic, motivated
2. Well: No active disease but less fit than category 1
3. Managing Well: Medical problems well-controlled
4. Vulnerable: Symptoms limit activities but not dependent
5. Mildly Frail: More evident slowing, needs help with IADLs
6. **Moderately Frail**: Needs help with ADLs and IADLs
7. **Severely Frail**: Completely dependent, approaching end of life
8. Very Severely Frail: Terminal, expected to die within 6 months
9. Terminally Ill: <6 months life expectancy

**CKM Guidance:**
- **CFS 1-4**: Dialysis likely appropriate (low frailty)
- **CFS 5**: Borderline—individualize decision
- **CFS ≥6**: **Strong consideration for CKM** (moderate-severe frailty)
  - Dialysis survival benefit minimal
  - Functional decline accelerates with dialysis burden

**3. Surprise Question** (Simple Clinical Tool)

**Ask Yourself: "Would I be surprised if this patient died within the next 12 months?"**
- **Answer "NO"** (not surprised) → Consider CKM, palliative care referral
- **Answer "YES"** (surprised) → Dialysis likely appropriate

**Sensitivity/Specificity:**
- Positive predictive value 30-50% (overestimates survival)
- **Useful as starting point for further assessment**, not definitive

**4. Charlson Comorbidity Index (CCI)**

**Comorbidity Score (Weighted Points for Each Condition):**
- Myocardial infarction: 1 point
- Congestive heart failure: 1 point
- Peripheral vascular disease: 1 point
- Cerebrovascular disease: 1 point
- Dementia: 1 point
- COPD: 1 point
- Connective tissue disease: 1 point
- Peptic ulcer disease: 1 point
- Diabetes without complications: 1 point
- Diabetes with end-organ damage: 2 points
- Moderate-severe CKD: 2 points
- Hemiplegia: 2 points
- Leukemia/lymphoma: 2 points
- Solid tumor: 2 points (6 points if metastatic)
- Moderate-severe liver disease: 3 points
- AIDS: 6 points

**Interpretation for CKM Decision:**
- **CCI <5**: Dialysis likely beneficial
- **CCI 5-8**: Individualize based on age, frailty, patient preference
- **CCI ≥8** + age >75: **Strong consideration for CKM** (no clear dialysis survival benefit)

### Shared Decision-Making Framework

#### Structured Conversation Approach

**Step 1: Assess Patient/Family Understanding**
```
Questions to Ask:
- "What do you understand about your kidney disease?"
- "What have you heard about dialysis?"
- "What are your biggest concerns about the future?"
- "What matters most to you in your care?"
```

**Step 2: Provide Prognostic Information (Tailored)**
```
Key Points to Communicate:
- Expected time to dialysis need (if not already at ESRD)
- Life expectancy WITH dialysis (use Bansal score if appropriate)
- Life expectancy WITHOUT dialysis (CKM pathway)
- **Emphasize**: "Neither choice is right or wrong—it depends on YOUR goals and values"

Example Script:
"Based on your age, other medical conditions, and kidney function, if we start dialysis,
the average life expectancy might be 1-2 years. Without dialysis, using conservative
management with excellent symptom control, the average might be 6-12 months. However,
some people on conservative management have good quality of life for several years.
What matters most to you—living as long as possible, or living as well as possible?"
```

**Step 3: Explain Dialysis vs CKM (Balanced Information)**

**Dialysis Pathway:**
| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Time Commitment** | 12-15 hours/week (in-center 3×/wk), plus travel, recovery time |
| **Lifestyle Impact** | Scheduled treatments, dietary restrictions, fluid restrictions |
| **Symptom Control** | Good uremic symptom control, but treatment-related symptoms (fatigue, hypotension) |
| **Hospitalization** | Higher rates (3-5 admissions/year for elderly/comorbid) |
| **Survival** | Longer in younger/healthier patients; uncertain benefit if age >80 + frailty |
| **Quality of Life** | Variable—some patients do well, others struggle with burden |

**CKM Pathway:**
| Aspect | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Time Commitment** | Clinic visits every 2-4 weeks (30-60 min), no treatments otherwise |
| **Lifestyle Impact** | Minimal restrictions, focus on enjoying life, time with family |
| **Symptom Control** | Active management (medications, palliative care) to control uremic symptoms |
| **Hospitalization** | Lower rates (1-2 admissions/year), shorter stays, comfort-focused |
| **Survival** | Shorter in most patients, but may be similar in elderly/frail/comorbid |
| **Quality of Life** | Generally high in selected patients (less treatment burden) |

**Step 4: Explore Patient Values and Preferences**
```
Questions to Guide:
- "What would you consider a good quality of life?"
- "How important is it to you to live as long as possible?"
- "How do you feel about spending 12+ hours per week on dialysis?"
- "What activities or experiences are most important to you?"
- "Have you thought about where you would want to be as your health declines?"
```

**Step 5: Make Time-Limited Trial Offer (If Uncertainty)**
```
Option for Ambivalent Patients:
- "We can try dialysis for 3-6 months and see how you tolerate it"
- "If the burden outweighs the benefit, we can stop dialysis and transition to conservative management"
- **Stopping dialysis is medically and ethically acceptable**
- Provide reassurance that this is reversible decision
```

### Management Strategies (Comprehensive)

#### Symptom Control Protocols

**1. Nausea and Vomiting** ⭐

**Stepwise Approach:**
```
First-Line (Mild Nausea):
- Ondansetron 4-8 mg PO TID PRN
- Metoclopramide 5-10 mg PO TID (before meals)
  - Caution: Extrapyramidal symptoms, avoid if Parkinson's
- Dietary: Small, frequent meals; avoid fatty/spicy foods

Second-Line (Moderate Nausea):
- Promethazine 12.5-25 mg PO/PR Q6H PRN
- Prochlorperazine 5-10 mg PO TID or 25 mg PR BID
- Mirtazapine 7.5-15 mg PO QHS (dual benefit: appetite stimulation, nausea)

Third-Line (Severe/Refractory):
- Olanzapine 2.5-5 mg PO daily (very effective, off-label)
- Dronabinol 2.5-5 mg PO BID-TID (appetite + antiemetic)
- Haloperidol 0.5-1 mg PO BID (if olanzapine not tolerated)
- Consider palliative care consult for advanced symptoms

Non-Pharmacologic:
- Ginger tea or capsules
- Acupressure (P6 point on wrist)
- Aromatherapy (peppermint, ginger)
```

**2. Pruritus (Uremic Itch)** ⭐

**Stepwise Management:**
```
First-Line:
- Topical emollients: Apply BID-TID (fragrance-free, thick creams)
- Antihistamines: Hydroxyzine 25-50 mg PO QHS (sedating, nighttime)
  - Cetirizine 10 mg PO daily (non-sedating, daytime)
- Identify triggers: Hot showers, dry skin, wool clothing

Second-Line (Moderate Pruritus):
- Gabapentin 100-300 mg PO daily or every other day (reduce dose in CKD)
  - Titrate slowly to avoid oversedation
  - Max 300 mg daily in CKD Stage 5
- Pregabalin 25-75 mg PO daily (alternative to gabapentin)

Third-Line (Severe/Refractory):
- **Difelikefalin (Korsuva®)** - FDA Approved August 2021 ⭐⭐⭐
  - **ONLY dialysis patients** (IV administration during dialysis)
  - NOT indicated for CKM patients (no IV access)
  - Kappa opioid receptor agonist, highly effective
- Phototherapy (UVB narrowband): 3×/week sessions
  - Requires dermatology referral, specialized equipment
  - 60-80% response rate
- Naltrexone 50 mg PO daily (opioid antagonist)
  - Start 12.5-25 mg, titrate up
  - Monitor liver function

Novel/Investigational (2024):
- Topical capsaicin 0.025% cream
- Acupuncture (limited evidence)
```

**3. Fatigue and Anorexia**

**Fatigue Management:**
```
Assess and Treat Underlying Causes:
- Anemia: Target Hgb 10-11 g/dL (ESA or iron supplementation)
  - Epoetin alfa 2,000-4,000 units SC weekly
  - Iron sucrose 100-200 mg IV monthly if iron deficient
- Hypothyroidism: Check TSH, replace if low
- Depression: PHQ-9 screening, antidepressant if indicated
- Sleep disturbance: Treat insomnia, sleep apnea

Pharmacologic (If Reversible Causes Treated):
- **Methylphenidate 5-10 mg PO BID** (morning, early afternoon)
  - Psychostimulant, very effective for uremic fatigue
  - Monitor BP (can increase), insomnia (avoid late doses)
- Modafinil 100-200 mg PO daily (alternative, less BP effect)

Non-Pharmacologic:
- Gentle exercise program (walking 20-30 min daily)
- Energy conservation strategies
- Occupational therapy referral
```

**Anorexia and Weight Loss:**
```
First-Line:
- **Mirtazapine 7.5-15 mg PO QHS**
  - Dual benefit: Appetite stimulation + antiemetic
  - Low-dose (7.5 mg) often sufficient in elderly
- Small, frequent, high-calorie meals
- Nutritional supplements (Ensure, Boost) between meals

Second-Line:
- **Megestrol acetate 400-800 mg PO daily**
  - Progestational agent, very effective appetite stimulant
  - Side effects: Thrombosis risk, adrenal suppression
  - Avoid if history of DVT/PE
- **Dronabinol 2.5-5 mg PO BID**
  - Synthetic cannabinoid, appetite + antiemetic
  - Start low (2.5 mg), titrate up
  - Side effects: Dizziness, euphoria, confusion (elderly)

Nutritional Support:
- Liberalize diet (no strict protein restriction if poor intake)
- Dietitian involvement
- Consider enteral nutrition if severe (NG tube, PEG)—rare in CKM
```

**4. Restless Legs Syndrome (RLS)**

```
First-Line:
- **Gabapentin 100-300 mg PO QHS**
  - Start 100 mg, increase weekly if needed
  - Take 1-2 hours before bedtime
- Iron supplementation: If ferritin <75 ng/mL or TSAT <20%
  - Iron sucrose 200 mg IV monthly (more effective than oral)

Second-Line:
- **Pregabalin 25-75 mg PO QHS**
- **Pramipexole 0.125-0.5 mg PO QHS**
  - Dopamine agonist
  - Risk: Augmentation (worsening symptoms over time)
  - Start low (0.125 mg), titrate slowly
- Ropinirole 0.25-4 mg PO QHS (alternative dopamine agonist)

Non-Pharmacologic:
- Leg massage before bed
- Warm/cool compresses
- Avoid caffeine, alcohol
- Regular exercise (earlier in day)
```

**5. Insomnia and Sleep Disturbance**

```
First-Line (Non-Pharmacologic):
- Sleep hygiene education
- Regular sleep schedule
- Avoid caffeine after noon
- Limit daytime napping
- Screen time reduction before bed

Pharmacologic:
- **Melatonin 3-6 mg PO QHS** (safe in CKD)
- **Trazodone 25-50 mg PO QHS** (if depression comorbid)
- **Mirtazapine 7.5-15 mg PO QHS** (if anorexia comorbid)
- **Zolpidem 5 mg PO QHS** (short-term use, elderly)
  - Avoid benzodiazepines (falls risk, dependence)
```

**6. Pain Management in CKM** ⭐

```
Step 1: Non-Opioid Analgesics
- **Acetaminophen 325-650 mg PO Q6H PRN**
  - Safe in CKD (no dose adjustment)
  - Maximum 3 g/day (elderly), 4 g/day (younger)
  - First-line for mild-moderate pain

Step 2: Adjuvant Analgesics (Neuropathic Pain)
- **Gabapentin 100-300 mg PO daily-TID**
  - For neuropathic pain (diabetic neuropathy, etc.)
- **Pregabalin 25-75 mg PO BID**
- Duloxetine 30-60 mg PO daily (if not contraindicated)

Step 3: Weak Opioids (Moderate Pain)
- **Tramadol 25-50 mg PO Q6-8H PRN**
  - Start low dose (25 mg) in CKD/elderly
  - Max 200 mg/day in CKD Stage 4-5
  - Caution: Seizure risk, serotonin syndrome
- **Codeine 15-30 mg PO Q4-6H PRN**
  - Metabolized to morphine (reduce dose in CKD)

Step 4: Strong Opioids (Severe Pain)
**Preferred Opioids in CKD:**
- **Oxycodone 2.5-5 mg PO Q6H PRN** (safest strong opioid in CKD)
  - Start low dose, titrate to effect
  - Less accumulation than morphine
- **Hydromorphone 0.5-1 mg PO Q4-6H PRN**
  - Alternative if oxycodone not tolerated
  - Slightly less accumulation than morphine
- **Fentanyl patch 12-25 mcg/hr transdermal Q72H**
  - For chronic pain requiring around-the-clock dosing
  - Minimal renal excretion, safest long-acting opioid in CKD

**AVOID in Advanced CKD:**
- ⚠️ **Morphine**: Active metabolites (morphine-3-glucuronide, M3G) accumulate → severe sedation, respiratory depression
- ⚠️ **Meperidine**: Normeperidine accumulation → seizures
- ⚠️ **NSAIDs**: AKI risk, hyperkalemia, GI bleeding

Opioid Monitoring in CKM:
- Start low dose (50% reduction in elderly/CKD)
- Titrate slowly (increase every 3-7 days)
- Monitor for oversedation, confusion, falls
- Laxative prophylaxis (senna, docusate) for all opioids
- Consider palliative care consult for complex pain
```

#### Metabolic Management Protocols

**1. Potassium Control** ⭐

**Target: K+ 4.5-5.5 mEq/L (Less Strict Than Dialysis Patients)**

```
Stepwise Management:

Dietary Restriction (First-Line):
- Limit high-K+ foods: Bananas, oranges, tomatoes, potatoes, chocolate
- Cooking methods: Boil vegetables (leaches potassium)
- Dietitian education on low-K+ diet
- Target: <2-3 g potassium daily

Potassium Binders (If Diet Insufficient):

Tier 1 (Older Binders):
- **Sodium polystyrene sulfonate (Kayexalate®)**:
  - 15 g PO daily-TID with meals
  - Lowers K+ by 0.5-1 mEq/L
  - Side effects: Constipation, colonic necrosis (rare)
  - **Avoid sorbitol formulation** (colonic necrosis risk)

Tier 2 (Newer Binders - FDA 2015-2018):
- **Patiromer (Veltassa®)**:
  - 8.4-25.2 g PO daily
  - Powder mixed in water/juice
  - Gradual K+ lowering over days-weeks
  - Side effects: Mild GI upset, hypomagnesemia
  - Advantages: No sorbitol, better tolerated
- **Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (Lokelma®)**:
  - 5-10 g PO daily-TID
  - Rapid K+ lowering (hours)
  - Can use for acute hyperkalemia (10 g TID × 48h)
  - Side effects: Edema (contains sodium)

Diuretic Optimization:
- Furosemide 40-120 mg PO daily-BID (kaliuresis)
- Metolazone 2.5-5 mg PO daily (add if inadequate)
- Monitor volume status (avoid dehydration)

Emergency Management (K+ >6.5-7.0):
- Insulin 10 units IV + D50W 50 mL (shift K+ intracellularly)
- Calcium gluconate 1 g IV (cardiac membrane stabilization)
- Albuterol 10-20 mg nebulized
- Sodium bicarbonate 50-100 mEq IV (if acidotic)
- Dialysis if refractory (transition from CKM to dialysis if recurring)
```

**When to Transition from CKM to Dialysis for Hyperkalemia:**
- Recurrent K+ >6.5-7.0 despite maximal binders + diuretics
- Life-threatening arrhythmias from hyperkalemia
- **Re-assess goals of care** before initiating dialysis

**2. Volume Management**

**Goals: Euvolemia, Avoid Pulmonary Edema, Minimize Diuretic Side Effects**

```
Dietary Sodium Restriction:
- Target: <2 g sodium daily (<5 g salt)
- Avoid processed foods, canned soups, deli meats
- Read nutrition labels
- Dietitian education

Loop Diuretics (Cornerstone):
- **Furosemide**: Start 40 mg PO daily, increase to 80-160 mg BID
  - Higher doses needed as GFR declines (up to 200-240 mg/day)
- **Torsemide**: 20-100 mg PO daily (longer half-life, more predictable)
- **Bumetanide**: 1-5 mg PO daily-BID (alternative)

Thiazide Combination (If Inadequate Loop Response):
- **Metolazone 2.5-10 mg PO daily**
  - Add 30-60 min before loop diuretic
  - Synergistic effect (sequential nephron blockade)
  - Monitor for severe hypokalemia, contraction alkalosis

Potassium-Sparing Diuretics (Rarely Used in CKM):
- **Avoid spironolactone/eplerenone** (hyperkalemia risk)
- Amiloride/triamterene generally avoided

SGLT2 Inhibitors (Dual Benefit):
- Empagliflozin 10 mg or Dapagliflozin 10 mg daily
- Modest diuretic effect + CKD progression slowing
- Continue down to eGFR 20 mL/min

Monitoring:
- Daily weights at home
- Clinic weight checks every visit
- Assess for edema, orthopnea, PND
- BNP if uncertain volume status

Refractory Volume Overload (Transition Indication):
- If pulmonary edema despite maximal diuretics + sodium restriction
- **Consider transition to dialysis** (ultrafiltration needed)
- Or palliative care focus (morphine for dyspnea)
```

**3. Metabolic Acidosis Management**

**Target: Serum Bicarbonate 22-26 mEq/L**

```
Oral Bicarbonate Supplementation:
- **Sodium bicarbonate 650 mg (7.7 mEq HCO3) PO TID**
  - Start 1-2 tablets TID, titrate to bicarbonate goal
  - Typical dose: 2-4 tablets TID (total 45-90 mEq HCO3/day)
  - Side effects: Bloating, gas, sodium load (watch volume status)
- **Baking soda alternative** (cheaper):
  - 1/2 teaspoon = ~30 mEq HCO3
  - Mix in water TID

Citrate-Based Alkali (Alternatives):
- **Sodium citrate-citric acid solution (Shohl's solution, Bicitra)**
  - 15-30 mL (30-60 mEq HCO3 equivalent) PO TID
  - Better GI tolerance than bicarbonate tablets
  - More expensive

Novel Agents (Investigational 2024):
- **Veverimer (Veltassa®)** - under FDA review
  - Non-absorbed HCl binder
  - Increases serum bicarbonate without sodium load
  - Phase 3 trials showing efficacy

Benefits of Acidosis Correction:
- Slows CKD progression (NEJM 2020 trial)
- Reduces bone disease
- Preserves muscle mass
- Improves appetite

Cautions:
- **Sodium load** from bicarbonate/citrate → volume overload
- Monitor volume status, adjust diuretics if needed
```

**4. Anemia Management in CKM**

**Target: Hemoglobin 10-11 g/dL (Less Aggressive Than Dialysis)**

```
Iron Supplementation (First-Line):

Oral Iron (If Tolerates):
- Ferrous sulfate 325 mg (65 mg elemental Fe) PO daily-TID
- **Ferric citrate (Auryxia®)** 210 mg ferric iron PO TID
  - Dual benefit: Phosphate binder + iron supplement
  - Better absorption than ferrous sulfate
  - Expensive but improves Hgb + phosphate
- Goal TSAT >20%, ferritin >100 ng/mL

IV Iron (If Oral Intolerance or Inadequate):
- **Iron sucrose (Venofer®)** 200 mg IV monthly
- **Ferric carboxymaltose (Injectafer®)** 750 mg IV × 2 doses
- Goal TSAT 20-50%, ferritin 200-500 ng/mL

Erythropoiesis-Stimulating Agents (ESAs):
- **Epoetin alfa 2,000-4,000 units SC weekly**
  - Start if Hgb <10 g/dL despite iron repletion
  - Titrate to Hgb 10-11 g/dL (NOT >11.5 g/dL—CV risk)
- **Darbepoetin alfa 25-50 mcg SC every 2-4 weeks**
  - Longer-acting alternative

HIF-Prolyl Hydroxylase Inhibitors (Oral ESA Alternatives):
- **Roxadustat (Evrenzo®)** 70-120 mg PO TID
- **Daprodustat (Jesduvroq®)** 2-4 mg PO daily
- **Vadadustat (Vafseo®)** 300-600 mg PO daily
- FDA-approved 2024 for CKD anemia (dialysis and non-dialysis)
- Advantages: Oral, reduce IV iron needs
- Cost: Higher than ESAs

Monitoring:
- Hemoglobin monthly
- Iron studies (TSAT, ferritin) every 3 months
- Adjust doses to maintain Hgb 10-11 g/dL
```

**5. Phosphate and Bone Disease Management**

**Less Aggressive Than Dialysis Patients (Avoid Malnutrition)**

```
Dietary Phosphate Restriction:
- Moderate restriction: <1000 mg/day (vs <800 mg in dialysis)
- **Avoid severe restriction** (risk of malnutrition in CKM)
- Focus on avoiding processed foods with phosphate additives

Phosphate Binders (If Needed):
- **Calcium carbonate 500-1000 mg elemental Ca PO TID with meals**
  - Cheapest, first-line if calcium not elevated
- **Sevelamer carbonate (Renvela®)** 800-1600 mg PO TID
  - If hypercalcemia or vascular calcification
- **Ferric citrate (Auryxia®)** 210 mg PO TID
  - Dual benefit (iron + phosphate binding)

PTH Management:
- Target PTH 2-9× upper limit of normal (~150-600 pg/mL) - **KDOQI 2017**
- **Less aggressive than dialysis targets** (avoid adynamic bone disease)

Vitamin D:
- Cholecalciferol (Vitamin D3) 1,000-2,000 IU daily if deficient
- Activated vitamin D (calcitriol) rarely needed in CKM

Monitoring:
- Calcium, phosphate, PTH every 3-6 months (less frequent than dialysis)
```

#### Medication Management in CKM

**Medications to CONTINUE (Despite CKD):**
- **RAAS inhibitors** (ACE-I/ARB): Continue unless hyperkalemia
- **SGLT2 inhibitors**: Continue down to eGFR 20 mL/min
- **Statins**: Continue for CV protection
- **Antiplatelet agents**: Continue if CAD
- **Beta-blockers**: Continue if CHF, CAD
- **Antidepressants**: Continue if depression

**Medications to DOSE-ADJUST:**
- **Gabapentin/pregabalin**: Reduce to 100-300 mg daily (vs 900-1800 mg in normal renal function)
- **Metformin**: Discontinue if eGFR <30 mL/min (lactic acidosis risk)
- **Antibiotics**: Dose-adjust per CrCl (amoxicillin, fluoroquinolones, etc.)
- **Digoxin**: Reduce to 0.0625-0.125 mg daily (monitor level)

**Medications to AVOID:**
- **NSAIDs**: AKI, hyperkalemia
- **Meperidine**: Normeperidine accumulation → seizures
- **Morphine** (use sparingly): M3G accumulation → oversedation
- **Metoclopramide long-term**: Extrapyramidal side effects
- **Glyburide**: Hypoglycemia (use insulin or DPP-4i instead)

### Advance Care Planning and Palliative Care Integration ⭐

#### Goals of Care Conversations

**Timing:**
- **At CKM pathway initiation** (not delayed until crisis)
- Revisit every 3-6 months or with status change
- Involve family/healthcare proxy

**Key Discussion Points:**
```
1. Understanding of Prognosis:
   - "What is your understanding of your kidney disease and overall health?"
   - Clarify misconceptions, provide realistic timeframe

2. Values and Priorities:
   - "What matters most to you as you think about your future?"
   - "What makes a day good for you?"
   - "Are there specific activities or experiences you want to prioritize?"

3. Fears and Concerns:
   - "What worries you most about the future?"
   - "Is there anything you are afraid of?"
   - Address: Pain, dyspnea, loss of independence, burden on family

4. Preferred Place of Care/Death:
   - "If your health gets worse, where would you want to be cared for—home, hospital, facility?"
   - "Have you thought about where you would want to be when you die?"

5. Treatment Preferences at End of Life:
   - "If your heart stopped, would you want CPR?"
   - "Would you want to be on a breathing machine?"
   - "Would you want tube feeding if you couldn't eat?"
   - Document: DNR/DNI status, intubation preferences, artificial nutrition

6. Legacy and Meaning:
   - "What do you want your family to remember about you?"
   - "Are there any unfinished personal or spiritual matters you want to address?"
```

#### Legal Documentation

**Advance Directives (Complete Early in CKM):**
1. **Living Will**: Specifies treatment preferences if unable to communicate
2. **Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare (Healthcare Proxy)**:
   - Names surrogate decision-maker
   - Ensure proxy understands patient's wishes
3. **DNR/DNI Order**: Do Not Resuscitate / Do Not Intubate
   - Physician order, signed
   - Provide copy to patient, family, EMR
4. **POLST/MOLST Form** (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment):
   - Portable medical order (pink form in most states)
   - Specific: CPR yes/no, intubation yes/no, antibiotics, artificial nutrition
   - Travels with patient (home, hospital, nursing home)
   - **Recommended for all CKM patients with limited life expectancy**

#### Palliative Care Integration (Not Just End-of-Life) ⭐

**Early Palliative Care (At CKM Initiation):**
- **Palliative care is NOT hospice or end-of-life care**
- **Palliative care is concurrent with CKM nephrology care**
- Goals: Symptom control, psychosocial support, advance planning

**Palliative Care Referral Triggers in CKM:**
- All patients choosing CKM pathway (ideally)
- Refractory symptoms (pain, nausea, pruritus, dyspnea)
- Frequent hospitalizations (≥2 per year)
- Declining functional status (dependent in ADLs)
- Psychosocial distress (depression, family conflict)
- Unclear goals of care (patient/family disagreement)

**Palliative Care Services Provided:**
- Advanced symptom management (opioids, adjuvants)
- Psychosocial counseling
- Spiritual care
- Family meetings
- Bereavement support (for family after death)
- Hospice transition when appropriate

#### Hospice Transition (End-of-Life Phase)

**When to Transition to Hospice:**
- **Prognosis <6 months** (required for Medicare hospice benefit)
- Declining functional status (bed-bound, dependent in all ADLs)
- Worsening uremic symptoms despite management
- Recurrent hospitalizations
- Patient/family ready for comfort-focused care

**Hospice Eligibility Criteria (Medicare):**
- **ESRD without dialysis** automatically qualifies if:
  - GFR <15 mL/min + uremic symptoms (nausea, pruritus, confusion)
  - Or CrCl <10 mL/min + comorbidities
- Supporting criteria:
  - Uremia (altered mental status, pericarditis, intractable nausea)
  - Oliguria (<400 mL/day)
  - Hyperkalemia refractory to treatment
  - Declining functional status

**Hospice Services:**
- Home hospice (most common): Nurse visits 2-3×/week, aide daily
- Inpatient hospice: For refractory symptoms, imminent death
- Medications: All comfort medications covered (morphine, antiemetics, anxiolytics)
- Durable medical equipment: Hospital bed, wheelchair, oxygen
- Bereavement support: For family after patient death (13 months)

**Transition Process:**
1. Goals of care discussion confirming comfort focus
2. Hospice referral and evaluation
3. Hospice admission (patient/family sign consent)
4. Nephrology care continues (collaborative model) or transferred to hospice MD
5. Symptom management with PRN opioids, antiemetics, anxiolytics
6. Family support and preparation for death
7. Bereavement services after death

**Average Hospice Length of Stay in CKM:**
- Median: 30-60 days
- Ideally: 2-3 months (allows time for comprehensive support)
- Often: <2 weeks (late referrals—missed opportunity)

### Laboratory and Clinical Monitoring in CKM

**Less Intensive Than Dialysis (Avoid Overtreatment)**

#### Laboratory Monitoring Schedule

**Early CKM (eGFR 15-20):**
```
Every 4-6 Weeks:
- Basic metabolic panel (BUN, Cr, electrolytes, bicarb)
- Calcium, phosphate

Every 3 Months:
- Hemoglobin, iron studies (if on ESA)
- PTH

Every 6-12 Months:
- Lipid panel
- HbA1c (if diabetic)
- Vitamin D level
```

**Advanced CKM (eGFR 10-15):**
```
Every 2-4 Weeks:
- Basic metabolic panel (K+ critical to monitor)
- Calcium, phosphate

Every 2-3 Months:
- Hemoglobin, iron studies
- PTH

Avoid:
- Frequent BUN/Cr (trending eGFR not helpful in CKM—not targeting dialysis start)
```

**Very Advanced CKM (eGFR <10, Symptomatic):**
```
Weekly to Every 2 Weeks:
- Basic metabolic panel (K+ especially)
- As needed based on symptoms

Focus Shifts to Symptom Control Rather Than Lab Values
```

#### Clinical Monitoring

**Every Visit:**
- Weight, blood pressure
- Volume status (edema, lung auscultation)
- Symptom assessment (standardized tool: POS-S Renal)
- Medication review and adjustment
- Psychosocial screening

**Palliative Outcome Scale - Symptoms Renal (POS-S Renal):**
- Validated tool for CKM symptom assessment
- 17-item questionnaire covering:
  - Physical symptoms: Pain, nausea, dyspnea, pruritus, fatigue
  - Psychosocial: Anxiety, depression, family concern
  - Spiritual: Peace, life completion
- Score 0-4 for each item (0 = no problem, 4 = overwhelming)
- Helps guide symptom management intensification

### Outcomes and Quality Metrics in CKM

**Quality Indicators (2024 Renal Physicians Association CKM Guidelines):**
1. **Advance directive documentation**: ≥80% of CKM patients
2. **Palliative care involvement**: ≥60% within 3 months of CKM start
3. **Symptom assessment at every visit**: 100% (use POS-S Renal)
4. **Hospitalization rate**: <2 admissions per patient-year
5. **Deaths at preferred location**: ≥70% (usually home)
6. **Hospice enrollment before death**: ≥50% (median ≥30 days)

**Typical CKM Patient Outcomes (2024 Data):**
```
Survival:
- Median survival from CKM start (eGFR ~10): 6-18 months
- Range: 3 months to 3+ years (highly variable by comorbidities)
- 25% alive at 2 years, 10% at 3 years

Symptom Control:
- Good-excellent symptom control: 75-85% (with active management)
- Refractory symptoms requiring specialist palliative care: 15-20%

Place of Death:
- Home: 60-70% (with hospice support)
- Hospital: 20-30% (declining with better CKM programs)
- Nursing home: 10-15%

Hospitalizations:
- Median: 1-2 per patient-year (vs 3-5 for dialysis)
- Most common reasons: Volume overload, hyperkalemia, infection

Hospice Utilization:
- Ever enrolled in hospice: 60-70%
- Median hospice LOS: 30-45 days
- Hospice LOS >7 days: 70% (allows meaningful support)
```

### Key Practice Points: Conservative Kidney Management ⭐

#### Board Exam High-Yield Concepts
1. **CKM Definition**: Active management of CKD Stage 4-5 without dialysis—NOT "doing nothing"
2. **Appropriate Candidates**: Age >75-80 + frailty (CFS ≥6), Charlson ≥8, limited life expectancy <12 months
3. **Bansal Score**: Predicts 6-month dialysis mortality; ≥8 points = high risk, consider CKM
4. **Survival**: Dialysis benefit unclear/absent in elderly (>80) + frail; CKM may offer better QOL
5. **Palliative Care**: Early involvement (at CKM decision), NOT just end-of-life—symptom control, psychosocial support
6. **Symptom Management**: Ondansetron/metoclopramide (nausea), gabapentin (pruritus/RLS), oxycodone (pain—safe in CKD)
7. **Potassium Control**: Diet + patiromer/Lokelma; transition to dialysis if recurrent K+ >6.5-7.0
8. **Advance Directives**: POLST form (physician order), DNR/DNI, healthcare proxy—complete early in CKM
9. **Hospice Criteria**: Prognosis <6 months, GFR <15 + uremic symptoms, declining functional status
10. **Quality Metrics**: Advance directive ≥80%, palliative care ≥60%, hospitalization <2/year, home death ≥70%

#### Clinical Pearls
- **CKM is not giving up—it's active, patient-centered care** focused on quality of life
- **Shared decision-making is paramount**—neither dialysis nor CKM is inherently "right"
- **Survival benefit of dialysis diminishes with age/comorbidity**—personalize the discussion
- **Liberalize diet in CKM to avoid malnutrition** (less strict restrictions than dialysis)
- **Oxycodone/hydromorphone preferred opioids**—morphine accumulates in CKD (M3G toxicity)
- **Gross hematuria is common and benign**—reassure patient
- **Palliative care early, hospice late**—palliative care at CKM start, hospice when prognosis <6 months
- **Most CKM patients die at home** (60-70%) with hospice support—achievable with planning
- **POLST form is key**—portable medical order, travels with patient, ensures wishes honored
- **Re-assess goals periodically**—CKM patients can change mind and start dialysis if circumstances change

### Modality Education

#### Hemodialysis Information
**In-Center HD:**
- 3 times weekly, 3-4 hours
- Staff-monitored treatments
- Social environment
- Transportation needs

**Home HD:**
- More frequent options
- Training required (2-6 weeks)
- Equipment/space needs
- Greater independence

#### Peritoneal Dialysis Information
**CAPD:**
- Manual exchanges, 4-5 daily
- Nocturnal options
- Continuous clearance
- Lifestyle flexibility

**APD:**
- Automated overnight
- Daytime dry periods
- Machine required
- Better for working patients

#### Transplant Information
**Living Donor:**
- Better outcomes
- Shorter wait time
- Donor evaluation process
- Surgical risks

**Deceased Donor:**
- Wait time 3-7 years
- Allocation system (KAS)
- Cold ischemia time
- Outcomes slightly lower

### Vascular Access Planning

#### Fistula First Approach
**Timing:**
- Create 6-12 months before need
- Allow 3-6 months for maturation
- Monitor maturation weekly
- Early intervention if needed

**Evaluation:**
- Vein mapping with ultrasound
- Physical examination
- Allen's test if radial
- Consider central stenosis

#### Catheter Last Strategy
**Tunneled Catheters:**
- Only if no other options
- Right IJ preferred
- Limit use to <3 months
- High infection risk

**Avoidance Strategies:**
- Early access planning
- Education on importance
- Regular follow-up
- Prompt intervention

### Psychosocial Preparation

#### Depression Screening
- PHQ-9 questionnaire
- Regular mental health assessment
- Early intervention
- Support groups

#### Anxiety Management
- Education reduces uncertainty
- Relaxation techniques
- Counseling services
- Peer support programs

#### Family Involvement
- Family education sessions

- Frailty assessment

- Simplified regimens
- Fall prevention

#### Dialysis Decision-Making
- Survival benefit less clear
- Functional status key factor
- Conservative management viable
- Shared decision-making essential

### Pediatric Patients

#### Special Considerations
- Growth and development
- Age-specific eGFR equations
- Congenital anomalies common
- Family-centered care
- Transition to adult care

#### Management Differences
- Growth hormone therapy
- Nutrition optimization
- Bone health monitoring
- Psychosocial support
- Education planning

### Pregnant Patients

#### Physiologic Changes
- GFR increases 50%
- Creatinine decreases
- Proteinuria may increase
- Blood pressure targets different

#### Management Goals
- Maintain eGFR >60
- Blood pressure <140/90
- Avoid teratogenic medications
- Monitor fetal growth
- Multidisciplinary care

#### Medication Adjustments
- ACEi/ARBs contraindicated
- Prefer labetalol, nifedipine
- Continue safe immunosuppression
- Adjust dosing for GFR changes

### Diabetic Patients

#### Aggressive Control Needed
- HbA1c target 7-8%
- Blood pressure <130/80
- SGLT2 inhibitors essential
- GLP-1 agonists beneficial
- Comprehensive foot care

#### Complication Monitoring
- Annual retinal exams
- Regular foot examinations
- Cardiovascular risk assessment
- Neuropathy screening
- Autonomic dysfunction testing

### Cardiovascular Disease Patients

#### High-Risk Management
- Aggressive risk factor modification
- Multiple medication classes
- Close cardiology collaboration
- Interventional considerations
- Device therapy planning

#### Medication Interactions
- Contrast avoidance strategies
- Anticoagulation management
- Diuretic optimization
- ACEi/ARB continuation
- SGLT2 inhibitor benefits

## 13. Key Take-Home Points

1. **Early Detection**: Screen high-risk patients annually with eGFR and UACR
2. **Accurate Staging**: Use KDIGO GFR and albuminuria categories for prognosis
3. **Etiology Identification**: Determine and treat underlying cause when possible
4. **Risk Stratification**: Use heat map and progression risk for management intensity
5. **Complication Management**: Aggressively treat hypertension, anemia, and MBD
6. **SGLT2 Inhibitors**: Use in most CKD patients for cardio-renal protection
7. **Conservative Management**: Consider in elderly/comorbid patients
8. **Transition Planning**: Start 12 months before anticipated ESRD
9. **Patient Education**: Essential for self-management and adherence
10. **Multidisciplinary Care**: Collaborate with primary care, cardiology, dietitians
