**Core Domain:** Clinical Nephrology I  
**Difficulty Level:** Intermediate-Advanced

**Clinical Impact:** High  
**Regulatory Requirements:** None specific

## 1. Renal Tubular Physiology & Pathophysiology

### Normal Tubular Function

#### Proximal Tubule (65% of reabsorption)
**Reabsorption Functions:**
- 65% of filtered sodium and water
- 100% of filtered glucose and amino acids
- 80-90% of bicarbonate
- 50% of filtered phosphate
- 65% of filtered potassium
- 20% of filtered calcium
- 90% of filtered uric acid

**Transport Mechanisms:**
- Na+/K+ ATPase on basolateral membrane
- Na+/H+ exchanger (NHE3) on apical membrane
- Na+/glucose cotransporters (SGLT2, SGLT1)
- Na+/phosphate cotransporters (NaPi-IIa, NaPi-IIc)
- Na+/amino acid cotransporters

#### Loop of Henle (25% of reabsorption)
**Thick Ascending Limb:**
- Na+/K+/2Cl- cotransporter (NKCC2)
- Reabsorbs 25% of filtered sodium
- Generates medullary concentration gradient
- Site of loop diuretic action
- Paracellular calcium and magnesium reabsorption

**Thin Descending Limb:**
- High water permeability (AQP1)
- Passive water reabsorption
- Concentrates tubular fluid

**Thin Ascending Limb:**
- Low water permeability
- Passive sodium chloride reabsorption
- Dilutes tubular fluid

#### Distal Tubule & Collecting Duct (10% of reabsorption)
**Distal Convoluted Tubule:**
- Na+/Cl- cotransporter (NCC)
- Site of thiazide diuretic action
- Calcium reabsorption via TRPV5 channel
- Early potassium secretion

**Collecting Duct:**
- Principal cells: sodium reabsorption (ENaC), potassium secretion
- Intercalated cells: acid-base regulation
- Water reabsorption via AQP2 (ADH-dependent)
- Final site of urine concentration

### Pathophysiological Mechanisms

#### Tubular Injury Patterns
**Acute Tubular Injury:**
- Ischemic or toxic insults
- Cell swelling and necrosis
- Loss of brush border
- Tubular obstruction
- Back-leak of filtrate

**Chronic Tubulointerstitial Fibrosis:**
- Persistent inflammation
- Fibroblast activation
- Extracellular matrix deposition
- Tubular atrophy
- Vascular rarefaction

#### Transport Defects
**Genetic Transport Defects:**
- Mutations in transport proteins
- Congenital syndromes
- Often present in childhood
- May be progressive

**Acquired Transport Defects:**
- Autoantibodies against transporters
- Drug-induced inhibition
- Metabolic disturbances
- Secondary to other renal diseases

## 2. Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)

### Classification & Etiology

#### Ischemic ATN
**Causes:**
- Prolonged hypotension (>60 minutes MAP <65 mmHg)
- Major surgery (cardiac, vascular, abdominal)
- Severe trauma with hemorrhage
- Sepsis with hemodynamic compromise
- Burns (>20% BSA)
- Cardiogenic shock

**Pathophysiology:**
- Medullary hypoxia (high metabolic demand)
- ATP depletion
- Cytoskeletal disruption
- Cell polarity loss
- Inflammatory cascade activation

**Risk Factors:**
- Pre-existing CKD
- Diabetes mellitus
- Advanced age
- Atherosclerotic disease
- Volume depletion

#### Nephrotoxic ATN
**Contrast-Induced Nephropathy:**
**Risk Stratification:**
- Baseline eGFR <45 mL/min/1.73m²
- Diabetes mellitus
- Dehydration
- High contrast volume (>100 mL)
- Multiple contrast exposures

**Prevention Strategies:**
- Pre- and post-procedure isotonic saline
- Minimize contrast volume
- Use low-osmolar or iso-osmolar contrast
- Consider alternative imaging
- N-acetylcysteine not proven effective

**Aminoglycoside Nephrotoxicity:**
**Mechanism:**
- Accumulation in proximal tubular cells
- Phospholipidosis
- Lysosomal dysfunction
- Cell necrosis

**Risk Factors:**
- High peak levels
- Prolonged therapy (>7 days)
- Pre-existing CKD
- Volume depletion
- Concurrent nephrotoxins

**Prevention:**
- Therapeutic drug monitoring
- Once-daily dosing
- Adequate hydration
- Avoid concurrent nephrotoxins
- Limit duration of therapy

**Amphotericin B Toxicity:**
**Mechanism:**
- Direct tubular cell membrane damage
- Renal vasoconstriction
- Distal RTA development
- Magnesium and potassium wasting

**Risk Reduction:**
- Liposomal formulations
- Aggressive hydration
- Electrolyte supplementation
- Sodium loading

**Other Nephrotoxins:**
- Acyclovir (crystal nephropathy)
- Methotrexate (precipitation)
- Tenofovir (proximal tubulopathy)
- Ifosfamide (Fanconi syndrome)
- Cisplatin (magnesium wasting)
- Calcineurin inhibitors (afferent vasoconstriction)

### Clinical Presentation

#### Early Phase (1-3 days)
- Rising serum creatinine
- Decreased urine output (oliguric in 50%)
- Urine sediment: muddy brown granular casts
- Fractional excretion of sodium >2%
- Urine osmolality <350 mOsm/kg

#### Maintenance Phase (3-10 days)
- Stable but elevated creatinine
- Continued oliguria or polyuria phase
- Fluid overload common
- Electrolyte abnormalities
- Need for renal replacement therapy in 20-30%

#### Recovery Phase (7-21 days)
- Gradual creatinine decline
- Polyuria with sodium wasting
- Tubular function recovery lags GFR
- Risk of hypovolemia and electrolyte loss
- Most recover baseline function

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Urine Sediment Analysis
**Characteristic Findings:**
- Muddy brown granular casts
- Renal tubular epithelial cells
- Occasional WBC casts
- Few RBCs unless glomerular involvement

**Differential Features:**
- Pre-renal: hyaline casts, bland sediment
- AIN: WBC casts, eosinophils
- Glomerular: RBC casts, dysmorphic RBCs

#### Urine Chemistries
**FENa (Fractional Excretion of Sodium):**
- >2%: Typical for ATN
- <1%: Suggests pre-renal azotemia
- Limitations: diuretic use, CKD, contrast nephropathy

**FEUrea (Fractional Excretion of Urea):**
- >50%: ATN
- <35%: Pre-renal
- More reliable with diuretic use

**Urine Osmolality:**
- <350 mOsm/kg: ATN
- >500 mOsm/kg: Pre-renal

#### Biomarkers
**NGAL (Neutrophil Gelatinase-Associated Lipocalin):**
- Rises within 2-6 hours
- Predicts need for RRT
- Distinguishes pre-renal vs ATN
- Higher in ischemic vs nephrotoxic

**KIM-1 (Kidney Injury Molecule-1):**
- Proximal tubule injury marker
- Specific for AKI
- Predicts outcomes

**IL-18 (Interleukin-18):**
- Inflammatory marker
- Predicts mortality
- Rises early in ATN

### Management Strategies

#### General Principles
- Remove/avoid nephrotoxins
- Optimize hemodynamics
- Maintain euvolemia
- Prevent complications
- Consider early nephrology consultation

#### Hemodynamic Support
**Fluid Management:**
- Isotonic crystalloids
- Avoid hypervolemia
- Monitor central venous pressure
- Consider diuretic challenge

**Blood Pressure Support:**
- Maintain MAP >65 mmHg
- Norepinephrine first-line
- Avoid excessive vasoconstriction
- Consider invasive monitoring

#### Specific Therapies
**Diuretic Challenge:**
- Furosemide 1-2 mg/kg IV
- Continuous infusion if responsive
- Goal: prevent fluid overload
- Does not prevent RRT need

**Loop Diuretic Resistance:**
- Add thiazide diuretic
- Consider metolazone
- High-dose continuous infusion
- Monitor for ototoxicity

#### Renal Replacement Therapy Indications
**Absolute Indications:**
- Refractory hyperkalemia (>6.5 mEq/L)
- Severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.1)
- Volume overload with pulmonary edema
- Uremic complications (pericarditis, encephalopathy)
- Certain toxin removal

**Timing Considerations:**
- Early vs delayed RRT controversy
- Individualized decision-making
- Consider biomarker trends
- Factor in comorbidities

### Prevention Strategies

#### High-Risk Patient Identification
- Pre-existing CKD
- Diabetes mellitus
- Advanced age
- Volume depletion
- Planned nephrotoxin exposure

#### Pharmacologic Prevention
- N-acetylcysteine (limited evidence)
- Statins (some benefit in cardiac surgery)
- Theophylline (contrast nephropathy)
- Sodium bicarbonate (controversial)

#### Non-Pharmacologic Prevention
- Adequate hydration
- Minimize contrast volume
- Avoid concurrent nephrotoxins
- Optimize hemodynamics
- Close monitoring

## 3. Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN)

### Etiology & Classification

#### Drug-Induced AIN (70-80% of cases)
**Common Culprits:**
**Antibiotics:**
- β-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins)
- Quinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin)
- Sulfonamides (TMP-SMX)
- Rifampin
- Macrolides (rare)

**Proton Pump Inhibitors:**
- Omeprazole (most common)
- Pantoprazole
- Esomeprazole
- Lansoprazole
- Rabeprazole

**NSAIDs:**
- Ibuprofen
- Naproxen
- Diclofenac
- Indomethacin
- COX-2 inhibitors

**Diuretics:**
- Thiazides (HCTZ, chlorthalidone)
- Furosemide
- Spironolactone

**Others:**
- Allopurinol
- 5-ASA compounds
- Phenytoin
- Carbamazepine

#### Infection-Related AIN
**Bacterial:**
- Leptospirosis
- Brucellosis
- Legionella
- Salmonella
- Streptococcal infections

**Viral:**
- Epstein-Barr virus (EBV)
- Cytomegalovirus (CMV)
- HIV
- Hepatitis B/C
- Hantavirus

**Fungal:**
- Histoplasmosis
- Candidiasis (rare)
- Aspergillosis (rare)

#### Systemic Disease-Associated AIN
**Autoimmune Diseases:**
- Sarcoidosis (non-caseating granulomas)
- Sjögren's syndrome
- Systemic lupus erythematosus
- IgG4-related disease
- Tubulointerstitial nephritis with uveitis (TINU)

**Other:**
- Inflammatory bowel disease
- Celiac disease
- Primary biliary cirrhosis

### Clinical Presentation

#### Classic Triad (Present in 30-40%)
- Fever (70-80%)
- Rash (30-50%)
- Arthralgias (40-60%)

#### Renal Manifestations
- Acute rise in creatinine (days to weeks)
- Non-nephrotic proteinuria (<1 g/day)
- Sterile pyuria with WBC casts
- Microscopic hematuria (50%)
- Eosinophiluria (Hansel's stain)

#### Temporal Relationship
- Typical onset: 7-14 days after drug exposure
- Can occur earlier on re-exposure
- May continue after drug discontinuation
- Variable recovery time (weeks to months)

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Laboratory Evaluation
**Urinalysis:**
- WBC casts (pathognomonic)
- Eosinophils (Hansel's stain)
- RBCs (occasional)
- Proteinuria (mild)

**Blood Tests:**
- Elevated ESR/CRP
- Eosinophilia (60-80%)
- Elevated IgE (occasional)
- Normal complement (usually)

**Drug Levels:**
- Consider if applicable
- Therapeutic drug monitoring
- Toxicity assessment

#### Imaging Studies
**Renal Ultrasound:**
- Usually normal
- May show enlarged kidneys
- Rule out obstruction
- Assess kidney size

**CT/MRI:**
- Not typically required
- Consider if atypical presentation
- Rule out other causes

#### Kidney Biopsy Indications
- Atypical presentation
- No improvement after drug discontinuation
- Need for definitive diagnosis
- Exclusion of other diseases
- Persistent renal dysfunction

**Biopsy Findings:**
- Interstitial edema and inflammation
- Lymphocyte and eosinophil infiltration
- Tubulitis (inflammatory cells in tubules)
- Granuloma formation (sarcoid, drug-induced)
- Minimal glomerular changes

### Management Strategies

#### Immediate Actions
**Drug Discontinuation:**
- Stop all potentially offending agents
- Review complete medication list
- Consider drug holidays
- Document reaction

**Supportive Care:**
- Adequate hydration
- Electrolyte management
- Monitor renal function
- Avoid further nephrotoxins

#### Corticosteroid Therapy
**Indications:**
- No improvement after 5-7 days off drug
- Severe renal failure
- Biopsy-proven AIN
- Systemic disease involvement

**Regimens:**
- Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day
- Duration: 4-6 weeks
- Taper gradually over 2-4 weeks
- Consider pulse steroids for severe cases

**Response:**
- Improvement usually within 1-2 weeks
- Complete recovery in 60-80%
- Partial recovery in 15-20%
- Persistent CKD in 5-10%

#### Special Situations
**NSAID-Induced AIN:**
- Often presents with nephrotic syndrome
- Minimal change disease component
- May require longer steroid course
- Higher risk of incomplete recovery

**PPI-Induced AIN:**
- Most common cause currently
- Often severe presentation
- May have delayed recovery
- Consider permanent PPI avoidance

**Re-challenge Considerations:**
- Generally contraindicated
- Only if essential and no alternatives
- Desensitization protocols (rare)
- Close monitoring if attempted

### Prognosis & Follow-up

#### Recovery Predictors
- Early drug discontinuation
- Younger age
- Absence of interstitial fibrosis on biopsy
- No baseline CKD
- Prompt steroid initiation

#### Long-term Outcomes
- Complete recovery: 60-80%
- Partial recovery: 15-20%
- Chronic kidney disease: 5-10%
- Risk of recurrence with re-exposure

#### Monitoring Protocol
- Serum creatinine weekly for 4 weeks
- Then monthly for 3 months
- Urinalysis at each visit
- Monitor for steroid side effects
- Document medication allergies

## 4. Renal Tubular Acidosis (RTA)

### Classification & Pathophysiology

#### Type 1 (Distal) RTA
**Pathophysiology:**
- Impaired distal acidification
- Inability to excrete hydrogen ions
- Defective H+-ATPase in collecting duct
- Urine pH cannot be lowered below 5.5

**Etiologies:**
**Genetic:**
- Autosomal dominant (SLC4A1 mutation)
- Autosomal recessive (ATP6V1B1, ATP6V0A4)
- Associated with sensorineural deafness

**Acquired:**
- Autoimmune diseases (Sjögren's, SLE, RA)
- Hypercalciuria and nephrocalcinosis
- Obstructive uropathy
- Renal transplantation
- Amphotericin B toxicity

**Clinical Features:**
- Growth failure in children
- Nephrocalcinosis and nephrolithiasis
- Bone demineralization (rickets/osteomalacia)
- Muscle weakness
- Polyuria and polydipsia

#### Type 2 (Proximal) RTA
**Pathophysiology:**
- Impaired bicarbonate reabsorption
- Defect in proximal tubule Na+/H+ exchange
- Bicarbonate wasting until serum level falls to threshold
- Urine pH can be acidified when serum HCO3- low

**Etiologies:**
**Isolated:**
- Usually sporadic
- May be genetic (rare)
- Transient in infants

**Secondary:**
- Fanconi syndrome (multiple transport defects)
- Cystinosis
- Wilson's disease
- Tyrosinemia
- Ifosfamide toxicity
- Heavy metals (lead, cadmium)
- Multiple myeloma

**Clinical Features:**
- Growth retardation
- Bone disease (less severe than Type 1)
- Polyuria
- Associated with other proximal defects

#### Type 3 (Mixed) RTA
**Features:**
- Combined distal and proximal defects
- Rare, usually genetic
- Carbonic anhydrase II deficiency
- Associated with osteopetrosis
- Cerebral calcifications

#### Type 4 (Hyperkalemic) RTA
**Pathophysiology:**
- Aldosterone deficiency or resistance
- Impaired potassium and hydrogen secretion
- Mild metabolic acidosis
- Hyperkalemia is prominent feature

**Etiologies:**
**Hyporeninemic Hypoaldosteronism:**
- Diabetic nephropathy (most common)
- Chronic interstitial nephritis
- NSAID use
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs

**Aldosterone Resistance:**
- Obstructive uropathy
- Sickle cell disease
- Pseudohypoaldosteronism type 1
- Amiloride-sensitive ENaC defects

**Clinical Features:**
- Hyperkalemia (often asymptomatic)
- Mild metabolic acidosis
- Normal anion gap
- May have hypertension

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Laboratory Evaluation
**Basic Metabolic Panel:**
- Low serum bicarbonate (<22 mEq/L)
- Normal anion gap (Types 1, 2, 4)
- Hyperkalemia (Type 4)
- Hypokalemia (Types 1, 2)

**Urine Studies:**
**Urine pH:**
- Type 1: >5.5 despite acidosis
- Type 2: Variable, can be <5.5
- Type 4: <5.5 (can acidify)

**Urine Anion Gap:**
- Positive: impaired NH4+ excretion (Types 1, 4)
- Negative: normal NH4+ excretion (Type 2)

**Fractional Excretion of Bicarbonate:**
- >15%: Type 2 RTA
- <5%: Type 1 RTA

#### Specialized Testing
**Ammonium Chloride Loading Test:**
- Confirm distal RTA
- Measure urine pH after acid load
- Cannot lower pH below 5.5 in Type 1

**Bicarbonate Titration:**
- Identify proximal RTA
- Give bicarbonate until serum normal
- Measure urinary bicarbonate loss

**Aldosterone Levels:**
- Evaluate Type 4 RTA
- Check renin activity
- Assess adrenal function

### Treatment Strategies

#### Type 1 RTA
**Alkali Therapy:**
- Sodium bicarbonate or potassium citrate
- Goal: serum HCO3- 22-24 mEq/L
- Dose: 1-3 mEq/kg/day divided
- Monitor for volume overload

**Potassium Management:**
- Potassium citrate preferred
- Corrects hypokalemia and acidosis
- Reduces stone formation
- Monitor serum potassium

**Stone Prevention:**
- High fluid intake
- Low sodium diet
- Thiazide diuretics if hypercalciuria
- Citrate supplementation

#### Type 2 RTA
**Bicarbonate Supplementation:**
- Higher doses needed (Type 1)
- 5-15 mEq/kg/day divided
- May need continuous supplementation
- Monitor for volume overload

**Treat Underlying Cause:**
- Ifosfamide: discontinue drug
- Cystinosis: cysteamine therapy
- Wilson's disease: chelation therapy
- Multiple myeloma: chemotherapy

**Address Associated Defects:**
- Phosphate supplementation
- Vitamin D therapy
- Growth hormone in children

#### Type 4 RTA
**Hyperkalemia Management:**
**Dietary Measures:**
- Low potassium diet
- Avoid potassium supplements
- Limit salt substitutes

**Medications:**
- Loop diuretics (furosemide)
- Thiazide diuretics
- Sodium bicarbonate
- Fludrocortisone (if aldosterone deficient)

**Avoid Potassium-Raising Drugs:**
- ACE inhibitors/ARBs (if possible)
- NSAIDs
- Potassium-sparing diuretics
- Beta-blockers (high doses)

#### Monitoring
- Serum electrolytes weekly initially
- Then monthly once stable
- Urine pH monitoring
- Growth parameters in children
- Bone density assessment

## 5. Fanconi Syndrome

### Definition & Pathophysiology

#### Definition
- Generalized proximal tubular dysfunction
- Multiple transport defects
- Can be inherited or acquired
- Affects solute and water reabsorption

#### Pathophysiology
- Proximal tubular cell injury
- Mitochondrial dysfunction
- Transport protein defects
- Energy metabolism impairment
- Secondary to various systemic diseases

### Etiology

#### Genetic Causes
**Cystinosis:**
- Autosomal recessive
- CTNS gene mutation
- Cystine crystal accumulation
- Most common inherited cause

**Hereditary Fructose Intolerance:**
- Aldolase B deficiency
- Accumulation of fructose-1-phosphate
- Symptoms after fructose ingestion

**Tyrosinemia Type I:**
- FAH gene mutation
- Succinylacetone accumulation
- Liver and kidney involvement

**Wilson's Disease:**
- ATP7B gene mutation
- Copper accumulation
- Liver disease, Kayser-Fleischer rings

**Dent Disease:**
- CLCN5 gene mutation
- X-linked inheritance
- Low molecular weight proteinuria

#### Acquired Causes
**Multiple Myeloma:**
- Light chain toxicity
- Cast nephropathy component
- Paraprotein-related injury

**Ifosfamide Toxicity:**
- Chemotherapy-induced
- Dose-dependent
- May be irreversible

**Heavy Metal Toxicity:**
- Lead, cadmium, mercury
- Occupational exposure
- Environmental contamination

**Tenofovir Toxicity:**
- HIV medication
- Mitochondrial toxicity
- Usually reversible

**Autoimmune Diseases:**
- Sjögren's syndrome
- Systemic lupus erythematosus
- Sarcoidosis

### Clinical Features

#### Proximal Tubular Defects
**Glycosuria:**
- Normal blood glucose
- Glucose in urine
- Polyuria, polydipsia

**Phosphaturia:**
- Hypophosphatemia
- Bone pain, fractures
- Rickets in children

**Bicarbonaturia:**
- Type 2 RTA
- Growth failure
- Bone demineralization

**Uric Acid Wasting:**
- Hypouricemia
- Gout symptoms (paradoxically)

**Aminoaciduria:**
- Generalized amino acid loss
- Low molecular weight proteinuria

**Other Defects:**
- Sodium wasting
- Potassium wasting
- Calcium wasting
- Water wasting (polyuria)

#### Systemic Manifestations
- Growth retardation (children)
- Bone disease (rickets/osteomalacia)
- Muscle weakness
- Fatigue
- Dehydration risk

### Diagnostic Approach

#### Laboratory Evaluation
**Urinalysis:**
- Glycosuria with normal glucose
- Phosphate crystals
- Amino acids (urine chromatography)
- Low molecular weight proteins
- Bicarbonate in urine

**Blood Tests:**
- Hypophosphatemia
- Hypouricemia
- Metabolic acidosis
- Hypokalemia
- Normal blood glucose

**Specialized Tests:**
- Urine amino acid chromatography
- Beta-2 microglobulin (LMW protein)
- Retinol binding protein
- Fractional excretion studies

#### Etiologic Workup
**Genetic Testing:**
- Cystinosis gene panel
- Wilson's disease testing
- Tyrosinemia screening
- Dent disease mutation analysis

**Systemic Disease Evaluation:**
- Serum/urine protein electrophoresis
- Autoimmune serologies
- Heavy metal levels
- Medication review

#### Kidney Biopsy
- Indications: unclear etiology
- Findings: proximal tubular degeneration
- May show crystal deposition
- Helps exclude other diseases

### Treatment Strategies

#### General Principles
- Treat underlying cause
- Replace lost substances
- Prevent complications
- Monitor growth and development

#### Specific Replacement
**Phosphate Supplementation:**
- Oral phosphate preparations
- Goal: serum phosphate 2.5-4.5 mg/dL
- Divide doses to avoid diarrhea
- Monitor calcium levels

**Bicarbonate Therapy:**
- Sodium or potassium bicarbonate
- Goal: serum HCO3- 22-24 mEq/L
- Higher doses than isolated RTA
- Monitor for volume overload

**Vitamin D Therapy:**
- Calcitriol (active vitamin D)
- Improves calcium absorption
- Helps bone mineralization
- Monitor calcium and phosphate

**Water and Electrolytes:**
- Adequate hydration
- Sodium supplementation if needed
- Potassium supplementation
- Monitor urine output

#### Disease-Specific Therapy
**Cystinosis:**
- Cysteamine therapy
- Dose: 60-90 mg/kg/day divided
- Improves growth and renal outcomes
- Monitor white blood cell cystine levels

**Ifosfamide Toxicity:**
- Discontinue if possible
- Supportive care
- May be irreversible
- Consider dialysis if ESRD

**Multiple Myeloma:**
- Chemotherapy for myeloma
- Bortezomib-based regimens
- Consider autologous stem cell transplant
- Supportive renal care

**Tenofovir Toxicity:**
- Discontinue tenofovir
- Switch to alternative ART
- Usually reversible
- Monitor renal function recovery

#### Monitoring
- Growth parameters (children)
- Bone age assessment
- Renal function tests
- Electrolytes weekly initially
- Bone density testing

## 6. Inherited Tubular Disorders

### Bartter Syndrome

#### Classification & Genetics
**Type I (SLC12A1):**
- NKCC2 gene mutation
- Neonatal presentation
- Severe polyhydramnios

**Type II (KCNJ1):**
- ROMK gene mutation
- Neonatal presentation
- Hyperkalemia in neonatal period

**Type III (CLCNKB):**
- CLC-Kb gene mutation
- Classic presentation
- Variable severity

**Type IV (BSND):**
- Barttin gene mutation
- Sensorineural deafness
- Severe presentation

**Type V (CASR):**
- Calcium-sensing receptor mutation
- Autosomal dominant
- Milder presentation

#### Pathophysiology
- Impaired sodium chloride reabsorption
- Volume depletion mimicking diuretic use
- Secondary hyperaldosteronism
- Increased prostaglandin E2 production
- Juxtaglomerular hyperplasia

#### Clinical Features
**Neonatal Form:**
- Polyhydramnios
- Premature birth
- Severe dehydration
- Growth failure
- Hypercalciuria, nephrocalcinosis

**Classic Form:**
- Polyuria, polydipsia
- Salt craving
- Growth retardation
- Muscle weakness, cramps
- Normal blood pressure

#### Laboratory Findings
- Hypokalemia
- Metabolic alkalosis
- Normal blood pressure
- Elevated renin and aldosterone
- Hypercalciuria (Types I, II)
- Hypomagnesemia (Type III)

#### Treatment
**Indomethacin Therapy:**
- 1-3 mg/kg/day divided
- Reduces prostaglandin production
- Improves growth and symptoms
- Monitor for GI side effects

**Electrolyte Supplementation:**
- Potassium chloride
- Sodium chloride
- Magnesium supplementation
- Monitor levels frequently

**Other Measures:**
- ACE inhibitors (reduce hyperaldosteronism)
- High fluid intake
- Monitor renal function
- Growth monitoring

### Gitelman Syndrome

#### Genetics & Pathophysiology
- SLC12A3 gene mutation
- Thiazide-sensitive Na+/Cl- cotransporter defect
- Autosomal recessive inheritance
- Milder than Bartter syndrome

#### Clinical Features
- Often asymptomatic
- Muscle weakness, cramps
- Tetany, paresthesias
- Salt craving
- Normal growth
- May present in adolescence/adulthood

#### Laboratory Findings
- Hypokalemia
- Metabolic alkalosis
- Hypomagnesemia (characteristic)
- Hypocalciuria (distinguishing feature)
- Normal blood pressure
- Elevated renin

#### Treatment
**Electrolyte Replacement:**
- Magnesium supplementation (high doses)
- Potassium chloride
- Oral sodium chloride

**Symptom Management:**
- Potassium-sparing diuretics
- NSAIDs (less effective than Bartter)
- ACE inhibitors (consider)

**Monitoring:**
- Electrolytes regularly
- Cardiac evaluation (QT interval)
- Growth parameters
- Quality of life assessment

### Liddle Syndrome

#### Genetics & Pathophysiology
- SCNN1B or SCNN1G gene mutations
- ENaC channel gain of function
- Autosomal dominant inheritance
- Mimics hyperaldosteronism

#### Clinical Features
- Early-onset hypertension
- Hypokalemia
- Metabolic alkalosis
- Low renin and aldosterone
- Family history

#### Laboratory Findings
- Hypertension (often severe)
- Hypokalemia
- Metabolic alkalosis
- Suppressed renin and aldosterone
- Sodium retention

#### Treatment
**ENaC Blockers:**
- Amiloride 5-10 mg daily
- Triamterene alternative
- Dramatic response
- Avoid spironolactone

**Blood Pressure Control:**
- Low sodium diet
- Additional antihypertensives if needed
- Monitor electrolytes
- Family screening

## 7. Drug-Induced Tubular Disorders

### Common Nephrotoxic Agents

#### Aminoglycosides
**Mechanism:**
- Accumulation in proximal tubular cells
- Phospholipidosis
- Lysosomal dysfunction
- Cell necrosis

**Risk Factors:**
- High peak levels
- Prolonged therapy
- Pre-existing CKD
- Volume depletion
- Concurrent nephrotoxins

**Prevention:**
- Therapeutic drug monitoring
- Once-daily dosing
- Adequate hydration
- Limit duration

#### Amphotericin B
**Mechanism:**
- Direct tubular cell membrane damage
- Renal vasoconstriction
- Distal RTA
- Magnesium and potassium wasting

**Risk Reduction:**
- Liposomal formulations
- Aggressive hydration
- Electrolyte supplementation
- Sodium loading

#### Cisplatin
**Mechanism:**
- Proximal tubular uptake via transporters
- DNA cross-linking
- Cell death
- Magnesium wasting

**Prevention:**
- Aggressive hydration
- Magnesium supplementation
- Amifostine (controversial)
- Alternative agents if possible

#### Ifosfamide
**Mechanism:**
- Metabolite chloroacetaldehyde toxicity
- Mitochondrial dysfunction
- Fanconi syndrome
- May be irreversible

**Risk Factors:**
- Cumulative dose >60 g/m²
- Prior cisplatin exposure
- Young age
- Pre-existing CKD

#### Tenofovir
**Mechanism:**
- Mitochondrial DNA polymerase gamma inhibition
- Proximal tubular dysfunction
- Fanconi syndrome
- Usually reversible

**Monitoring:**
- Serum creatinine every 3-6 months
- Urine protein and glucose
- Phosphate levels
- Consider alternative if toxicity

### Prevention Strategies

#### Risk Assessment
- Identify high-risk patients
- Review medication list
- Assess baseline renal function
- Consider alternative agents

#### Monitoring Protocols
- Baseline and serial creatinine
- Therapeutic drug monitoring
- Urine output monitoring
- Electrolyte assessment

#### Protective Measures
- Adequate hydration
- Avoid concurrent nephrotoxins
- Dose adjustments for GFR
- Early nephrology consultation

## 8. Clinical Algorithms & Decision Trees

### Acute Interstitial Nephritis Evaluation

```
Acute Kidney Injury + Urine Abnormalities
        ↓
Medication Review (Recent New Drugs?)
        ↓
Clinical Features: Fever, Rash, Arthralgias?
        ↓
Urinalysis: WBC Casts, Eosinophils?
        ↓
Laboratory: Eosinophilia, Elevated ESR/CRP?
        ↓
Action: Stop Suspected Drugs
        ↓
Re-evaluate in 5-7 days
        ↓
Improving? → Continue monitoring
Not improving? → Consider steroids + biopsy
```

### Renal Tubular Acidosis Workup

```
Metabolic Acidosis + Normal Anion Gap
        ↓
Serum Potassium Level
        ↓
Low K+ → Consider Type 1 or 2 RTA
High K+ → Consider Type 4 RTA
        ↓
Urine pH Assessment
        ↓
pH >5.5 → Type 1 RTA (distal)
pH <5.5 → Type 2 or 4 RTA
        ↓
Bicarbonate Titration Test
        ↓
FE HCO3- >15% → Type 2 RTA (proximal)
FE HCO3- <5% → Type 1 RTA (distal)
        ↓
Aldosterone/Renin Levels (if Type 4 suspected)
```

## 9. Key Take-Home Points

1. **ATN Recognition**: Identify muddy brown casts and FENa >2% for diagnosis
2. **AIN Suspicion**: Always consider drug-induced AIN in AKI with fever, rash, or eosinophils
3. **RTA Classification**: Use urine pH and serum potassium to differentiate RTA types
4. **Fanconi Syndrome**: Look for multiple proximal tubular defects together
5. **Drug Causation**: Always review medications in tubular disorders
6. **Genetic Considerations**: Consider inherited syndromes in children and young adults
7. **Treatment Principles**: Replace lost substances and treat underlying cause
8. **Monitoring Importance**: Close electrolyte monitoring essential in all tubular disorders
9. **Recovery Expectations**: ATN and AIN usually recover, but may progress to CKD
10. **Prevention Focus**: Avoid nephrotoxins and maintain adequate hydration
